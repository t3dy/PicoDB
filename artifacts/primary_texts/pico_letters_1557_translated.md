# Pico della Mirandola: Letters

*From the Opera Omnia, Basel 1557. The Epistolae section begins at the end of the Commento section and runs through line 10410. A Testimonia section (tributes and letters by others) follows lines 10410ff.*

---

## Letter to Aldo Manuzio

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA ALTO MANUTIO, S.

*[Date: Florence, 11 February 1490. Addressee: Aldo Manuzio (Aldus Manutius, 1449–1515), the Venetian printer and scholar — here addressed as a friend before he founded his famous press.]*

I am sending you the Homer you asked for — later than you wished and than I ought to have sent it. Such, my dear Aldo, is the crush of business upon me that I have no room even to breathe. I have long since enslaved myself — as you know — to letters, and they, as lords, exact from me so severe a daily reckoning of reading and writing that I can scarcely keep my health. As for what you say you plan to do: gird yourself for philosophy — but on this condition: that you remember there is no philosophy that calls us away from the truth of the mysteries. Philosophy seeks the truth; theology finds it; religion possesses it. Farewell. Florence, 11 February 1490.

## Letter to Ermolao Barbaro, Patriarch of Aquileia (on his election)

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, HERMOLAO BARBARO PATRIARCHAE AQUIL., S.

*[Undated. Addressee: Ermolao Barbaro the Younger (1454–1493), humanist scholar, commentator on Aristotle and Pliny, later appointed Patriarch of Aquileia by Pope Innocent VIII in 1491.]*

I hear that you have been elected Patriarch of Aquileia. I congratulate those people on having the best of shepherds, the Church universal on gaining its most zealous soldier, and you on being given an occasion to display and enlarge your virtue. There are, broadly speaking, three ways of life: the civic, the contemplative, and the religious. In the first we require prudence; in the second, learning; in the third — as though it were the summit of all living — we require, in addition to those two, also holiness. You, hitherto, have been proved by your city to be most diligent and most prudent in conducting public affairs and managing great matters of state; all lovers of learning regard you as a champion and defender of the liberal arts. That what remained should be accomplished by you, we were never in any doubt; and we rejoice with all our hearts that in future the Church shall owe you no less than your city has owed you, and no less than letters have owed you hitherto.

## Letter to Ermolao Barbaro (on the Hebrew books and Flavius Mithridates)

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, HERMOLAO SUO, S.

*[Date: Ferrara, 30 May 1492. Addressee: Ermolao Barbaro. This letter contains the famous passage about Pico's Hebrew studies with Flavius Mithridates and the arrival of Kabbalistic manuscripts. The passage appears at the tail of a longer letter; the opening of that letter begins at line 9851.]*

*[The opening of the letter, from line 9851, runs as follows:]*

What am I to make of this, my dear Ermolao, that you load me daily with new kindnesses, as though you owed something to me, when it is I who have long owed everything to you? Your brother Antonio came here recently on his way home, and I asked what Ermolao was doing. "This," he said: "he loves you, praises you, exalts you to the sky — before me, before others, before the Pope himself — and he does it so constantly, so vigorously, as though he had nothing else to do." Friends I have there also write to me from time to time; one in particular, Cosimo Padulius — a man worthy of being loved by you above all others [OCR: some words garbled here] — writes how you speak and feel about me always with the greatest honour, and of your extraordinary and unheard-of warmth, you who never think you have said enough in praise of men, and if you treat me so, who already love and revere you as much as any man can — though I cannot love you more or be more deeply in your debt.

*[The letter then continues with the Hebrew books passage:]*

But at present, my hands and my books — certain Hebrew volumes in which I have been immersed without ceasing for a full week, day and night, until they have nearly put out my eyes. For the man who brought them here, a certain Sicilian Jew [Siculus quidam Hebraeus — Flavius Mithridates], intends to leave within twenty days; and while I am disentangling myself from them, do not expect so much as a single letter from me, for I cannot leave them for a moment, lest he depart before I have worked through them all. When I am free of this business I shall overwhelm you with letters — you know how pressed I am. If ever you are prepared to do something for my sake, work by whatever means you can to ensure that the prince of Bari [OCR: "Btrri" — almost certainly "Barri," i.e., the Prince of Bari] does not grow weary waiting for my arrival, for I would disrupt all my studies, to which — as you know better than anyone — I am wholly devoted, though I care for nothing beyond them. Whether it would vex him more, or displease me more, I cannot quite determine. Farewell, and fear God; and think daily that you must die. Ferrara, 30 May 1492.

## Letter to Marsilio Ficino (requesting Iamblichus)

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, MARSILIO FICINO, S.

*[Undated. Addressee: Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499), philosopher, head of the Platonic Academy in Florence, Pico's friend and mentor. This is a brief, collegial note.]*

Those who press their friends with many arguments to get what they desire do an injustice to friendship, as though friendship itself could not accomplish what the blandishments of eloquence can. I shall therefore deal with you simply, as the goodwill long-established between us demands. At this time I urgently need, for my studies, the Platonist Iamblichus. If you send him to me, you will bind me to you in perpetual obligation. I shall have him for twenty days, or at most a month; after that he will be returned at once by messenger. In the meantime, make use of my library and all my possessions as your own. Farewell.

## Letter to Angelo Poliziano (on Epictetus and the life of philosophy)

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA ANGELO POLITIANO, S.

*[Undated. Addressee: Angelo Poliziano (Angelo Ambrogini, 1454–1494), poet, humanist, professor of Greek and Latin at Florence, Pico's closest friend. The letter discusses a visit during which Epictetus — probably a copy of Poliziano's Latin translation — was read aloud.]*

Never were my affections more elegantly or more gaily received than in these recent days spent with you; and inasmuch as they were befriended indeed, they most especially congratulate themselves, and owe you a great deal. For who would not be willing to die by that sword? And yet they grieve that it was done more indulgently than was proper, and are in two minds as to whether, out of love itself, you struck at my affections merely to shake them loose. And thus they waver — so far as to distrust their own judgment, ordinarily most weighty, in their own case. They trust themselves nonetheless, and declare themselves free from chains, not knowing how to stay indoors, demanding freedom and public safety.

But what shall I say of the charm of your Epictetus? A speech at once playful and fit to make even Cato laugh! There he was, barely on the threshold, spreading wide his cloak: "Here," he said, "are my spears and arrows — if you do not know Greek, here I am, ready to strike back at any of you who should prove bold." Who could have kept from laughing, seeing that Stoic philosopher joking so wittily? We did indeed keep from his weapons — both because he had threatened to repay any injury, and because his hide had grown so thick that such light blows could not penetrate it. With such reverence as we owed, then, we received the old man. And when at last he sat down among us he began to philosophize on ethics — and in Latin, too — not so much because those present knew no Latin (for there were people in that company who knew Greek) as because, thanks to you, he tasted Latin more brilliantly. He did not waste his effort: he did not stop speaking until he had made us all, from Peripatetics, into Stoics. And once we had approved all that he urged, it was a fine thing to see men who had shortly before been soft and delicate, now become the most enduring of all — men who can indeed be struck by others, but can be harmed only by ourselves; who will never struggle against fate; who will wish that what is not ours should come about only as God wills, and who will never blame or reproach them, nor grieve, nor remonstrate, nor know how to serve or to be conquered. Let us philosophize in deed, not in word.

[Page break in OCR — some lines missing or confused here; the text resumes:]

Our Emanuel was fuming as he read your [letters], unable to contain the Attic Athens. You are the man, my dear Angelo — away with flattery — to whom one or two, not to say none, of our contemporaries can be compared. If there were more like you, these times would have no reason to envy antiquity. Press on, I beg you, and retrieve letters as much as you can from the rust into which they have fallen, so that the splendour of the Roman tongue may not be wholly extinguished by the injuries of time. Always forge something new to aid and illuminate the Latin cause. And let what you have at home come out at last for the common benefit of scholars. I love and long for everything of yours just as I love you yourself — all the more because I have set you before me as the model upon which I am to be formed; and you are one whom all ought to follow but few can attain. Farewell. I dare promise nothing even moderate about myself; but may that Father of Lights, most clement, who has thus far given from that source something that made men hope something of us, give also in the future, so that we may not disappoint the good opinion now so well established. I am hastening to Rome, where we shall put our studies to the test. If we make any progress, it is God's gift; praise and thanks be to him. If we fall short, it is our weakness; charge it to us. For the rest, I am yours — I who, on account of your integrity of life and character, have scarcely yielded first place even to our Jerome in loving you. Farewell. From Fratta [Fratta Polesine].

## Letter to Tommaso Medio

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, THOMAE MEDIO, S.

*[Undated. Addressee: Tommaso Medio, a humanist friend. The letter praises his comedy Gyrotus.]*

I do not remember ever having been more solidly delighted than lately when I read your Gyrotus — a most charming little tale by any measure, both in the liveliness of its plot and in the learning of its style, challenging even the ancient comedies. For what shall I say of the new ones, which are plainly clumsy and tasteless, easily betraying the immaturity of their authors? It was truly worthy to be dedicated by name to Ermolao Barbaro — I cannot say whether more mine than yours — to whom alone, I believe, all men of letters owe as much as they scarcely owe to antiquity. That you did not deprive me of it was in keeping with that wholly evident goodwill toward me which you have shown all along. There is nothing I hold dear enough that, if I were to send it to you as a kind of loan, I would not think the gratitude repaid in full. The new comedy you promise me, I am already waiting and longing for it. And there is nothing I desire more, except one thing — your coming to us in person. If I did not have to leave here within ten days — as I believe you learned from our Sita [?OCR unclear] — I would certainly have prevailed upon you to come. One can infer from what you write how much delight there will be in your company, in your very presence, in the sound of your living voice; for you love all that is ours as well as we love it ourselves, and while from your natural facility of mind you indulge everyone, that very thing happens in my case above all — for everything in me flows from your source. I strive to emulate you, to follow in your footsteps; I neither cease nor intend to cease. Would that I might some day catch up with you. But I grieve that the distance between us means that I cannot hear you, as you usually do, gravely traversing the highest peaks of philosophy in my presence. For although the same things you say could be sent to me by letter — which I most wish you would grant me — yet the living voice has I know not what quality that moves and distresses the pupil more. Farewell.

## Letter to Girolamo Donato

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA HIERONYMO DONATO, S.

*[Undated. Addressee: Girolamo Donato (1457–1511), Venetian humanist and diplomat, later a cardinal.]*

I have received your letter — charming and learned — by which I have been no less delighted than I once was by your company when I was in Padua. Whenever the opportunity arose to enjoy you, I was always eager and most desirous of it, beyond even your expectation. For it was not hidden from me how sweet and gracious was Donato's mind, how overflowing and filled on every side with all good disciplines and every virtue. But such is the truth, my dear Girolamo: the free and ready availability of what we most desire makes us lazy and negligent. For since we hope that what we pass over today can very easily be done tomorrow, the result is that through this supine procrastination of ours — with our eyes closed — the year slips away. But since things have turned out this way, you have done a very fine thing in rousing me now as well, dozing at this kind of duty, with your letter — a kind which I shall use against you more often in the future, and I very much wish you to do the same. As for your poems, I certainly enjoyed the charm of the little book and the most elegant composition of its verses while I was staying with you for many days in Padua; but if I am not mistaken, they were returned to Ramusio before I left. As for everything else that is mine or concerns me, be assured that you may use it as your own, or else you will certainly be doing an injustice to me and to our friendship. Farewell.

## Letter to Angelo [Poliziano] (sending poems for criticism)

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA ANGELO, S.

*[Undated. This letter accompanies a book of light Italian or Latin verses being sent to Poliziano for correction.]*

I have gathered my slight Muses — in which, while youth permitted, I played at writing about my loves — into five little books, and I am sending you the first, planning to send the rest: in this one, I would test you as a friend, not as a flatterer. For they come to you under these conditions: to be chastised and beaten, to pay the penalty for their errors with the nail and the critical obelisk. Apply yourself to them therefore as an equitable judge, not an unjust one; that is to say, as a strict one, not an indulgent one. For what greater injustice than to deceive a friend who expects everything of you? I am not of so delicate a taste that I shrink from another's corrections; I do not think so highly of my little book that I would either ignore the censor or charge him with fault. Indeed, perhaps neither you nor anyone else would believe how little this work of mine satisfies me, or how much, even in the parts I like best, I fear lest I be — as that man said — a Suffenus [a conceited bad poet, from Catullus]. We are all deceived, and do not see the pack on our own back. Therefore do not deny your efforts to a most dear friend in an honest and liberal matter, who asks with so much study, so from the heart. And if I were the kind of man from whom you could receive a return in this sort of thing, I would not suppress that Greek saying: that Lysippus and Apelles were accustomed to make mutual use of each other's work in turn. Farewell.

## Letter to Angelo Poliziano (recalling amatory elegies left in Florence)

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA ANGELO POLITIANO, S.

*[Undated.]*

In former years, when I was in Florence, I left with you four amatory elegies which I had composed — as one does — almost in a spirit of childish play, for the sake of exercise, during that period. They were insipid, unlearned, and slight, easily betraying the youth and ignorance of their author. Yet you approved them — not on their merits, but out of your kindness — or indulgently; and for that reason, to give proof that you had approved them, you made copies. You were not unaware that the most powerful spurs to virtue in young minds are these: that, enticed by desire for praise, and having tasted a little glory, they strive thereafter to drink more deeply. For just as despair weakens and prostrates spirits so that they lie and flee, so hope, there is no doubt, lifts and inflames them. But now my age is too old for that protection, and what was previously subscribed to as youthful error will now be charged to me as a fault — that I have not, with more mature years, either better revised or destroyed them. Therefore, the poems of mine that you have with you — if you love me — either send them back to me, or tear them up, or commit them to the fire. All these things, more polished and many others, which have fallen from my pen since I left here in leisure, I shall shortly commit to your file. Meanwhile, those that cannot live without a friend's loss should be condemned with a capital vote. Furthermore, your Epictetus, and whatever you have translated from Homer up to this day, likewise whatever you have composed on Julian de' Medici in the vernacular, and whatever else you have written in Latin — all of these send to me, I very much wish. I ask this of you with as much earnestness as I desire it, and with as much impatience; and do not refuse on the grounds that they may not yet be brought to the umbilicus [i.e., fully finished rolls], since if everything of yours is thus finished, I promise and pledge it to you now by way of pre-announcement that it will remain with me in private. Farewell.

## Letter to Marsilio Ficino (on combining Plato and Aristotle)

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, MARSILIO FICINO, S.

*[Undated. This is one of the most important theoretical letters: Pico explains his plan to synthesize Platonic and Aristotelian philosophy, and asks Ficino for his book on the immortality of the soul.]*

It has always been my judgment, most learned Marsilio, that there is nothing a man should labor at more than this: that from the nobility of the soul — which, as Trismegistus attests, we are reckoned next to Jupiter — we should by no means degenerate through sloth and lethargy. And this above all has always been my aim: to acquire for myself, from whatever source I can, the cultivation of the mind and training in the good arts. This resolve of mine, though it had remained unshaken from boyhood, was nonetheless greatly stirred and inflamed when I was with you in former years by your exhortations; and never more ardently than from that day to this have I devoted myself wholly to letters. For three years now, Marsilio, I have been living among the Peripatetics, and I have omitted nothing, insofar as it lay within me, to be admitted to Aristotle's household as one not unworthy of that family. And though I have not attained this abundantly — since I have scarcely passed the threshold — yet I have progressed far enough that I would not hesitate to claim the name of a diligent student, if not of the learned and erudite (which my age does not permit).

But since it has always been the opinion of you and of the most learned men, that whoever mingles the Academic with the Peripatetic — thereby holding both sects more rightly and more richly — I have decided that I must undertake this task: that, to the utmost of my ability, and with all the diligence and assiduity I can muster, I should compare Plato with Aristotle, and in alternating studies Aristotle with Plato. For this I needed you first as a guide, and now as a helper; and that your humanity and goodwill toward me should not fail me in this undertaking — so honorable and liberal — I judge it would be most amply fulfilled if you were to send me your book on the immortality of the soul, that I may use it as a kind of pathfinder in proceeding along the Platonic path. For who, when approaching Ficino — in whom, if the doctrine of the Pythagoreans were true, I would believe Plato to have come to life again — would not promise himself the greatest things in that branch of learning? Only do not fail me; though I would press you with many arguments, the very honorable reason you most esteem presses you by itself. For if one ought, in certain circumstances — as Cicero our own, and the wise Chilon, and Theophrastus the most grave of philosophers judged — to deviate somewhat from the path for the sake of friends, will it not be a disgrace to a most dear friend to have failed in so honorable a request? Bring it about, therefore, that what I have long been pursuing through your exhortation, I may at last attain through your aid as well. For there is no one who can help me in this matter besides you — since, as with many other beautiful and excellent things, so also the care for Platonic philosophy among the men of our age has wholly fallen into disuse. Farewell.

## Letter to Ermolao Barbaro (brief friendly note with request for Ptolemy)

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, HERMOLAO SUO, S.

*[Undated.]*

I write to you not because I have anything worthy of you to write, but only to provoke a reply — otherwise I have neither cause nor subject for writing. For I have always condemned the view of those who think one must write to friends in order to discharge this as a kind of debt of friendship. For this whole business of writing and sending letters belongs indeed to friendship, but to friendship that is weak and somewhat tepid — that cannot stand on its own strength but needs support from elsewhere in order to hold together. But friendship that has already struck deep roots in the innermost recesses of the soul — what need, pray, has it of the props of letters? I will give you therefore, Ermolao Barbaro, my letters for this reason alone: to draw forth yours, which I most desire. You on your part, so that I may not be disappointed in my desire for yours, will send me yours most abundantly. For just as it belongs to a weak soul to bring in letters as if they were crutches and props for friendship, so to make oneself readily useful and beneficent to friends is a mark of liberality and exceptional goodwill. Farewell; and if it is not inconvenient, send me Ptolemy's Musica, which will be here for a few days. For why should I not boldly request anything from one to whom I long since vowed and dedicated all that I have?

## Letter to Ermolao Barbaro (in response to a beautiful letter)

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, HERMOLAO, S.

*[Undated.]*

Never have I pleased myself more, or felt more indebted, than at this time, when by one letter of mine to you I have called forth so sweet, so learned, so thoroughly characteristic a letter from you. That you were away from Padua while I was there, and that I missed your company for two years, was the reason I had for writing this to you — nor can that exchange, whatever it may be, fail to delight me, since your letter has so abundantly not merely fulfilled but surpassed all my desires. For, to say nothing of the elegance, grace, learning, and sweetness of the letter — the reading of which alone demands that I repay the kindness in full — how much do I value the fact that you look with such affection and such generosity into the errors of those you love, in order to correct them, and having looked in gently, indulge them lest you vex them?

## Letter to a Religious Father (F.B.C.TH.) [probably Fra Baptista of Mantua, a Carmelite theologian]

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, F. B. C. TH. [Fratri Baptistae Carmelitae Theologo?]

*[Date: Florence, 15 January 1490. The addressee is addressed as "father" and appears to be a religious man who has urged Pico toward piety and divine philosophy. Possibly Fra Baptista Mantuanus, the Carmelite theologian and poet.]*

Greetings, most beloved father. Here is your Apollonius — which, if I did not owe it to you for your virtuous offices toward me, I would owe certainly for these letters alone, in which you entreat me: such great love is in them, such humanity. For it is both the mark of supreme love to call one there, to where you have already arrived — that is to say, to the purification of the soul, which is true felicity — and the mark of supreme humanity to urge me on as one who is an equal, to whom you can dictate terms as to a son. Would that it might one day come about that we might philosophize together piously in some retreat; you would find in me nothing so primary as the desire to join piety with wisdom. You would find me most firmly persuaded of this: that there is no wisdom which blots out in us the image of the eternal Wisdom and makes us like the brutes in their senselessness. I have as witness my companion Angelo [Poliziano], that I count as daily gain from my studies whatever makes me more equipped and stronger for the contest of purifying the soul. This manifold handling of disciplines — if letters can promise us anything — merely tints the surface and, as it were, paints the face; it does not make us truly beautiful. And a sound, firm, robust mind we can hope for from nowhere else than from integrity of life, from character, and from divine religion — so that you compel me, as I might say, already willing to be more willing. But also aid me with your sacred prayers, that I may be able to do what both I desired and you wished me so ardently to desire. Farewell, most loving father, whom I love above all others and carry always in my eyes. Florence, 15 January 1490. I await all your works. My Poliziano, the delight of all literature, bids me send you his greetings.

## Letter to Ermolao Barbaro (sending early literary works; apology for their weakness)

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, HERMOLAO BARBARO, S.

*[Undated. This is one of the more substantial letters — Pico describes his regret at having missed Ermolao in Padua, praises his learning, and sends along some early compositions with apologies for their weakness.]*

I remember that when I was deliberating which gymnasium in Italy I should choose for my philosophical studies, though much drew me to Padua, I went there all the more willingly so that my love for you — which I had long nurtured in my mind — might through mutual acquaintance be made openly known to you. I was in Padua for two years, and yet — who would believe it? — I neither saw Ermolao, nor knew him except as I had known him long before by repute. So it often happens that we despise by a certain lazy procrastination the opportunity offered to us, and it slips away — since it is slippery — to others. I have been most deeply repentant of this matter, and am still, and the more so because the learning of yours in all things, which I had known so greatly by report, I now have recognized with these eyes, with these ears, while I heard you in the Roman Themistius [Barbaro had been working on a translation and commentary of Themistius] discoursing — with that mouth of yours — on the nature of the soul, on the first principles of things, on the science of reasoning. Good God, what prudence, what judgment, what memory, what wisdom shone forth in everything. These gifts will easily bring you wherever you aim, lead you wherever you direct your thoughts. I fear only for your health, which — as you have been accustomed to tell me — has been pressed down and weakened from early age up to this day, by many illnesses, though weakened rather than broken; so much has your genius moved itself, that it knows not how to be overcome, but fights strenuously against fortune's blows with its own perpetual steadfastness. Take care no less of your health than of your fortune, since where your life is taken away [OCR uncertain here], there are none of these things left.

That you speak of me honorably to everyone is what you do in accordance with your humanity and your long goodwill toward me, and I owe you as much for it as anyone can be owed. But I fear lest you weave your own web back again, by calling into the public those youthful trifles of mine, which flowed from me in lighter years. For they are all clumsy, quite unlearned, or let Retia [?OCR uncertain] deal most kindly with them — they are certainly not the things that can sustain whatever opinion men may have now perhaps conceived of me from your account. Whatever they are, I judged them to be sent, that I might satisfy you even at the cost of my entire reputation. But I would ask you nonetheless to treat all these lighter things lightly, for if you should at last desire more serious things from me, I have weightier ones in hand, which have not yet been brought to the umbilicus [i.e., fully completed]. And even these things I have sent needed a more finished polish. But both the times and my philosophical studies — to which I devote myself above all — have turned me away from the task. We shall return someday to the Muses, however, and if we produce anything worthy of your ears, we shall take care that it is conveyed to you at once. Farewell.

## Letter to R.C. [perhaps Roberto Capodivacca or another patron]

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, R. C., S.

*[Undated. Pico thanks a distinguished figure for generous overtures of friendship, likely mediated through Guarino and Poliziano.]*

Our friend Matthaeus, most obliging man, conveyed to me on your behalf the greatest greetings, and declared most openly with what love and goodwill you attend to me, promising furthermore in your name your effort and assistance whenever I might need to make use of these. Would that I were adequate to this courtesy, most gracious father! For to receive me into your protection, and to be numbered among your own — that I have always both desired and hoped. But to find you freely offering yourself to me, meeting me so benignly, with such friendly and such liberal promises, as if calling me — if I may say so, though through you I may say it — to your friendship: this has surpassed both my hope and my every wish. For what is it, and how much, to be invited by one by whom it would have been enough not to be excluded? Although in doing this you have done something worthy of yourself, if not of me — for I might on the strength of my merits not be admitted, but in the strength of your humanity I could not fail to be invited. How much I rejoice on my own behalf, therefore, or how much I owe you, I cannot begin to explain, since I cannot even conceive it. Certainly I owe you so much the more for this in that you owe me nothing (nothing whatsoever) on any account. Let Guarino [the addressee's connection to Battista Guarino is implied] —

## Letter to Antonio Picimannus

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, ANTONIO PICIMANNO, S.D.

*[Date: 9 July 1489. Addressee: Antonio Picimanno, a Venetian nobleman.]*

Nothing in my entire life has given me more pleasure than the letter I received from you recently — full of love, of courtesy, of humanity. May I be so happy as to tell you the truth: unless perhaps that day was more pleasurable to me on which you yourself and your Grimani — I know not whether more yours than mine — came to meet me and saluted me with friendly honour. Although you had as it were borrowed a mask, and would not reveal yourselves, but presented yourselves to me under I know not what charming fiction of names, your most sweet genius, your singular learning, your lovable gravity, liberal modesty, your turn of mind, your speech, your most elegant conversation all represented to me Grimani and Picimannus as openly and truly as they are. And thus, though the names could be deceived, I could not be cheated of the pleasure that was owed me from you; you could disguise your names, but not the virtue which by its own splendour knows not how to hide or lie concealed. That grave discussion of natural things, those keen ecclesiastical arguments, those most exact judgments — what could they announce to me other than you yourselves? I congratulated myself privately, in my heart, while I listened to you, that our age, which I had thought produced not one Grimani, not one Picimannus, had more abundantly produced two Grimanis, two Picimanni. And when our Poliziano told me who you were, I began then to congratulate not so much my age as my own judgment — for I seemed to have recognized in you all those things which were in them, that is, which consistent testimony from all sides showed to be in you. But see how your praises carry me sideways and draw me away from the empty letter! Though the same thing calls me thither even unwilling — but let me restrain myself, lest I appear to wish to repay with mutual praises the many with which you have heaped me, which, whether from inclination of mind or from judgment, I do not know — whether they were a greater honour or greater pleasure to me. I owe much, my dear Picimannus, to that spirit of yours toward me. But see to it — whenever it happens that there is some trial of me, that is, clearly, of my goodwill toward you — you will find a man who loves your virtue and pursues it so earnestly that I will not yield first place even to your own Grimani in loving you. Farewell. 9 July 1489.

## Letter to an Unknown Friend (on Hebrew and Chaldean letters; Mithridates)

**Latin heading:** IOANNES PICVS MIRANDVLA, IGNOTO AMICO, S.D.

*[Date: From Fratta, 10 November 1486. This is one of the most important documentary letters: it discusses Pico's Hebrew and Kabbalistic studies, the figure of Flavius Mithridates, the Josephus question, Chaldean letters, and the Kabbalah's testimony on Hebrew script. The "unknown friend" was connected to Roberto Salviati and also knew Mithridates.]*

Whoever you are, be our friend — since, as I have learned through the chirograph of our Roberto Salviati, you are most closely bound to him. I have read your letter to Mithridates, which I was unable to deliver to him in his absence. I began to love you when reading it — not so much because Roberto loves you, but because I learned that you love letters and (what is of greater importance) that you are devoted to the study of sacred things and the Law. And since on the matters you were asking about I seemed to be able to satisfy you in some degree, wishing thus to make a good beginning of our friendship, I wished to let you know what I think — which I could do far more richly if I had here those friends of mine, that is, my books, to consult; but they have gone ahead of me to Rome like my advance guard, and I myself, already with hat and boots on, on the point of departure, have delivered this [letter] to you as familiar and as hasty as possible.

As to your inquiry about Josephus: know that the genuine Josephus is not to be found among the Hebrews. What exists is an epitome of Josephus — a kind of abridgement — in which there are many fabrications, and what is read there about the ten tribes who did not return by right of post-Babylonian restoration: several Hebrews have confessed to me that these passages are spurious and adulterate; and therefore no overall credence is to be given to their Josephus. In the Greek Josephus I know there are certain passages that make honourable and faithful mention of Christ, but I would not assert that they are entirely the same as those read in the Latin codices without first re-reading the Greek exemplar, of which I presently have no copy here.

Now to come to what you desire about Chaldean and Hebrew letters: I have often inquired of Mithridates, and of many Hebrews, about those matters read in Jerome in the "Galeate prologue," where he writes that the Samaritans and Hebrews once shared the same characters, and that Ezra after the restoration of the temple under Zerubbabel invented other letters — those now in use. About this change they all profess ignorance; and (what is more) they hold it as certain that Moses and all the ancient Patriarchs used the same letter-forms now used in Hebrew literature. A view that, now that it comes to mind, the science of Kabbalah most strongly confirms — for the Kabbalistic writers, who were in the synod with Ezra himself, maintain and demonstrate that Moses concealed divine mysteries under the very letter-forms and figures by which we now have the Law written; which would be entirely impossible if Moses had used other characters than those we now use. Yet to defend the saying of Jerome I have only this one passage, which I recently read in a commentary on the book that the ancient father Abraham published on the Creation [Sefer Yetzirah]: for when the commentators interpret those thirty-two gates which it mentions, they say that number is composed of the ten numerations [Sefirot] and twenty-two letters. The remaining letters — which are the final forms of mem, tsade, pe, kaph, and nun [five final letters of the Hebrew alphabet] — they say were never doubled, and that their forms were added by the bishops or perfects [OCR uncertain: "epifcopis fiue perfectis" — perhaps "scribes" intended]. This is perhaps what Jerome meant, whose testimony both deserves to be and is indeed considered most sacred by everyone, and most sacred it is considered by me.

As for the Chaldean alphabet you ask for — you could not have gotten it even from Mithridates; nothing through him, even though you can use me to obtain it from him who can do all things. For he refused in every way to teach me the Chaldean language unless I first swore — indeed, with a sworn formula — never to transmit it to anyone. Our Girolamo Benivieni can testify to this: he happened to be present while Mithridates was teaching me, and the furious man threw him bodily out of the house. But so that you may not have written entirely in vain, as a substitute for the Chaldean take the Arabic alphabet, which is being sent to you written in my own hand. There remains nothing for me to write except that I, whoever you may be, cannot but love you most dearly — if I know you to be a most devoted lover of the good arts. Farewell. From Fratta, 10 November 1486.

## Letters of Fra Baptista Mantuanus, Carmelite Theologian, to Giovanni Francesco Pico della Mirandola, on the Death of Giovanni Pico

**Latin heading:** EPISTOLAE DUAE FRATRIS BAPTISTAE MAN[TUANI], CARMELITAE THEOLOGI DOCTISSIMI ET ELOQUENTISSIMI AD IOANNEM FRANCISCUM PICUM MIRANDULAM CONCORDIAECOMITEM, DE MORTE IOANNIS PICI EIUS PATRUI.

### First Letter

*[Date: Mantua, 25 November 1494.]*

Illustrious lord: when I lately heard of the death of Giovanni Pico your uncle, that most illustrious and most learned of men, I was so stunned in spirit that neither my feet nor my mind were sufficiently performing their office. I came close to complaining against both God and nature for having so quickly removed so great a light from the world, so great an ornament of Italy, so great a glory of our age — as though they begrudge their goods to the earthly commonwealth with no regard for us, and cut him down, cut him down, I say, while he was still growing, still flourishing, still nourishing all men with the most abundant fertility of his genius. But remembering the sentence of Paul — "the judgments of God are a great abyss" — I restrained myself lest I think impiously and rashly of God and of his works.

In the passing of this man, literary studies, the knowledge of things, and integrity of character have suffered a great eclipse. For — as you know — in your uncle, life and holiness were so joined with learning in languages and knowledge of human and divine things that in one and the same man one seemed to see Jerome and Augustine come back to life. And would that he had finished, before his death, that work on the concordance of Plato and Aristotle! For its title promised the entire philosophy of the ancients, and the disclosure of the most recondite and most worthy of investigation matters. All his little works — those he completed, and also those he left unfinished — must be collected with all diligence and care; for since they flowed from so great a felicity of genius, they cannot but be most worthy of being read, loved, and revered by all posterity. To urge you to this duty, I need not: I am confident enough that you are so persuaded and so inclined. There was lacking to your family this immortal honour: that upon the most ancient nobility, the abundance of wealth and resources, the glory of military affairs, should also be placed such excellence of wisdom — so that all things most worthy of the highest praise among mortals might flow together into your one house. And you, Giovanni Francesco, should strive with all your strength to possess your uncle's virtue as an inheritance, and that his spirit may be doubled in you, as the spirit of Elijah in Elisha. This we expect of you, this we require. When you have gathered his works together, I pray, make me a participant in them too; and it will be well worth my while to apply myself thus for the sake of so great a pleasure. A couplet in his praise, composed on the spot — though I am not pleased with it — I shall append nonetheless. Farewell, best of princes, and love me. Mantua, 25 November 1494.

> *Pico Giovanni, learned in the heavens, the elements, and God,*
> *As a young man holy in his life, he died and was made blessed.*

### Second Letter (from Baptista Mantuanus to Giovanni Francesco Pico)

Illustrious Count Giovanni Francesco: the most honourable delay of your reply — that which dogmatically and paranetically [i.e., in both doctrine and exhortation] embraces all — is the greatest burden and beyond human capacity; what you owe to your genius, or certainly to your Maker, that at so tender an age you have attained what it would be most impudent to hope for in extreme old age. These things, Pico, are not yours; they belong to nature, they belong to God — though if anything is yours, these things most of all are yours. Consider how much less yours can be called the nobility of fortune, health, and beauty — goods in which you yield to none, no less than those which are more truly yours. Toward these I urge you to turn; upon these revolve day and night — how great is the name of him who enriched you so richly that you can never repay or even reckon what you possess. Reflect how powerful is he who willed that you possess these things; how much he loves you, who chose you from so many thousands; who, when you were almost a child, enabled you to surpass old men; who, though not yet grown to manhood, turned the circles of learned men to wonder at your learning. Unless you see this, unless you consider it, I declare to you now openly and proclaim it with a free voice: you are not as great as you are. Immense and infinite is what I have long promised myself of you, and still promise — but anticipated opinion no speech, no declaration can match; I do as those do who, the more they admire someone and wish to explain what they feel, the less they achieve what they want — so that although they conceive something great in their mind, words fail them, and they seem to have struggled rather than spoken. Therefore do not listen to these words, which I neither write to you for others to see, but understand rather those greater things which I say inside myself about you, alone and in silence — greater, by Hercules, by as much as thought exceeds speech. Salute Marsilio and your Salviati. As for Poliziano, there is no need to trouble yourself, since shortly — as I hear — he will be with me, than which nothing in this whole embassy could have been more pleasant to me. The library here is being built up daily, larger and more splendid; I rejoice beyond measure. Letters owe much to the Florentines, and among the Florentines to the Medici, and among the Medici to Lorenzo, who fosters this hereditary pride of his family with no less care than the republic, of which he has the right of administration and governance. Farewell. Milan, 1 January 1487.

## Ermolao Barbaro to Roberto Salviati

**Latin heading:** HERMOLAUS BARBARUS ROBERTO SALVIATO, S.D.

*[Date: Milan, 12 days before the Kalends of November 1488 — i.e., 21 October 1488. Ermolao reports on his work, specifically his editions of Aristotle, Themistius, and Dioscorides, and praises Pico's Apologia.]*

You are well, Salviati — if you suspect anything beyond my pressing business to be the reason I have not yet replied to you, this is the usual and hackneyed ransoming of a silence. But by Hercules, unless you accept this, you would appear to have deserved ill of my love, fidelity, and goodwill toward you. Your letters are always most welcome to me, but those beyond measure in which you mention and speak of my Mirandola — nothing is more wonderful than he in letters; no one whom I prefer to have praised, celebrated, and exalted to the sky; no one from whose praises I can less depart: in which matter I am perhaps excessive, though I seem to be intemperate to nobody, so much praise is there in him, so utterly without limit, that now there is no room for ambition or flattery. I have read his Apologia: in it, why does he grant me so much — I who merit so little? Why does he address someone of ordinary rank — not to say of the ranks — with so honourable a preface? Especially in that work, than which nothing is more profound, more vigorous, more acute — on which yet I shall better defer my judgment a little while, until I have read it all through completely. As for what you want to know of what I am doing: what else can I say but that I study? Public responsibility takes somewhat from my attention — or rather from my efforts. Indeed I press harder, and exact the tribute more severely; what a greater diligence has taken from me, I make up in proportion to the amount of business added to me — the more business has accrued, the more eagerness and energy it has instilled. And so all those intervals of leisure which as a private man I devoted to health, I now distribute to public concerns; so from one cause I lose and diminish, and from another I recoup. I believe I have written to you before, or someone who heard from me has told you: the Instrument of Aristotle — for thus the Peripatetics generally call logic — likewise the eight books of the Physics, and his three books of rhetoric, I have now translated into Latin. Dioscorides is now complete; I am now putting the finishing touches on him; the dowry of my daughter is ready, and she will shortly be given in marriage [a metaphor for publication]. Then, freed of that burden, I shall tackle Aristotle whole and entire; and unless God calls me elsewhere, he will within roughly two years of the Dioscorides edition be finished, at least in the logical works, with the commentaries on the Posterior Analytics which we have already completed. After those are sent out, I shall take up the natural and divine [works]. Also the Rhetoric and Poetics — partly with commentaries, partly with annotations — equipping all the volumes of that philosopher to the best of my ability. For there are also in the Problems and in the History of Animals various things that need clarity and light; these I shall pass over quickly with a brief annotation. I plan to undertake nothing else new in my whole life — for I must restrain, indeed suppress, this greediness, or rather this improbity of my genius, lest while wishing to be held fertile and productive, it prove merely laborious and ambitious. I find it truer every day than the common saying: to embrace everything and [to accomplish] nothing. Farewell. Milan, 21 October 1488.

## Ermolao Barbaro to Giovanni Pico della Mirandola (on the Heptaplus)

**Latin heading:** HERMOLAUS BARBARUS IOANNI PICO MIRANDULAE, S.D.

*[Date: Venice, the day before the Nones of September 1489 — i.e., 4 September 1489. Ermolao praises Pico's Heptaplus.]*

I received your Heptaplus the day before yesterday, delivered by the hand of Salviati, and read it so eagerly that not even on the first day could I drink it all in at one sitting without pausing for breath. Three things delight me wonderfully in it. First, that your prose both teaches and moves — the very quality that the ancient theologians are known to have observed in the sacred Scriptures, and the Pythagoreans and Platonists in natural philosophy as well, while the more recent writers labor only to teach. Second, that amid such an abundance of material and such impunity of plundering, you keep such chaste and innocent hands — so that in this kind of writing you seem to have followed no other model than yourself; and this is now something common neither with the ancients nor with the moderns, but peculiar and your own; in which respect (I will open my mind to you) I am so affected that, since these things which you have said were not said by those predecessors, they seem to me to have said almost nothing. Third, I see that you are initiated not so much in the rites of Aristotle as in those of Plato and his school, and have drawn from there full draughts into this lucubration. I omit how many excellent things from Plotinus you have touched on, swiftly and elegantly, so that you seem to have not so much read as learned by heart whatever is contained in his four and fifty volumes, arranged by Porphyry into six sets of nine. Nor can I pass over the fact that you conduct yourself so evenly between the new and the ancient theologians that you neither shun the simple majesty of the latter nor are swept away by the ambitious subtlety of the former. Thus it comes about that while wishing to imitate the one, and being able to imitate the other, you have culled the best of each kind — which we hope you will do also in the interpretation of the Davidic poem. Farewell. Venice, the day before the Nones of September 1489.

## Ermolao Barbaro to Giovanni Pico della Mirandola (brief friendly letter)

**Latin heading:** HERMOLAUS BARBARUS IOANNI PICO MIRANDULAE, S.D.

*[Date: Rome, the Ides of August 1490 — i.e., 13 August 1490.]*

Greetings, most gracious Mirandola. Truly I praise you everywhere so willingly that nothing gives me greater pleasure — not only because nothing is more pleasantly heard than truth (as Plato says), but also because, as Paemo says, it is far more pleasant to speak the truth than to hear it. What happens without any effort, as it is in Aeschylus, Menander, and Euripides — truth often emerges in ways not sought, and is extracted even from the most unwilling man, with no questioning but by its own force. This is that tyranny which you, following your candour, interpret as humanity and goodness. And Hercules, you are already so great that one who does not praise you detracts from his own judgment and reputation. Those who favour and desire your success are advancing their own interests, consulting their own advantage, redeeming their own reputation. There are many names in your diary that you do not know; your creditors owe you. Many follow the counsel of Prodicus: they give in order to receive; one hand washes the other, as Epicharmus sings. Certainly, the less you owe your creditors, that is your praisers, the more you owe yourself — who make them your creditors whether they will it or not. O unheard-of kind of thing: to be a creditor of another's debt, not to be a debtor oneself! But I am too occupied to be able to play with you any longer. As for you — good gods! — how free you are, that you allow anyone to be at leisure. I beg you to tolerate me this far; for a longer jest I do not ask; I ask merely a brief little yawn. Farewell together with my Poliziano. Rome, the Ides of August 1490.

## Ermolao Barbaro, Venetian Orator and Patriarch of Aquileia, to Giovanni Pico

**Latin heading:** HERMOLAUS BARBARUS ORATOR VENETUS, PATRIARCHA AQUILEIENSIS IOANNI PICO, S.D.

*[Date: Rome, the day before the Kalends of April [year not specified — likely 1491].]*

All is well, all is in order — that you congratulate me most earnestly on the patriarchate of Aquileia, and wish and predict for me things far greater than I could either hope for if most desirous, or deserve if most modest. I know your piety toward me; I see how much you esteem me; I recognise the force of your love. I have nothing to compare with it. Would that there might someday come a time when I could live with you, speaking of nature, of God, [of holy things together — OCR: some words garbled here]. Now there is wonder and a weariness of learning [?OCR: "Nunc stupor & otiasis scientiae" — possibly "weariness from the fasting of learning" or similar]. You will read these patiently; your heart will not be exalted, nor will your eyes be lifted — for why should you take satisfaction in yourself, knowing that these goods you possess are not yours, not from your own private store, but received from another and belonging to the Lord? Forgive me: the north wind of truth and the west wind of charity have swept and carried me sideways. Farewell. Rome, the day before the Kalends of April.

## Angelo Poliziano to Giovanni Pico della Mirandola

**Latin heading:** ANGELUS POLITIANUS IOANNI PICO MIRANDULAE SUO, SALUTEM.

*[Undated. Poliziano thanks Pico for dedicating a commentary "On the One and Being" (De uno et ente) to him — the work now known as the De ente et uno.]*

I have burned almost always — perhaps too recklessly — yet burned always, with desire for undying fame; as though, since what I wrote could barely be repaid by even the oldness of my name, that surviving glory would count for nothing. But since what I wrote seemed barely able to promise even that — you arose, Pico, to accomplish what I could not: namely, by dedicating to me your Commentary on the One and Being (for so you make the title), in which you recall the streams flowing through the Lyceum and the Academy to one source, and join our theology too, more as one philosophy than a double one, with philosophy itself. What then should I seek for Glaucus's herb [the herb that made one immortal], if I am to live for ever through you? And with you too! The posterity will one day recount that there was once a certain Poliziano whom even Pico himself — the light of all learning — held in such esteem that he did not hesitate to dedicate to him a most beautiful book on the highest of subjects. Therefore I give you thanks for immortality — but immortal thanks. Farewell.

## Marsilio Ficino to Germano de Ganay (on the Death of Pico)

**Latin heading:** MARSILIUS FICINUS FLORENTINUS, GERMANO DE GANAY, PARISIIS PRAESIDENTI, S.D.

*[Undated but late 1494. Germano de Ganay was President of the Paris Parlement; this letter from Ficino confirms Pico's death.]*

You wish to be informed of the death of Pico della Mirandola — which seems still uncertain to you. You wish to grieve more deeply, Germano; for since you have grieved deeply up to now at this ambiguous loss, once better informed you will grieve most deeply, I think. Perhaps it were better to believe an uncertain loss than to be tortured by a certain one. Alas, what are you asking, my most longed-for Germano? I would prefer still to be uncertain myself, and I cannot at present make you more certain without tears. Last November — on the day that the great King Charles of France entered our city of Florence — our Mirandola departed from us, afflicting men of letters with nearly as much grief as the King meanwhile brought the citizens joy. Thus the tutelary spirit of the place and people wisely compensated the joy of the people with the mourning of the learned, and in exchange for the extinguishing [OCR: text breaks and continues later] ...that nothing is more dear to me than to love, cultivate, and venerate those who excel in genius or are singular in learning. I could not but love your Pico della Mirandola above all, and admire him — a man most worthy of all admiration. When he recently dedicated to you his book on the sevenfold interpretation of the six days of Genesis, the first fruits of his studies — a work excellent not by my judgment alone but by the judgment of all — I desired to take care that it should be published in a correct edition at my expense, not doubting that I would thereby satisfy both my love for him and the common utility of scholars. And it was added that I hoped you would not find it unwelcome if what he communicated to you of physical and divine mysteries should at last through me become common to all. Farewell.
