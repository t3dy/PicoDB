## Main Findings

Jayne's work gives PicoDB a reception and mediation model for Ficino. Ficino's influence is powerful, but it does not travel as simple direct discipleship. It moves through translation, commentary, literary popularization, pupils, critics, readers, marginalia, and vernacular or national reception. That model helps sharpen the Pico-Ficino relationship: Pico can depend on Ficino's Platonic infrastructure while also correcting him and becoming a mediator through whom Ficinian materials circulate in altered form.

Jayne's 1952 English Renaissance article is especially useful because it refuses the lazy formula "Ficino caused Renaissance Platonism." Ficino is the fountainhead of Renaissance love Platonism, but his effect often travels indirectly through Italian and French literary forms. In the prose period, Jayne makes Pico a major intermediary: More and Colet could find Ficino's Christian Platonist synthesis more forcefully through Pico than through Ficino directly.

For the *Commento*, Craven and Allen confirm what the project has been building from Allen and Pugliese: the text is not a neutral extension of Ficino's *De amore*. Jayne's introduction and notes present the *Commento* as composite, confusing, and aggressively anti-Ficinian. Craven summarizes it as three works masquerading as a fourth: poetic theology, commentary on the *Symposium*, and treatise on love presented as commentary on Benivieni's canzone. He also stresses that Pico judges Ficino wrong in methodology, mythology, and love theory.

The Ficino *De amore* reviews supply the comparator. Jayne frames Ficino's work as less a line-by-line commentary on Plato's dialogue than an exposition of Ficino's own doctrine of love. Ficino uses the *Symposium* to defend personal love by placing it inside a cosmic and respectable order. The portal should therefore compare Pico and Ficino as rival commentators who both exceed their ostensible texts: Ficino exceeds Plato's *Symposium*, while Pico exceeds Benivieni's canzone and Ficino's *De amore*.

The Colet reviews add methodological caution. Jayne's *John Colet and Marsilio Ficino* uses letters and marginalia to reconstruct influence, correction, and resistance. Reviewers accept the value of the evidence but warn that marginalia do not automatically form a coherent doctrine and that influence may reinforce ideas already present rather than determine them. This is directly applicable to PicoDB's Pico-Ficino method: borrowings, corrections, and shared authorities must be tagged separately.

## Additions to the Pico-Ficino Essay

1. Add Jayne as a secondary but important guide for Ficino's love Platonism and its literary reception.
2. Treat the *De amore* as Ficino's own love doctrine only partly housed inside a Platonic commentary form.
3. Treat Pico's *Commento* as a rival commentary form: a work that uses Benivieni's poem as occasion for poetic theology, love theory, and anti-Ficinian correction.
4. Add the "indirect influence" model: Ficino's influence is often mediated by Pico, Benivieni, Bembo, Castiglione, French poets, Stanley, and later translators.
5. Add a method rule from Colet: evidence of reading or admiration is not evidence of discipleship; marginalia, corrections, and later use must be separated.
