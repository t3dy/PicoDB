## Bibliographic Anchor

Brian P. Copenhaver, *Magic and the Dignity of Man: Pico della Mirandola and His Oration in Modern Memory* (Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press / Belknap Press, 2019), Chapter 12, "Pico Consults and Disputes," pp. 375–419. The chapter covers Oration sections III–IV (paragraphs 16–55 in Copenhaver's numbering), moving through seven expositions of the Cherubic life (sections 1–4 of Oration III, covering §§16–35) and then Pico's rhetorical self-defense of the 900 Conclusions and his concord program (Oration IV, §§36–55). The chapter is the longest analytical unit in Part Three ("Pico's Oration"), which reads the Oration section by section.

---

## Section Function

Chapter 12 is the structural heart of Copenhaver's close reading of the Oration. It covers the middle portion of the speech: the "consults" movement (Oration III), in which Pico seeks out authoritative witnesses for the Cherubic life — Paul, Jacob, Job, Moses, Zoroaster, and Abraham — drawing on a dense weave of Neoplatonism, Kabbalah, Hermetism, and Christian mysticism; and the "disputes" movement (Oration IV), in which Pico defends the legitimacy of his Roman disputation, explains the architecture of the 900 Conclusions, and articulates the concord program. Copenhaver's argument in this chapter is twofold. First, he demonstrates that the Oration's middle section is not humanist rhetoric about human dignity but a sustained, densely sourced mystical curriculum organized around ascent, catharsis, self-extinction, and deification. Second, he shows that the 900 Conclusions are not a miscellany but a carefully structured document, organized to embody concord and to move from familiar scholastic authorities toward increasingly arcane wisdom — culminating in Kabbalah. Copenhaver uses these sections to dismantle the modern myth (promoted by Burckhardt, Cassirer, Garin, and others) of Pico as a Renaissance humanist celebrating human autonomy: the Oration's real message is ascetic self-annihilation in the Godhead.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

**Oration III: The Seven Expositions of the Cherubic Life (§§16–35)**

With more than four-fifths of the Oration still to come after the famous "chameleon" passage, Pico turns from exhortation to instruction. The remainder of Oration III enacts a "consultation" of authoritative witnesses: Paul, Jacob, Job, Moses, Zoroaster, and Abraham, each furnishing one or more of seven successive expositions of the Cherubic life — the curriculum of ethics, dialectic, natural philosophy, and theology that leads to mystical union.

The first consultation (§§16–19) is with Paul, whose vision of the third heaven is interpreted through Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite. The Areopagite's three-stage mystical ascent — catharsis, illumination, perfection — structures Pico's curriculum, though Pico revises *epopteia* ("seeing" divinity) to insert natural philosophy between the lower cathartic stages and theological completion. The goal is "unconditional withdrawal" and assimilation: not to know something new but to become something else, to enter God's nameless shadows. Copenhaver notes that Pico traces the Areopagite's "occult tradition (*kruphia paradosis*)" as a theurgic, esoteric, and ecstatic mysticism fully as otherworldly as the Cherubic life. The Kabbalist dimension enters immediately: the Areopagite's negative theology of a hidden God parallels Kabbalist reticence about S1, the highest Sefirah (the Crown, *Ein Sof*, the No-End), and thesis T2K34 explicitly links this convergence to Christian mysteries of Trinity and Incarnation.

The second consultation (§§17–19) is with Jacob, whose dream-ladder furnishes a cosmic image of ascent. Pico draws on a Targum in which angels ascend and descend Jacob's ladder to examine his face, carved into the Throne of Glory as in Ezekiel's vision. Copenhaver identifies three strands woven together: Neoplatonist ladder-philosophy (Plotinus, Origen, John Climacus), rabbinic Throne mysticism (*Bahir*, Targum), and Abraham Abulafia's *Ve-zot*, which describes a spherical cosmic ladder of 360 rungs containing the letters of the Torah and equates the ladder (*sullam*, 130) with the doubled divine Name *Adonai* (65 + 65 = 130). The "passing from center to center" of Pico's text is connected to Ibn al-Sid al-Batalyawsi's *Book of Imaginary Circles*, known to Italian Jews, which pictures a cosmic Soul with angelic messengers descending a straight line or ladder to enable human ascent. The Titanic dismemberment of Dionysus / Osiris and Apollo's reunifying role translates into sefirotic terms via thesis T2K59, where a single Hebrew letter *bet* encodes Father, Son, and the Sabbath-rest in a "fourfold arrangement" derived from Menahem Recanati's Torah commentary. Copenhaver stresses that nothing in the Oration's surface text would prompt a Christian reader to think of the Hebrew alphabet without access to T2K59: the thesis is the decoder for the speech.

The third consultation (§§20–25) introduces Job, here read through a layered set of sources: Maimonides, Gersonides (whose naturalist Aristotelian commentary Pico used in his Latin translation of Job, prepared with his Jewish collaborator Flavius Mithridates), Nahmanides (Ramban), and Abulafia. Pico's question to Job — what did God want from his angelic armies? — is answered immediately: peace. Job 25 (Bildad's speech) provides the proof-text: "He makes peace on high." Behind this, Copenhaver shows, lie thesis T1K24 (Southern Water = Michael at S4, Northern Fire = Gabriel at S5, their reconciliation at S6 = *shamayim* = fire + water) and a dense angelological theodicy. Empedocles enters as the pagan sage who shows that Nature is "born of war": his teaching that Strife and Friendship govern the cosmos is read alongside the "mythology of the Magi" (Chaldean Oracles, via Plutarch) to argue that philosophy cannot achieve the peace theology promises. The soul must pass through the warfare of nature before reaching the holiest peace. Metempsychosis threads through: thesis T1K41 ("every good soul is a new soul coming from the East"), Job's putative pre-natal covenant, Empedocles' teaching that souls migrate even into plants, and Kabbalist transmigration (*gilgul*) from the *Bahir* and *Zohar*. The final movement of this section is the "death of the kiss" (*binsica* in T2K13, *neshiqah*), the rapturous extinction of self in God: souls receive the King of Glory not as a guest but as a spouse, wish to die in themselves to live in their spouse. Sources cited include Maimonides (reading patriarchal death-by-kiss as conjunction with the Agent Intellect), Gersonides, Ibn Bajja (Avempace), Farabi, and Ibn Tufayl (*Hayy ibn Yaqzan*, translated by Pico in 1493), whose mystic reaches "his own obliteration." The *Bahir*'s sefirotic reading of Psalm 45 (the royal bride, S10 = Shekinah, "the King's daughter within is glorious") provides the nuptial-death imagery, including an explication of *zahab* (gold) as encoding the male-female coupling of Sefirot. Copenhaver notes that Pico was careful not to expose the sexuality within the Godhead — disturbing to Christian ears — to his audience.

The fourth consultation (§§26–32) is with Moses, whose Tabernacle provides the spatial architecture for Pico's four-stage curriculum. Copenhaver shows that Pico remaps the traditional three-zone Tabernacle (outer courtyard, sanctuary, Holy of Holies) onto a four-stage curriculum (ethics, dialectic, natural philosophy, theology), drawing on the Zohar's description of the Tabernacle as a microcosm and Abulafia's equation of the Tabernacle with the human body and cosmos. The Proclus thesis on the soul's "vehicle" shedding garments acquired in descent through planetary spheres is linked to Zoroastrian theses (T2Z8–9) on the soul's threefold wrapping. The goal is *epopteia* — a glimpse of God while "drunk on the nectar of eternity" — produced by "Socratic frenzies." Copenhaver traces the Bacchic imagery through thesis T2Z6 (Chaldean Oracles aphorism 11: "double drunkenness of Bacchus and Silenus"), T2Or24, and T2K17 (David's drunkenness on God's bounty = Kabbalist "purest wine"), showing that the interlocking triads of Orphic, Chaldean, and Kabbalist wine theology encode the same ascent structure. Apollo / Bacchus / Dionysus / Jesus are identified. Delphic maxims ("know yourself," "nothing in excess") and Plutarch's analysis of the Delphic *E* (numeral 5 = *he* in Hebrew = soul's feminine principle) introduce numerological convergence between Greek and Kabbalist inquiry. The Pythagorean instructions ("feed the cock," etc.) from Iamblichus's *Protrepticus* map onto the stages of the curriculum. The cock's function is to "nourish our soul's divine part with knowledge of divinity" before philosophy and theology nurture the soul to expire in ecstasy.

The fifth and sixth expositions blend into a critique of different modes of mystical ascent. Pico's mysticism is aligned with Plotinus and Porphyry (self-extinction in the One) rather than Augustine (lifelong conformity to the Trinity through the soul's faculties, grounded in the Incarnation). Copenhaver notes pointedly that the Incarnation appears only once in the Oration — in a list of doctrines supported by Kabbalah — confirming that Pico's mysticism is "Dionysian, Kabbalist and Neoplatonic," not Augustinian.

The seventh exposition (§§33–35) is organized around Zoroaster and Abraham, mediated by the rivers of Eden (T1K11: out of Eden = S2, the river = S3, the four branches = S4, S5, S6, S10), four archangels (Gabriel/west/ethics, Isaac-Gabriel/north/dialectic, Jacob/east/natural philosophy, Abraham-Michael/south/theology), and a sefirotic geography mapping the Tabernacle's spatial logic onto cardinal directions. Abraham's journeys always going south (Genesis 12) are decoded via Joseph Gikatilla's Kabbalist reading: Abram draws the channels from *Kedem* to Beth-El through S4 (Piety/Love on the right side of the sefirotic tree), ascending from S10 toward S2 while staying on the merciful right side and avoiding Isaac's northern Judgment at S5. Theses T1K14, T2K37, and T2K42 culminate this movement: Abraham "saw the Day of Christ through a straight line and rejoiced" because S2 (where Jesus is enthroned as Wisdom/*Reshit*) stands directly above Abraham's S4, making the Johannine "before Abraham was born, I am" (John 8) a sefirotic demonstration of Christ's priority. The three archangels Raphael (ethics/dialectic, S10), Gabriel (natural philosophy, S5), and Michael (theology, S4; "God's likeness," high priest who sacrifices souls by the kiss) complete the ascent structure.

**Oration IV: The Defense of Disputation and the Concord Program (§§36–55)**

Copenhaver then reads Oration IV as Pico's meta-commentary on the 900 Conclusions — a rhetorical defense of the disputation project and an architectural description of the Conclusions' structure. Pico defends his youth (citing Paul's advice to Timothy, Job's Elihu), the legitimacy of scholastic disputation (tracing it through Plato, Aristotle, Gorgias, and Cicero), and the number 900 (invoking Gorgias who disputed "all questions in all the arts," making 900 a modest limit). Copenhaver shows that the self-defense is also a map of the Conclusions: Pico explains their two-part structure (T1: 400 theses "regarding" the views of named authorities; T2: 500 theses "following my own opinion"), the six medieval scholastics of T1 (Albert the Great, Aquinas, Francis of Meyronnes, Scotus, Henry of Ghent, Giles of Rome), the eight Arab Peripatetics (Averroes, Avicenna, Alfarabi, Avempace), the five Greek commentators, and the Platonists (Plotinus, Porphyry, Iamblichus, Proclus — 55 theses for Proclus alone). The Conclusions move from familiar to alien: Latin scholastics → Arab Peripatetics → Greek commentators → Platonists → Pythagorean mathematics → Chaldean theology → Hermetic Trismegistus → Kabbalist sages (T1), then concord theses → new philosophy → new theology → new mathematics → magic → Kabbalah (T2). Copenhaver highlights hidden structural clues: thesis 600 is the first of the Timaean-numerical set (part C), and 600 is the value of the closed Hebrew letter *mem* — an esoteric invitation to contemplation that no Christian reader could have recognized.

The concord thesis T2Ur1 — "There is no problem of natural philosophy or theology about which Aristotle and Plato do not agree in meaning and substance even though they seem to disagree in words" — is identified as the first and master claim of T2. Nine of the first seventeen T2 theses deny conflicts between Thomist and Scotist positions; three reconcile Avicenna and Averroes. Copenhaver emphasizes that Pico's nephew Gianfrancesco confirmed the concord project as the great unrealized aspiration of his uncle's philosophical career. Brucker's eighteenth-century denunciation of this as syncretism ("Oriental and Platonic lunacy") is cited to set up the historiographical problem the book addresses.

Copenhaver also gives close attention to the condemned thesis T2M9 ("There is no knowledge that gives us more certainty of Christ's divinity than magic and Kabbalah") and Pico's logical defense of it in the *Apology* as a disjunctive hypothetical. He contextualizes it in the climate of Innocent VIII's 1484 witchcraft endorsement (*Summis desiderantes affectibus*, printed with the *Malleus Maleficarum* in 1486) and identifies Pico's rashness: mocking Roman prelates for not knowing the word "Kabbalah" in print was unwise.

Throughout, Copenhaver's historiographical argument is that prior modern readers — whether Cassirer's dignitas-humanist Pico, or Farmer's structuralist Pico, or Wirszubski's crypto-Kabbalist Pico — have all missed the integrative mystical logic that ties every element of the Oration together: a systematic curriculum of ascent whose ultimate aim is the annihilation of the self in God.

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: Oration III is not rhetorical amplification but a series of authoritative consultations (Paul, Jacob, Job, Moses, Zoroaster, Abraham) each providing one of seven expositions of the Cherubic life, organized as a mystical curriculum (p. 375).

**Claim 2**: The Areopagite's *kruphia paradosis* is the template for Pico's mystical curriculum — a three-stage (catharsis, illumination, perfection) ascent to "God's nameless shadows" — but Pico inserts natural philosophy as a bridge between the ethical/dialectical stages and theological completion (pp. 376–377).

**Claim 3**: Pico's thesis T2K34 explicitly ratifies the Trinity and Incarnation as the Christian doctrines most "alien to Judaism" confirmed by both Kabbalah and the Areopagite's negative theology (p. 375).

**Claim 4**: Abulafia's *Ve-zot* is the primary Kabbalist source for Pico's ladder imagery, including the identification of *sullam* (130) with the doubled divine Name *Adonai* (65 + 65), and for the "secret of the ladder" as a spherical cosmic structure (pp. 378–379).

**Claim 5**: Thesis T2K59's "fourfold arrangement" uses the Hebrew letter *bet* to encode Father, Son, and Sabbath-rest, deriving a sefirotic Trinity from Menahem Recanati's Torah commentary; this is invisible to any reader without the thesis (pp. 379–380).

**Claim 6**: Gersonides' naturalist commentary on Job — not primarily Kabbalist sources — was the commentator Pico followed when preparing his Latin translation of Job with Flavius Mithridates, and Gersonides' astrological determinism and conjunction theory shaped Pico's account of Job and magia (pp. 380–381).

**Claim 7**: Thesis T1K24 encodes the angelological peace of Job 25 in sefirotic terms: Michael (Southern Water, S4) and Gabriel (Northern Fire, S5) reconcile at S6 (*shamayim* = fire + water) (p. 381).

**Claim 8**: Metempsychosis from the *Bahir* and *Zohar* (and Empedocles and Nahmanides) underpins Pico's reading of Job's covenantal pre-natal innocence, reflected in thesis T1K41 ("every good soul is a new soul coming from the East") (pp. 382–383).

**Claim 9**: The "death of the kiss" (*binsica*, thesis T2K13) — rapturous self-extinction in God — is the terminus of the entire Cherubic program, sourced from the Zohar, Maimonides, Gersonides, and Ibn Tufayl's *Hayy ibn Yaqzan* (translated by Pico in 1493) (pp. 385–388).

**Claim 10**: The *Bahir*'s sefirotic reading of Psalm 45 — with the letter *bet* encoding male-female (zayin + he) union in *zahab* (gold) — furnishes Pico's nuptial-death imagery; S10 (Shekinah) is both the royal bride and the soul; S6 (Tiferet = Jacob = Christ) is her spouse, per thesis T1K17 (pp. 384–386).

**Claim 11**: Pico's four-stage curriculum (ethics → dialectic → natural philosophy → theology) remaps the Tabernacle's spatial zones, drawing on the Zohar's and Abulafia's equations of the Tabernacle with the human body and the cosmos (pp. 389–390).

**Claim 12**: Pico's mysticism is aligned with Plotinus and Porphyry (self-extinction, flight from the body) rather than Augustine (lifelong discipline, Incarnation-grounded), evidenced by the Incarnation appearing only once in the Oration and only in a list of doctrines confirmed by Kabbalah (p. 388).

**Claim 13**: The Pythagorean instructions (from Iamblichus's *Protrepticus*) map directly onto the four curriculum stages: "feed the cock" = theology; "do not urinate facing the Sun" = ethics; "do not sit on the bushel" = dialectic; "do not trim nails during sacrifice" = management of passions (pp. 395–396).

**Claim 14**: Abraham's journeys always going south are decoded by Joseph Gikatilla's Kabbalist commentary: Abram ascends from S10 toward S4 (Piety/Love) via the right (merciful) side of the sefirotic tree, giving the philosophical geography of the Oration a precise sefirotic substrate (pp. 399–402).

**Claim 15**: Theses T2K37 and T2K42 use the sefirotic spatial arrangement — S2 (Christ/Wisdom/*Reshit*) directly above S4 (Abraham) — to prove Johannine Christology: Abraham saw Christ "through a straight line" (p. 402).

**Claim 16**: The 900 Conclusions are architecturally divided into five major parts (A: concord; B: new philosophy/theology; C: new mathematics; D: magic; E: Kabbalah), with hidden structural markers including thesis 600 = the closed Hebrew *mem* (value 600) marking the boundary of the mathematical section (pp. 417–419).

**Claim 17**: T2Ur1 — that Plato and Aristotle "agree in meaning and substance even though they seem to disagree in words" — is the master concord thesis, the first of 17 reconciliation theses and a program Gianfrancesco Pico described as his uncle's great unrealized project (p. 416).

**Claim 18**: Pico's defense of T2M9 in the *Apology* (using a disjunctive hypothetical) was logically clever but evasive, and his mockery of Roman prelates for ignorance of Kabbalah in print was reckless given the climate of Innocent VIII's witchcraft endorsements (pp. 405–406).

**Claim 19**: Pico's charge that he was "the first" to bring Platonic philosophy into public disputation was an overreach — Ficino, had he seen the printed Oration, should have objected — though priority for public disputation on this topic was more defensible (p. 415).

## Technical Vocabulary

***binsica*** (T2K13): Hebrew *be-neshiqah* = "by/in the kiss"; the death of the kiss (*mitat neshiqah*) — mystical death of patriarchs as union with the Agent Intellect; distinguished from ordinary bodily death (p. 387)

**S1–S10**: Sefirot (singular: Sefirah); the ten divine attributes / hypostases in Kabbalah. S1 = Keter (Crown); S2 = Hokmah (Wisdom); S3 = Binah (Understanding); S4 = Hesed (Love/Piety); S5 = Gevurah / Din (Judgment); S6 = Tiferet (Beauty); S9 = Yesod (Foundation); S10 = Malkut/Shekinah (Kingdom/Presence) (passim)

**Shekinah**: S10; divine Presence / God's Dwelling; feminine; the Sefirah closest to the lower world; bride, daughter, exile; also identified with the Congregation of Israel (pp. 386–388, 393, 447–448)

**Tiferet**: S6; Beauty; male partner; identified with Adam, Jacob, Christ in Pico; central position on sefirotic tree (pp. 386–387)

***epopteia***: Greek; "seeing," "gazing at divinity"; final stage of mystery-cult initiation; Pico revises to insert natural philosophy before it (pp. 377, 390)

***binsica* / *mitat neshiqah***: death of the kiss; the ideal mode of mystical death in which the soul departs the body, not the body the soul (p. 387)

***pardes***: Hebrew acronym for four hermeneutic levels: *peshat* (literal), *remez* (allegorical/philosophical), *derash* (homiletic), *sod* (secret) = also "paradise / garden" (p. 399)

***sullam***: Hebrew "ladder" (numerical value 130 = twice *Adonai* 65); key term in Abulafia's ladder mysticism (p. 378)

***gilgul***: Hebrew transmigration of souls; thesis T1K41; connected to Job's narrative and Empedocles (pp. 382–383)

***kruphia paradosis***: Greek (Areopagite); "occult tradition"; the esoteric mystical teaching transmitted only to initiates (p. 377)

***stilus Parisiensis***: "Parisian style/method" of formal scholastic disputation; the format of the 900 Conclusions (p. 407)

***quolibet / quodlibetal***: scholastic dispute format — "by anyone, about anything"; Pico's planned Roman disputation would have been quodlibetal (p. 407)

***bereshit***: Hebrew "in the beginning" (Gen. 1:1); also readable as *bara shit* ("created six"); S2 = *Reshit* (Beginning); T1K9 (p. 385)

***zahab***: Hebrew "gold"; decoded letter by letter in *Bahir*: zayin (male) + he (feminine soul) + bet (dyad/pair) = sefirotic nuptial union encoded in Psalm 45's "golden garment" (pp. 384–385)

***epopteia* / *epoptic***: Greek "seeing"; Plato and Aristotle call the final part of philosophy "epoptic" (Plutarch via Pico); the culminating vision of divinity (pp. 390–391)

***iunges / jynxes***: Chaldean Oracles; magical birds (wagtails or wrynecks); sympathetic conduits of divine energy; precursors of the English "jinx" (p. 445 — in Ch. 13 overlap material)

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Guide to the Oration**: This chapter is the primary Copenhaver lens for the Oration's middle sections (III–IV). It is prerequisite for any claim about what the Oration actually argues. Chapters 11 and 13 are the flanking units.

**Myth being tested**: The Burckhardt-Cassirer-Garin myth that the Oration is primarily a humanist manifesto about human dignity and freedom. Copenhaver's Oration III shows that the "chameleon" passage in Oration I–II is an overture for a rigorous mystical curriculum whose destination is self-annihilation in God — the opposite of Renaissance individualism.

**Historiographical register**: Copenhaver is explicitly anti-presentist. He insists that the Oration is written for a specific audience (Roman cardinals, learned Jews), in a specific occasion (the planned 1487 Roman disputation), and with a specific set of sources (Kabbalist manuscripts translated by Flavius Mithridates) unavailable to most prior scholars. The Conclusions are the decoder for the Oration, not a separate project.

**Concord outcome**: Copenhaver shows that Pico's concord is not naïve eclecticism but a structured program: Plato with Aristotle (T2Ur1), Scotus with Aquinas, Averroes with Avicenna, and (climactically) Kabbalah with Christianity. Brucker's denunciation as syncretism is the Enlightenment misreading Copenhaver argues against throughout the book.

## Relation to Pico Works

**Oration III, §§16–35**: This is Pico's sustained mystical curriculum, where the speech is most dense and most Kabbalistic. The surface text uses Jacob's ladder, Job's angelic armies, Moses's Tabernacle, Zoroaster's rivers, Abraham's journeys; the subtext (decoded by the Conclusions' Kabbalist theses) maps every image onto sefirotic positions and processes. No sentence in these sections should be read without the Conclusions open alongside.

**Oration IV, §§36–55**: The rhetorical and architectural explanation of the Conclusions. Pico names the six medieval scholastics, the Arab Peripatetics, the Greek commentators, the Platonists, and then describes the five-part structure of T2 (A–E). This is where the concord program is stated most explicitly.

**Key thesis clusters**:
- **T2K34, T2K8**: Kabbalah confirms Trinity, Incarnation, Pauline theology
- **T2K59**: *Bet*-theology → sefirotic Trinity (Father/Son/Sabbath)
- **T1K24, T1K28**: Angelological peace of Job; birds/angels in the world
- **T1K11, T1K14, T2K37, T2K42**: Eden's rivers and Abraham's journeys = sefirotic ascent; Christ as Wisdom (S2) seen by Abraham (S4) "through a straight line"
- **T2K11, T2K13**: Death of the kiss; *binsica*; danger of unpurified Kabbalist practice (Azazel)
- **T1K41**: Metempsychosis / new souls from the East
- **T1K9, T1K10, T1K17**: Creation, Paradise, and sefirotic nuptials (*bet* = S10 married to S6)
- **T2Z6, T2Z8–9, T2Or24, T2K17**: Interlocking Chaldean/Orphic/Kabbalist wine-theology (double drunkenness)
- **T2Ur1**: Master concord thesis; first of T2
- **T2M9**: Condemned thesis; magic and Kabbalah as knowledge of Christ's divinity

## Uncertainties

1. **Ethiopic script in the text** (p. 398): The source file renders four Ethiopic/Syriac place-names from Eden's rivers in Ethiopic characters (ቄሥተ, ኩጰሮነ, ነሆራ, ራሓማኗተ). These appear to be correctly transcribed. Their identification as "Syriac or Aramaic, written in Ethiopic script" is Copenhaver's; it may reflect OCR ambiguity between scripts in the original PDF.
2. **"Adelandus Arabs"** (p. 413): Copenhaver tentatively identifies this Platonist in T1 as Adelard of Bath, a twelfth-century Englishman. This is not settled and may require further investigation in Farmer's catalogue.
3. **"Avenzoar" from "Abumasar"** (p. 418): Copenhaver notes that the person Pico cites as "Avenzoar" from Abu Ma'shar is chronologically impossible — the physician Ibn Zuhr died in the twelfth century, Abu Ma'shar in the ninth. The gnomological citation "a person who knows numbers knows everything" may be from Arabic gnomological literature in Latin translation, not either thinker's authentic work.
4. **Thesis numbering in the Conclusions** (p. 418): Copenhaver notes that the Oration claims "72 novel teachings in physics and metaphysics" but the 1486 headnote has 71, confirmed by the 1532 edition. Pico reserved 72 for the Kabbalist finale. This is a minor textual discrepancy in the Oration itself, not an OCR problem.
5. **Line 15528**: "ἀrone of Glory" — likely OCR error for "Throne of Glory" (Greek *ἀ* replacing *Th*). Confirmed by context. This OCR substitution pattern (*ἀ* for *Th*) recurs throughout the markdown source.
