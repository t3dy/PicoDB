It is followed immediately by the Conclusion (p. 451), which traces the book's historiographical argument about modern misreadings of Pico.

## Section Function

Chapter 13 is the payoff chapter of Copenhaver's entire textual argument. Parts One and Two of *Magic and the Dignity of Man* dismantled the modern reception myth — the "Oration on the Dignity of Man" as a humanist manifesto celebrating self-fashioning and human excellence — by tracing its invention through Cassirer, Garin, Gentile, and the postwar American general education curriculum. Part Three then reads the *Oration* on its own terms as a preface to a disputation, not as an independent philosophical document. Chapter 13 covers the final three sections of the *Oration* (V–VII, paragraphs 56–72), in which Pico defends first natural magic (Orat. V, §§56–62), then Kabbalah (Orat. VI, §§63–68), and finally closes with a brief return to Orpheus and a defiant challenge to his Roman clerical audience (Orat. VII, §§69–72).

What these sections accomplish, in Copenhaver's reading, is the revelation of the *Oration*'s actual doctrinal purpose: not to celebrate the dignity of the human person, but to present a program of ascent through magic and Kabbalah toward mystical union, and to co-opt Christian clerical authority for a set of propositions — drawn from natural magic, Jewish theosophy, Neoplatonist demonology, and Kabbalistic Christology — that the Church found alarming enough to condemn. The chapter argues that Pico's defense of natural magic and Kabbalah are not rhetorical ornaments appended to an otherwise humanist speech; they are the *Oration*'s core, and understanding them means reading them against the *900 Conclusions* (*Conclusiones nongentae*, 1486), which the speech was composed to introduce.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

### Oration V: The Defense of Natural Magic (Orat. §§56–62)

Pico opens his defense of magic by drawing the fundamental terminological distinction that structures the entire section: Latin *magia* translates two Greek concepts that must be held apart. *Mageia* is natural and admirable; *goêteia* is demonic and hateful. The Persian *magus* was originally a servant of God, a religious specialist, not a sorcerer. Copenhaver traces Pico's source tradition carefully, showing that this distinction was available in ancient law, philosophy, and religion, but was never as clean or stable as Pico required. Augustine, for instance, acknowledged that *magia*, *theurgia*, and *goetia* had distinct Latin meanings, but held that all demonic communication was evil — a position that ruled out any "purely natural magic" of the kind Pico wished to establish (p. 421).

Copenhaver argues that this Augustinian legacy left Pico — and Ficino — with very little basis for the clean division between good and bad magic they needed. A purely natural magic involving no personal agency at all was unknown to Greek theorists and rejected by many Christians (p. 421). The alternative Pico adopted was essentially an exoteric/esoteric split: natural magic for public defense, angel-magic kept quiet. His theses on magic in the *Conclusiones* (T2M) demonstrate this strategy. The first four propositions distinguish good magic from bad; the next ten discuss magical forces in support of Christian doctrine. Then T2M15 — "No magical action can have any effect unless it has an act of Kabbalah, explicit or implicit, linked with it" — reveals the deeper structure: natural magic is ultimately dependent on Kabbalah for its efficacy (p. 421).

Pico's theoretical basis for natural magic comes primarily from Plotinus, who described magic as working through sympathy — "there is an agreement of like forces and an opposition of unlike" — with no interfering personal agency or demonic mediation. Magic is always already in Nature; the magician exploits causes rather than creating them. Pico's axiom, drawn from Plotinus, is that the magus operates "not so much by working wonders as by diligently serving nature as she works them" (p. 425). The thesis T2M13 crystallizes this: "Doing magic is nothing but marrying the world." The vine-and-elm image from Ovid's *Vertumnus*, the jynxes (*iunges*) of the Chaldean Oracles, Synesius on sympathetic spells, and Apuleius's naturalist defense against sorcery charges all support this framework (pp. 424–425).

However, Copenhaver is precise about the limits of Plotinus's utility for Pico. Plotinus did not consistently distinguish *mageia* from *goêteia*; his concern was the hierarchy of the soul, not a typology of magic. The crucial Neoplatonist development was the post-Plotinian subdivision of magical force into personal beings — angels and demons — carried out by Porphyry and especially Iamblichus, whose theurgy converted magical sympathies into a hierarchical system of divine energies, lower and higher theurgies, and ritual practices (pp. 425–427). Once erotic metaphysics became demonology, a purely natural magic was impossible: demons were always already there. Iamblichus recognized this, and so did Pico: thesis T2K13 warns that "one who makes a mistake in the work or comes to it unpurified will be devoured by Azazel" (p. 427).

Copenhaver then reads Pico's claim that natural magic "excites man to astonishment at God's works" as positioning magic as stage 5 in the *Oration*'s seven-step regimen: ethics → dialectic → natural philosophy → theology → magic → Kabbalah → mystical union. This is a crucial structural insight that Copenhaver maps explicitly (p. 427). Magic is not an end in itself but a penultimate step, "epoptic magic" that stimulates religious astonishment and prepares the soul for Kabbalah's deeper intimations. The most provocative magic thesis, T2M9 — "There is no knowledge that gives us more certainty of Christ's divinity than magic and Kabbalah" — must be read in the context of T2M6–8, which establish that Christ's miracles surpassed both magic and Kabbalah (as secondary causes) and that all effects ultimately refer to God as First Cause. In the *Apology*, Pico clarified that T2M9 compared magic and Kabbalah not to revealed theology but to ordinary scholastic natural philosophy of sublunary change. Because magic and Kabbalah look higher — to the stars and beyond — they produce nobler, more certain knowledge by the norms of natural philosophy (pp. 427–428). The additional structural claim: acts of magic need Kabbalah to be effective (T2M15), but acts of Kabbalah can be effective without magic (T2M26). Kabbalah's domain exceeds Nature; the exculpation of natural magic neither applies to Kabbalah nor need do so.

### Oration VI: The Defense of Kabbalah (Orat. §§63–68)

Pico's defense of Kabbalah in section VI deploys two interrelated arguments: (1) Kabbalah is ancient, divinely authorized esoteric wisdom with impeccable credentials traceable to Moses at Sinai; (2) Kabbalah, when read correctly, confirms the central dogmas of Christianity, above all the Trinity, and can be weaponized against Jews in theological dispute.

On the antiquity and legitimacy of Kabbalah, Pico tells the story from Ezra (Esdras): God revealed not just the public Law to Moses but also a private interpretation commanded to remain hidden; Ezra's scribes produced 70 esoteric books alongside the 24 canonical ones (pp. 433–434). Copenhaver reads this narrative strategy carefully: Pico is not just presenting Kabbalah as interesting Jewish mysticism but claiming that it constitutes an ancient oral Torah, received and transmitted in secret succession from Moses through Joshua to the high priests. The Hebrew word *kabbalah* means "reception" or "tradition" — as Abulafia explained in *Ve-Zot* — emphasizing legitimate chain-of-transmission (p. 436). To buttress this claim, Pico marshals a crowd of witnesses for the principle of sacred secrecy: Hilary on hidden mysteries of the Law, Origen on the pearl not to be cast before swine, the Areopagite on apostolic knowledge transmitted only "from mind to mind, by speech, not writing," Pythagoras's prohibition against sharing his notes, Plato's Pythagorean rule of concealment, and Aristotle's letter to Alexander about published-yet-unpublished secrets (pp. 434–436).

Copenhaver notes that Pico's co-optive strategy with Kabbalah is "malicious but sincere": Pico genuinely believed he had new proofs of Christian doctrines from Jewish sources, and he thought that the books of Kabbalah — subsidized in Latin translation by Sixtus IV — described "a religion not so much Mosaic as Christian" (p. 431). The key theses are T2K5 and T2K7. T2K5 argues that any Hebrew Kabbalist following Kabbalistic principles "is forced inevitably to grant precisely — without addition, subtraction, or variation — what the Catholic faith of Christians declares about the Trinity and each divine person, Father, Son, and Holy Spirit." T2K7 claims that the name *Jesus* in Hebrew signifies "God, the Son of God, and the Wisdom of the Father united through the third person of the Deity (who is the hottest fire of Love) to human nature" (p. 443). This is full Kabbalistic Christology.

The Trinitarian architecture Copenhaver reconstructs is intricate. T2K6 assigns the three great fourfold divine names — *Ehyeh* (S1), *yhwh* (S6), *Adonai* (S10) — to Father, Son, and Holy Spirit respectively. Three separate Trinities can be identified in the ten Sefirot: the undescended (S1, S2, S3), the descending (S1, S6, S10), and the fully descended (S1, S9, S10) (pp. 444–445, Figure 21). T2K33 extends the Trinitarian reading by interpreting the Hebrew word *Ish* (man, alef-yod-shin) as a complete Trinitarian reminder through Kabbalah. Copenhaver shows that Pico's reading of *Ish* via the *Bahir* both follows and deliberately exceeds the *Bahir*'s own allocation: rather than contradiction, Pico saw "not contradiction but completion, in a different dimension, of one Trinitarian insight by others" (p. 445).

Running through the Kabbalah section is a largely silent numerological argument. Copenhaver demonstrates that the number 72 — derived from the Tetragrammaton *yhwh* through triangular addition (10+15+21+26) — is the count of the Kabbalist theses in T2K and is deliberately kept secret in the *Oration* (pp. 432–433). The number of the Orphic Hymns (86) encodes the numerical value of *Elohim*. The total count of 900 theses is itself a "mystical number," as Pico told Benivieni. Pico was practicing numerology as he wrote, but staying silent about numerology as a method, because revealing it would have made the speech too long, too hard, and too dangerous: a person who knew the secret of 72 would gain access to angel magic of a kind that risked demonic retaliation (pp. 432–433).

Copenhaver also gives sustained attention to Flavius Mithridates (Guglielmo Moncada), the converted Sicilian Jew who served as Pico's primary translator of Hebrew and Aramaic Kabbalistic texts. Flavius's Latin translations sometimes inserted Trinitarian clues absent in the originals — an editorial tendency that inflated the apparent Christian content of Kabbalah. Copenhaver notes that Innocent VIII (Cardinal Cibò, Flavius's former employer) later condemned thirteen of Pico's theses, suggesting that the pope's prior experience with Flavius may have colored his reception of Pico's judgment (p. 441). The "Ethiopic" characters in the *Oration* manuscript (Figure 20, p. 442) — used by Flavius to transliterate Hebrew and Aramaic to pass off as 'Chaldean' — are a concrete instance of the book's broader argument that Pico's exotic authority was frequently manufactured.

### Oration VII: Close and the Orphic Coda (Orat. §§69–72)

Section VII is brief. Pico returns to the Orphic Hymns as a final illustration of how ancient numerical mysteries were dismissed as nonsense by those who could not read them. Copenhaver reads this Orphic coda as strategically placed after Kabbalah to provide his Roman clerical audience with a gentler analogy: because the Hymns were already known by name and reputation, Pico implicitly argued that Kabbalah — no more enigmatic than Orphic poetry — deserved equal respect (p. 446). The deeper connection is structural: just as the Hymns encode a Pythagorean arithmetic theology (confirmed by Iamblichus and Proclus), so Kabbalah encodes a numerological Trinitarian theology. Both require esoteric hermeneutics to unlock. Pico also mentions Zoroaster only to pass over him quickly: the prophet as inventor of magic had a dark side — sorcery that enslaves to "God's enemies" — that Pico preferred not to dwell on (p. 448). The chapter ends with Pico's closing appeal to the "Apostolic Senate" of cardinals: a trumpet-blast challenge to begin the disputation that never actually took place.

### Copenhaver's Major Revisionary Claims

The chapter's governing argument is that the *Oration* has been radically misread because readers — from Burckhardt through Cassirer, Garin, and the postwar Western Civ tradition — excerpted its opening pages about the chameleon man and the free will of the soul while ignoring that these sections are a prelude to a program of magical-Kabbalistic ascent toward mystical union. The *Oration* is not a dignity text; it is an introduction to a disputed set of propositions about the best way to be saved, and magic and Kabbalah are not peripheral curiosities but the heart of Pico's program. The regimen of seven steps, culminating in Kabbalah and then union, is the book's spine. Copenhaver also insists that Pico's Kabbalistic Christology — his claim that Kabbalah forces Jews to affirm the Trinity — is anti-Jewish apologetics, not ecumenism, and certainly not an early celebration of religious pluralism or human dignity. The inclusion was historic but "not irenic and not about dignity" (p. 451).

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: Latin *magia* translates two Greek concepts that must be distinguished: *mageia* (natural, admirable) vs. *goêteia* (demonic, hateful); this distinction was universal in ancient law, philosophy, and religion (p. 420).

**Claim 2**: Augustine acknowledged distinct meanings for *magia*, *theurgia*, and *goetia* in Latin but held that all demonic communication is evil — a position that undermined any clean distinction between good and bad magic available to Pico (p. 421).

**Claim 3**: The Augustinian legacy left Pico and Ficino with "little basis for a clean distinction between good and bad magic"; a purely natural magic involving no personal agency was unknown to Greek theorists and rejected by many Christians (p. 421).

**Claim 4**: Pico's strategy in the *Conclusiones* was to defend natural magic publicly while keeping angel-magic (a non-natural, higher type) quiet; thesis T2M15 links magic to Kabbalah, revealing that the distinction is not as clean as the *Oration* suggests (p. 421).

**Claim 5**: Pico derived his core axiom for natural magic from Plotinus: the magus operates "not so much by working wonders as by diligently serving nature as she works them" — but Plotinus did not consistently distinguish *mageia* from *goêteia* (pp. 424–425).

**Claim 6**: T2M13 ("Doing magic is nothing but marrying the world") epitomizes Pico's naturalist theory of magic through sympathetic forces (p. 520).

**Claim 7**: After Porphyry and Iamblichus personalized love-forces into a demonology, a purely natural magic became impossible; once erotic metaphysics became demonology, demons were always already present (pp. 426–427).

**Claim 8**: Magic occupies step 5 in Pico's seven-step regimen of ascent (ethics → dialectic → natural philosophy → theology → magic → Kabbalah → union); it is not an end but a penultimate preparation (p. 427).

**Claim 9**: T2M9 ("There is no knowledge that gives us more certainty of Christ's divinity than magic and Kabbalah") must be read against T2M6–8: Christ's miracles are supernatural (not magical or Kabbalistic), and all effects ultimately refer to God as First Cause; the comparison is to natural philosophy, not to revealed theology (pp. 427–428).

**Claim 10**: Acts of magic require Kabbalah to be effective (T2M15); acts of Kabbalah can be effective without magic (T2M26); Kabbalah's domain transcends the natural order (p. 428).

**Claim 11**: Pico's defense of Kabbalah combines two claims: (a) it is ancient divinely authorized secret wisdom from Sinai; (b) it confirms Christian dogma, including the Trinity, Incarnation, and divinity of the Messiah, and can be used against Jews (pp. 429–431).

**Claim 12**: Pico's tactic of using Kabbalah against Jews was "malicious but sincere" — he genuinely believed Kabbalah proved Christian truth; the T2K theses claim that books of Kabbalah describe "a religion not so much Mosaic as Christian" (p. 431).

**Claim 13**: The number 72, derived from the Tetragrammaton by triangular addition, is the count of T2K theses and encodes an angel-magical secret deliberately kept out of the *Oration* because of its danger (pp. 432–433).

**Claim 14**: T2K5 claims that any Hebrew Kabbalist following Kabbalistic principles "is forced inevitably to grant precisely — without addition, subtraction, or variation" the Catholic doctrine of the Trinity (p. 443).

**Claim 15**: T2K7 claims that the name *Jesus* in Hebrew signifies "God, the Son of God, and the Wisdom of the Father united through the third person of the Deity ... to human nature" — full Kabbalistic Christology (p. 443).

**Claim 16**: Three Trinities are identifiable in the ten Sefirot: undescended (S1, S2, S3), descending (S1, S6, S10), and fully descended (S1, S9, S10); T2K6 assigns *Ehyeh* (S1) to the Father, *yhwh* (S6) to the Son, *Adonai* (S10) to the Holy Spirit (pp. 444–445).

**Claim 17**: Flavius Mithridates's Latin translations of Kabbalistic texts sometimes inserted Trinitarian clues absent in the originals, inflating the apparent Christian content of Kabbalah (p. 443).

**Claim 18**: The Orphic coda (Orat. VII) functions as a rhetorical bridge to Kabbalah: because the Hymns were known by reputation, arguing their numerical mysteries deserved respect implicitly licensed the same respect for Kabbalist numerology (p. 446).

**Claim 19**: The *Oration* is not a dignity text; it is a preface to a magical-Kabbalistic-mystical program, and its famous opening sections about the chameleon man are a preamble, not the substance (pp. 420–450 passim; stated explicitly in Conclusion, p. 451).

## Technical Vocabulary

**mageia / goêteia**: Greek pair distinguishing natural/admirable magic from demonic/hateful sorcery; Pico's fundamental distinction in Orat. §56; never as stable in ancient sources as Pico needed (pp. 420–421)

**magia**: Latin term covering both *mageia* and *goêteia*; Augustine used it with *theurgia* and *goetia* (p. 421)

**goêteia**: Greek; demonic sorcery calling on "matter-bound and unclean spirits"; name derived from *goôn tôn thrênôn* (chanters of dirges); even used by Plotinus sometimes as synonym for *mageia*, complicating Pico's project (pp. 420, 424, 425, 426)

**magus** (pl. *magoi*): Persian religious specialist, originally enemy alien to Greeks but also "servant of God" in Pico's reading; the Latin *magus* gave English "magician" (p. 420)

**iunges (jynxes)**: Magical birds (wagtails or wrynecks) in the Chaldean Oracles, used as symbols of God's sympathy-binding thoughts; also a toy and a spell; Synesius used them to explain how sympathetic magic works across cosmic distances (pp. 424–425)

**sympathy (sympatheia)**: The principle of natural attraction between like things in Plotinus and the Neoplatonists; the theoretical backbone of natural magic without demonic agency (pp. 424–425)

**theurgy (theurgia)**: "God-working"; ritual practice using natural objects to channel divine energies; developed by Porphyry and Iamblichus as complement (and eventually superior) to philosophical ascent (pp. 425–426)

**Sefirot (Numerations)**: The ten divine attributes/emanations in Kabbalah; Pico maps Trinitarian assignments onto them: S1 = Father, S2/S6 = Son, S3/S10 = Holy Spirit in different configurations (pp. 384–385, 444–445)

**Eyn-sof ('No-End')**: The infinite, unnameable God beyond the Sefirot; T2K4 distinguishes it from the numbered Sefirot as "removed and uncommunicated" (p. 444)

**Tetragrammaton (yhwh)**: The four-letter divine Name (yod-he-waw-he); its numerical value 26; by triangular addition yields 72, the secret number of T2K theses (pp. 432–433)

**kabbalah**: Hebrew; literally "reception" or "tradition"; the secret oral interpretation of the Law transmitted from Moses through Joshua; divided by Pico (T2K1) into practical (Sefirot) and visionary/speculative (Names) branches (pp. 436, 437–438)

**revolving the alphabet**: Pico's phrase for Abulafia's ecstatic meditation technique combining Hebrew consonants with vowels in systematic permutation, chanting divine Names to achieve prophetic trance (pp. 438–439)

**Merkabah / Chariot**: Ezekiel's vision of God's Throne; interpreted by Maimonides as philosophical theology; Pico's triple Chariot in T2K2 maps onto a threefold philosophy of divine, middle, and sensible natures (pp. 438–439)

**philosophia catholica**: Pico's term in T2K2 meaning "comprehensive" or "universal" philosophy (as opposed to "particularizing" *particularis* philosophy), probably corresponding to Abulafia's alphabet-revolving as entry point to ecstatic Kabbalah (p. 437)

**speculativus**: Latin analog of Greek *theôrêtikos*; the "visionary" kind of Kabbalah concerned with Names and ecstatic ascent, in T2K1–2 (p. 438)

**vehicle (ochêma)**: The Platonist astral body or soul-carrier that accumulates astral influence as the soul falls through the heavens; T2Pla45 identifies the "sentience in nature" of Alkindi and others with this vehicle; a way to ground magical power without appealing to demons (p. 423)

**epoptic magic**: Copenhaver's term for the astonishment-inducing visionary function of natural magic in Pico's regimen — magic as a mode of contemplation that precedes Kabbalah (p. 427)

**72**: The secret numerical value of T2K, derived from the Tetragrammaton; also the number of angels concealed in three verses of Exodus 14; too dangerous to publicize in the *Oration* (pp. 432–433)

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Myth being tested**: The "Oration on the Dignity of Man" as a humanist manifesto celebrating the free creative potential of the individual human person. Copenhaver shows that this reading depends on excerpting the *Oration*'s opening pages about the chameleon man while ignoring everything from §56 onward — roughly half the speech and all of its most distinctive, most dangerous content.

**Historiographical register**: Copenhaver operates as a revisionist internal to the history-of-philosophy tradition rather than as a cultural historian or sociologist of knowledge. His revisionism is philological and doctrinal: he insists on close reading of the Latin and Greek texts, attention to the *Conclusiones* as the *Oration*'s primary context, and recovery of the Kabbalistic and magical sources that the twentieth-century reception ignored.

**Guide for scholars**: Chapter 13 is essential for any scholar working on Pico's magic, Kabbalah, or the *Oration*. It is the most sustained English-language reading of *Oration* sections V–VII against the *Conclusiones* and the *Apology* simultaneously. It provides: the fullest English exposition of the T2M and T2K theses as a coherent system; the numerological argument about 72; the three-Trinities architecture; and the structural claim that magic is step 5 and Kabbalah step 6 in a seven-step ascent toward union.

**Concord outcome**: The chapter shows that Pico's philosophical concord — the reconciliation of Plato and Aristotle, of pagan and Christian wisdom — has a specifically soteriological function: it is not intellectual synthesis for its own sake but preparation of the soul for a Kabbalistic-magical ascent that transcends philosophy. Concord is a propaedeutic, not the goal.

## Relation to Pico Works

**Oration on the Dignity of Man** (*Oratio de hominis dignitate*): The chapter provides a sustained reading of §§56–72, the three sections devoted to magic and Kabbalah. The key interpretive move is reading these sections not as autonomous philosophical statements but as a preface to the disputed theses, especially the T2M and T2K propositions. The seven-step regimen diagram (p. 427) is the architectural key to the *Oration*'s structure.

**900 Conclusions** (*Conclusiones nongentae*, 1486): The chapter reads the *Oration* as inseparable from the *Conclusiones*. The theses cited most extensively: T2M4, T2M6–9, T2M13, T2M15, T2M18, T2M26 (magic); T2K1–7, T2K10, T2K13, T2K17, T2K33 (Kabbalah); T2Or4–5, T2Or21 (Orphic). The thesis T2M15 ("No magical action can have any effect unless it has an act of Kabbalah, explicit or implicit, linked with it") is identified as the key hinge between the magic and Kabbalah sections. T2M9 ("There is no knowledge that gives us more certainty of Christ's divinity than magic and Kabbalah") is the most notorious thesis and receives the fullest exposition.

**Apology** (*Apologia*, 1487): Used to clarify T2M9 (the "type of knowledge" compared to magic and Kabbalah is natural philosophy, not revealed theology); to confirm the high price of Pico's Kabbalist library; and to establish William of Auvergne as the scholastic authority for natural magic as non-demonic "natural actions." The *Apology* is thus used throughout as a gloss on the *Conclusiones*.

**Specific passages:**
- T2M13: "Doing magic is nothing but marrying the world" — epitomizes Pico's naturalist theory
- T2M15: Kabbalah required for any effective magic — the key structural claim linking the two disciplines
- T2M9: "No knowledge more certain of Christ's divinity than magic and Kabbalah" — the most notorious proposition, clarified in the *Apology*
- T2K5: Kabbalists forced to grant the Trinity "without addition, subtraction, or variation" — the core Kabbalistic Christology claim
- T2K6: Trinitarian assignment of Hebrew divine names to Sefirot S1, S6, S10
- T2K7: Name of Jesus in Hebrew as Trinitarian signifier
- T2Or4: David's Psalms for Kabbalah, Orphic Hymns for natural magic — the symmetrical bridge

## Uncertainties

**OCR / text rendering**: The Hebrew divine names (Ehyeh, yhwh, Adonai) printed in T2K6 and surrounding theses were left blank by the 1486 printer; Copenhaver follows Wirszubski's reconstruction. The Markdown source renders these in Hebrew Unicode (אהיה, יהוה, אדני), which is accurate.

**Ethiopic characters**: The Ethiopic script passage from the *Oration* manuscript (Figure 20, p. 442) is reproduced in Unicode in the Markdown source (በረናሠ ሀ ሐያ ሚጠበዐ ሜሥታኔ ዌናዳደ ሜመሐለጰተ ጋረማሀ ኮ ወኮ). Accuracy relative to the actual manuscript cannot be verified without the image.

**Figure 21 (Three Trinities in the Sefirot)**: Referenced at p. 445 but described in text rather than reproduced in the Markdown source. The diagram shows S1/S2/S3, S1/S6/S10, and S1/S9/S10 as three distinct Trinitarian configurations.

**Page count**: The chapter runs pp. 420–450 (not including the Conclusion beginning p. 451). The Markdown source renders running heads ("Pico Defends Magic and Kabbalah") at approximately every 85 lines, confirming pagination.

**Dattilo**: Copenhaver identifies "Dattilo" as Hebrew *Joab*, "a common name," and says his identity is "unknown" (p. 443). This remains unresolved in the scholarship.

**Zoroaster theses (T2Z)**: Copenhaver notes Pico "chose to leave Zoroaster aside" in Orat. VII despite 15 Zoroaster theses; the Syriac materials Pico described to Ficino may have been doctored or concocted by Flavius — a hypothesis flagged as uncertain (p. 448).
