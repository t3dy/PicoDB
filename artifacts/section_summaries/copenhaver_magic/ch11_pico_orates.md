Part Three ("Pico's Oration"), Chapter 11 of 14. The chapter carries an epigraph from Isaiah: "The prudence of the prudent shall be hid."

## Section Function

Chapter 11 opens Part Three of the book, which shifts from the long historiographical survey of Parts One and Two (what modern scholars have made of the Oration) to Copenhaver's own close reading of the Oration text itself. This chapter serves as the threshold and apparatus chapter for that reading. It does three things simultaneously: (1) it establishes the methodological key to the Oration — secrecy, codes, and esoteric concealment — and identifies Kabbalah as the primary hidden grammar; (2) it provides the intellectual context for Pico's Kabbalah by tracing his debts to Abraham Abulafia, Menahem Recanati, Moses Maimonides, and the translator Flavius Mithridates; and (3) it begins the close reading of Oration sections I and II (paragraphs 1–15 in Copenhaver's outline), demonstrating through these two sections how the chapter's methodological claims are verified in the actual texture of the text. The chapter is thus both prolegomenon and first installment of a continuous close reading that continues through Chapters 12 and 13.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

**Section 1: Secrets and Codes (pp. 339–346)**

Copenhaver opens with a claim that constitutes the chapter's governing thesis: "Silence and secrecy are methods in the Oration, a speech that hides much of its message" (p. 339). The Oration, on this reading, is a deliberately opaque text in which "things stand for other things — allusions buried in allusions, obscure phrases hinting at sacred truths" (p. 339). Any translation will be opaque because the original is opaque. This framing is not a lament about scholarly difficulty but a positive description of the text's design: Pico modeled his speech on the esoteric communicative conventions of Jewish Kabbalah, in which God commanded that mysteries of the Law be kept from the populace and reserved for the perfect.

Copenhaver establishes the bibliographic situation of the *900 Conclusions* with precision. The theses were printed once with Pico's consent, by Eucharius Silber in Rome on December 7, 1486, in an unpaginated volume with no title page. The posthumous 1532 edition numbered every thesis. The Oration functions as an introduction to this collection, and Copenhaver insists throughout that the Oration cannot be understood without the theses. He provides a notation system (Appendix B) for tracking individual theses: T1 and T2 for the two groups (400 and 500), followed by the set abbreviation and number within the set.

The 900 theses are themselves a Kabbalistic construction. Pico's final and culminating set in T2 consists of 72 "Kabbalist Conclusions according to his own opinion" — a number Copenhaver shows is not coincidental but derives from the 72 names of angels encoded in three verses of Exodus 14 (each with exactly 72 letters), arranged and read vertically to produce 72 three-letter angelic names totaling 216 letters, which together spell a name of God. This method of reading — exploiting Hebrew as a system where every letter is also a numeral — is *gematria*, and it provides the deep grammar of Pico's concluding theses. The 1532 edition prints 72 theses against the heading's "71," and Copenhaver argues 72 was the author's intention.

The section establishes the key division within Pico's Kabbalah. His first 47 theses on Kabbalah in T2 reproduce secrets of "Hebrew Kabbalist sages"; the final 72 are his own conclusions "according to my own opinion," inspired by a broader group of "Hebrew sages." The most important single source for Pico's own Kabbalah is Abraham Abulafia. Because no Latin versions of Abulafia's writings existed, Pico commissioned translations from the Sicilian Jewish convert Shmuel bin Nissim Abulfaraj — self-styled Flavius Mithridates — who translated thousands of pages for the prince, sometimes doctoring the texts. The key Abulafia text is *Ve-zot li-Yehuda* (And This is to Yehuda), a letter written after 1287 to Rabbi Yehuda Salmon, translated by Flavius under the title *A Brief Summary of Kabbalah*. Copenhaver establishes that this text, along with Recanati's *Commentary on the Pentateuch* (which transmitted Zoharic theosophy) and the *Bahir* (translated by Flavius), were Pico's principal Kabbalistic sources.

The character of Abulafia's Kabbalah is explained at length. Abulafia's letter opens with an acrostic preface assembled from fifteen propositions from Bible and Talmud, introduces two "secrets" — the "ladder" and "impregnation" — and singles out teachers who understand the "secret of the levirate" through interwoven allusions to Genesis, Deuteronomy, and Ruth. This Talmudic shorthand assumes mastery of a shared textual world that no Christian reader would have possessed. Pico imitated this bewildering style in the Oration.

Copenhaver also demonstrates that Pico's passion for secrecy was reinforced not only by Abulafia but by the Zohar, perhaps mediated through Recanati's commentaries. He quotes a passage from the Zohar in which two rabbis parse the word *zot* (זאת, "this") from Leviticus as a divine feminine power — "all Her forces" — a reading developed in the theosophical Kabbalah that Abulafia opposed but that shared Abulafia's esoteric communicative style. Pico wrote most of his Oration in this style.

The chapter also locates an apparent contradiction in Pico's self-presentation: in a famous 1485 letter to Ermolao Barbaro, Pico had defended philosophy against rhetoric, calling rhetorical trickery "pure lies, pure fakery, pure illusion" and claiming that philosophy "presents herself naked, visible from all sides and to every eye." Yet the Oration, written shortly after, demands secrecy and practices obscurantism. Copenhaver reads this reversal not as hypocrisy but as the result of Pico's immersion in Kabbalist methods in the intervening period.

**Section 2: Abulafia's Ladder (pp. 347–351)**

Copenhaver focuses on the doctrine of the "ladder" as the structural metaphor for mystical ascent in Abulafia's Kabbalah. Maimonides, though not a Kabbalist, prepared the ground by explaining biblical prophecy through the Aristotelian-Islamic theory of the Agent (Active) Intellect: all real understanding is transmitted through a single hyper-cosmic Intellect, and prophets are informed by it through their faculty of imagination (Moses excepted). This theory — which Maimonides treated as a secret to be shielded — was radicalized by Averroes, for whom there is literally only one intellect understanding universals, and mystical extinction of the individual self in that intellect is the soul's "ultimate happiness" and "final ascent." Averroes expressed this in his *Letter on the Possibility of Conjunction* (surviving only in Hebrew, accessible to Pico through his teacher Elia del Medigo, the Averroist Jew).

Abulafia's ecstatic mysticism combined Maimonidean and Averroist themes with his own contemplative practice of meditating on the letters and numbers of divine names. In his letter, Abulafia wrote: "All the prophets... and all the ancients of the gentile sages made inquiry, wishing to know the level and the height of the ladder of the soul, from the lowest to the very top." The ladder's summit is conjunction with S10, the Shekinah, the lowest Sefirah, also identified with the Agent Intellect. S10 "impregnates" the human soul with her androgynous power, enabling the mystic to contemplate divine names and conceive Understanding (S3). The ultimate state is the mystic *becoming* the Intellect. Pico's thesis on Plotinus — "human happiness is supreme when our particular intellect is fully and wholly conjoined with the First Intellect" — confirms that he absorbed this doctrine.

Copenhaver traces Abulafia's division of Kabbalah: Abulafia charged that "masters of the Kabbalah of Numerations" (Sefirot) had taken a wrong road, profaning sacred unity by treating divine attributes as distinct entities and "cutting off the plants" — the Sefirot as trees in Eden. Two theses (T1K4 and T1K5) reproduce this Zoharic reading: Adam's sin was to cut the Kingdom (S10) off from other plants (the higher Sefirot), and this primordial separation mirrors Israel's exile. T1K36 adds that "Sodom sinned by severing the Last Plant." Pico's thesis T2K1 directly paraphrases Abulafia's division of Kabbalah into knowledge of Sefirot (*Numerationes*) and knowledge of divine Names (*Shemot*), "similar to theoretical and practical knowledge." The previous 47 theses derived mainly from Recanati's Zoharic theosophy; the final 72 drew primarily on Abulafia's Names-based mysticism.

**Section 3: Counting on Flavius (pp. 351–358)**

Having established Abulafia's division of Kabbalah, Copenhaver turns to Pico's attempt to Christianize and reunify it. Pico introduced Kabbalah in the middle of the Oration and reserved its full account for the end. When he first mentioned the number 70 (the number of volumes of hidden wisdom compiled by sages for Esdras), he was gesturing toward the 72 theses that would confirm Christian articles of faith from Hebrew principles.

The chapter now demonstrates the pervasive presence of Kabbalistic numerology in the theses. T2K62 correlates the nine Beatitudes in the Gospel with the "novenary of nine Numerations." T2K68 applies the "method of number" to formal arithmetic. T2K65 uses number transposition to prove that the Shema ends with the name of Jesus. Thesis T1K19 demonstrates that letters in the name of the evil demon (Satan, שׂטן) and of God (the Triagrammaton, יׁשו) share the same numerical value when reduced by the "transposition" technique that assigns only unit values to Hebrew letters: ׂשטן = 3+9+7 = 19 (reduced); יׁשו = 19 (reduced). Moreover, T1K19 is itself thesis number 19 in its set — a fact no one in the audience could have known since the theses were unnumbered.

The number 900 for the total theses is similarly constructed. Pico wrote to Girolamo Benivieni that 900 was a "mystical number" — a "symbol of the soul rushing back into itself when agitated by the frenzy of the Muses." The numerological explanation: the Hebrew letter *tsade* in final form (ץ) has value 900; the "straight tsade" is the "true branch" of David's royal line; and 900 ties all 900 theses to the 72 concluding Christological Kabbalist conclusions, which use Kabbalah, rabbinical wisdom, and gentile wisdom to prove Jesus as crucified Messiah. T2Pla1, the thesis about the Timaeus's lambda series (1, 2, 4, 8 and 1, 3, 9, 27), happens to be thesis number 600 of the 900 — linking Plato's world-soul arithmetic to the Hebrew letter *mem* (both forms equal 40 and 600). T2K41 adds: "In Kabbalah one can know through the mystery of the closed mem why Christ sent a Paraclete after him."

T2K21 is one of Copenhaver's most detailed examples of Christological Kabbalah: "One who has joined a statement of the Kabbalists, saying that the Numeration called Just and the Redeemer is also called *zeh*, with a statement by Talmudists, saying that Isaac went like *zeh* carrying his cross, will see that what was prefigured in Isaac was fulfilled in Christ." The Kabbalist word *zeh* (זה, "this," masculine) is assigned to S9, the Foundation, the Just One. Rabbis saw the shape of the letter *zayin* (ז) as Isaac carrying wood. But Copenhaver reveals that Flavius salted the texts behind this thesis with propaganda from Ramón Martí's *Dagger of Faith* — a medieval anti-Jewish polemic — without Pico's knowledge. The prince was deceived by his own translator.

The portrait of Flavius Mithridates here is extensive and remarkable. Born in Sicily around 1450, converted around 1465, later a priest and teacher of Hebrew and Arabic, Flavius inserted approximately 600 personal glosses into his translations — a running commentary in "melodrama in zany Latin" — blasting papal masters, mocking Pico's wealth and good looks, demanding that Pico procure boys for him, and embedding sexual jokes alongside sacred theosophy. Copenhaver uses this material not for salacious color but to establish that the relationship between Pico and his most important Kabbalist informant was chaotic, adversarial, and shaped by systematic deception.

**Section 4: Oration I, 1–10 (pp. 358–365)**

Copenhaver provides his outline of the Oration, a seven-part structure of nested sevens with 72 smaller divisions, keyed to a translation in Appendix A. He cautions explicitly: "The following outline... may or may not show how he was thinking" — the structure is an interpretive imposition, though structural signals exist in the text.

Section I (paragraphs 1–10) concerns Adam's indeterminacy and capacity for self-fashioning. The famous opening word — the quotation from the Hermetic *Asclepius* that "man is a great miracle" — is immediately contextualized: in the original, man is a miracle because of his *middle status* in a cosmos connecting gods above with beasts below, not because of any indeterminate freedom. Pico omits the *Asclepius*'s view of "contented" humans and its phrase matching "the relentless asceticism in his speech." Copenhaver's point is sharp: the *Asclepius* does not support Pico's claim that man has "no fixed seat," even though Pico locates the unfixed human "at the center of the world."

God's address to Adam in the Oration is Pico's invention — words God never spoke in Genesis — and Copenhaver identifies its source as Kabbalistic precedent for the post-biblical divine speech off the record. Before Adam, in the Oration's account, God "had already applied laws of arcane Wisdom" — and this Wisdom is S2, the second Sefirah, a womanly divine figure whose "mystic laws shape the creative forces that flow from the hidden God to visible creatures." In the lower reaches of creation, her laws become laws of Nature that regulate natural magic. Copenhaver makes explicit what Quattrocento Christians could not have known: the opening of the Oration is already Kabbalistic, and no contemporary audience would have detected it.

The shape-shifting figures in section I — the chameleon, Proteus, and Metatron — are carefully distinguished. The chameleon's classical and biblical dossier is almost entirely negative: in Talmud it defiles by contact; in Pliny it is monstrous; in Plutarch it signifies flattery; in Aristotle it stands for vicious inconstancy. Proteus is similarly ambiguous — divine in the Orphic Hymns (where he "changes blessed matter into many forms") but linked to deception and mutability in Plato's *Ion* and Aristotle. The only *glorious* change in section I is Enoch becoming Metatron, the highest angel and Jewish equivalent of the Agent Intellect. Abulafia had written in a text translated for Pico that "what takes our intellect from potency to act is an Intellect... called by many different names... He is the Prince of the World and Mattatron... Our sages often call him Henoch." Pico's thesis T2K10 confirms this identification, equating Metatron with what "Orpheus names Pallas, Zoroaster the Father's Mind, Mercury God's Son, Pythagoras Wisdom, Parmenides the Intelligible Sphere." The Oration, however, introduces Metatron with "their more secret theology" while providing no Kabbalistic context whatsoever.

Copenhaver's most pointed revisionary claim appears here: Pico's program in the Oration, "often misunderstood as a proto-romantic individualism that aggrandizes the human self, actually liquidates it" (p. 361). The goal of mystical union as Pico describes it — following Dionysius the Areopagite's "darkness of unknowing," where "there is neither oneself nor anyone else" and "every human self has dissolved in the depths of the Godhead" — eliminates rather than celebrates humanity. "Deification so conceived... eliminates humanity — along with any merely human trait like dignity, though dignity was not the prince's concern" (p. 361). The human freedom given to Adam before the Fall collides with the Christian doctrine of sin's slavery — a tension the Oration "never resolves or even addresses" (p. 365).

**Section 5: Oration II, 11–15 (pp. 365–374)**

Section II proposes that humans must live like angels — specifically Cherubim. The Oration's phrase about the "rank (*dignitas*) and glory" of angels is about angels, not humans: humans are exhorted to emulate angelic *dignitas*, not to possess an inherent dignity of their own. Copenhaver cites a Kabbalist comment known to Pico that explicitly deflated human arrogance, noting that man "differs not at all from the beasts."

The three highest angelic orders — Seraphim (fire, love), Cherubim (light, contemplation), Thrones (mass, judgment) — are organized by Pico into a curriculum: from Thrones' judgment through Cherubic contemplation to Seraphic love and annihilating union with God. The Cherub is the "knot" tying these orders together, identified (through thesis T2K10) with Pallas Athena, herself identified with S2 (Wisdom), the same Sefirah whose "arcane laws" shaped creation at the Oration's opening. Copenhaver here demonstrates the structural closure of the chapter's argument: the womanly Wisdom of section I and the Pallas-Cherub of section II are the same entity, S2.

The angelology is tracked through Dionysius the Areopagite's *Celestial Hierarchy* (nine ranks organized in three triads), Kabbalist texts known to Pico (T1K2 lists nine hierarchies with Hebrew names), and Michael's role (T1K1: Michael sacrifices "souls of animals that reason" — i.e., humans — to God). T2K67 explains heavenly fire and water as the Love of S4 and Judgment of S5, mediated by S6 (the Heavens). T1K24 confirms Job's reference to peace "on high" as pointing to S4 (Southern Water) and S5 (Northern Fire). The morning, afternoon, and evening prayers of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob map onto S4, S5, and S6 respectively.

The chapter closes with Ficino's late recollection of Pico's charismatic arrival in Florence and its miraculous coincidence with the completion of Ficino's Plato translation. Copenhaver situates this as documenting a genuine intellectual relationship while noting its retrospective idealization. The broader point: Ficino "preferred explicating to obfuscating" while Pico "meant to mystify." Pico's esoteric philosophy was a "poetic theology" in the tradition of Porphyry's *Cave of the Nymphs* and Proclus's commentaries on Plato — a tradition encoded in enigmatic verse and accessible only to prepared initiates. Until Wirszubski's book was published and Bausi used his findings to edit the speech, for nearly five centuries "the prince had his way."

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: The Oration is deliberately opaque; its method is secrecy and its content is hidden; "any translation, including Appendix A of this book, will be opaque because the original is opaque" (p. 339).

**Claim 2**: Kabbalah's esoteric communicative conventions — divine command to hide mysteries from the populace — are the Oration's structural model, borrowed from Maimonides and Pico's Kabbalist reading (p. 339).

**Claim 3**: The *900 Conclusions* were printed only once with Pico's consent (Rome, Eucharius Silber, December 7, 1486), unpaginated, without a title page; the Oration cannot be understood without the theses (pp. 339–341).

**Claim 4**: The number 72 for the culminating Kabbalist theses is derived from the 72 names of angels encoded in Exodus 14; the 1532 edition's heading says "71" but 72 was Pico's intention (pp. 340–341).

**Claim 5**: Pico's primary Kabbalistic source for his own 72 concluding theses was Abraham Abulafia, translated by Flavius Mithridates; Abulafia is unnamed in both the Oration and the Conclusions despite controlling their structure (pp. 341–342, 422).

**Claim 6**: Pico's 47 first-group Kabbalist theses derived primarily from Menahem Recanati's Zoharic commentary; his own 72 derived from Abulafia's Names-based mysticism (pp. 341, 350–351).

**Claim 7**: The Oration imitates the bewildering, allusion-packed, shorthand communicative style of Talmudic and Kabbalist writing as modeled by Abulafia's *Ve-zot li-Yehuda* (pp. 343–348).

**Claim 8**: Pico's 1485 letter to Barbaro, which championed naked philosophical transparency against rhetorical obfuscation, directly contradicts the Oration's method of secrecy; this reversal reflects his immersion in Kabbalah in the intervening period (p. 346).

**Claim 9**: Theology and devotional practice (including mystical ascent) are the main topics of Pico's Kabbalah; the 72 theses make Kabbalah into Christology and Trinitarian theology (pp. 346–347).

**Claim 10**: Abulafia divided Kabbalah into two parts — knowledge of the Sefirot (*Numerationes*) and knowledge of the divine Names (*Shemot*); Pico reproduced this division in T2K1 and it governs the architecture of the 900 Conclusions (pp. 349–351).

**Claim 11**: Abulafia charged that theosophists "cut off the plants" — violated divine unity by treating Sefirot as distinct — and this charge is encoded in Pico's theses T1K4, T1K5, and T1K36 (pp. 349–350).

**Claim 12**: The number 900 for the total theses is a Kabbalistic construction, derived from the value of the final *tsade* (ץ = 900), the "true branch" of David's royal line, tying the theses to the Messianic Christological argument of the concluding 72 (pp. 353–354, 357–358).

**Claim 13**: Thesis T1K19 uses Hebrew letter transposition (*gematria*) to show that Satan and the Triagrammaton (name of Jesus, יׁשו) share the numerical value 19; T1K19 is itself number 19 in its set — a coded joke invisible to any audience (pp. 351–352).

**Claim 14**: T2Pla1 (Plato's lambda series from the *Timaeus*) is thesis 600 of 900, linking the world-soul arithmetic to the Hebrew letter *mem* (= 40 and 600); T2K41 links the closed *mem* to the mystery of the Paraclete (pp. 352–353).

**Claim 15**: Flavius Mithridates doctored several texts he translated for Pico, including salting the texts behind T2K21 (Isaac prefiguring Christ) with propaganda from Ramón Martí's anti-Jewish *Dagger of Faith*; Pico did not know he had been tricked (pp. 355–356).

**Claim 16**: Flavius inserted approximately 600 personal glosses into his translations, including sexual demands, mockery of Pico, and sordid jokes alongside sacred theosophy; his relationship with Pico was systematically deceptive (pp. 355–357).

**Claim 17**: Copenhaver's outline divides the Oration into seven parts with 72 smaller sections; he explicitly cautions that this is an "imposition" and that Pico never laid out a blueprint (pp. 357–358).

**Claim 18**: God's address to Adam in the Oration is Pico's invention, modeled on post-biblical Kabbalist texts where God speaks off the record; it is not in Genesis (pp. 359–360).

**Claim 19**: The "arcane Wisdom" that God applied before creating Adam is S2, the second Sefirah; no Quattrocento Christian would have detected this Kabbalist presence at the Oration's opening (pp. 359–360).

**Claim 20**: The chameleon, Proteus, and similar figures of shapeshifting are presented negatively in section I; the only glorious transformation is Enoch becoming Metatron (the Angel of the Agent Intellect) (pp. 362–365).

**Claim 21**: Pico's program in the Oration is "often misunderstood as a proto-romantic individualism that aggrandizes the human self" but "actually liquidates it"; the goal of mystical union, following the Areopagite, eliminates individual humanity and any merely human dignity (p. 361).

**Claim 22**: "Deification so conceived, the goal promoted by Pico's speech, eliminates humanity — along with any merely human trait like dignity, though dignity was not the prince's concern" (p. 361).

**Claim 23**: The Oration introduces a theological problem it never resolves: God promised freedom to Adam before the Fall, but sin makes humans slaves; this gap "erodes or perhaps destroys the bearing of God's speech on the unglorified human condition" (p. 365).

**Claim 24**: The angelic *dignitas* praised in section II is the dignity of angels that humans are called to emulate, not an inherent human dignity; the Kabbalist comment cited by Pico confirms that humans "differ not at all from the beasts" (pp. 365–368).

**Claim 25**: Pallas Athena, identified through T2K10 with Metatron, is also S2 (Wisdom), tying the chapter's opening Kabbalist figure to its closing Cherubic-life argument in a structural loop (pp. 372–373).

**Claim 26**: For nearly five centuries, until Wirszubski's scholarship and Bausi's edition, Pico successfully concealed his Kabbalist method from readers (p. 357).

## Technical Vocabulary

**Gematria** (גמטריה): Kabbalistic hermeneutic assigning numerical values to Hebrew letters; used by Pico to relate names of God, Satan, Jesus, and the Sefirot. Each Hebrew letter is both a character and a numeral (alef = 1, bet = 2, etc.); words therefore have total numerical values that encode hidden meanings. Pico's "transposition" technique assigns only unit values (excluding tens and hundreds) to derive equivalent reduced values.

**Sefirot** (singular Sefirah, שְׂפִירָה): The ten divine emanations or attributes through which the hidden God reveals himself in Kabbalistic theology. Copenhaver describes them as "at the core of Kabbalist storytelling." The tree arrangement runs from S1 (Crown/No-End, *Keter*) through S2 (Wisdom, *Chokhmah*) and S3 (Understanding, *Binah*) down to S10 (Kingdom/Shekinah, *Malkhut*). Pico's theses encode relationships among Sefirot throughout.

**Shemot** (שְׁמוֹת, "Names"): Abulafia's preferred path in Kabbalah, focused on meditating on letters and numbers of divine names to achieve mystical union; contrasted with the Sefirot-based path.

**Numerationes** (Latin for Sefirot): Flavius's Latin rendering of *Sefirot*; used in Copenhaver's quotations from the Abulafia translation.

**Tsade** (צ / ץ): Hebrew letter; *tsade* in final form (ץ) has numerical value 900; Copenhaver shows this is why Pico chose 900 as his total number of theses. The "straight tsade" (= final tsade, "true branch" of David) versus "bent tsade" (medial form) distinction is rabbinic and known to Zohar authors.

**Mem** (מ / ם): Hebrew letter; medial form (מ) = 40; final "closed mem" (ם) = 600. T2K41 links the closed mem to the mystery of the Paraclete. T2Pla1 (thesis 600) links the Timaeus lambda series to mem through Pythagorean arithmetic.

**Zeh** (זה) and **Zot** (זאת): Hebrew demonstrative pronouns ("this," masculine and feminine respectively); both carry enormous Kabbalist significance. *Zeh* (masculine) = S9, the Foundation, the Just One/Redeemer, used in T2K21 to identify Isaac and Christ. *Zot* (feminine) = divine feminine power in the Zohar reading of Leviticus.

**Zayin** (ז): Hebrew letter whose shape rabbinic interpreters saw as Isaac carrying wood for sacrifice — "like one who carries his cross on his shoulder." Used in T2K21 to link Isaac with Christ.

**Alef** (א), **Bet** (ב), **Gimel** (ג): First letters of Hebrew alphabet; each is also a numeral (1, 2, 3). The system enables gematria throughout.

**Tetragrammaton** (יהוה, YHWH): The unutterable four-letter name of God; by proxy pronounced *Adonai*. The Name of 72 is derived from three verses of Exodus 14 (each 72 letters) aligned and read vertically.

**Shekinah** (שְׁכִינָה, "She Who Dwells"): S10, the Kingdom, the lowest Sefirah and locus of divine presence in the world; also the Agent Intellect in Abulafia's reading; her androgynous power "impregnates" human souls in Abulafia's mysticism.

**Metatron** (מטטרון): The highest angel, identified in Kabbalah with Enoch (Hanok), stationed at the divine throne. Pico identifies Metatron with the Agent Intellect (thesis T2K10) and equates him with Orpheus's Pallas, Zoroaster's Father's Mind, Mercury's God's Son, Pythagoras's Wisdom, and Parmenides's Intelligible Sphere.

**Conjunction** (*adeptio*, *conjunctio*): The goal of Abulafia's and Averroes's mysticism: union of the human intellect with the Agent Intellect, resulting in the individual soul's extinction in the divine mind.

**Poetic theology** (*theologia poetica*): Pico's own phrase for his esoteric philosophical method, aligning himself with Porphyry's *Cave of the Nymphs*, Proclus's commentaries, and Ficino's expositions.

**Peshat, Midrash, Sheqel, Kabbalah**: The four Jewish hermeneutic methods parallel to Christian fourfold exegesis (literal, allegorical, tropological, anagogical). Kabbalah is the anagogical equivalent, described in the *Apology* as "loftier and more divine than the others, leading us up from earthly things to the heavens."

**Eyn-Sof** (אֵין סוֹף, "No-End"): The nameless, limitless hidden God of Kabbalist theology; the first manifestation is S1 (the Crown).

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Guide scholars**: This chapter is essential reading for anyone working on the Oration text itself, Pico's Kabbalah, the *900 Conclusions*, or the reception of Abulafia in the Latin West. It supersedes any reading of the Oration that treats it primarily as a humanist manifesto or an argument about human dignity. Scholars of Renaissance magic should read the chapter alongside the earlier historiographical Parts One and Two, since Copenhaver is consistently arguing against the received tradition while doing his own reading.

**Myth being tested**: The "dignity of man" reading of the Oration — the tradition (surveyed and demolished in Parts One and Two) that the Oration is a philosophical proclamation of human freedom, individuality, and dignity as inherent human endowments. Chapter 11 demonstrates at the textual level that: (a) the Oration's opening on human indeterminacy does not celebrate human nature but argues for its transformation and eventual self-liquidation in mystical union; (b) the word *dignitas* in section II refers to angelic dignity that humans are commanded to emulate, not their own inherent dignity; (c) the Kabbalist framework of the speech, invisible to every reader without Wirszubski and Bausi, makes the "dignity of man" reading not merely incomplete but positively misleading about what the speech argues.

**Historiographical register**: Copenhaver is working in the tradition of intellectual history with close textual analysis of primary sources in Latin, Hebrew, and Aramaic. He deploys Kabbalist textual scholarship (Wirszubski), editorial philology (Bausi), and intellectual biography (Flavius Mithridates as historical figure). The chapter is explicitly anti-presentist: modern readings have been imposed on an ancient text whose actual meaning has been concealed both by Pico's own design and by five centuries of ignorance about his Kabbalist sources.

**Concord outcome**: Pico's attempt at concord in the Oration is a Christianizing synthesis: he sought to reunify Abulafia's Names-based Kabbalah and Recanati's Sefirot-based Kabbalah under a Trinitarian and Christological framework. The concord project is not philosophical eclecticism for its own sake but a specific theological program aimed at proving that Jewish wisdom, properly decoded, confirms Christian doctrine — including original sin, atonement, and the Messianic identity of Jesus.

## Uncertainties

**Ethiopic script in the Oration manuscript**: Copenhaver notes (p. 365) that one partial and unique manuscript of the Oration includes Ethiopic script (probably supplied by Flavius) at the point attributing words about human variability to "the Persian Evantes." Copenhaver defers fuller treatment to Chapter 13 (Figure 20). The identification of "Evantes" remains unresolved.

**"Abdala the Saracen"**: At the Oration's opening, Pico cites "Abdala, a Saracen" to confirm the Hermetic saying about man as a great wonder. Copenhaver notes this figure "has not been identified: the name is common for Arabs and Muslims" (p. 359). This is an open philological problem.

**Structural outline**: Copenhaver explicitly cautions that his seven-part, 72-section outline "may or may not show how he was thinking" and that "any pattern imposed on the speech will be just that — an imposition" (p. 357). The outline is an interpretive framework, not a document of Pico's intention.

**Recanati's Commentary translation**: Copenhaver notes that Flavius's Latin translation of Recanati's *Commentary on the Pentateuch* is now lost; Pico's access to Zoharic theosophy through this text must be inferred from the theses and the Oration itself (p. 342).

**Pico's knowledge of Aramaic**: Copenhaver suggests Pico "perhaps could not have handled" the Zohar's difficult Aramaic and probably lacked a direct translation of the Zohar (p. 342). This is an inference, not a certainty.

**Flavius's chronology**: When Flavius first met Pico is unknown; Copenhaver gives 1486 as certain and "perhaps earlier" as possible, with Ficino reporting a debate between Flavius and Elia del Medigo at Pico's Florence house in an unspecified period (p. 355).
