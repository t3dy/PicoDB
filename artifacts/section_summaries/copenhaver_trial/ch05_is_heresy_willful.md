## Section Function

Chapter 5 is the argumentative culmination of the book. Chapters 1–4 examined specific heretical claims on narrow metaphysical or theological grounds (the eucharist, the incarnation, divine embodiment, the soul's fate). Chapter 5 steps back to ask the overarching normative question that makes heresy a legal and moral category at all: must heresy be willful to be heresy? If Pico's condemned theses were not products of willful rejection of orthodox teaching — if, as he argued, no one chooses beliefs in the decisive voluntarist sense the court assumed — then his theological errors, however numerous, could not constitute heresy in the legally actionable sense. Q8 is thus simultaneously Pico's most philosophically ambitious defense and his most strategically significant one. Copenhaver uses Q8 to demonstrate that the *Apology* was no mere rhetorical exercise but an engagement with the most technically demanding questions in late medieval epistemology and moral psychology.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

The chapter opens by presenting the text of Pico's eighth Question (*Q8*) in both its *Conclusions* and *Apology* versions (pp. 200–201). The *Conclusions* thesis is prefaced by a remarkable disclaimer: Pico labeled it merely "acceptable (*probabiliter*)" rather than "demonstrated" or "certain," a framing he omitted from the *Apology* — a tactically consequential revision, since the *probabiliter* label reduced the thesis's legal exposure. The thesis itself, Q8T, states that no one's opinion is exactly (*praecise*) thus and so because he wants to have this opinion, and likewise no one believes something to be true exactly because he wants to believe it so. Its corollary, Q8C, asserts that it is not within any person's free power (*libera potestas*) to believe an article of faith to be true when he decides and to believe it not true when he decides.

Copenhaver then opens a long contextualizing arc (sections 5.1–5.2) through the history of doxastic epistemology — the philosophical study of the ethics of belief. Drawing on modern analytic philosophy (Schwitzgebel, Chignell, Bishop, Alston, Cohen), he establishes a framework for reading the medieval sources: beliefs are today treated as mental *states*, often implicit and involuntary. Pico and his contemporaries, by contrast, treated belief, faith, opinion, and knowledge as mental *acts* — acts of intellect and will with graded reliability. This act/state distinction is the pivot on which the entire chapter turns.

The primary medieval authorities are Aquinas, Augustine, Abelard, Hugh of Saint Victor, and Peter Lombard. Aquinas's account from *De veritate* 14.1 and the *Summa Theologiae* 2–2.1–7 is traced in detail (pp. 203–211). For Aquinas, the intellect has two operational levels: first-level grasp of simple concepts, and second-level judgment of composite propositions. Among second-level acts, understanding (*intellectus*) and knowing (*scientia*) operate with necessity, while believing (*credere*) is a volitionally guided act that is firm but not certain. Faith, as a kind of believing, requires an act of will that commands the intellect's assent to sacred truth held as a good — thus faith is, for Thomas, "subject to free will (*subjacet libero arbitrio*)." Augustine's canonical definition, *credere est cum assensione cogitare* ("to believe is to think while assenting"), reinforced the volitional dimension: assent was an act of will.

Pico challenged the dominant Thomist position directly. His adversary on the commission, Bishop García, repeatedly cited Thomas and concluded that Q8T and Q8C were "erroneous and suggestive of heresy (*erroneam et haeresim sapientem*)" (p. 212). García's argument was simple: since believing is meritorious, it must be an act of free will; Q8C denies the necessary freedom; therefore Q8C is heretical. Pico's defense strategy was to align himself not with Aquinas but with the late medieval nominalist tradition — Albert the Great, Durand de Saint-Pourçain, Henry of Hesse (Langenstein), Henry of Oyta, Pierre d'Ailly, and Robert Holcot — all of whom in various ways limited the will's power over doxastic acts. Pico recalled that "almost the whole University of Paris" held this view when he studied there (p. 212), and he cited d'Ailly's *Sentences* commentary for the crucial claim that the will cannot do its doxastic work "*praecise*" — exactly and only — by its own command (p. 213).

The adverb *praecise* is the chapter's technical fulcrum (pp. 213–217). Copenhaver demonstrates that the word is derived from *praecidere* ("to cut off in front"), meaning "exactly and only, to the exclusion of all other factors." Its appearance fourteen times in a few pages of Q8 is strategic: Pico's claim is not the radical involuntarist claim that the will plays *no* role in belief, but the weaker claim that the will alone — exactly, exclusively — cannot be the sole determinant of belief. This distinction allowed Pico to position himself between García's strong voluntarism (the will is the decisive cause of belief) and Holcot's radical involuntarism (the act of believing is not in a person's free power at all).

The inferential architecture of Q8 receives sustained analysis (pp. 213–220). Q8T and Q8C are related as main thesis and corollary: if Q8T is true, Q8C follows. Pico's defensive claim was that the *praecise* restriction in Q8T carries over to Q8C, and therefore Q8C does not deny *all* freedom in believing — only the kind where will is the sole, exact, and exclusive cause. García accepted this reading but dismissed it as "quibbling," arguing that "no sane person doubts that the will alone, once information has been grasped, is the exact (*praecisa*) cause of bringing an act of believing to bear" (p. 217). Pico then distinguished two senses of "being in free power": (a) will commands the act given only a grasp of terms, with no other condition needed — which Q8T denies; and (b) will can choose, given all other necessary conditions, whether to assent or not — which Q8C affirms. This two-path analysis allowed him to maintain both that faith is not produced by will alone and that the act of faith is genuinely free in the sense required for merit.

Section 5.3 (pp. 218–228) analyzes Pico's five formal arguments against strong doxastic voluntarism. The first is a topical argument *a maiori*: since opinion cannot be willed (Aristotle, *De anima* 427b14–28a24, via Averroes's *Long Commentary*), and opinion belongs more within the will's range than belief (because opinion wavers while belief is firm), belief also cannot be willed. The argument is logically tangled — Copenhaver notes that Pico inverts the "more/less" assignments mid-argument — but the direction of the case is clear. The second argument runs from appearances (*apparentiae*) to beliefs: since a person cannot will the same thing to appear both true and false, and since believing contradicts the appearance that the believed thing is false, believing cannot be placed on each contradictory side by will alone. The third argument is the most metaphysically technical: the intellect as pure potency is indifferent to all acts including belief; before it can proceed to a believing act it requires *specificatio* — the delimiting of its object to one type rather than another — a pre-volitional operation that the will cannot perform. The fourth argument is empirical: when asked why they believe in the Christian faith rather than Islam, believers cite reasons (prophetic foretelling, scriptural harmony, miracle, reason, the Church's stability), not acts of will. Duns Scotus had endorsed this same eight-reason list (p. 224). The fifth argument, borrowed from Albert the Great, is the "tyrannical will" argument: if belief were determined exactly by willing alone, with no reasons counting, then choosing to believe Christ over Muhammad would be no more rational than the reverse; it would be the act of a tyrant, and Christians could not claim their faith was more reasonable than Muslim disbelief (pp. 224–225).

Section 5.4 (pp. 225–228) examines Robert Holcot's position in detail. Holcot held the radical view that belief in articles of faith is *not* in a person's free power *at all* — without Pico's *praecise* qualification. Pico cited Holcot strategically: "I must say, I've never read that he was condemned on this issue." The implication is clear — if Holcot's extreme position was not condemned, Pico's moderate version can hardly be heretical. Copenhaver shows the dialectical use Pico made of Holcot's three senses of belief (concurring/testimonial/miraculous), his account of merit as obedience to divine law rather than as a function of freedom, and his claim that negligent non-belief is sin. But Pico ultimately distanced himself from Holcot's most radical positions — that miraculous compulsion to believe could occur and that negligent believing counted as sinful — calling them "not correct."

Copenhaver's overall conclusion (developed more fully in the book's Conclusions section, pp. 233–) is that Q8 is Pico's most principled and philosophically serious defense in the *Apology*, but also one mounted under tactical pressure rather than from deep theological commitment. The "novel ideas about faith and will in Q8 were Robert Holcot's, and Pico watered them down to accommodate tradition" (Conclusions, p. 242). Whether this amounts to heresy turns on whether the court's Thomist voluntarism was the only possible orthodox position — a claim that the broad nominalist tradition, from d'Ailly through Holcot, had consistently contested.

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: The *Conclusions* version of Q8T was prefaced with a disclaimer labeling it merely "acceptable (*probabiliter*)" rather than demonstrated, a framing Pico omitted in the *Apology* as a tactical revision (p. 200).

**Claim 2**: The word *praecise* ("exactly and only, cutting off all other factors") appears fourteen times in Q8 and is the syntactic and argumentative fulcrum of the entire defense (pp. 213, 226).

**Claim 3**: Pico and his medieval contemporaries treated faith, belief, opinion, and knowledge as mental *acts* of intellect and will, not mental *states* in the modern analytic sense (p. 203).

**Claim 4**: For Aquinas, belief (*credere*) is a second-level act of intellect commanded by the will; faith is therefore "subject to free will (*subjacet libero arbitrio*)" and meritorious (p. 209, citing *ST* 2–2.4.1).

**Claim 5**: Bishop García condemned Q8T and Q8C as "erroneous and suggestive of heresy" on Thomist grounds: believing is meritorious, hence free, hence willful (p. 212, citing *Garcia* 1489).

**Claim 6**: Six of the seven medieval theologians named in Q8 were critics of Thomism; only Albert the Great was not (p. 212).

**Claim 7**: Pico recalled that "almost the whole University of Paris" held the nominalist position against strong doxastic voluntarism when he studied there (p. 212).

**Claim 8**: Cardinal d'Ailly's *Sentences* commentary used the exact word *praecise* in the same doxastic context as Q8, providing Pico with direct precedent for his restricted formulation (pp. 213, 216).

**Claim 9**: Q8T and Q8C are inferentially linked as thesis and corollary; the *praecise* restriction in Q8T carries over to Q8C, so Q8C does not deny all freedom in believing, only willing as sole and exclusive cause (pp. 213–214, 217).

**Claim 10**: Pico distinguished two senses of "free power to believe": (a) will commanding assent by grasping terms alone — which he denied; and (b) will freely choosing to assent given all other conditions — which he affirmed (pp. 218–219, citing Q8.7–9).

**Claim 11**: The first argument against doxastic voluntarism is topical (*a maiori*): since opinion cannot be willed, and opinion falls within the will's range more than belief does, belief also cannot be willed (pp. 219–220, citing Q8.11 and Holcot 1518a).

**Claim 12**: The third argument introduces *specificatio* — pre-volitional delimiting of the intellect's object to a type — which the will cannot perform; this is Pico's most metaphysically demanding claim (pp. 222–223, citing Q8.14–16).

**Claim 13**: The fifth argument (the "tyrannical will" argument) draws on Albert the Great: if will were the exact and sole cause of belief, choosing Christianity over Islam would be no more rational than the reverse, making both equally tyrannical and irrational (pp. 224–225, citing Q8.18–19 and *Albertus, De anima* 3.4.5).

**Claim 14**: Pico deployed Holcot's radical involuntarism tactically: "I've never read that he was condemned on this issue" — implying Pico's more moderate position was a fortiori safe (p. 226, citing Q8.21).

**Claim 15**: Holcot's account of merit relocates meritoriousness from freedom to conformity with divine law, allowing involuntary acts (including a baby's death, a stumbling foot) to merit eternal life (pp. 226–227, citing Q8.22–23).

**Claim 16**: Pico rejected Holcot's most radical conclusion — that someone facing overwhelming miraculous evidence would be obligated to believe — calling it "not correct" (p. 228, citing Q8.25).

**Claim 17**: Copenhaver's own verdict in the Conclusions: the novel ideas about faith and will in Q8 were Holcot's; Pico adapted them tactically and watered them down to accommodate tradition (p. 242).

## Technical Vocabulary

**actus credendi** / **actus intellectus** / **actus voluntatis**: act of believing / act of intellect / act of will — the three mental operations whose relationship Q8 is about (p. 202)

**praecise**: "exactly and only, to the exclusion of all other factors" — from *praecidere* ("cut off in front"); the key restriction in Q8T appearing 14 times in a few pages (p. 214)

**libera potestas** / **liberum arbitrium**: free power / free choice — the standard vocabulary for voluntary self-determination; used in Q8C (p. 200)

**formido ad oppositum**: fear of the opposite — the characteristic mark of *opinio*, which wavers between p and not-p; contrasts with the firmness of *fides* and *scientia* (p. 202)

**specificatio** (verb: *specificare*): "species-making" — the pre-volitional act of delimiting the intellect's object to a type, which the will cannot perform and which must occur before believing is possible (pp. 222–223)

**assensio** / **assensus**: assent — the intellect's acceptance of a proposition as true; required for both belief and faith; classically defined by Augustine as the volitional element in *credere* (pp. 203–204)

**cogitare**: to think deliberatively — Augustine's term in *credere est cum assensione cogitare*; distinguishes belief from direct intuition (pp. 203–204)

**existimatio** / **existimare**: to reckon, estimate, have an opinion — the Aristotelian-Averroist term for *doxa*; Abelard used it to define faith, provoking accusations that he reduced faith to uncertain conjecture (pp. 206–208)

**probabiliter** / **probable** (*probabile*): acceptable, defensible — Pico's disclaimer in the *Conclusions* preamble, meaning the thesis is philosophically defensible but not demonstrated; contrasts with *firmiter* (firmly) and *certo* (certainly) (pp. 200–201)

**a maiori**: topical argument "from the greater" — if what belongs more is absent, what belongs less is also absent; Pico's first formal argument in Q8 (pp. 219–220)

**imperare** / **imperium**: to command / command — the will's directive act over the intellect; Holcot and d'Ailly both restrict the will's *imperium* in doxastic contexts (pp. 210, 215)

**habitus mentis**: habit of mind — Aquinas's location of faith as a stable disposition, present in both intellect and will, that disposes the soul toward believing (p. 208)

**determinativus**: delimiting — describes the will's eventual role in the cognitive process; it delimits but only as one delimiter among others, not as the sole and exclusive cause (p. 223)

**doxastic ethics** / **doxastic voluntarism**: modern terms for the philosophical study of right/wrong belief, and for the doctrine that beliefs are under voluntary control; Copenhaver uses these anachronistically but accurately to categorize Pico's and Holcot's positions (pp. 183, 203)

**fides formata** / **fides informis**: formed faith / formless faith — Aquinas's distinction: faith animated by love (*caritas*) vs. the faith demons possess intellectually without loving God (p. 209)

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Guiding myth under test**: The progressive-humanist Pico — the icon of Renaissance freedom who celebrated human dignity and autonomy in the *Oration* — might be expected to defend something like radical intellectual freedom in Q8. Copenhaver's chapter tests this assumption and finds it inadequate. Pico's Q8 is not a humanist defense of rational autonomy; it is a technically precise piece of late medieval doxastic epistemology, conducted entirely within the scholastic framework, aimed at reducing the court's ability to call his errors willful.

**Historiographical register**: Anti-Burckhardtian. Copenhaver consistently frames Pico's *Apology* as a product of medieval scholastic culture, not Renaissance humanism. The Conclusion (p. 242) explicitly states: "humanism was not Pico's point of view or persona in the Conclusions and Apology." His non-sectarian use of nominalists, Thomists, and Scotists alike exemplifies his *concordia* ideology but shows no progressive trajectory.

**Concord outcome**: Pico's *concordia* ideology appears here not as speculative syncretism but as a courtroom tactic: by recruiting authorities from all scholastic traditions against his prosecution, he demonstrated that the orthodox consensus was not Thomist but genuinely plural. The chapter's key historiographical intervention is that Pico's *concordia* project began as philosophical utopianism in the *Conclusions* and was retroactively pressed into service as legal defense in the *Apology*.

**Scholarly debate flagged**: Whether Pico's Q8 defense was philosophically coherent or merely expedient. Copenhaver leans toward "expedient but not dishonest": Pico's position is real but opportunistically positioned, selected for tactical strength rather than deep personal commitment. The "novel ideas about faith and will in Q8 were Robert Holcot's, and Pico watered them down."

## Relation to Pico Works

**Apology, Q8** (*De libertate credendi*): The primary text. Q8 is Pico's most technically elaborate Question in the *Apology*, spanning ~27 pages. Its defense strategy is layered: cite Aristotle and Averroes for the opinion half of Q8T; cite Augustine and the nominalists for the belief half; cite d'Ailly's *praecise* qualification to narrow the claim; cite Holcot's more radical involuntarism to make Pico's position look moderate; distinguish two senses of "free power" to rescue Q8C; then supply five formal arguments. The text of Q8 is reproduced in section 5.5 (pp. 228–232).

**900 Conclusions**: The *Conclusions* version of Q8T includes a *probabiliter* preface omitted from the *Apology*, and uses the word *falsum* (false) where the *Apology* uses "not true" — small differences Copenhaver reads as potentially significant (p. 200). The *Conclusions*' quodlibetal genre required no systematic compatibility between theses; Q8T was one of 900 exercises in display, not a confession of faith.

**Oration on the Dignity of Man**: The *Oration*'s famous theme of human dignity and freedom of self-determination is conspicuously absent from Q8's argument. Q8 limits rather than celebrates human volition. This creates a tension that Copenhaver does not directly address in this chapter but that the Conclusions section implies: the *Oration*'s humanist image of Pico and the *Apology*'s scholastic Pico are not the same person. The *Oration* was never published by Pico and was not part of the legal record.

**Heptaplus**: Not directly relevant to Q8. The *Heptaplus*'s account of intellectual ascent involves acts of the intellect but not in the voluntarist/involuntarist terms of Q8.

**De ente et uno**: Pico's later treatise on being and unity (left unfinished, contested with Antonio Cittadini) reflects a continuing scholastic engagement; Copenhaver mentions it in the Conclusions section as evidence that Pico never abandoned disputational habits.

## Updates Existing Writing

**`free_will.md`**: The existing essay almost certainly frames Pico's conception of free will primarily through the *Oration*'s image of the self-fashioning human. Q8 requires a second pole: Pico's *Apology* restricts voluntary control over belief by denying that the will is the *praecise* and sole cause of doxastic acts. The essay should acknowledge this tension — or explicitly explain that the *Oration* and *Apology* address different questions (self-fashioning of one's nature vs. voluntary control of belief).

**`aquinas.md`** (already written): Copenhaver establishes that Pico never cited Thomas in Q8, though he cited him respectfully throughout the rest of the *Apology*. The Thomist position on doxastic voluntarism is *the enemy position* in Q8, held by Bishop García. The Aquinas essay should note this Q8 reversal.

**`condemned_theses.md`**: Should include a note on the *probabiliter* preface in the *Conclusions* version of Q8T — this is the primary example of Pico's disclaimer strategy in the *Conclusions* being suppressed in the *Apology*.

**Scholar profile for Copenhaver**: The chapter confirms that Copenhaver's method is to deploy modern analytic philosophy (propositional attitudes, doxastic ethics, act/state distinction) as a framework for illuminating medieval epistemological debates — not as an anachronistic imposition but as a precise analytical toolkit that clarifies what was at stake.

## Uncertainties

1. **Bishop García's identity**: Copenhaver refers to "Bishop García" throughout but does not give a full first name or biographical entry in this chapter. The figure appears as the prosecution's lead theological assessor. Cross-check against the book's index or other chapters for full identification.

2. **OCR / textual issues in the source Markdown**: The OCR has some artifacts in the chapter. Specifically, the diagrams of the Thomist epistemological hierarchy (intellect levels labeled with placeholder symbols like "" and "S" and "") were rendered incompletely — the original likely used schematic labels (e.g., I = *intellectus*, S = *scientia*, B = *credere*, O = *opinio*) that did not survive OCR. The argument about the reliability scale (p. 204, p. 210) should be verified against a physical copy.

3. **Jean Cabrol**: Copenhaver cites Cabrol (1900–07) as a five-volume edition of an early-fifteenth-century Thomist polemic against nominalism. The precise title and provenance of this edition should be verified; it is not a frequently cited work in standard Renaissance scholarship.

4. **Michael Scot as translator**: Copenhaver notes the first Latin *De anima* (from an Arabic text) "may have been translated by Michael Scot around 1236" (p. 206). The "may have" signals scholarly uncertainty about this attribution.

5. **Pico's direct quotation accuracy**: Copenhaver warns repeatedly that Pico's citations of scholastic sources in the *Apology* were sometimes corrupt or inaccurate (see Conclusions, p. 240). Claims attributed to Holcot, d'Ailly, or Albert in Q8 should be verified against the original texts before being treated as fully reliable characterizations of those thinkers' positions.
