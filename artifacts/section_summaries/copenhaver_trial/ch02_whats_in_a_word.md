## Section Function

Chapter 2 performs the essential conceptual groundwork for Copenhaver's central argument about Pico's heresy trial. Its thesis: that the Latin family of "suppo- words" (suppositum, supponere, suppositio and derivatives) operated simultaneously in two distinct philosophical domains—metaphysics and semantics—and that Pico, like all his medieval predecessors, deployed this terminology across both domains without ever flagging the crossing. This unexplained ambiguity, Copenhaver argues, was not mere carelessness but a structural feature of the scholastic vocabulary that Pico inherited, and it was directly consequential for how his condemned theses Q4 (on the incarnation) and Q6 (on the eucharist) could be read. Before the reader can understand why Pico was condemned, or why his defenses in the Apology succeeded or failed, she must understand what these suppositional words meant and how dangerously polysemous they were. The chapter thus functions as a conceptual glossary, a history of scholastic semantics, and a cultural-contextual frame that explains both why the humanists found Pico's language risible and why his ecclesiastical judges may have misread his technical arguments.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

The chapter opens with a set-piece on humanist mockery. Pico's Apology contained more than fifty thousand words, densely packed with medieval neologisms—nouns like *assumptibilitas*, *impassibilitas*, *substantialitas*; verbs like *dogmatizare* and *substentificare*; neologistic adjectives and adverbs—including the key word *suppositabilis*. Copenhaver uses this as a springboard to a tour of Renaissance comic literature: Rabelais's *Gargantua* and *Pantagruel*, where "Johnny Smutskin" harangues a giant with absurdist scholastic logic ("*Omnis clocha clochabilis, in clocherio clochando . . .*"), and the fake Library of Saint Victor catalogue ridiculing contemporary Parisian scholastics (Béda, Tartaret, Major). The comedy of Rabelais, Copenhaver shows, was not idle entertainment: it expressed a programmatic humanist assault on medieval philosophical Latin, an assault organized around exactly the terminology—suppositiones, restrictiones, ampliationes—that Pico trusted and defended. The genealogy of this mockery runs through Petrarch, Coluccio Salutati (whose 1398 letter to a Bologna professor attacked "equivocating with significations and suppositions"), Thomas More (*parva logicalia* jokes), Juan Luis Vives (*Against Fake Logicians*, 1520), and finally Rabelais. Thomas Hobbes brought the assault to philosophical seriousness in 1651, attacking the "-nesses, -tudes and -ties" of scholastic language as meaningless noise, targeting Francisco Suárez's Catholic theology of transubstantiation.

Against this cultural backdrop, Copenhaver pivots to his analytical project. The suppo- words were not gibberish. They were a millennial vocabulary tracking a crucial philosophical problem: the borderland between metaphysics and semantics. A suppositum could be a metaphysical entity—a primary substance, a self-standing individual, the divine hypostasis—or it could be a semantic entity, the referent that a term "stands for" in a propositional context. These are not the same thing, and the difference matters enormously for theological claims, yet medieval writers traversed the border constantly without marking it.

Copenhaver introduces a formal apparatus of some twenty relational types (Table 2.1), labeled with Greek letters for semantic relations and Roman letters for metaphysical ones, to map all the uses these words were put to across the tradition. The principal metaphysical relations are: A (taking-on, as in the incarnation), B (being-in or inhering), F (founding, as substance founds accidents), G (grounding, as God grounds creatures), I (identity), and T (taxonomy, genus/species hierarchy). The principal semantic relations are: Γ (signifying), Δ (propositional), Θ (referring), Ξ (grammatical), Σ (anaphora), Φ (predicating), Ψ (standing-for), and Ω (suppositing in the technical terminist sense). The general relation R subsumes all others.

The chapter then traces the history of this vocabulary from classical Latin (where *supponere* meant chiefly physical "putting under" or deceptive "substituting"—as in Roman comedy, swapping one baby for another) through Boethius and Priscian (sixth century), Peter Helias (twelfth century), Abelard and Gilbert of Poitiers (twelfth century), up through Peter of Spain's *Summaries of Logic* (c. 1250), William of Ockham's *Summa of Logic* (early fourteenth century), Thomas Aquinas, John Duns Scotus, and the Scotist commentator Francisco Liceto (sixteenth century).

Boethius was the founding architect: translating Greek philosophy into Latin for the medieval West, he used suppo- words in nine different relational senses—metaphysical, semantic, and grammatical—without distinguishing them. His theological work *Against Eutyches* established that Greek ὑποστάσεις ("hypostases") could be rendered as *substantiae suppositae*—things "put under" accidents—a metaphysical F relation. But he also noted that God was not "put under" in this way; God was in a G relation of grounding to creation, not an F relation of founding. Priscian, Boethius's contemporary, introduced suppo- words into Latin grammar, using them in founding (F), referring (Θ), taxonomical (T), replacing (Q), and grammatical (Ξ) relations, often mixing metaphysics with semantics without discrimination. Peter Helias (d. 1166), commenting on Priscian, added further precision about anaphora (Σ relation) and the grammatical roles of pronouns, but likewise straddled the boundary.

Abelard and Gilbert of Poitiers, also twelfth-century, applied the vocabulary to pressing theological questions—the Trinity and the incarnation—and added semantic complexity (signifying Γ, anaphoric Σ relations) to the already rich metaphysical fabric. Abelard warned students that applying Aristotle's categories directly to God was dangerous; Gilbert carefully distinguished the F relation (substance founding accidents) from the G relation (Creator grounding creatures), showing why God could not be a *suppositum* in the ordinary founding sense.

The theoretical peak came with Peter of Spain's *Summaries of Logic* (c. 1250) and then Ockham's refinement. Peter introduced supposition theory to students as the semantics that explained truth conditions in propositions: signification (signifficatio) was a word's lexical meaning, while supposition (suppositio) was the relation a term bore to what it stood for within a propositional context. Supposition thus tracked truth—allowing logicians to handle tense, scope, and context that signifficatio alone could not handle. Types of supposition included personal (standing for ordinary objects), material (standing for the word itself, as in "Man is a noun"), simple, discrete, and various subtypes. Ockham refined and systematized this theory.

Aquinas occupied a strategic position in Copenhaver's account: he was aware of the semantic suppositum and actually invoked supposition theory explicitly in one passage of the *Scriptum*, distinguishing a "metaphysical supposit" from a "linguistic supposit (*suppositum locutionis*)." But this explicit acknowledgment of the distinction was rare; Thomas more commonly used the polysemous vocabulary without flagging the crossings. In the great *Summa Theologiae*, discussing the proposition "God is a human" (*Deus est homo*), he marshaled semantic suppo- words (the verb *supponere* for "stand for") alongside metaphysical suppo- words (the noun *suppositum* for the divine hypostasis) in the same argument, with the same seamlessness that characterized the entire tradition.

Scotus, for his part, defined the metaphysical suppositum with great precision: a self-standing, incommunicable individual that was the ultimate subject of properties and the sole agent of action. Divine supposita were uncreated, plural (in the Trinity), individually non-repeating; created supposita were the ordinary case. Scotus examined the proposition "God is a human" by unpacking it as "the Word is the supposit which is a human" (*Verbum est suppositum, quod est homo*)—a semantic analysis that nevertheless used suppo- words without semantic burden. Scotus's sixteenth-century commentator Francisco Liceto made the crossing explicit: the predicate *homo* in the proposition "supposits for the supposit (*supponit pro supposito*)"—verb semantic, noun metaphysical.

The chapter concludes by returning to Pico. In the 18,000-word *Conclusions*, Pico used suppo- words only five times; after condemnation, the 56,000-word *Apology* used them nearly 150 times. The surge reveals the strategic indispensability of this medieval terminology for his legal self-defense. Two of the five Conclusions uses were in the condemned theses Q4 and Q6. Q4 restricted the usual theological claim that God could make any nature whatever a supposit, granting this only for rational natures. Q6 hypothesized that, on the usual account of "the possibility of suppositing with regard to any creature," Christ's body could be on the altar without conversion of the bread's substance—a challenge to standard transubstantiation doctrine. A fifth thesis dissented from the standard textbook claim that treatises on supposition belonged to logic, suggesting instead that metaphysics was their original domain. Copenhaver reads this as Pico's gesture toward the very ambiguity this chapter has mapped: the prince wanted the metaphysical suppositum to be primary. But neither the *Conclusions* nor the *Apology* resolved this question.

The chapter ends with an intellectual biography of Pico's relationship to humanist culture. Despite writing brilliantly elegant Latin in his letters (the 1485 letter to Ermolao Barbaro in particular), Pico defended scholastic philosophical speech as philosophically necessary: philosophy, unlike rhetoric, sought truth, and for that purpose medieval terminology was more adequate than classical style. In the letter, Pico's resurrected scholastic voices the argument that philosophers were entitled to their own conventions. Scotus with "crude, tasteless, non-Latin words" who gets nature and God right beats Lucretius writing beautiful falsehoods. In practice, Pico was "more like Scotus than Barbaro"—but the humanist world that would mock him through Rabelais was already gathering.

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: Pico's *Apology* contained more than fifty thousand words, many of them medieval neologisms including *suppositabilis*, *assumptibilitas*, *impassibilitas*, *substantialitas*, and the definite article *ly* (appearing thirteen times)—linguistic features that humanists found risible. (p. 45)

**Claim 2**: Rabelais's comedy about scholastic language was a culturally organized attack on the same vocabulary Pico used; the genealogy of this attack runs from Petrarch through Salutati (1398), More (1515), Vives (1520), Rabelais (1530s), and Hobbes (1651). (pp. 46–52)

**Claim 3**: Coluccio Salutati's 1398 letter attacked scholastic logic for "equivocating with significations at one moment and suppositions at another"—showing that the polysemy of suppo- words was noticed even by hostile outsiders. (p. 50)

**Claim 4**: Thomas Hobbes attacked scholastic "-nesses, -tudes and -ties" as meaningless sounds, targeting the doctrine of transubstantiation specifically, and identifying Francisco Suárez as the leading Catholic target. (p. 52)

**Claim 5**: The suppo- vocabulary tracked the border between metaphysics and semantics, mapping relations of "putting under" that could be (a) a physical fact, (b) a metaphysical relation (substance founding accidents, Creator grounding creatures), or (c) a semantic relation (a term standing for an entity in a propositional context). (pp. 53–54)

**Claim 6**: The medieval concept of *suppositio* differed from *significatio* in that significatio was a property of words in isolation (lexical meaning), while suppositio was a property of terms in propositional context, and suppositio therefore did the truth-conditional work that signification alone could not do. (pp. 56–59)

**Claim 7**: Peter of Spain's *Summaries of Logic* (c. 1250) gave supposition theory its first effective textbook vehicle, classifying types of suppositing (personal, material, simple, discrete, etc.), but his account was "the least convincing part of his book." (pp. 57–59)

**Claim 8**: Ockham's *Summa of Logic* refined supposition theory by defining material supposition (a term standing for itself rather than its significate) with precision, enabling distinctions between use and mention crucial for theological propositions about God. (p. 59)

**Claim 9**: In the *Apology*, Pico used suppo- words in nearly 150 instances, mainly to defend himself on points of metaphysics, but also in semantic and logical senses—and he never flagged the crucial distinction between these applications, as his medieval predecessors had not flagged it for nearly a millennium. (p. 62)

**Claim 10**: Boethius established the foundational Latin vocabulary for suppo- words in nine distinct relational senses (A, Δ, Θ, F, G, P, Q, T, Φ), transmitting Greek philosophical vocabulary to the medieval West through translations and commentaries on Aristotle and Porphyry. (p. 72)

**Claim 11**: Priscian's grammar introduced suppo- words into Latin grammatical analysis in founding (F), referring (Θ), taxonomical (T), replacing (Q), and grammatical (Ξ) relations, blending metaphysics with semantics in ways that shaped the entire medieval tradition. (pp. 73–78)

**Claim 12**: Peter Helias (d. 1166) extended Priscian's suppo- vocabulary in his Summa on Priscian to distinguish two types of substance—one that substands (supports properties) and one that subsists (exists independently)—using suppositum for the substanding type, in a move between F (founding) and Θ (referring) relations. (pp. 74–78)

**Claim 13**: Abelard's *Logic* and *Christian Theology* further developed the polysemy of suppo- words, distinguishing F (founding), G (grounding), Π (listing), P (putting), Σ (anaphoric), T (taxonomical), and Φ (predicating) relations, and explicitly noting that applying Aristotelian categories to God was philosophically dangerous. (pp. 79–81)

**Claim 14**: Gilbert of Poitiers, in his commentary on Boethius, used suppo- words primarily in metaphysical (F, G, T) relations but also in semantic and propositional (Δ, P) contexts, and explicitly described predication as a human speech-act of "putting Christ under the term human." (pp. 82–85)

**Claim 15**: Ockham's *Summa of Logic* placed the semantic verb *supponit* immediately adjacent to the metaphysical noun *suppositum* without signaling the distinction—exemplifying the widespread practice that Copenhaver identifies as the structural feature of the tradition. (p. 69)

**Claim 16**: Thomas Aquinas was unusual in explicitly noting the metaphysical/semantic distinction, distinguishing a "linguistic supposit (*suppositum locutionis*)" from a metaphysical hypostasis in the *Scriptum*, but he was "a long way from Ockham's mature supposition theory." (pp. 86–88)

**Claim 17**: In the *Summa Theologiae* (3.16.1), Aquinas analyzed "God is a human" using four metaphysical and six semantic suppo- words in the same passage, "usually deploying these polysemous words without noting the crucial differences in play." (pp. 91–92)

**Claim 18**: Scotus defined supposita as self-standing, incommunicable individuals, ultimate subjects of properties, agents of action—and applied the concept to God, where divine supposita in the Trinity were uncreated, plural, and individually non-repeating; but he was "less explicit about the semantic side" than Aquinas or Ockham. (pp. 88–91)

**Claim 19**: Francisco Liceto's sixteenth-century commentary on Scotus *Ord.* 3.7.1 was the clearest instance in the tradition of someone making the metaphysical/semantic distinction explicit, noting that the predicate of "the Word is a human" "supposits for the supposit"—verb semantic, noun metaphysical. (p. 90)

**Claim 20**: Pico used suppo- words only five times in the *Conclusions* (18,000 words) but nearly 150 times in the *Apology* (56,000 words)—the increase showing that this vocabulary became "indispensable" only when he needed it to defend condemned theses. (pp. 95–96)

**Claim 21**: Conclusion Q4 restricted the standard scholastic claim that God could supposit any nature, limiting it to rational natures only; this was metaphysical use of the suppo- vocabulary. (p. 95)

**Claim 22**: Conclusion Q6 applied the concept of "the possibility of suppositing with regard to any creature" to the eucharist, claiming that Christ's body could be on the altar without conversion of the bread's substance, treating transubstantiation as a matter of suppositional metaphysics rather than physical transformation. (pp. 95–96)

**Claim 23**: Conclusion 16 of Pico's "80 philosophical conclusions" dissented from the standard teaching that "treatises on suppositions belong to logic," implying that metaphysics—not logic—was the primary domain of the suppositional vocabulary. (p. 96)

**Claim 24**: In his 1485 letter to Ermolao Barbaro, Pico defended scholastic Latin against humanist criticism by arguing that philosophy needed its own conventions, that meanings were either natural (in which case philosophers as experts on nature set them) or conventional (in which case philosophers could set their own), and that clarity served truth better than elegance served persuasion. (pp. 97–98)

**Claim 25**: Pico argued in the Barbaro letter that scholastic philosophical style was like God's speech in scripture: "serious and simple, not flashy and ornate." He preferred "grandeur in rough speech rather than graceful delicacy." (p. 98)

**Claim 26**: Despite his mastery of classical Latin prose (demonstrated by the Barbaro letter itself), Pico "in practice was more like Scotus than Barbaro" in his philosophical writing, and explicitly refused to stand "where Rabelais would stand in the next century: with 'humanists.'" (p. 98)

**Claim 27**: The suppo- vocabulary was displaced from philosophical discourse not primarily by philosophical criticism but by (a) the rise of Ramist logic, which replaced terminist semantics; (b) the predominantly Protestant identity of post-Cartesian philosophers, for whom Trinitarian and Christological dogma was either alien or repugnant; and (c) the failure of scholastic theologians to clearly define the metaphysical/semantic distinction before their vocabulary collapsed under doctrinal overextension. (pp. 92–94)

## Technical Vocabulary

**suppositum** — Latin noun, "what is put under"; in metaphysics: a primary substance, self-standing individual, divine hypostasis; in semantics: the referent a term stands for in a propositional context. Polysemous across the entire tradition.

**supponere** — Latin verb, "to put under"; in metaphysics: to ground, found, or be the substance underlying accidents; in semantics: to stand for, to supposit for. Classical senses include physical substitution and deceptive replacement.

**suppositio** — Latin noun, "the act of putting under" or "supposition"; in logic/semantics: the technical property of terms whereby they stand for things in propositional context; distinct from *significatio* (lexical meaning).

**significatio** — "signification"; lexical meaning of a term independent of propositional context; a property of isolated words, not terms-in-propositions.

**persona / hypostasis** — "person" / Greek hypostasis = Latin *substantia supposita*; the individual self-standing entity in the Trinity or in Christ; both Greek and Latin terms converge on metaphysical suppositum.

**assumptio / assumere** — "taking on"; the theological relation by which God takes on human nature in the incarnation; often nearly synonymous with suppositio in Christological contexts.

**communicabilitas / incommunicabilitas** — "shareability / unshareability"; Scotus's key feature of divine supposita: formally unshareable, contrasting with shareable essence.

**haecceitas** — "thishereness"; Scotist term for individual differentiator; not suppositum but closely related.

**quidditas** — "whatness"; the essential nature of a thing, corresponding to secondary substance.

**res naturae** — "thing of a nature"; Thomistic technical term; Thomas classified it as a name of first imposition (naming things), while *suppositum* was a name of second imposition (naming belonging of particulars to natures).

**parva logicalia** — "little logicals"; the innovative section of Peter of Spain's *Summaries of Logic* dealing with supposition, ampliation, restriction, and appellation; the primary humanist target.

**personalis suppositio** — "personal supposition"; the standard type where a term stands for ordinary objects (despite the name requiring no persons).

**materialis suppositio** — "material supposition"; where a term stands for itself as a word (use/mention distinction).

**suppositabilis** — "able to be supposited"; Pico's neologism appearing in the *Apology*, epitomizing the technical medieval vocabulary that humanists mocked.

**substantia prima / secunda** — "primary substance / secondary substance"; Aristotelian distinction: primary = individual (Socrates), secondary = species or genus (human, animal).

**A relation / G relation / F relation** — Copenhaver's notation: A = "taking-on" (incarnation), G = "grounding" (Creator to creation), F = "founding" (substance to accidents); all expressed by suppo- words in different contexts.

**Ω relation** — Copenhaver's notation for the technical terminist semantic "suppositing" relation; the strict sense established by Ockham and later terminists.

**id quod est / id quo est** — "that which is" / "that by which it is"; Gilbert of Poitiers's key Boethian distinction between existing individual and formal principle of existence.

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Guide scholars**: Copenhaver is primarily a historian of philosophy and philology. His scholarly virtues here are analytical precision (the relational schema in Tables 2.1–2.5), comprehensive primary-source coverage (from Cicero through Liceto), and attention to the long durée of philosophical vocabulary. He treats the history of logic and semantics as indispensable to understanding theological heresy proceedings.

**Myth being tested**: The chapter implicitly tests and rejects two myths: (1) that the humanist critique of scholastic language was philosophically correct (Copenhaver shows the vocabulary was doing real philosophical work); and (2) that the medieval suppo- vocabulary was either purely semantic (a subset of logic) or purely metaphysical (a subset of theology)—it was irreducibly both.

**Historiographical register**: Analytic history of philosophy, closely allied with the history of logic and linguistics (De Rijk, Spade, Kann, Read traditions), but deployed in service of a narrative about intellectual culture and heresy prosecution. The chapter draws on comic literature (Rabelais) and religious history (Hobbes's anti-Catholicism) to contextualize technical philosophy. Copenhaver's own earlier work (*Copenhaver 2017, 2018*) is cited for context.

**Concord outcome**: Pico's trial involved real philosophical ambiguities, not mere ignorance. The judges who condemned Q4 and Q6 may have been reading suppo- words in the wrong register—treating what was a technical metaphysical argument as if it were a semantic or logical dodge, or vice versa. The chapter prepares a charitable reading of Pico's intentions while establishing the structural conditions that made misreading likely.

## Relation to Pico Works

### *Conclusiones* (1486/7)
- Five uses of suppo- words in 18,000 words
- **Q4** (condemned): restricts God's power to supposit any nature to rational natures only — *non assentior communi sententiae theologorum dicentium posse Deum quamlibet naturam suppositare, sed de rationabili tantum hoc concedo* (p. 95)
- **Q6** (condemned): applies "possibility of suppositing with regard to any creature" to eucharist — possibility claim about Christ's body on altar without conversion (*sine conversione substantiae panis in corpus Christi vel paneitatis annihilatione*) (pp. 95–96)
- **Kabbalah thesis** (not condemned): "*naturae humanae in unitate suppositi unitum*" — Kabbalist name Jesus proves the Trinity through suppositum language (p. 96)
- **Trinity thesis** (not condemned): "Three divine persons together could have supposited a single nature" (p. 96–97)
- **Philosophical Conclusions thesis 16** (not condemned): "Treatises on suppositions don't belong to logic" — implies metaphysics as primary domain (p. 96)

### *Apologia* (1487)
- Nearly 150 uses of suppo- words in 56,000 words
- Primary vehicle for Pico's self-defense after papal condemnation
- Suppo- words used "mainly on points of metaphysics" but also in logical and semantic senses
- The transition from five uses in Conclusions to 150 in Apology shows the vocabulary became strategically indispensable for defense

### Letter to Ermolao Barbaro (April 1485)
- Written in elegant classical Latin; demonstrates Pico's mastery of humanist style
- Defends scholastic philosophical language by resurrecting a scholastic to argue against Barbaro
- Argues philosophy needs its own linguistic conventions; clarity over elegance; Scotus over Lucretius
- Marks Pico's principled rejection of the humanist camp; shows he refused to be where "Rabelais would stand in the next century" (p. 98)

### Letter to Lorenzo de' Medici (summer 1484)
- Poetry letter; circulated in humanist style; mentioned briefly as one of two letter-essays (p. 97)

## Uncertainties

1. **Peter of Spain's identity**: Copenhaver notes the "faulty identification" of Peter of Spain as Pope John XXI (dating from the late thirteenth century) was informative but incorrect. The author of the *Summaries of Logic* is "almost certainly not" the future pope. This is well-established but worth flagging for any citation of "Peter of Spain."

2. **Rabelais's ignoring of Pico**: Copenhaver's observation that Rabelais ignored Pico is presented as a historical puzzle requiring explanation, but the explanation offered (time, place, language) is acknowledged as speculative. The assertion that Rabelais "was curious about Kabbalah" (p. 46, citing Screech) sets up the puzzle but doesn't resolve it.

3. **Ockham's authorship of the *Centiloquium***: Copenhaver consistently flags "often attributed to Ockham" or uses "the author of the *Centiloquium*" language, reflecting scholarly uncertainty about whether Ockham wrote this text. This matters because the *Centiloquium* is cited for the most dramatic logical-acrobatic claims about what God could supposit. The uncertainty should be noted in any PicoDB entry.

4. **The relational schema (Tables 2.1–2.5)**: Copenhaver explicitly states his identification of relata is "provisional, by no means well established" and that his analysis represents a "reconnaissance" rather than a definitive account. Tables 2.4 and 2.5 are explicitly described as possibilities for which "sufficient textual evidence" is lacking. The schema is an analytical tool, not a claim about medieval self-understanding.

5. **OCR issues in source file**: The source markdown file contains OCR artifacts (Greek letters sometimes garbled, relation symbols sometimes rendered as empty rectangles or generic alphanumeric placeholders). The formal relational schema in Tables 2.1–2.5 uses variables (α, β, γ, δ, ε) for relata that may not render accurately in all OCR outputs. Specific relation-type identifications from the tables should be verified against the printed text.

6. **Suppositabilis in Q4 vs. Q6**: The chapter states Q4 used *suppositare* and Q6 used *suppositationis*; verifying these specific forms against the Apology Latin (Africanus edition) would strengthen precision in any treatment of the condemned theses.
