## Section Function

Chapter 1 performs the book's foundational polemical and interpretive work: it establishes, through sustained close reading of the *Conclusions* and *Apology*, that Pico della Mirandola was a scholastic philosopher, not a humanist, in the three books he published during his lifetime. The chapter does this by analyzing the trial of 1487 in its institutional context (showing that the outcome was predetermined), reconstructing the structure of the *Apology* as a response to the commission's thirteen condemned theses, and demonstrating that eleven of those thirteen Questions are "scholastic through and through" in form, content, and authority base. The chapter also introduces the book's three central analytical sub-problems — the theology of divine embodiment that ties most condemned theses together; the theory of *suppositio* (both metaphysical and semantic) that structures Pico's defense; and the epistemological question of doxastic constraint on heresy charges — while situating the Q1 thesis on Christ's descent to Hell in the political context of the Pecock affair. It sets up Chapters 2–5 by showing what philosophical problems and what technical vocabulary the rest of the book must unpack.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

**Opening problem.** Copenhaver opens by observing that Pico's three published books — the *900 Conclusions* (1486), the *Apology* (1487), and the *Heptaplus* (1489) — are not written like humanist prose. The *Conclusions* was composed in "the way the most famous disputants talk in Paris," not in splendid Roman Latin (p. 7). This immediately foregrounds the chapter's thesis: the public Pico is a scholastic, not a humanist.

**The trial structure and its predetermination.** The papal commission appointed by Innocent VIII in February 1487 comprised sixteen members (twelve attended the first meeting on March 2, presided over by Bishop Jean Monissart of Tournai). Copenhaver establishes that the process was politically rigged: Innocent's initial charge described Pico's theses as "confused, deviant, intolerable, irregular, obscure, scandalous, and reeking of heresy" before any deliberations began; the commission completed its verdicts by mid-March — "lightning-fast by Vatican standards" — with all votes recorded as unanimous; and the pattern of proceedings shows that after hearing Pico's testimony, the commissioners ignored his explanations and found him guilty as charged (p. 8). The key technical criterion repeatedly deployed by the commissioners was *ex vi verborum* ("from the meaning of the words"), cited in four verdicts. Bishop Pedro Garcia, a Thomist Dominican, was the most doctrinally significant member; he later published a book-length rebuttal of the *Apology* in 1489.

**Pico's combativeness documented.** A famous passage from the *Apology* (quoted at length, pp. 8–10) shows Pico in verbal combat with two commissioners over Origen: he mocks the citation of a dubious world history ("Bundle of Dates"), puns on *fasciculus/fascibus* (bundle/fasces), and makes a scatological pun on *testis/testes* (witness/testicles, after Plautus). Copenhaver reads this as evidence of aristocratic arrogance but also as proof that the commission's hostility was a "mission from the start" (p. 11).

**The Apology as scholastic product.** The structure of the *Apology* follows the commission's order almost exactly. Thirteen condemned theses produce thirteen Questions (Q1–Q13). Copenhaver lists them (p. 13): Q1 Hell, Q2 Sin, Q3 Worship, Q4 Incarnation, Q5 Kabbalah, Q6/Q9/Q10 Eucharist, Q7 Origen, Q8 Belief, Q11 Miracle, Q12 God, Q13 Soul. The connection among most of them, Copenhaver argues, is the problem of *divine embodiment* (p. 14): Q4 (incarnation) establishes that God has a body; Q6/Q9/Q10 (eucharist) insist that God's body looks like bread; Q1 (descent) separates Christ's dead body from his soul in Hell. Q5 (Kabbalah) and Q7 (Origen) are outliers from this pattern. All but Q5 follow the *quaestio* form of medieval scholastic debate; Q7 is more philological; Q5 is formally sui generis (p. 15).

**The eucharistic theology (§1.2).** Three condemned theses concern the eucharist — the flashpoint of European theology since Berengar of Tours in the eleventh century. Copenhaver reconstructs each:

- *Q6*: Pico proposes that, without bread changing into Christ's body (*conversio*) and without eliminating *paneitas* (breadness), Christ's body can be present on the altar "in keeping with sacramental reality" (*veritas sacramenti*) — but understood *de possibili* (as possible), not *de sic esse* (as actual). This is the "sustaining of breadness" (*substentatio panitatis*) using Scotist vocabulary of *suppositatio*. Half of Q6 is taken verbatim from Jean Cabrol's *Defenses of the Theology of Thomas Aquinas*, without acknowledgment.
- *Q9*: Someone who holds that an accident cannot exist unless it *exists-in* (*inexistat*) can still accept eucharistic doctrine even while holding that bread's substance does not remain — a claim about the metaphysics of accidents surviving the removal of their substance.
- *Q10*: The words of consecration (*hoc est enim corpus meum*) are taken "materially, not as signifying" — i.e., via *suppositio materialis* in medieval logic, where a term refers to itself as a word, not to a thing. Pico cites the Ordinary Gloss on Gratian's *Decretum* in support.

Pico's authority base for Q6 includes Pierre de la Palud (*Paludanus*), Jean Cabrol, John Duns Scotus, Aquinas, and others. Copenhaver identifies Cabrol as Pico's major undisclosed source throughout Q6 (pp. 12, 32). The broader context for Q9 and Q10 is medieval supposition theory, from Peter of Spain's *Summaries of Logic*, which Thomas More famously mocked as "little logicals that have little logic in them" (p. 18). That same apparatus was Pico's main defense for these theses.

**Historical context: Learned heretics (§1.3).** To explain why the commission attacked the Hell thesis first, Copenhaver introduces Bishop Reginald Pecock of Chichester — "the only bishop before the Reformation to lose his see as a heretic" — whose downfall turned on the Harrowing of Hell in 1457. Pecock had argued that the descent clause of the Apostles' Creed was a later invention without scriptural basis. After a formal process, his books were burned and he was stripped of his see. The episode was still reverberating through four subsequent pontificates when Pico's trial began; Copenhaver argues that "the corporate memory of Vatican officials responsible for Pico's inquisition surely reached back... to the Pecock disaster" (p. 21). This explains why papal interrogators chose the Hell thesis as their opening attack: "the Pecock debacle had politicized the underworld" (p. 21).

**The Q1 thesis (§1.4).** The full text of the Q1 thesis: "Christ did not truly, and as far as real presence is concerned, descend to Hell, as proposed by Thomas and on the usual approach, but only in regard to the effect" (p. 22). The commission ruled the thesis "false, erroneous, heretical and contrary to the truth of sacred scripture... notwithstanding any explanation produced about the conclusion" — the verdict was pre-emptive (March 5, 1487, before the *Apology* existed). Pico denied any rejection of the Creed: "no one should understand me to say that Christ's soul did not descend into Hell." His argument exploited the distinctions within the Q1 thesis: clauses (i) "did not truly" and (ii) "as far as real presence is concerned" were modified by (iii) "as proposed by Thomas and on the usual approach," leaving only (iv) "only in regard to the effect" as his actual claim. Aquinas had himself distinguished Christ's *essential* presence in Hell from his *effective* presence, so Pico claimed to be following Thomas rather than rejecting him.

**Metaphysics of angelic location and Hell (§1.4–1.8).** The placement of Christ's bodiless soul in Hell raised the same metaphysical problem as the placement of angels: how can a spirit without a body be "in a place"? Copenhaver traces Pico's engagement with the medieval doctrine of three modes of spatial presence: *circumscriptive* (by bodily measure), *definitive* (by delimitation, for spirits), and *repletive* (God's omnipresence). Étienne Tempier's 1277 condemnations in Paris had included articles on angelic location; Godfrey of Fontaines had argued that several Tempier articles "plainly cannot go with one another" — a precedent Pico needed to discredit the documents the commission used against him. Pico's own conclusion on Q1: a separated substance (like Christ's soul or an angel) is present in a place *definitively*, not *circumscriptively*; "the basis for the presence of Christ's soul was what he did there" (p. 35). This aligns him with Scotus against Aquinas on the mode of Christ's presence in Hell.

**Doxastic ethics (§1.5).** The fourth and final section of Q1 shifts from metaphysics to epistemology: were there limits on the Church's authority to compel beliefs by a heresy charge? Pico introduced the concept of "doxastic bondage": even the greatest authorities (Augustine, Jerome, Gregory) could only believe what they believed — fallibly, provisionally. No belief could be demonstrated like a geometric theorem; it could only be held on faith or credence. The Q8 thesis (also condemned) denied *doxastic voluntarism* — the view that one can choose to believe or disbelieve at will. If one cannot simply choose one's beliefs, then charges of heresy for holding them are epistemically unjust. But both sides cared more about metaphysics than epistemic ethics, and Q8 received little attention in the trial record.

**Ockham as ethical guide (§1.8).** Pico's defense of the Q1 thesis turns at its climax on a piece of logical analysis (the "coupled extreme," *copulatus extremus*) derived from Ockham's *Summa Logicae*: the *and* in Q1 connects a "coupled extreme" (a doubly qualified subject), not a "coupled proposition" (two independent assertions). Negating the whole does not entail negating each part. To argue from "Christ did not truly and as far as real presence is concerned descend to Hell" to "Christ did not truly descend" is "quite childish and comes from a person completely ignorant of logic and philosophy — not to mention theology" (p. 37–38). Pico then invoked Ockham's *Dialogus* — written when Ockham himself was in trouble with the papacy — to argue that a pope who misuses heresy charges to promote his own interests is an arch-heretic. "Pico's ethical grievance was not inspired by humanists like Leonardo Bruni, civic pioneers of new secular virtues that this pious prodigy had no notion of or interest in" (p. 39).

**Concordism vs. scholasticism (§1.6).** Pico's project in the *Conclusions* combined two grand schemes: philosophical *concordia* (reconciling Plato/Aristotle, Scotus/Thomas, Averroes/Avicenna) and Christian Kabbalah (Jews' mysticism as the path to Christian salvation). Q1 instantiated concordism: Pico argued that Thomas and Scotus "do not differ about angels on their manner of being in a place" (a non-condemned thesis from the *Conclusions*, p. 29). But concordism as a reform program was a "total failure" — philosophers ignored Pico's plea (p. 29). Later historians of philosophy called it "syncretism" and condemned it: Brucker wrote that it "caused philosophy's corruption and debasement" (p. 30). In the desperate circumstances of the *Apology*, concordism lapsed into "tactical relativism" — if Pico's disagreements with saintly authorities were superficial, why condemn them?

**The Conclusions' scholastic authority base (§1.7–1.9).** Q1 cited two dozen scholastic authorities (listed pp. 25, 30). No classical pagans appeared; the scene was "exclusively Christian" (p. 33). Pico worked primarily from Aquinas, Durand de Saint-Pourçain, Henry of Ghent, Scotus, and Jean Cabrol's compilations — the last a largely unacknowledged source throughout. The *Apology* was dictated in haste ("the work of twenty long nights," p. 32). Pico explicitly contrasted the scholastic authority list in the *Apology* (Albert, Thomas, Scotus, Giles, Francis, Henry) with the exotic names of the undelivered *Oration* (Aglaophemus, Dardanus, Xalmolsides — lifted from the suppressed speech). This contrast is the chapter's structural endpoint. The full roster of Pico's thirty-plus medieval authorities in the scholastic Questions is tabulated (pp. 41–42), followed by the entirely different name-set from the Oration's fragment. In the *Apology*, no Neoplatonist philosopher appeared in any Question.

**Thirteen concluding propositions (§1.9).** Copenhaver ends Chapter 1 with thirteen propositions "in a spirit of disputation — like those defended by Pico's Apology" (p. 44): (1) Pico, like Hus and Pecock, faced mortal danger from heresy charges; (2) such charges were real threats to important people; (3) Pico's danger was European in reach; (4) his public career in philosophy belongs to the late medieval period; (5) Pico's philosophy should be studied by medievalists as well as Renaissance scholars; (6) Pico's writing in his three printed books was not humanist; (7) *Conclusions* and *Apology* were scholastic; (8) the *Heptaplus* is harder to classify but not humanist in the way of the *Oration*; (9) both *Conclusions* and *Apology* were derivative and reactive rather than creative; (10) Pico applied concordism to a non-partisan scholasticism; (11) Ockham, "one of the 'barbarous Britons,'" inspired Pico's ethics; (12) the ethics of the *Apology* is medieval, not humanist; (13) these propositions are offered in a spirit of disputation.

## Claims and Subclaims

- **Claim 1**: Pico's three published books were written in scholastic, not humanist, Latin; the *Conclusions* used "the way the most famous disputants talk in Paris, not the splendid Roman tongue." (p. 7)
- **Claim 2**: Innocent VIII's commission was pre-determined to convict: "indications that the trial's outcome was fixed in this way are both chronological and documentary." (p. 8)
- **Claim 3**: The commission moved "lightning-fast by Vatican standards" from February to mid-March 1487, recording all votes as unanimous and ignoring Pico's testimony. (pp. 7–8)
- **Claim 4**: The key technical criterion used by the commission was *ex vi verborum* ("from the meaning of the words"), cited in four of their verdicts. (p. 8)
- **Claim 5**: The connection among eleven of the thirteen condemned theses is the problem of divine embodiment: Q4 (incarnation), Q6/Q9/Q10 (eucharist), and Q1 (Hell descent) are all variations on the same metaphysical puzzle. (p. 14)
- **Claim 6**: Q5 (Kabbalah) was "utterly unintelligible to his judges in Rome and completely detached from the scholastic philosophizing that informed all the other condemned theses." (p. 15)
- **Claim 7**: Q7 (Origen) is philological in form, resembling Eusebius rather than Aquinas, and foreshadows Erasmian patristic philology — but is anomalous in the *Apology*. (p. 15)
- **Claim 8**: Half of Q6 came "straight (more or less) from Cabrol's Defenses of the Theology of Thomas Aquinas in four volumes, which Pico owned," without acknowledgment. (p. 12)
- **Claim 9**: Pico's Q10 thesis applies *suppositio materialis* to the words of consecration (*hoc est corpus meum*), citing the Ordinary Gloss on Gratian's *Decretum* in support. (pp. 16–17)
- **Claim 10**: Medieval supposition theory — from Peter of Spain's *Summaries of Logic* — was Pico's "main line of defense for the eucharistic propositions in Q9 and Q10." (p. 18)
- **Claim 11**: Bishop Reginald Pecock's loss of his see over the Harrowing of Hell (1457) politicized that doctrine for the Vatican, explaining why the commission attacked Q1 first. (p. 21)
- **Claim 12**: The Q1 verdict (March 5, 1487) was issued before the *Apology* existed; Pico wrote the *Apology* because of it. (p. 22)
- **Claim 13**: Pico's Q1 thesis denies not that Christ descended to Hell, but only that his descent was "as proposed by Thomas and on the usual approach" — a qualified disagreement with the Thomist account of real/essential presence. (pp. 22–24)
- **Claim 14**: Aquinas himself distinguished Christ's effective presence in Hell from his essential presence, so Pico claimed his own position was Thomistically defensible. (pp. 22–23)
- **Claim 15**: Pico's theory of Christ's location in Hell: a separated substance is present *definitively*, not *circumscriptively*; "the basis for the presence of Christ's soul was what he did there." (p. 35)
- **Claim 16**: The commission's deployment of the 1277 Tempier condemnations against Pico was discredited by Godfrey of Fontaines, who had argued those articles "plainly cannot go with one another." (pp. 32–33)
- **Claim 17**: Pico introduced "doxastic bondage" as the condition of all believers, including all Church authorities: even Augustine and Jerome "could only believe what they believed." (pp. 24–25)
- **Claim 18**: Pico used Ockham's *Summa Logicae* analysis of the "coupled extreme" to show that negating the Q1 thesis as a coupled proposition does not entail denying that Christ descended to Hell. (pp. 36–38)
- **Claim 19**: Pico cited Ockham's *Dialogus* — written when Ockham himself had trouble with the papacy — to argue that a pope who misuses heresy charges is an arch-heretic. (p. 38)
- **Claim 20**: "Pico's ethical grievance was not inspired by humanists like Leonardo Bruni, civic pioneers of new secular virtues that this pious prodigy had no notion of or interest in." (p. 39)
- **Claim 21**: Philosophical concordism was "a total failure": philosophers ignored Pico's plea, and later critics from Brucker onward condemned it as "syncretism" causing philosophy's corruption. (pp. 29–30)
- **Claim 22**: The *Oration*, the text most associated with Pico's reputation, was silenced by Innocent VIII; none of the exotic names from the *Oration* (Aglaophemus, Dardanus, Xalmolsides) appear in any Question of the *Apology*. (pp. 42–43)
- **Claim 23**: "The public Pico, who was not modern or even pre-modern, was an astonishing medieval thinker, not the trumpeter of a forward-looking renaissance." (p. 41)
- **Claim 24**: The *Apology* was "the work of twenty long nights," dictated in haste with help from unnamed collaborators in Rome. (pp. 32–33)
- **Claim 25**: Pico applied concordism not in a historicist way but in a procedural-philosophical way: authorities appear as "abstract vehicles of theological positions," not as historical agents. (p. 15 n. 16)

## Technical Vocabulary

- **ex vi verborum** (Latin): "from the meaning of the words" — the commission's criterion for condemning Pico's theses as stated; cited in four verdicts (pp. 8, 22)
- **de virtute sermonis / secundum vim verborum** (Latin): "in virtue of its language / according to the meaning of its words" — Pico's mirroring of the commission's criterion to frame his own defense (pp. 22, 35)
- **secundum rigorem sermonis** (Latin): "strictly speaking" — the commission's stronger formulation of the *vis verborum* criterion (p. 22)
- **paneitas** (Latin, scholastic neologism): "breadness" — the formal principle constituting something as bread; key to Q6's eucharistic argument (pp. 11, 15)
- **suppositatio** (Latin, scholastic): the act of a substance being "supposited" or "taken on" by another — both metaphysical (incarnation, Q4/Q6) and semantic (Q9/Q10) senses in play (pp. 11, 16, 39)
- **suppositum** (Latin, scholastic): the ultimate bearer of a substance's existence; what is "there" as an individual (pp. 39–40)
- **substentatio panitatis** (Latin, scholastic): "sustaining of breadness" — Pico's term for the mechanism by which *paneitas* persists in the eucharist without substantial change (pp. 11, 15)
- **suppositio materialis** (Latin, scholastic logic): a type of supposition in which a term refers to itself as a word, not to an external thing — applied in Q10 to *hoc est corpus meum* (pp. 16–17)
- **de possibili / de sic esse** (Latin, scholastic): "about what is possible" vs. "about how things are" — modal distinction at the heart of Pico's defense in Q6 (p. 11)
- **communis via** (Latin): "usual approach" or "shared approach" — the Thomist-Scotist consensus on Christ's real presence in Hell that Pico's Q1 thesis rejected (pp. 23, 29)
- **copulatus extremus** (Latin, scholastic logic): "coupled extreme" — a proposition with a compound subject or predicate, where negation applies to the whole, not each part; Pico's logical key to Q1 (pp. 36–38)
- **syncategoremata** (Latin/Greek, scholastic logic): logical connectives like "and," "or," "if" — their analysis was the basis for Pico's Q1 defense via Ockham (p. 37)
- **circumscriptive / definitive / repletive** (Latin, scholastic): three modes of spatial presence: by bodily measure (bodies), by delimitation (spirits/angels), by omnipresence (God) — framework for Q1 on Christ's soul in Hell (pp. 34–35)
- **fides quaerens intellectum** (Latin): "faith seeking understanding" — Anselmian axiom invoked by Pico to justify philosophical inquiry into dogma (p. 24)
- **descensus ad inferna / infernum** (Latin): "descent to Hell" — the Creed clause at issue in Q1 (pp. 20–24)
- **triduum** (Latin): the "three days" between Christ's death and resurrection, during which the descent to Hell was held to have occurred (p. 20)
- **quaestio** (Latin, scholastic): the formal disputational unit of medieval theology — the form of eleven of Pico's thirteen *Apology* Questions (p. 15)
- **doxastic bondage** (Copenhaver's term, derived from Pico): the condition of believers who can only believe what they believe — no one, including Church authorities, can will themselves to believe otherwise (pp. 24–26)
- **inexistere** (Latin, scholastic): "to exist-in" — the relation of accident to substance; Q9 proposes that even if accidents can exist only by *inexisting*, the eucharistic dogma can still be maintained (pp. 17–18)
- **ampliation** (Latin, scholastic logic): the semantic extension of a term's range of application, especially in modal propositions — relevant to Q9's argument (p. 18)

## Scholarly Values Overlay

- **Guide scholars applied**: Copenhaver primarily, with Edelheit (2008) as an important foil. On scholasticism and the *Apology*, Copenhaver diverges from Edelheit's view that Pico tried to question scholasticism from a "historical-critical perspective"; Copenhaver insists Pico's dialectic is "purely philosophical" and treats past authorities as "abstract vehicles of theological positions," not historical agents. Hankins (2019) cited on civic humanism and Bruni.
- **Myth or label being tested**: The myth of "Pico as humanist" — specifically the assumption that because Pico is grouped with Ficino and the Florentine academy, and because the *Oration* reads as a humanist manifesto, his public philosophical identity was humanist. Copenhaver demolishes this: the three published books are scholastic (two) or hard to classify but not conventionally humanist (*Heptaplus*).
- **Secondary myth tested**: The related myth that the *Oration* represents Pico's characteristic voice. Copenhaver shows that the *Oration* was silenced by the Pope, that its exotic name-list (Aglaophemus, Xalmolsides, Orpheus) appears in no Question of the *Apology*, and that the *Oration* fragment prefixed to the *Apology* was "pasted on" hastily after all thirteen Questions were drafted.
- **Historiographical register**: Intervention against Renaissance humanism as an interpretive frame for Pico. Copenhaver aligns with a medievalist reading: Pico's primary intellectual formation was in scholastic theology (Paris, Padua), his philosophical community included Cittadini, Pomponazzi, and Vernia, and his Ockham-derived ethics derives from a medieval anti-papalist tradition, not a Petrarchan or Brunian civic tradition.
- **Concord outcome**: Pico's concordism was a failure (p. 29): philosophers ignored it, later critics condemned it. Copenhaver presents this without nostalgia — the chapter diagnoses rather than celebrates Pico's project.

## Relation to Pico Works

- **900 Conclusions (1486)**: Analyzed structurally; the thirteen condemned theses are identified; the non-humanist scholastic register of its Latin is established (p. 7). Multiple specific theses cited by page: Q1 thesis at Con. pp. 39–42; eucharistic theses at Con. pp. 41–4; incarnation at Con. pp. 41–2; Hermetica theses at Con. p. 24; Plotinus theses at Con. pp. 14–15; Averroes theses at Con. pp. 7–9.
- **Apology (1487)**: The central object of Chapter 1. Q1 analyzed exhaustively (pp. 22–38). Q6 analyzed as to eucharistic metaphysics and Cabrol-dependence (pp. 11–12). Q9 and Q10 analyzed as to metaphysics of accidents and supposition semantics (pp. 16–18). Q4 introduced (incarnation thesis, pp. 39–40). Q5 characterized as outlier (pp. 15, 43). Q8 introduced on doxastic voluntarism (pp. 24–25). The *Apology*'s hasty composition (twenty nights) and its prefixed *Oration* fragment analyzed (pp. 32–33, 42–43).
- **Oration (undelivered, unpublished in Pico's lifetime)**: Analyzed as the suppressed first address; its exotic name-list contrasted with the *Apology*'s scholastic authorities (pp. 42–43). The *Oration* fragment prefixed to the *Apology* is described as pasted on hastily and "incompatible" with the book's scholastic body (pp. 42–43).
- **Heptaplus (1489)**: Mentioned but not analyzed. Its Latin style described as "mixed" without the roughness of *Conclusions/Apology*; the question of its humanist or scholastic character left open (p. 40 n. 86).

## Uncertainties

1. The exact composition of Pico's research team in Rome is unknown and unknowable given the circumstances (p. 33). The *Apology*'s dependence on Cabrol, Guido Terrena, and other scholastic compilers is documented, but how Pico accessed them so rapidly under trial conditions is unresolved.
2. The identification of the "magister" whom Pico attacked for quantifying angelic location (p. 28) is not made explicit in the chapter. Copenhaver suggests it may have been Bishop Garcia, but this is tentative.
3. Copenhaver's footnote 10 (p. 12) notes a likely scribal error in the *Apology* text at Apo. p. 70 (*non conversione divinitatis in carnem nec supple econverso*) which appears to have crept in from Cabrol (1483–4) — the direction of textual transmission is uncertain.
4. The *Centiloquium* attributed to Ockham: Copenhaver treats it as a text Pico "may" have known (p. 39) and as "widely attributed to Ockham." Its authenticity was debated in the twentieth century and is not settled.
5. Page numbers in this chapter are derivable from the running page markers in the OCR markdown (e.g., "## Page 21" = book p. 8), so all page references have been cross-checked against the section markers. No OCR anomalies observed in this chapter.
