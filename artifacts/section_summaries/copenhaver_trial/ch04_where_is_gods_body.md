## Section Function

Chapter 4 is the eucharistic centerpiece of the book's argument about Pico's trial. Three of the thirteen condemned propositions (Q6, Q9, Q10) concern the Eucharist, and Copenhaver devotes more space to them than to any other doctrinal cluster. The chapter serves three functions simultaneously. First, it provides the medieval theological and philosophical backstory for eucharistic controversy from Berengar of Tours through the Fourth Lateran Council to Scotus and Ockham — establishing that the field Pico entered was genuinely contested and that his position had recognized precursors. Second, it gives the first full scholarly account of Jean Quidort's "impanation" theory and demonstrates, through close textual comparison, that Pico's Q6 derives almost entirely from Jean Cabrol's Thomist synthesis rather than from Quidort directly — a finding that revises the historiography. Third, it develops the book's central methodological claim: that Pico's heresy charges have been misread because the judges (and later scholars) ignored his explicit appeals to modal logic, failing to distinguish his problematic (de possibili) statements from assertoric (de sic esse) claims. The chapter thus advances the book's argument that Pico was neither naive nor heterodox but philosophically agile and strategically audacious in a way that backfired.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

Chapter 4 opens with a framing image: Cosimo Rosselli's 1486 fresco in the Church of Sant' Ambrogio in Florence, whose lay confraternity bore the name Corpus Domini — the Lord's Body. The association of Pico with this devotional context establishes the chapter's central question: how can God's body be really present in consecrated bread? This question, Copenhaver argues, was not a marginal theological puzzle but a burning controversy across several centuries of Catholic theology and canon law that Pico stepped into with his Q6 thesis (pp. 145–146).

The chapter's first long section (4.1) surveys the tradition systematically. The eucharistic miracle required that Christ's body be really present on the altar even though worshippers saw only bread. Aquinas's Tantum ergo captured the theological imperative: "let faith provide support where the senses fail." Copenhaver traces three competing theories of what happened to the bread's substance at consecration — the A-theory (bread's substance remains alongside Christ's body), the B-theory (bread's substance is annihilated, accidents remain), and the C-theory (bread's substance is changed or transubstantiated into Christ's substance) (pp. 151–152, citing Cardinal Pietro Capuano, c. 1200). Peter Lombard had already identified these possibilities in the twelfth century without calling any of them heretical, insisting only on Christ's real presence. The Fourth Lateran Council (1215) under Innocent III formally introduced the word "transubstantiation" into canon law but, as Copenhaver demonstrates, neither the Council decree nor Innocent's own 1202 letter treated competing theories as definitively heretical — a point the canonist Giovanni d'Andrea confirmed in the 1330s when he noted that Henry of Susa had approved all three approaches as compatible with real presence (pp. 153–154). Aquinas broke from this irenic tradition: in both the Scriptum on the Sentences and the Summa of Theology he rejected the A-theory of remaining as heresy, arguing from his Aristotelian physics of motion (motus-mutatio) that if the bread's substance persisted after consecration, Christ's body would have had to travel from heaven to the altar under its own power — which contradicted the dogma of Christ's impassible glorified body (pp. 155–157). This Thomist aggressive exclusion of the A-theory as heretical is the historical background for why Pico's Q6, which defended an A-theory, drew the particular charge it did.

Section 4.2 introduces the key medieval precursor: Jean Quidort (John O'Sleep, d. 1306), a Dominican Thomist who nevertheless proposed an A-theory of bread remaining by analogizing eucharistic "embreadment" (impanation) with incarnation. Quidort's starting point was the problem of sharing properties (communicatio idiomatum): if the bread-substance remained on the altar alongside Christ's body, there could be no single supposit to ground that sharing unless the Word took the bread's nature (paneitas, breadness) on in the same formal manner that, by incarnation, the Word took on human nature. Impanation was, in Quidort's theory, the eucharistic analog of incarnation: the Word sustained two non-divine natures, humanness and breadness, through its divine supposit, with Christ's bodiness mediating between the Word and the consecrated breadness (pp. 158–165). Quidort acknowledged the risk of this analogy and was indeed censured and banned from teaching; he appealed to Rome but died in 1306 with the case pending. Copenhaver establishes that Quidort's full Determination was known only in one manuscript and was not printed until 1686 — so Pico could not have read it directly. What Pico read instead was Jean Cabrol's 1432 Thomist synthesis, which reported Quidort vaguely as "a certain John of Paris" and described his theory through the hostile summaries of Durandus and Paludanus. Copenhaver's key philological finding is that more than half of Q6 reproduces Cabrol's wording of Quidort, often verbatim and in the same order, and that some garbled passages in the 1487 Apology are only correctable by comparison with Cabrol's transmission of Paludanus's account of Quidort — not from Quidort's own text (pp. 167–169). Ockham, Copenhaver notes, had also kept the A-theory philosophically alive by invoking divine omnipotence to remove the obstacle Aquinas had identified, without committing to it as doctrine (pp. 163–165).

Section 4.3 turns to Pico's Q6 text itself. Copenhaver reads the Q6 thesis as a modal proposition in which Pico explicitly signals that he is speaking de possibili, not de sic esse: "let the disputation that I have provided suffice. After all of it, I hold the views that agree with my conclusion as explained by what I have said." Pico's strategic move was to present his A-theory as a theoretical possibility, not an assertion of how the Eucharist actually operates, and to argue that possibility-statements cannot be heretical in the same way that factual assertions can be. He illustrated this logic with a paradigm from Trinitarian theology: "The Father is incarnate" (de sic esse — false and heretical) versus "The Father can be incarnated" (de possibili — true and orthodox). His judges apparently collapsed this distinction, reading his Q6 thesis as assertoric. Copenhaver's assessment is measured: the judges were wrong and rigid, but Pico was also strategically clumsy, having introduced the phrase "I say that (dico quod)" — an assertoric marker — inside an otherwise problematic thesis, creating a modal tangle. The Q6 thesis was thus simultaneously more defensible than the judges acknowledged and less well-defended than it needed to be (pp. 169–175). Copenhaver also notes that Q6 is mostly not about its own thesis: after a few paragraphs stating and explicating the proposition, the rest (about 44,600 words out of 45,000) is an "academic exercise" cataloguing patristic authorities and surveying A-, B-, and C-theories — which Pico himself said was separate from his actual defense. He was showing off his scholastic knowledge more than protecting himself (pp. 173–175).

Section 4.4 moves to Q9, which is brief. The Q9 thesis proposes that someone who holds the general logical-metaphysical principle that an accident cannot exist without existing-in a subject can still hold to the eucharist while also holding (with the Thomist C-theory) that bread does not remain. Copenhaver reconstructs Pico's argument: the A-theory which Q6 explored is abandoned in Q9's -clause, and the point instead is that even on Thomist premises (C-theory, no remaining bread), it remains possible to conceive of a eucharist in which accidents have a subject — because the existence (esse) of the bread's substance might persist even after its essence is gone, through a distinction (Thomistic, essence/existence) that would provide a ground for accidents without requiring the substance to remain. Pico drew this argument from Pierre de la Palud (Paludanus) directly, not via Cabrol (pp. 176–179). The logical backbone of Q9 was Pico's claim that his thesis was a "divided modal" (modalis divisa), not a composite modal — a distinction between the forms "an accident cannot exist without existing-in" (mD, implicitly modal) and "that an accident exists without existing-in is impossible" (mK, explicitly modal with visible dictum). Copenhaver explains carefully that both express the same proposition, but mK makes the modal character visible in a way that mD does not (pp. 179–181). This matters because modal ampliation — which Pico also invoked — allowed the term "sacrament of the eucharist" in his thesis to refer not only to the actual sacrament but to any possible eucharist, broadening the scope of what he was exploring. The judges, Pico charged, committed a fallacy of affirming the consequent: from the fact that in the actual Eucharist there are no accidents with subjects, they wrongly inferred that no possible eucharist could have accidents with subjects (pp. 181–186). Copenhaver concludes that even if Pico's logical diagnosis of the judges' mistake was correct, his Q9 defense was "badly underargued" — the three interlocked technical moves (divided modal, ampliation, affirming-the-consequent fallacy) needed more development than the brief Q9 supplied.

Section 4.5 treats Q10, the shortest eucharistic thesis: "The words 'this is my body' that are said in the consecration are taken materially and not as signifying." Copenhaver traces this claim to the Standard Gloss (Glossa ordinaria) on Canon 25 of Gratian's Decretum (De consecratione, Dist. 2, c. 25, titled Timorem), where the canonical glossators had concluded that the demonstrative pronoun hoc in hoc est corpus meum "indicates nothing" because it is used materialiter — materially rather than signifying. The theological puzzle was sequential: when a priest first said hoc, the transubstantiation had not yet occurred (it required the complete formula), so hoc could not indicate Christ's body; but neither could it indicate the bread, since the bread was not Christ's body. The gloss resolved this by treating hoc as a word used materially — about the word itself rather than about a referent — effectively a claim from supposition theory (pp. 186–192). Innocent III, Aquinas, Scotus, Durand de Saint-Pourçain, and Ockham had all engaged with this problem, with diverging outcomes. Aquinas had actually opposed the material reading, insisting that the words of consecration must be taken signifficative (as signifying) for the sacramental act to be effective — otherwise the priest was merely narrating Christ's words and the miracle would fade into a memorial. Durandus and Scotus accepted the narrative (recitative) reading that Aquinas rejected (pp. 193–195). Pico's Q10 thesis cited the canonical gloss as his authority and deployed supposition theory to argue that the priest "narrates" Christ's original words, so the priest's utterance supposits for Christ's — the priest's "this is my body" stands in for Christ's "this is my body" in the way a theatrical performer stands in for a character. The judges called this "scandalous and contrary to the usual view of saintly teachers" — a milder verdict than those for Q6 and Q9 (pp. 186–195). Copenhaver notes that canon law, by Pico's time, had never officially rejected the gloss, giving Pico a strong canonical footing that he exploited.

The chapter closes with the full text of Q9 (Ninth Question: On Accidents in the Sacrament) and Q10 (Tenth Question: On the Words of Consecration) in Copenhaver's English translation, with numbered paragraph divisions (pp. 195–199). These translations were not previously available and the chapter notes that the Latin texts of the 1487 Apology for Q9 require emendation.

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: Three of the thirteen condemned propositions in Pico's Apology (Q6, Q9, Q10) concern the Eucharist, making eucharistic theology the single most heavily represented topic in the heresy proceedings (pp. 145, 162 [table]).

**Claim 2**: Peter Lombard's *Sentences* identified three theories of eucharistic change (A/remain, B/annihilate, C/transubstantiate) without calling any of them heretical, insisting only on Christ's real presence (pp. 152, 154–155).

**Claim 3**: The Fourth Lateran Council (1215) introduced "transubstantiation" into canon law, but neither the Council decree nor Innocent III's 1202 letter treated competing explanations as definitively heretical; the canonist Giovanni d'Andrea in the 1330s confirmed that all three theories remained legally permissible (pp. 153–154).

**Claim 4**: Aquinas was unusual among medieval theologians in insisting on the C-theory and denouncing the A-theory of remaining as heresy, arguing from Aristotelian motion-theory that any arrival of Christ's body on the altar had to proceed from the bread's own change, not from Christ's local movement through space (pp. 155–157).

**Claim 5**: Aquinas's position was summarized and schematized by his Dominican follower Hervé de Nédellec, whose digest introduced the term "assumptio" (taking-on) as the key to distinguishing impanation from incarnation (pp. 156–157).

**Claim 6**: Jean Quidort (d. 1306) proposed an A-theory of eucharistic remaining through analogy with incarnation: just as the Word took on (assumed) human nature, so it could take on breadness (paneitas), producing a kind of eucharistic impanation with a single divine supposit on the altar (pp. 158–162).

**Claim 7**: Quidort was censured and banned from teaching; his *Determination* survived in a single manuscript and was not printed until 1686, making it unavailable to Pico directly (pp. 158, 165).

**Claim 8**: Pico's knowledge of Quidort came through Jean Cabrol's 1432 Thomist synthesis, which reported Quidort vaguely as "a certain John of Paris" and transmitted his theory via the hostile accounts of Durandus and Paludanus (pp. 167–168).

**Claim 9**: More than half of Q6 reproduces Cabrol's version of Quidort's theory, often in the same words and order (p. 168).

**Claim 10**: Garbled passages in the 1487 Apology's Q6 are only correctable by comparison with Cabrol's transmission via Paludanus — not from Quidort's own text — proving Pico's indirect dependence (pp. 168–169).

**Claim 11**: Bishop Garcia, reviewing the Apology in 1489, recognized that Pico "chases after John of Paris" on the Q6 issue, even though Pico himself never mentioned Quidort in Q6 (pp. 168).

**Claim 12**: Pico's strategic defense of Q6 was to argue de possibili: he was exploring a theoretical possibility about the eucharist, not asserting a fact; many statements that would be heretical de sic esse are not heretical de possibili (pp. 169–171).

**Claim 13**: The Q6 thesis contains the assertoric phrase "I say that (dico quod)" inside an otherwise problematic statement, creating a modal tangle that undermined Pico's own defense (p. 170).

**Claim 14**: The judges who condemned Q6 as erroneous — not heretical — were somewhat less harsh than in their verdict on Q1 (Hell), which they called heretical; Copenhaver judges the judges wrong on Q6: the doctrine of eucharistic consecration was genuinely fluid and no accusation of serious heresy was sustainable (p. 171, 174).

**Claim 15**: Q6 is almost entirely an "academic exercise" cataloguing patristic authorities (the Damascene, Pseudo-Dionysius, Augustine, Ambrose, Gregory, Peter Lombard, Berengar) and eucharistic theories — fewer than 400 words out of 45,000 actually address the thesis itself (pp. 171–173, 174).

**Claim 16**: The word *suppositum* and its cognates appear eighty-two times in Q6, used almost always in a metaphysical sense; the one exception, where supponit pro is used semantically in analyzing a Pauline proposition, shows Pico moving between metaphysical and logical-semantic modes of the same terminology without flagging the switch (pp. 174–175).

**Claim 17**: The Q9 thesis was judged erroneous (not heretical) by the commission; Q10 was judged only "scandalous and contrary to the usual view of saintly teachers" — the mildest of all the verdicts (pp. 175–176).

**Claim 18**: The Q9 thesis abandons the A-theory defended by Q6: its -clause accepts "the usual approach" that bread's substance does not remain after consecration (Thomist C-theory), but its main purpose is to show that even on Thomist premises, the impossibility-of-accidents-without-subjects principle does not rule out all possible eucharists with accidents in a subject (pp. 176–179).

**Claim 19**: The Q9 defense rests on three interlocked logical moves — divided modal, modal ampliation of "sacrament of the eucharist," and a fallacy of affirming the consequent charged against the judges — but the brief Q9 develops none of these adequately (pp. 179–186).

**Claim 20**: Q10's thesis — that "this is my body" is taken materially, not as signifying — is grounded in the Standard Gloss (Glossa ordinaria) on Canon 25 of Gratian's Decretum, which had never been formally rejected by the Church, giving Pico a canonical footing (pp. 186–188, 195).

**Claim 21**: Aquinas had opposed this material reading, insisting that the words of consecration must be taken signifficative for the sacramental act to be effective; Durandus and Scotus accepted the narrative (recitative) reading (pp. 193–195).

**Claim 22**: Pico's Q10 defense used supposition theory to argue that the priest's utterance supposits for Christ's original speech act, not for a present material reality — a sophisticated but entirely traditional semantic move (pp. 194–195).

## Technical Vocabulary

**Transubstantiation** (transsubstantiatio): The C-theory of eucharistic change; the bread's substance is changed into Christ's substance while the bread's accidents persist. Formally endorsed by Fourth Lateran (1215). Aquinas's preferred theory, aggressively defended against A and B alternatives.

**Impanation** (impanatio): The A-theory analog of incarnation: the Word "embreadments" itself by taking on breadness (paneitas) as a nature, just as in incarnation the Word took on humanness. Quidort's proposal; the analogy Pico's Q6 exploits.

**Paneitas** (breadness): The formal nature of bread as distinct from the bread's substance; the concept Quidort's A-theory required the Word to assume in eucharistic impanation. Appears in the Q6 thesis.

**Suppositatio** (taking-on or suppositing): The act by which a divine supposit takes on a created nature; the governing metaphysical concept in Q6. Pico used the term eighty-two times in Q6, almost always in the metaphysical sense.

**Suppositum**: A concrete subsistent individual in scholastic metaphysics; the "supposit" of the Word (second person of Trinity) that takes on natures by incarnation or eucharistic suppositing.

**Communicatio idiomatum** (sharing of properties): The theological doctrine that properties of Christ's human nature can be predicated of the divine person and vice versa, possible only within a single supposit; Quidort's A-theory needed this to work with one divine supposit on the altar.

**Species** (appearances): The sensory qualities (color, shape, taste, weight) of the bread that persist after consecration on all three theories; the standard term for what worshippers see on the altar.

**Conversio** (change/conversion): The general Aristotelian term for the transformation at consecration; in the C-theory, the bread's substance changes into Christ's substance.

**De possibili / de sic esse**: Pico's key modal distinction: statements about theoretical possibility versus assertoric statements about how things actually are. Central to his defense strategy throughout the Apology.

**Modalis divisa / modalis composita** (divided modal / composite modal): Logical distinction in supposition theory: a divided modal places the modal operator within the proposition ("an accident cannot exist without existing-in"), while a composite modal makes the dictum explicit as a clause ("that an accident exists without existing-in is impossible"). Pico invoked this in Q9 to explain why his thesis was a possibility-statement, not a flat assertion.

**Ampliation** (ampliatio): A division of supposition theory: a modal verb in a proposition "ampliates" the supposition of the subject term to cover not only actual items but also possible or future ones. Pico used this in Q9 to argue that "sacrament of the eucharist" in his thesis covered possible eucharists, not only the existing one.

**Fallacy of affirming the consequent**: The inference "if p then q; q; therefore p" — logically invalid. Pico accused his judges of committing this fallacy in Q9 by reasoning from the actual absence of accidents with subjects in the Eucharist to the impossibility of any possible eucharist with accidents having subjects.

**Materialiter / significative**: Terms from supposition theory: a word used materialiter refers to itself as a word (material supposition); used significative it stands for what it normally signifies (personal supposition). Q10's thesis: the hoc in hoc est corpus meum is used materially, not as a signifying demonstrative pointing at a referent.

**Recitative** (narrative): The mode in which a priest speaks the words of consecration — not making first-person assertions but narrating Christ's original speech act. Accepted by Quidort, Durandus, Scotus; rejected by Aquinas.

**Haecceitas** (thishereness): Scotus's term for individual difference; Quidort used it in his Determination (possibly borrowing from Scotus), which may have weakened his case by deploying a Franciscan technical term in a Dominican context.

**Circumscriptivus / definitivus**: The distinction between enclosing (locally placed) and delimiting (non-locally present) modes of being in place; invoked in Ockham's theory of Christ's presence in the Eucharist; later ridiculed by Hobbes.

**Assumptio** (taking-on): The metaphysical act by which a divine supposit assumes a created nature; key for both Christology (Word assumes human nature in incarnation) and eucharistic impanation (Word assumes breadness); appears in Hervé's digest of Thomas's C-theory as the move that Thomists deny and Quidort affirms for the eucharist.

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Copenhaver's method**: Close philological comparison of Pico's Q6 with Cabrol's Thomist synthesis and Quidort's *Determination* (via Martin 1975 / Pattin 1977 critical editions) to establish precise textual dependence — a deeply archival and textual philological value.

**Myth being tested**: The long-standing view that Pico was a reckless syncretist who inadvertently stumbled into heresy through excessive boldness. Copenhaver's argument: Pico was philosophically sophisticated and knew exactly what he was doing; his modal distinctions were legitimate scholastic moves. The heresy charges failed to engage with his actual argument.

**Historiographical intervention**: Copenhaver is the first scholar to document the direct Pico–Cabrol–Quidort textual chain in detail, correcting previous accounts that treated Pico's eucharistic position as a vague gesture toward "impanation." The identification of Cabrol as the actual intermediary source is original to this book.

**Concord outcome**: The chapter does not promote a "concordist" reading of Pico (unlike the approach to Kabbalah or Neoplatonism elsewhere in the scholarship). Instead, it argues for Pico as a *scholastic debater* who used the existing tradition's tools — modal logic, supposition theory, the legal openness of eucharistic doctrine — to protect himself. The move is less philosophical concord than legal and argumentative self-defense.

**Tone on the judges**: Consistently critical of the commission for ignoring Pico's modal distinctions; more sympathetic to the view that canon law genuinely left open the space Pico was exploiting.

## Relation to Pico Works

**900 Conclusions** (1486): Q6 (Conclusion 6 in the condemned set) and Q9, Q10 are the starting point; Copenhaver provides the Latin texts of both versions (Conclusions and Apology) with variants in footnotes. The Conclusions heading described this group of twenty-nine propositions as "my own opinion and quite different from the usual way of talking about theology" — which undercut Pico's later claim that they were mere explorations of possibility.

**Apology** (1487): Q6 (pp. 60–75 in the 1487 edition), Q9 (pp. 103–105), Q10 (pp. 105–106). Chapter 4 provides Copenhaver's English translations of Q9 and Q10 in full for the first time. The Latin text of Q9 requires emendation; Q10 is very short and harder to judge. Copenhaver's edition of the Apology forthcoming in the I Tatti Renaissance Library will supply full textual details.

**Oration** (1486): Referenced briefly as conspicuously silent on the incarnation — Pico did not foreground a theological doctrine that, Copenhaver suggests, would have complicated the dignity-of-man theme and was also technically difficult to integrate (pp. 165–166).

**Q4 thesis**: Cross-referenced repeatedly; Q4 and Q6 look contradictory at first (Q4 says only a reasoning nature can be taken on by God; Q6 defends taking-on of non-rational breadness). Copenhaver notes Pico never addressed this apparent conflict. The chapter's treatment of Q6's hypothetical modal structure ("if one takes the usual approach about possibility") is meant to show how this contradiction dissolves under careful reading.

## Uncertainties

1. **OCR issues**: The source file's page headers are garbled (e.g., "? 147", "'? 149" etc.) rather than proper chapter-section headers. These do not affect content but signal that the PDF-to-Markdown conversion degraded visual elements. No content appears to be missing.

2. **A/B/C-theory labeling in the OCR text**: Copenhaver's original text uses letters in a specific way for the three eucharistic theories (the OCR renders them inconsistently). The chapter uses "‐theory," "-theory," "-theory" in the printed text, apparently labeling them alphabetically. The reconstruction here (A = remaining, B = annihilating, C = transubstantiating) is based on context and appears reliable but was not explicitly confirmed by a complete consistent key in the text.

3. **Milo Crimi** (mentioned in footnote 86 as offering an interpretation of Q9's divided modal): Not otherwise identified in the chapter; possibly a research assistant or collaborator. Unclear if published work by this person exists.

4. **Cabrol manuscript vs. print**: The chapter cites both a 1483–4 print edition and the 1900–07 modern edition; the relationship between them is not explained. The details Copenhaver uses to establish textual dependence are reserved for the forthcoming I Tatti edition and not all provided here.

5. **Cardinal Pietro Capuano**: Identified by Copenhaver as providing the earliest clear three-way classification of eucharistic theories (p. 152), citing MS Munich, Staatsbibliothek, Cod. lat. 14508, fol. 60r. This is not a well-known identification in the historiography; Copenhaver appears to be making an original attribution. Confidence: high, but flagged as unverified against other scholarship.
