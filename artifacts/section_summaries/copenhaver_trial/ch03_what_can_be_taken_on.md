## Section Function

Chapter 3 is the first deep-dive case study in Copenhaver's prosecution-and-defense reconstruction. After Chapter 2 established the metaphysical vocabulary of *suppositum*, *natura*, person, and related terms, Chapter 3 applies that vocabulary to a single condemned thesis — Q4 — which concerned the Incarnation and the question of which natures God could "take on" (suppositare). The chapter has three interlocking purposes: (1) to trace the deep Christological and scholastic background (John of Damascus → Peter Lombard → Aquinas → Henry of Ghent → Capreolus) that Pico was drawing upon; (2) to expose the metaphysical content of Q4 — specifically Henry of Ghent's Quodlibet 13.5 — as philosophically serious and not heterodox; and (3) to vindicate Pico's epistemic strategy of "non-assent" (non assentior) as distinct from denial or heretical assertion. The chapter culminates in Copenhaver's own critical edition and translation of Q4, making it the primary textual-editorial contribution of this chapter.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

The chapter opens with a compressed history of Christological controversy from the Council of Nicaea (325) through the Council of Chalcedon (451) and the theology of John of Damascus (d. c.750), the last Greek theologian studied by medieval Latin authors. Copenhaver's purpose is to show that Pico's Q4 thesis is not an eccentric speculation but engages a problem constitutive of orthodox Latin theology: how can the divine Word, eternal and immaterial, "take on" (assumere/suppositare) a created nature in the Incarnation? The key Chalcedonian settlement — one divine person (the Son) with two natures, divine and human — generated a cascade of further questions: could the Father or Spirit have become incarnate? Could God have taken on a non-human, non-rational, or inanimate nature? Copenhaver shows that Peter Lombard's *Sentences* (bk. 3) canonized this question-space, and that Aquinas in both his *Scriptum on the Sentences* and the *Summa Theologiae* gave different answers to it, with the *Summa* restricting full incarnational "fitness" to human natures alone while the *Scriptum* allowed any creature under God's absolute power.

Into this established debate Copenhaver inserts Capreolus (Jean Cabrol, d. 1444), the fervent fifteenth-century Thomist whose *Defensiones* Pico demonstrably read. Capreolus argued that God could take on a stone, provided it bore at least a vestigial likeness to the divine. It is in Capreolus's discussion of this contested terrain that the unusual verb *suppositare* — "to make a supposit" — appears in the same cluster of Thomist arguments, linking Pico's choice of terminology to his scholastic reading program. Copenhaver documents that *suppo-* words appear twenty-seven times in Q4, eighteen of which are Pico's own (not borrowed from Henry), and that Pico's preference for the unusual *suppositare* over the standard *supponere* was a deliberate technical marker (pp. 106–107).

The chapter then moves to the translation problem. Five modern renderings of the Q4 thesis (Italian, French, and two German versions) differ materially on the key verb *suppositare*, producing "assume," "assume as form," "be the substantial subject," "appear," and "take on." Copenhaver's own rendering — "make any nature whatever the supposit" — preserves the factitive force that a parallel phrase at Q4.26 makes explicit: *substantificare et suppositare*, to make something a substance and a supposit.

The bulk of the chapter (sections 3.3 and 3.4) reconstructs Henry of Ghent's metaphysics as presented in *Quodlibet* 13.5. Copenhaver's argument here is that one cannot understand what Pico is doing in Q4 without working through Henry's elaborate metaphysical system, which Pico reproduced at length (Q4.5–23) as his primary evidence. Henry distinguished between three modes of being in created substances: existential (X), essential (E), and subsistent (S). The S element — a creature's self-standing ontic autonomy — is what makes a nature capable of being a "supposit." The nature/supposit pair is not a real distinction in creatures, Henry argued, but an intentional one: the supposit delimits the universal essence into a concrete particular without adding any real item to the composition. Crucially for Christology, when God takes on a nature by "co-envelopment" (circumincessio, his Latinization of John of Damascus's perichôrêsis), the human nature's S-being is displaced and replaced by the divine supposit's S-being, while the essential (E) and existential (X) being of the human nature are preserved. This is why Christ has two natures but one supposit.

Henry's key move — his "change of mind" (p. 111) — was to argue that only intellectual natures (rational souls, angels, and by subordination the bodies they animate) can be taken on. His argument proceeds by distinguishing two modes of God's presence in creatures: the natural inflow (illapsus, α) whereby God sustains every creature, and the supernatural co-envelopment (circumincessio, β) by which God takes on a nature. The α-presence has two natural acts: α1 for passive persistence and α2 for active existence. The β-presence also requires two supernatural acts: β1, the nature's subsistence in a divine supposit, and β2, the beatific activity of seeing and loving God. Henry's key principle, drawn from Aristotle (*Meteorologica* 4), is that being exists only for the sake of action. A creature that cannot reach β2 — the beatific vision — cannot be elevated to β1. Only intellectual natures (those with potency for intellect and will) have the capacity for β2, and therefore only they can undergo β1. Non-intellectual natures are excluded not because of any failure of divine omnipotence, but because of their own "ugly neediness" (turpis indigentia), as Pico summarizes Henry's position at Q4.27 (p. 128).

Henry also addressed the hard case of the "triduum" — the three days Christ's body lay in the tomb after the Crucifixion. During this period, the body was separated from the rational soul; was the ensouled body a divine supposit in those three days, implying God can take on an unreasoning nature? Henry's answer, reproduced by Pico, is that the body during the triduum was subordinated to an intellectual soul even when separated from it, and the soul's rational substance mediated for the body, so the composite retained an intellectual character. Pico adds his own distinction (Q4.28–29): the restriction on taking-on applies only to what God takes on primarily and as a whole capable creature. The triduum body was taken on only as a part of the whole that was primarily taken on (human nature in full); it was never "primarily" assumed in the required sense. He also argues that delimiting the dependence of a bodily part by the whole body does not make that part a supposit — only the eternal sharing of the human property φ between the Word and the human nature of Jesus constitutes genuine suppositing, whereas the triduum body merely shared the contingent property ψ ("lying in the tomb") by synecdoche.

Copenhaver's section 3.5 ("Pico's Henry") reads Pico's deployment of this material as a defense strategy. Pico presented Henry's change of mind as a "tu quoque" gambit redesigned as an "illi quoque" — his judges could not condemn him for holding a position that Henry of Ghent, "the Stately Doctor," had also held and that Scotus, Godfrey of Fontaines, Bernard de Gannat, Durand, Thomas the Englishman, Robert of Collotorto, and John of Ware had all engaged without calling it heretical (Q4.24, p. 127). The chapter's final analytical move is the most philosophically precise: Pico's Q4 thesis is framed as non assentior — "I do not assent" — not as "I deny" or "I assert the opposite." Copenhaver argues at length (section 3.2) that this epistemic framing was not a post-hoc cover invented under fire. The Q4 thesis was written the same way in the original Conclusions, and the assenting/asserting distinction was a philosophically defensible one. Refusing to assent to proposition r does not entail asserting not-r. A sleeping person or a small child does not assent to "God can make any nature the supposit," yet no one would call them heretics. The court's verdict, that the Q4 thesis "in virtue of its language" (de virtute sermonis / vi sermonis) detracted from God's omnipotence, simply confuses non-assent with denial. Copenhaver takes seriously Pico's logical rebuttal: the judges were either incompetent (they confused the logical relationship) or malevolent (they knew better and acted in bad faith). The chapter closes with Pico's rhetorical flourish about "donkey theology" — the scandalous implications of the opposing view, that God could become a donkey, a stick, be damned to Hell, or be identified with the Devil (Q4.33) — turning the scandal argument against his accusers.

Copenhaver's historiographical intervention is to insist that Q4 is not anti-rational mysticism or reckless speculation, but a rigorous scholastic defense engaged with the most technical philosophical theology of the Latin tradition. Pico's use of Henry of Ghent is not ornamental: he mastered Henry's metaphysical arguments well enough to summarize them accurately, to add his own refinements on the triduum case, and to deploy them strategically before a hostile court.

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: The Chalcedonian settlement (451) defined Christ as one person with two natures, divine and human, establishing the constitutive problem of Christological metaphysics that all subsequent Latin theology navigated. (pp. 100–101)

**Claim 2**: John of Damascus's *Fountain of Knowledge* (d. c.750) was the last Greek theological text studied by Latin medievals and became a major authority on Christology via Peter Lombard's *Sentences*, bk. 3. His concept of perichôrêsis ("going around"), Latinized as circumincessio, was the key term for describing the interpenetration of divine and human natures in Christ. (pp. 101–106)

**Claim 3**: Peter Lombard's *Sentences* (bk. 3) canonized the question of what God could have taken on in the Incarnation — explicitly raising the cases of women, other humans, and non-human beings — and authorized continued speculation about "modes of incarnation." (pp. 101–102)

**Claim 4**: Aquinas gave different answers in the *Scriptum on the Sentences* vs. the *Summa Theologiae*: the *Scriptum* allowed that under absolute power God could take on any creature; the *Summa* restricted fitness to human nature alone on grounds of "rank" (human rationality) and "need" (original sin requiring redemption). (pp. 104–105)

**Claim 5**: Capreolus (Jean Cabrol, c.1380–1444) reverted to the *Scriptum*'s more permissive position and argued God could take on a stone, using *suppositare* among other *suppo-* words. Pico demonstrably read Capreolus, establishing the source of his unusual term *suppositare*. (pp. 105–106)

**Claim 6**: The Q4 thesis in the *Conclusions* was already framed as non assentior ("I do not assent"), not as a doctrinal assertion — this was not invented under fire for the *Apology* but reflects Pico's original careful framing. (pp. 110–111, 133)

**Claim 7**: Five modern translations of the Q4 thesis render *suppositare* in materially different ways, showing that the central technical term of the thesis has not been understood by its scholarly translators. Copenhaver's reading — "make any nature whatever the supposit" (factitive) — is supported by the parallel Q4.26: *substantificare et suppositare*. (pp. 107–108)

**Claim 8**: Henry of Ghent's *Quodlibet* 13.5 constitutes the largest single presence in Pico's Q4 — larger than Pico's own contributions — yet the Conclusions listed Henry's theses without mentioning the incarnation at all, and Henry appears nowhere in the Conclusions' Q4 thesis entry. Henry thus entered Q4 specifically as a defense resource. (pp. 108–109)

**Claim 9**: The printed 1487 text of Q4 (the *Apology*) contains more than two dozen errors in Henry of Ghent's Quodlibet excerpt, rendering it sometimes untranslatable. These errors result from scribal or typesetter corruption, not from Pico's ignorance of Henry; Pico's composition of Q4 shows he understood Henry's arguments before the damage was done. (pp. 108–110)

**Claim 10**: Henry of Ghent's metaphysics distinguishes three elements of created being — essential (E), existential (X), subsistent (S) — with the S-element (the supposit) differing from the nature "not really but only in intention." This intentional (not real) distinction grounds the Christological claim that in the Incarnation the divine supposit can replace the human S-being while the E and X of human nature remain. (pp. 114–117)

**Claim 11**: Henry's key argument is that being exists only for the sake of action (Aristotle, *Meteorologica* 4): a nature that cannot reach the second supernatural act (β2 — beatific vision of God) cannot be elevated to the first (β1 — subsistence in a divine supposit). Only intellectual natures have the potency for β2, so only they are suppressible (suppositabiles) in the theological sense. (pp. 121–124)

**Claim 12**: Henry's analysis of the triduum (Christ's body in the tomb for three days) concluded that the body, even separated from its soul, remained intellectual by subordination to the soul that mediated for it, and could not count as evidence that God takes on unreasoning natures primarily. (pp. 122–124)

**Claim 13**: Pico's own contribution in Q4.28–30 was a philosophical refinement beyond Henry: the triduum body was taken on only as a part (not as a whole primarily assumed creature), and "delimiting a body's dependence" by the Word does not constitute making it a supposit in the strict sense. The human nature shares the property "human" (φ) with Christ eternally; the triduum body shared only the contingent property "lying in the tomb" (ψ) semantically by synecdoche. (pp. 128–130)

**Claim 14**: The papal commission's verdict — that Q4 "in virtue of its language" (vi sermonis) detracts from God's omnipotence — commits a basic logical error: failing to assent to r does not entail asserting not-r. Pico identified four errors in the commission's reasoning: (1) confusing non-credere with discredere; (2) ignoring Henry's stature; (3) if omnipotence was genuinely insulted, the thesis was fully heretical (not merely "suggestive"), so the charge itself was incoherent; (4) condemning the thesis de virtute sermonis when its language does not entail Henry's view. (pp. 132–135)

**Claim 15**: The "donkey theology" examples — "God is a donkey," "God is a stick," "God can be damned to Hell," "the Devil can be God" — were not hysteria but actual test-cases produced by logicians exploring absolute power. The opposing view (that God can take on any nature) generates these statements; Pico's restriction avoids them, making his thesis less scandalous than what his accusers preferred. (pp. 131–132)

## Technical Vocabulary

**suppositare** (factitive verb): "to make [something] a supposit"; Pico's preferred unusual verb over *supponere*; used 18 times in Q4 in Pico's own text; the key term in the Q4 thesis; factitive meaning confirmed by parallel *substantificare et suppositare* at Q4.26. Copenhaver: "to make any nature whatever the supposit."

**suppositum**: the concrete, individuated, self-standing bearer of a nature; in Christology, the single divine supposit (the second person of the Trinity) who has both divine and human natures; in Henry's metaphysics, the S-element of metaphysical composition in creatures.

**natura (U vs. P)**: universal nature (humanness as shared by all humans) vs. particular nature (Socrates's own humanness); in the Incarnation, Christ's particular human nature (P) is taken on by the divine supposit.

**perichôrêsis / circumincessio**: Greek / Latin term for the mutual "going around" or interpenetration of divine persons in the Trinity, and of the two natures in Christ; John of Damascus's key term, Latinized awkwardly as *circumincessio* by Western translators; in Henry of Ghent, the supernatural β-mode of divine presence in a nature taken on (contrasted with natural *illapsus*).

**illapsus / α-presence**: God's natural "inflow" sustaining every creature; supports α1 (passive persistence) and α2 (active existence); contrasted with β-presence (co-envelopment).

**circumincessio / β-presence**: God's supernatural co-envelopment of a nature taken on; enables β1 (subsistence in a divine supposit) and β2 (beatific vision); available only to intellectual natures.

**esse subsistentiae (S-being)**: subsistent being; the self-standing ontic autonomy of a creature; in the Incarnation, the human nature's S-being is displaced/absorbed by the divine supposit's S-being.

**esse essentiae (E) / esse existentiae (X)**: essential being (what a thing is) / existential being (that it is); both are preserved in the human nature of Christ even as the S-being is taken over by the divine supposit.

**intentional distinction**: Henry's technical term for the difference between a nature (E) and its supposit (S) in creatures; more than a merely conceptual/mental distinction, less than a real distinction; what makes "Socrates is humanness" false while "God is godness" true.

**assumptibilis / suppositabilis**: "able to be taken on" / "able to be made a supposit"; technical terms for creaturely capacity in the incarnation debate.

**potentia obedientialis**: "obediential potency"; Aquinas's term for a creature's passive capacity to receive whatever God produces in it, not from natural capability; mentioned in the background of the debate on taking-on.

**de virtute sermonis / vi sermonis**: "in virtue of its language"; the court's standard for finding that a thesis "suggests heresy" — not that it asserts heresy outright, but that by strict reading of its terms it implies something heterodox. Pico disputed that his words, strictly read, entailed any denial of omnipotence.

**non assentior**: "I do not assent"; the first-person epistemic framing of Q4 thesis, distinguishing it from every other condemned thesis in the Apology; Copenhaver argues this was not a tactical dodge but a philosophically precise epistemic stance.

**triduum**: the three days Christ's body lay in the tomb (Good Friday to Easter Sunday); the hard theological case for the Q4 debate, because the body was then separated from the rational soul; key test for whether God can take on an unreasoning nature.

**beatific vision / beatiﬁca operatio**: the eternal vision and love of God enjoyed by blessed souls in heaven; in Henry's metaphysics, the second supernatural act (β2) for which an intellectual nature must have potency in order to be taken on.

**communicatio idiomatum**: "sharing of properties"; the semantic doctrine that properties of Christ's human nature can be predicated of the divine person and vice versa; used by Pico to explain how "Christ is in the tomb" could be true by synecdoche during the triduum.

**res naturae**: Henry's term for "the thing of which the nature is the nature" — the concrete supposit as distinct from the abstract universal nature (quidditas).

**quod est / quo est**: "what is" / "by which it is" — Henry's formula for the metaphysical composite of supposit and nature in creatures.

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Guide scholar**: Copenhaver functions as a philosopher-historian: his primary loyalty is to the precision of scholastic philosophical theology, not to humanist narrative or Renaissance intellectual biography. He reads Q4 as a piece of rigorous philosophical argument embedded in a legal crisis, not as a document of Pico's "Renaissance individualism" or his supposed synthesis of all philosophies.

**Myth being tested**: The chapter implicitly contests two rival myths: (1) the myth of Pico as reckless syncretist who offended the Church by combining Christianity with Kabbalah and Neoplatonism — Q4 is entirely internal to Latin scholastic theology with no kabbalistic content; (2) the myth of Pico as an early martyr for intellectual freedom whose condemnation was arbitrary persecution — Copenhaver shows both that the judges had intelligible grounds for concern and that Pico had a valid philosophical defense, making the case genuinely contested rather than merely a power-play.

**Historiographical register**: Copenhaver writes against the tradition (implicit in many Renaissance studies) of treating Pico's theological theses as incidental to his "real" Neoplatonist or kabbalist agenda. For Q4, he argues that Pico was a careful scholastic philosopher engaging one of the deepest problems in Latin theological metaphysics.

**Concord outcome**: None. Q4 involves no Pico-characteristic syncretist gesture toward Plato or Kabbalah. It is a purely internal debate within Christian scholastic theology about Christological metaphysics. Copenhaver's rendering of Q4 as serious philosophy within the tradition is itself a kind of rehabilitation, but it is rehabilitation through technical rigor, not through grand claims about Pico's concord project.

## Relation to Pico Works

**Pico, *Conclusiones* (900 Theses), 1486**: Q4 thesis stated there in the same non assentior form; two theses assigned to Aquinas (one on the Trinity "suppositating" a nature; one on designated matter as principle of individuation) but Henry of Ghent absent from the Conclusions' Q4 entry. Six scholastics listed, but Henry's thirteen theses there concern angels, demons, illumination, relations, and Trinity — not incarnation.

**Pico, *Apologia conclusionum suarum*, 1487**: Q4 (Apo. pp. 41–47; Afr. pp. 134–152) is the primary text analyzed throughout the chapter. Copenhaver's translation at pp. 136–144 includes silent emendations to the corrupt 1487 edition. The Q4 defense is structured as: (§§1–4) the epistemic case for non-assentior framing; (§§5–23) extended excerpt from Henry's Qdl. 13.5; (§§24–31) Pico's summary and defense of Henry; (§§32–35) rhetorical and logical arguments against judges; (§§36–39) four-point rebuttal of the court's verdict. Total length c.3,500 words; the Henry excerpt occupies over half.

**Trial transcript** (Dorez and Thuasne, 1897, pp. 124, 129): Surviving record of Pico's oral response to the commission and the court's formal verdict, both quoted at length in section 3.5; used by Pico himself in Q4.35 to structure his four-point rebuttal.

## Updates Existing Writing

No conflicts with existing essays noted from the read material. However, if any existing PicoDB content characterizes Pico's condemned theses primarily in terms of Kabbalah, Neoplatonism, or humanist provocation, Chapter 3 is a direct correction: Q4 is a purely scholastic-theological argument with no kabbalistic or Neoplatonist content. It engages Henry of Ghent, Capreolus, John of Damascus, Peter Lombard, and Aquinas — not Plotinus, Ficino, or Jewish mystical sources.

If any existing content describes Pico as simply "denying God's omnipotence" in Q4, this is the misreading that Copenhaver's chapter refutes at length. Pico withheld assent to a particular statement about omnipotence; he never asserted its denial.

## Uncertainties

1. **Textual corruption in Q4**: More than two dozen errors in the 1487 printed text of Q4 are acknowledged by Copenhaver, who silently emends the translation. The full critical apparatus is deferred to the forthcoming ITRL edition. Any quotation from the 1487 text of Q4.5–23 should be treated as potentially corrupt; Copenhaver's translation is more reliable than the printed Latin.

2. **Henry of Ghent's Qdl. 13.5 text**: The edition used is Jos Decorte (1985); Copenhaver notes at Q4.18 a specific case where text missing from the 1487 Apology is recoverable from Decorte's edition. Other lacunae in the Pico text may be recoverable from this modern edition but are not individually flagged in chapter 3 (reserved for ITRL).

3. **Pico's seven named allies at Q4.24**: Copenhaver identifies Scotus, Godfrey of Fontaines, Bernard de Gannat, Durand, Thomas the Englishman (Thomas Sutton), and offers probable identifications for the remaining two as William (John) of Ware and Robert Orford (of Collotorto or Colethorpe), marked as "probably" (p. 127, n. 74). These identifications are tentative.

4. **Capreolus as direct source**: The inference that Pico read Capreolus is based on the presence of *suppositare* in both texts and Pico's known scholastic training, but no direct documentary evidence (e.g., a citation in the Conclusions or Apology) is provided; this is a highly plausible inference but not demonstrated proof.

5. **OCR quality in source markdown**: The source file is a machine-converted PDF. The Greek terms (perichôrêsis, phusis, prosôpon) and logical symbols used in Copenhaver's text (the Greek letters α, β with numerical superscripts for act-types) may be rendered inconsistently or incorrectly in the markdown. Any quotation of Greek or symbolic notation from this source should be verified against the print edition.
