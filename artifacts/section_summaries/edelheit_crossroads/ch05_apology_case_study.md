## Section Function

Chapter 5 opens Part 2 of Edelheit's book ("Scholastic Traces and Influence") and functions as its methodological centrepiece. Where Part 1 established Pico's general non-dogmatic, inclusive method and his positioning between Thomists, Scotists, and humanists, Chapter 5 tests that thesis against a single rich primary text — the *Apology* of 1487 — to demonstrate concretely how deep and wide Pico's scholastic competence actually was. The chapter argues that the *Apology*, composed under pressure of the papal commission's condemnation of 13 of the 900 Theses, forced Pico to deploy scholastic methods and authorities in full-scale argumentative form, making it the clearest surviving evidence of his scholastic formation. The chapter functions simultaneously as a document study (demonstrating what the *Apology* contains and how it is structured), a reception history (tracing which scholastic masters Pico read carefully and drew on), and a polemical reframing (arguing that modern scholarship's neglect of the *Apology* is itself a symptom of the "humanist Pico" bias that Edelheit seeks to overturn).

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

Edelheit opens by contrasting the 900 Theses, which he calls "a frozen and somewhat silent monument," with the *Apology*, which is a "dynamic" exercise in scholastic discourse. The *Apology* is dynamic because the papal commission's condemnation forced Pico to provide full-scale arguments — not bare theses — in defense of each of the 13 condemned propositions. This is why the *Apology* reveals Pico's scholastic competence far more transparently than the theses alone. Edelheit's first structural observation is that Fornaciari's decision to call the 13 sections *quaestiones* (rather than *disputationes*) is entirely justified: the text follows the logic of the scholastic *quaestio disputata*, with Pico presenting arguments, counter-arguments (*sed contra*), and resolutions in the standard manner, while also using the text rhetorically against his accusers (pp. 149–150).

Pico's broader method, already described in Chapter 4, is again summarized: he refuses to be a committed Thomist or Scotist, follows no one dogmatically, and adopts an inclusive approach that brings a broader range of authorities into view than either scholastics or humanists typically entertained (pp. 151–152). Within the *Apology* this method manifests as an extraordinarily wide roster of scholastic interlocutors.

The chapter then proceeds through four extended case studies, each keyed to a specific question in the *Apology*:

**Question 5 (De magia naturali et cabala Hebraeorum, pp. 154–192 of the Apology).** Edelheit uses this question to demonstrate how Pico applies standard scholastic distinctions — especially the distinction between *scientiae humanitatus inventae* (humanly invented sciences, including natural magic and Kabbalah as parts of natural science and revealed theology respectively) — to topics that appear exotic but are handled in fully orthodox scholastic terms. Pico's defense includes a *sed contra* move, an etymological digression on *magi*, a biblical reference (the Magi of Matthew), and the recruitment of William of Auvergne, Robert Grosseteste, and Albert the Great as patristic and scholastic precedents for natural magic. Albert in particular is described as a "most sacred and Catholic man" who personally practiced natural magic — a characterization Edelheit notes is consistent with how Pico treats Albert in the dedicated theses discussed in Chapter 6. Kabbalah is similarly defended by citing five Christian witnesses (Ezra, Paul, Origen, Hilary, and the Gospel) before combining them with Jewish sources (pp. 153–154).

**Question 1 (De descensu Christi ad inferos, pp. 34–104 of the Apology).** Edelheit argues at length that this first question — on whether Christ descended into hell as a soul, and in what sense He was present there — is the richest single demonstration of Pico's scholastic mastery. The discussion focuses on Pico's extensive use of Henry of Ghent, but Edelheit situates Henry within a much larger web of interlocutors. The question's four-part structure follows classical quaestio form: first, Pico's own position and its orthodoxy; second, a defense of the thesis's wording as not heretical; third, an attack on the commissioners as themselves erring in faith; fourth, a further attack demonstrating that their condemnation was rash (pp. 154–155).

Pico's substantive philosophical position is that Christ was present in hell only through His *operatio* (operation or effect), not through His substance. Against the Scotist view — that the *finitio* and *limitatio* of an essence is what grounds a separated substance's presence in a place — Pico argues (following Henry of Ghent's Quodlibet II, q. 9) that for incorporeal substances, just as a corporeal substance is in a place through its *quantitas* (which is external to its essence), so an incorporeal substance is in a place through its *operatio* (which is an effect transitioning outward). Edelheit provides detailed parallel quotations between Pico's *Apology* and Henry's Quodlibet text to show how carefully and accurately Pico followed Henry, sometimes pushing Henry slightly further than Henry himself was willing to go (pp. 156–163). He also shows that Henry's frank admissions that he does not know how to explain certain connections (his characteristic non-dogmatic agnosticism) were precisely what Pico needed: if even so authoritative a thinker as Henry could not resolve these questions, then the papal commission had no business calling Pico heretical for proposing them as matters of theological speculation only.

Related to this is Pico's use of Henry's Quodlibet VII, q. 9 (on the double limitation of created beings) and his working through the abstraction problem via the concepts of *unitas* and *punctus*: a point belongs to quantity and has a natural position; an incorporeal substance is completely abstracted from position. Pico cites Henry on the impossibility of understanding an angel's relation to place on the model of a point (pp. 159–162), and then turns Henry's description of those who cannot think beyond the imagination (the *melancholici* who make good mathematicians but are *inepti* as natural philosophers) against his critics, adding — in a move that goes beyond Henry's text — that such critics are worst of all at theology (p. 162).

Edelheit shows that Thomas Aquinas is also a complex presence in this question. Pico is a careful critical reader who identifies development and inconsistency within Thomas's corpus: in Sentences 1, Thomas seems to agree with Pico that operation grounds presence; in Sentences 4 and the Summa's third part, Thomas seems to side with the Scotists. Erring about Thomas's view, Pico insists, is not the same as erring in faith (pp. 167–168). Durandus of Saint-Pourçain is then deployed to show that even the "wrong" interpretation of Pico's thesis — that Christ was in hell only *effectualiter* and not *praesentialiter* — was held as a probable and defensible view by a celebrated Dominican theologian whose books are read daily in Dominican schools (pp. 168–170). The recruitment of Guido Terrena (a Carmelite) and William of Ockham at the end of the question extends the network further, with Ockham cited positively (unusually for Pico) on the principle that ambiguous propositions must be understood by the *intentio* of the author rather than by common usage (pp. 170–172).

**Question 3 (De adoratione crucis et imaginum, pp. 120–132 of the Apology).** Edelheit uses this question to illustrate how Thomas Aquinas, for Pico, is one authority among many and one that can be explicitly found less reasonable than others. Pico declares that Thomas's position on the adoration of images seems "less reasonable" to him than the position of Durandus, Henry of Ghent, William of Ware, Robert Holcot, and Peter of Aquila. The argumentative structure is again a standard scholastic dialectic: first, Thomas's view is presented (*sed contra*); then the doctors Pico follows are presented; and the commission's condemnation is shown to rest on a misunderstanding. Edelheit traces the specific doctrine — that images should be honoured *per accidens* and *rememorative* (by accident and in a commemorative manner) — through Durandus, Holcot, Henry, and Francis of Mayronnes (pp. 172–175). The particular nuance that the humanity of Christ should be adorned only by *iperdulia* and not by *latria*, on which Henry, William of Ware, and all the Scotists agree, illustrates how Pico can invoke Scotist and non-Scotist authorities together (p. 173).

**Question 4 and 6 (on God's assumption of a non-rational nature, and on the Eucharist).** Edelheit traces Pico's use of Henry of Ghent's Quodlibet XIII, q. 5 (on whether the Word could assume a non-intellectual nature) in Question 4, where Henry is shown to hold a *more* radical position than Pico — denying that God can assume any kind but a rational nature — thereby making the commission's condemnation of Pico's weaker position self-defeating (pp. 162–164). In Question 6 on the Eucharist, Edelheit reads Pico's long technical debates on *assumptio*, *conversio*, and *annihilatio* as further evidence of full scholastic competence: Pico engages with Thomas Aquinas, Durandus, Peter of Palude, and John of Damascus in detail, weighing their positions critically, sometimes agreeing with one citation from Thomas against another, sometimes leaving philosophical problems explicitly unresolved. Pico's own methodological remark — that in matters of faith, the view more consonant with Apostolic tradition and Church teaching should be preferred even if it creates more theoretical difficulties — is identified by Edelheit as genuinely scholastic in spirit: this is the standard mode of scholastic discourse, where *auctoritas* and *ratio* operate in tension (pp. 174–182).

**Question 8 (De libertate credendi).** The chapter closes with Pico's eighth question, where Pierre d'Ailly, Enrico Totting da Oyta, Henry of Langenstein, and Robert Holcot are recruited on the question of whether believing is a free act. Pico deploys Holcot's unusual view that the act of believing is meritorious not because it is free but because it conforms to divine law which promises a reward, and that God could alter that law — but then Pico explicitly distances himself from Holcot's view while using Holcot's prominence to turn the condemnation back on the commissioners (pp. 183–185).

The chapter ends with Edelheit's summary formulation: Henry of Ghent, Durandus of Saint-Pourçain, and Robert Holcot are Pico's most frequently cited scholastic masters in the *Apology*; together they constitute what Edelheit calls Pico's "scholastic shield" — a rich, diverse, non-Thomist and non-Scotist scholastic arsenal that demonstrates the existence of a far wider tradition than the two dominant schools of the late fifteenth century, and that Pico uses to show the papal commission's condemnations are theologically indefensible (pp. 185).

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: The *Apology* is the clearest evidence of Pico's "great familiarity with, and competence in, the scholastic methods and practices from the thirteenth century up to his own time." (p. 149)

**Claim 2**: Pico functions as a *speculum scholasticorum* — a mirror of scholastic thinkers — whose reading of the scholastic tradition provides "a unique insight into the ways in which philosophy … was in a state of profound transition at the end of the fifteenth century." (p. 149)

**Claim 3**: Modern scholarly neglect of the *Apology* is itself a sign of the "general scholarly negligence of the important dialogue between Pico and the scholastic tradition," a pattern that began with the 1532 editors who omitted much of the text because Pico "tantum scholastice cum adversariis agit." (p. 149, n. 1)

**Claim 4**: Fornaciari's editorial decision to call the 13 sections *quaestiones* rather than *disputationes* is justified, since Pico himself refers to his project as a *scholastica exercitatio* and the *Apology* functions as a set of *quaestiones disputatae*. (pp. 149–150)

**Claim 5**: Pico's method — "committed to know the doctrines of all the schools but he follows no one" — enabled him to "go beyond the two prominent discursive strategies of his time: the scholastic and the humanist," creating a new inclusive discourse. (pp. 151–152)

**Claim 6**: The distinction between *scientiae humanitatus inventae* and revealed theology that Pico applies to natural magic and Kabbalah in Question 5 is "a very standard scholastic distinction." (p. 153)

**Claim 7**: Pico's scholastic mastery is most visible in Question 1 (*De descensu Christi ad inferos*), which together with Question 7 on Origen is the longest in the Apology. (p. 154)

**Claim 8**: Henry of Ghent is "only one among a whole host of scholastic thinkers" cited in the *Apology*, but his authority is particularly strategic for Pico because Henry was among the theologians who advised Étienne Tempier in 1277, making him the authority for Proposition 204 of the Paris condemnations. (pp. 157, 163)

**Claim 9**: Pico follows Henry's argument in Quodlibet II, q. 9 "quite accurately" up to the point where Henry declines to determine a positive answer, but "seems to push Henry a bit towards his own position" when Henry is still "very cautious and uncommitted." (p. 157)

**Claim 10**: Henry's "non dogmatic and inclusive approach, combined with a flavour of skepticism" was precisely what Pico needed, and Henry's frank admissions of ignorance were strategically recruited as evidence that these questions fall outside the domain of necessary truth of faith. (pp. 157–158)

**Claim 11**: Pico is a critical reader of Thomas who identifies developmental inconsistencies within Thomas's corpus; "Thomas for Pico is not an authority but rather an author and one source among many." (pp. 167–168)

**Claim 12**: Erring about Thomas's opinion is not the same as erring in faith, and inconsistency in Thomas's followers is evidence that condemnation of Pico's thesis on this basis is unfounded. (p. 168)

**Claim 13**: Durandus of Saint-Pourçain's continued use in Dominican schools — despite his disagreements with Thomas — shows that even the "wrong" interpretation of Pico's thesis was defensible by a major scholastic authority, and accusing Pico of heresy on this basis would necessarily make Durandus a posthumous heretic, which is absurd. (pp. 168–170)

**Claim 14**: Pico's positive citations of Ockham in Question 1 "should be compared to other references to the 'moderns' found in Pico's works, most of which tend to be negative and highly critical." (pp. 171–172)

**Claim 15**: In Question 3, Pico explicitly finds Thomas's position on the adoration of images "less reasonable" than those of Durandus, Henry of Ghent, William of Ware, Holcot, and Peter of Aquila — demonstrating that Thomas is not treated as a supreme authority. (p. 172)

**Claim 16**: In Question 4, Henry of Ghent holds a *more extreme* position than Pico (denying God can assume any but rational nature), so that the commission's condemnation of Pico's milder restriction would logically condemn Henry too. (pp. 162–164)

**Claim 17**: Pico's methodological remark in Question 6 — that in matters of faith one should prefer the view more consonant with Apostolic tradition even if it creates more theoretical difficulties — reflects genuine scholastic epistemological priorities. (pp. 181–182)

**Claim 18**: Henry of Ghent, Durandus of Saint-Pourçain, and Robert Holcot together form Pico's "scholastic shield," reflecting the richness and diversity of the scholastic tradition beyond the Thomist–Scotist binary. (p. 185)

**Claim 19**: Henry of Ghent's Summa, art. I, q. 2, "must be considered as an important source for late medieval, Renaissance, and Early-Modern skepticism," where Cicero's *Academica* and Augustine's *Contra Academicos* are widely cited. (p. 164)

**Claim 20**: The Scotists and Nominalists are sometimes cited together by Pico, meaning that the categories *via antiqua* and *via moderna* must be used "very cautiously" in fifteenth-century contexts. (p. 155)

## Technical Vocabulary

**Quaestio disputata**: Formal scholastic debate format (question, arguments, counter-argument, determination); Edelheit argues the *Apology* follows this structure despite being written as a self-defense. (pp. 149–150)

**Scholastica exercitatio**: Pico's own term for the enterprise of the 900 Theses and the *Apology*; "scholastic exercise." (pp. 149–150, and Apology p. 364)

**Scientiae humanitatus inventae**: Humanly invented sciences (as opposed to revealed theology); the scholastic category Pico uses to situate natural magic and Kabbalah. (p. 153)

**Speculum scholasticorum**: "Mirror of scholastic thinkers"; Edelheit's phrase for Pico's function in the *Apology*. (p. 149)

**Sed contra**: Standard scholastic rhetorical move (presenting the opposing argument before offering a refutation); Edelheit identifies it in Question 5 and Question 3 of the Apology. (pp. 152, 171)

**Definitive / commensurative**: Technical terms for two modes of local presence. A corporeal substance is in a place *commensurative* (proportionately, through its quantity); an incorporeal substance can be in a place *definitive* (definitively, in a bounded manner not through quantity but through operation or nature). The Scotist position is that a separated substance's *finitio* and *limitatio* grounds its *definitive* presence; Pico's position (following Henry) is that *operatio* grounds it instead. (pp. 155–157, 166)

**Finitio / limitatio / determinatio**: Scotist vocabulary for the intrinsic determination of a substance's essence that grounds its presence in a place; Pico explicitly rejects this account. (p. 155)

**Operatio / effectus**: The competing account (Pico's, following Henry and Durandus): it is through operation or effect, not substance, that an incorporeal being is in a place. (pp. 155–157)

**Situs naturalis / situs mathematicus**: Henry of Ghent's distinction between natural position (physical) and mathematical position (abstract); Pico follows this in his abstraction argument. (p. 158)

**Unitas / punctus**: A point belongs to the category of quantity and has a position (*situs*); a unity (and by analogy an angel or soul) is more abstract than any quantity and has no position. Pico uses the contrast to defeat the objection that an incorporeal substance must be somewhere as a point is. (pp. 159–162)

**Adoratio latriae / iperdulia / adoratio per accidens / rememorative**: A hierarchy of worship in Question 3. *Latria* = supreme adoration due to God alone; *iperdulia* = the special veneration due to Christ's humanity; *per accidens* and *rememorative* = the way in which images should be honoured, by accident and commemoratively rather than in themselves. (pp. 172–175)

**Assumptio / conversio / annihilatio / transubstantiatio**: Alternative explanations of what happens to the bread in the Eucharist. Pico discusses *assumptio* (the bread's substance is assumed by the divine supposite), *conversio* (it is converted into Christ's body), and *annihilatio* (it is annihilated); he favors *conversio* as more consonant with Apostolic tradition. *Transubstantiatio* (transubstantiation, the Thomist term) is treated as a form of *conversio*. *Communicatio idiomatum* (communication of properties) is also discussed. (pp. 174–181)

**Paneitas**: "Breadness" — the substance of the bread as distinct from its accidents; a technical term in the Eucharist debate, used by Peter of Palude and discussed by Durandus. (pp. 177, 180)

**Via antiqua / via moderna**: The traditional distinction between "old way" (Thomism, Scotism, realism) and "new way" (Nominalism, Ockhamism); Edelheit notes that in Pico the Scotists and Nominalists can appear on the same side of a debate, which requires care in applying these labels. (pp. 155, 172)

**Diaspora ton philosophon / diaphonia ton philosophon**: "Disagreement between philosophers" (the classical skeptical argument from disagreement); Pico uses it in Question 1 to argue that disagreements among patristic writers show that none of them speaks with infallible truth. (p. 170)

**Doctor subtilis / doctor subtilissimus**: Pico is described as aiming at the level of Scotus ("doctor subtilis") — Edelheit calls Pico *doctor subtilissimus*. (p. 165)

**Sed contra**: See above. Also: the structure of Question 3's defense turns on a classic *sed contra* move where Thomas's position is presented first, then overridden by a majority of doctors. (pp. 152, 171–172)

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Historiographical tradition**: Edelheit belongs to the revisionist strand of Renaissance philosophy scholarship that challenges the exclusively humanist framing of Pico inherited from Eugenio Garin. More specifically, he argues for a "third way" between the "humanist Pico" and older confessional or neo-Thomist frameworks: Pico as a sophisticated, non-dogmatic scholastic practitioner whose method is irreducible to either humanist philology or school philosophy. Edelheit aligns himself with scholars like Sebastiano Gentile and Michael Allen who have attended to Pico's philosophical formation in detail, and against Garin and those influenced by him who emphasized the *Oration* and the Neoplatonic dimension to the exclusion of the scholastic.

**Myth being tested**: The "humanist Pico" — the idea that Pico's significance lies primarily in his humanism (especially as represented by the *Oration*), his Neoplatonism (especially through Ficino), and his concord of Plato and Aristotle, with the scholastic element treated as scaffolding to be discarded. Edelheit tests this myth by showing that the *Apology* — the text that the 1532 editors removed because Pico "tantum scholastice cum adversariis agit" — is, precisely because of its thoroughly scholastic character, the richest evidence of Pico's formation.

**Historiographical register**: Close reading of the *Apology* as a technical scholastic document; source identification and parallel-text comparison (especially Pico vs. Henry of Ghent); attention to the *reception* of scholastic masters (what did it mean in 1487 to cite Durandus, Henry of Ghent, or Holcot?); mild revisionism with respect to earlier scholarship on Pico's sources (going beyond Caroti).

**Concord outcome**: Pico's inclusive method is itself the concord thesis: not a doctrinal synthesis of Plato and Aristotle, but a methodological synthesis in which scholastic practice, patristic authority, humanist philology, and philosophical eclecticism are held together without subordinating any one to any other. The *Apology*'s scholastic armor and the *Oration*'s humanist voice are two expressions of the same inclusive enterprise.

**Myth about the heresy trial**: The usual narrative treats the condemnation of the 13 theses as a clash between a bold humanist and conservative scholastic theologians. Edelheit's scholastic reading reverses this: Pico defends himself *as a scholastic*, using scholastic methods and scholastic authorities, to show that the commission's condemnation is itself scholastically indefensible. The irony is that Pico's best defense against the scholastic establishment is the scholastic tradition's own diversity.

## Relation to Pico Works

**The *Apologia* (1487)**

This chapter is the most sustained analysis of the *Apologia* in the book. Edelheit reads all 13 questions but focuses on:

- **Question 1** (*De descensu Christi ad inferos*, pp. 34–104): Christ's soul only descended; presence = operation, not substance; Henry of Ghent, Thomas Aquinas, Scotus, Durandus, Godfrey of Fontaines, Giles of Rome, Guido Terrena, Ockham.
- **Question 3** (*De adoratione crucis et imaginum*, pp. 120–132): Images adored *per accidens* only; Thomas less reasonable than Durandus, Henry, Holcot, William of Ware, Peter of Aquila.
- **Question 4** (*An suppositari a Deo possit natura irrationalis*, pp. 134–152): Henry of Ghent holds more extreme position than Pico; long citation from Henry's Quodlibet XIII, q. 5.
- **Question 5** (*De magia naturali et cabala Hebraeorum*, pp. 154–192): Standard scholastic epistemological distinction; William of Auvergne, Grosseteste, Albert as precedents.
- **Question 6** (*De sacramento Eucharistiae*, pp. 196–242): Assumptio vs. conversio vs. annihilatio; Durandus, Thomas, Peter of Palude, John of Damascus.
- **Question 7** (*De salute Origenis*, pp. 244–320): Pico's historical awareness; doctrine condemnation vs. soul condemnation.
- **Question 8** (*De libertate credendi*, pp. 320–338): Pierre d'Ailly, Totting da Oyta, Langenstein, Holcot on free act of belief.

The specific condemned thesis for Question 1 is: "Christus non veraciter et quantum ad realem praesentiam ut ponit Thomas et communis via descendit ad inferos sed solum quo ad effectum." (Apology p. 58)

The specific condemned thesis for Question 3 is: "Nec crux Christi nec ulla imago adoranda est adoratione latriae etiam eo modo quo ponit Thomas." (Apology p. 120)

The specific condemned thesis for Question 4 is: "Non assentior communi sententiae theologorum dicentium posse Deum quamlibet naturam suppositare sed de rationabili tantum hoc concedo." (Apology p. 134)

**The 900 Theses (1486)**

The chapter situates the *Apologia* as an extension and defense of the 900 Theses. Edelheit notes that Pico himself calls the 900 Theses a *scholastica exercitatio* in the Apology's epilogue and that the Apology represents the same exercise conducted under compulsion. The 13 theses dedicated to Henry of Ghent (to be discussed in Chapter 10), the 16 theses on Albert the Great (Chapter 6), and the theses on Thomas (Chapter 7) are all prefigured by the *Apology*'s scholastic authorities.

## Uncertainties

- **OCR quality**: The source file is a markdown conversion of a scanned PDF. Some passages contain minor OCR artifacts (e.g., missing spaces, occasional ligature issues), but the Latin quotations from Pico and Henry of Ghent appear largely intact and are cross-checked by Edelheit's footnotes. The text is reliable.
- **Fornaciari edition**: Full bibliographical details of Fornaciari's critical edition of the *Apology* are not given in the chapter text (the footnote says only "Pico, Apology"); the imprint details must be retrieved from the book's bibliography. The edition is apparently recent (referenced as a critical edition better than the 1532 and 1557 prints), but the publication year and publisher are not stated in this chapter.
- **Page range confirmation**: The chapter runs pp. 149–185 based on the running headers and the appearance of Chapter 6 immediately after p. 185. This range is confirmed by the table of contents (line 155 of the source).
- **Robert of Orford identification**: The editor (Fornaciari) identifies "Robertus de Collotorto" as "the Oxford master Robert of Orford" (p. 396, n. 12 of the Apology); this identification is presented as fact by Edelheit but is not independently verified in this chapter.
- **Question numbering**: Edelheit refers to the 13 sections as "questions" (following Fornaciari's edition); the 1487 editio princeps uses "disputationes." Scholars using other editions may number or label these differently.
