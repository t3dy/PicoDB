Maria Fabianus Moos (vols. 3–4) (Paris, 1929–1947). Additional Aquinas texts drawn from the Leonine critical editions and the Corpus Thomisticum online. Pico primary text: S.A. Farmer (ed.), *Syncretism in the West: Pico's 900 Theses* (1998). The chapter covers all 45 of Pico's theses *secundum Thomam Aquinatem* from the *900 Theses* (1486).

## Section Function

Chapter 7 is the longest chapter in Part 2 of Edelheit's study ("Pico and Scholastic Philosophy"), spanning 60 pages and treating 45 theses — by far the largest single set Pico dedicated to any scholastic thinker. Its function is to establish, through exhaustive thesis-by-thesis contextualization, that Pico possessed deep, first-hand familiarity with Aquinas's texts across the full range of his output, and that this familiarity was formative for Pico's own philosophical vocabulary and positions. The chapter also serves a comparative function: by tracking where Pico sharpens, simplifies, or subtly redirects Thomas, and by identifying moments where Pico appears to use Thomas as a counterweight against William Ockham, Edelheit constructs an image of Pico as a selective but serious Thomist, not a casual borrower. The chapter's argument is pivotal for Edelheit's overall thesis that Pico's project is a sophisticated, internally coherent engagement with the scholastic tradition rather than an ornamental humanist synthesis.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

Edelheit opens the chapter with two foundational observations. First, Pico's library contained more works by Aquinas than by any author except possibly Aristotle — Kibre's catalogue records his *Opuscula*, *De potentia dei*, commentary on Lombard's *Sentences* (multiple volumes, including Peter of Bergamo's *Concordantiae* of Thomas's apparent self-contradictions), *De esse et essentia*, *De veritate*, parts of the *Summa theologiae*, the *Summa contra gentiles*, *Quaestiones de duodecim quodlibeta*, commentaries on multiple Aristotelian natural-philosophical works, and more (pp. 214–215). Second, Pico draws on Thomas's *Sentences* commentary more heavily than on the *Summa theologiae* — a fact already noted by Farmer — which is itself historically significant as a reminder that Renaissance philosophers did not prioritize the same texts we do today (p. 214).

Edelheit's analytical strategy is consistent throughout: for each of the 45 theses he (1) quotes Pico's thesis in Latin, (2) identifies the specific Aquinas text(s) that supply its conceptual basis, (3) quotes extensively from Thomas in Latin, and (4) evaluates what Pico preserved, what he dropped, and what he added or changed. The cumulative result is a portrait of a reader who consistently compresses elaborate Thomistic arguments into single, highly condensed propositions — often accurately, occasionally over-simplifying, and occasionally pushing Thomas beyond what he explicitly said.

The theses fall into broad thematic clusters. The first five (theses 1–5) deal with Trinitarian theology and divine knowledge of contingencies: the filioque (thesis 1, from *Quaestiones disputatae de potentia*, q. 10, a. 5), temporal procession of the Holy Spirit according to gratia gratum faciens (thesis 2, from *ST* I, q. 43, a. 3), and God's infallible foreknowledge of future contingencies (theses 3–5, from *ST* I, q. 14, a. 13). Edelheit notes that thesis 1 is not conciliarist but side with the Western Catholic position against the Greek Orthodox — an atypical move for Pico, who elsewhere cultivates concordance (p. 215). On theses 3–5, Edelheit shows that Thomas's distinction between necessity *secundum quid* (under divine sight) and contingency *in relation to causes* — a distinction of considerable complexity — is collapsed in Pico's formulations, though accurately (pp. 216–218).

The cluster on grace and salvation (theses 6–9) is especially revealing because Edelheit tracks not only what Pico took from Thomas but what he was arguing against, namely William Ockham. Thesis 8 states that even through God's absolute power, a soul with grace cannot be unaccepted, and a soul without grace cannot be accepted. This directly contradicts Ockham's doctrine of *potentia Dei absoluta*, according to which God's acceptance of the soul is entirely contingent and requires no supernatural grace. Edelheit concludes that "Pico is responding to Ockham's challenge by pushing a notion which is found in Thomas a bit beyond its original scope and context" (p. 220). Thesis 9 on *condign merit* (*de condigno*) involves Thomas's distinction between *aequalitas quantitatis* and *aequalitas proportionis* from the *Sentences* commentary II, d. 27, q. 1, a. 3 — a distinction Pico accurately captures but without Thomas's careful hedging (pp. 221–222). Notably, on this thesis there is no disagreement with Ockham (p. 222).

The intellect-will debates are central to theses 12, 19, and 28. Thesis 12 declares that beatitude is essentially in the act of the intellect, with a corollary that neither *fruitio* nor any act of the will is essentially beatitude — a stronger intellectualist position than Thomas himself explicitly commits to. Edelheit documents Thomas's nuanced acknowledgment that the will's *quietatio* is "quasi formaliter complens rationem beatitudinis" and that Thomas preferred a contemplative rather than practical intellect for beatitude, while noting that Ockham opted for both intellect and will and John Pecham for the will — making Pico's formulation a polemical intellectualist overstatement of Thomas (pp. 224–227). Thesis 19 examines sin in the will: Pico states that there can be no sin in the will without a defect in reason. Edelheit demonstrates that this argument is actually refuted by Thomas in the *Sentences* (where Thomas argues sin belongs to the will); Pico's thesis finds positive backing only in *ST* I–II, q. 77, a. 2, where Thomas (following a Socratic argument via Aristotle) says the will would never strive after evil unless reason contained some error or ignorance. Edelheit concludes that "Pico's awareness of Thomas's earlier account and his preference for the later conclusion" explains this selective reading (p. 237). Thesis 28, declaring that "the whole of liberty consists essentially in reason," is grounded in *Quaestiones disputatae de veritate*, q. 24, a. 6, where Thomas acknowledges reason as the first origin of free choice while assigning the immediate act of choosing to the will — Pico's thesis compresses this into an unqualified rationalist claim that requires the full Thomistic context to understand (pp. 250–252).

The metaphysical cluster (theses 21–33) contains some of the chapter's most philosophically significant material. Thesis 23 — "the only thing that *unum* adds to *ens* is the privation of division" — is drawn from Thomas's *Quaestiones quodlibetales* X, q. 1, a. 1, which follows Aristotle and Averroes in rejecting Avicenna's view that *unum* adds a genus of measure to *ens*, and instead defines *unum* simply as *ens indivisum* (p. 244). Edelheit notes this thesis is directly linked to Pico's *De ente et uno* (1490–91), the major work addressed in Chapter 16 of the book, and thus provides a direct genetic link between the 1486 theses and the later philosophical treatise. Thesis 24 (subject and proper *passio* are really distinguished) is traced to Thomas's commentary on Aristotle's *Physics*, Book 7, Lect. 5, and thesis 25 (*forma generatur per accidens*) to Thomas's commentary on Aristotle's *Metaphysics*, Book 7, Lect. 7. Edelheit observes that Ockham in *Quodlibeta septem* 5, q. 18 drew an analogy between *ens/unum* and *passio/subiectum* — wondering whether Pico had Ockham's analogy in mind while formulating theses 23 and 24 back-to-back (p. 247). Thesis 26 reproduces Thomas's definition of *materia signata* as the principle of individuation from *ST* I, q. 75, a. 4 (p. 248). Thesis 31 on the real distinction between essence and existence in all created beings comes directly from Thomas's *De ente et essentia*, with Thomas's triple taxonomy: God (essence = existence), intellectual created substances (essence without matter, but existence limited), and composite substances (existence finite, quiddity individuated by signed matter) (pp. 255–256).

On Trinitarian and Christological matters (theses 10, 16, 17) Edelheit repeatedly notes that Pico's theses are accurate but theologically unrefined — Pico drops qualifications essential to the full scholastic theological discourse and is "not sensitive enough to some of the theological nuances and implications found in Thomas" (p. 231). This is not a failure of competence but a reflection of the fact that the *900 Theses* project was not a professional theological exercise (p. 231).

On the epistemological cluster (theses 22, 39, 40), thesis 39 (phantasm as secondary and instrumental agent in the production of intelligible species) is particularly notable: Edelheit identifies this formulation as Pico's own — it is "not found in Thomas" (p. 265) but is implied in Thomas's anti-Averroist argument at *ST* I, q. 76, a. 2. Thesis 22 (God is not seen through *species* but His essence is applied to the intellect as intelligible species) reflects what Edelheit calls "a standard Neoplatonic emphasis" on Pico's part, shaping a Thomistic discussion into a language more congenial to Platonic epistemology (p. 240). Thesis 40 (difficulty of understanding stems partly from the intellect and partly from the intelligible object) uses Aristotle's *Metaphysics* Alpha minor via Thomas but generalizes where Thomas committed to limits on understanding separated substances in this life (p. 266).

The chapter concludes with Edelheit's summary assessment: Pico is consistently accurate but not exhaustive; he selects, compresses, and occasionally shifts emphasis according to his own interests; his terminology is often his own (*conceptus interuniti*, *actus entitativus positivus*, *plurificatio*); and several theses appear designed as polemical responses to Ockham routed through Thomas. This last point — Pico using Thomas against Ockham — is identified as deserving further research (p. 273).

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: Aquinas's works in Pico's library outnumbered those of any other author except possibly Aristotle. (p. 214, citing Kibre)

**Claim 2**: Pico used Thomas's *Sentences* commentary as a primary source more than the *Summa theologiae*, reflecting Renaissance priorities different from modern ones. (p. 214)

**Claim 3**: The purpose of contextualizing the 45 Thomistic theses is to demonstrate that Pico was familiar with the full argumentative context of each. (p. 215)

**Claim 4**: Thesis 1 (filioque) aligns Pico with the Western Catholic Church against the Greek Orthodox, departing from his usual concord-seeking posture. (p. 215)

**Claim 5**: Theses 3–5 (divine foreknowledge of contingencies) accurately compress Thomas's *ST* I, q. 14, a. 13, but lose the crucial distinction between necessity under divine sight and contingency relative to proximate causes. (pp. 216–218)

**Claim 6**: Thesis 7 (God's will does not impose necessity on things willed) is drawn from *ST* I, q. 19, a. 8, where Thomas establishes that God wills some things necessarily and some contingently. (pp. 218–219)

**Claim 7**: Thesis 8 (grace necessary for divine acceptance even under absolute power) is Pico's Thomistic response to Ockham's doctrine of *potentia Dei absoluta*, pushing Thomas beyond his original scope. (pp. 219–221)

**Claim 8**: Thesis 9 (condign merit) accurately reflects Thomas's distinction between *aequalitas quantitatis* and *aequalitas proportionis* but omits his cautious hedging in *Sentences* II, d. 27, q. 1, a. 3. (pp. 221–222)

**Claim 9**: Thesis 12 (beatitude essentially in the intellect) commits Thomas to a sharper intellectualism than Thomas explicitly holds, positioning Pico against Ockham and Pecham on this question. (pp. 224–227)

**Claim 10**: Thesis 15 (incapacity of bodies for suffering after resurrection from the soul's dominion) shows Pico as "an insightful reader of Thomas who knows how to relate different points of emphasis," but lacking theological sensitivity. (p. 231)

**Claim 11**: Thesis 19 (no sin in the will without defect in reason) is found in a *refuted* argument in Thomas's *Sentences* II, d. 39, q. 1, a. 1, but has positive support in *ST* I–II, q. 77, a. 2; Pico chose the later, positive formulation. (pp. 236–237)

**Claim 12**: Thesis 21 (no plurification of angels within one species) is rooted primarily in Thomas's *Sentences* II, d. 3, q. 1, a. 4, and *De ente et essentia* 4, where the word *plurificatio* itself appears — possibly the direct source of Pico's thesis. (pp. 239–240)

**Claim 13**: Thesis 23 (unum adds only privation of division to ens) is the clearest direct bridge between the 1486 theses and the *De ente et uno* (1490–91). (p. 244)

**Claim 14**: Ockham's *Quodlibeta septem* 5, q. 18 draws an analogy between *ens/unum* and *passio/subiectum* that may have been in Pico's mind when formulating back-to-back theses 23 and 24. (p. 247)

**Claim 15**: Thesis 26 (*materia signata* as principle of individuation) reproduces Thomas's *ST* I, q. 75, a. 4 terminology directly. (p. 248)

**Claim 16**: Thesis 28 (all liberty essentially in reason) compresses Thomas's nuanced division — reason as first origin, will as immediate chooser — into an unqualified rationalist claim without the qualifications Thomas provides. (pp. 250–252)

**Claim 17**: Thesis 31 (real distinction of essence and existence in creatures) is directly from Thomas's *De ente et essentia*, with Pico using his own vocabulary (*existentia* rather than Thomas's *esse*). (pp. 255–256)

**Claim 18**: Thesis 35 (*homo est risibilis* not in the second mode of *per se* predication) positions Pico against Ockham's view that *risibilitas* is merely a second-mode predication with no real correlate in the common nature. (pp. 259–261)

**Claim 19**: Thesis 39 (phantasm as secondary and instrumental agent in production of intelligible species) is Pico's own formulation, only implied in Thomas — Thomas does not use this language. (pp. 264–265)

**Claim 20**: Throughout the chapter Pico's own technical vocabulary (*actus entitativus positivus*, *conceptus interuniti*, *plurificatio*, *angelus beatior subiective*) does not appear in the Thomas passages he is drawing on, reflecting Pico's active reinterpretation. (pp. 240, 244, 254, 256–257, 265)

**Claim 21**: Pico appears in several theses (8, 35 at minimum) to use Thomas as a polemical counterweight against Ockham; this pattern deserves further research. (p. 273)

**Claim 22**: Overall, Pico is accurate but lacks Thomas's theological precision, a fact Edelheit attributes to the fragmentary thesis format and Pico's non-specialist position as philosopher rather than professional theologian. (pp. 231, 273)

## Technical Vocabulary

**esse / existentia**: Being or existence. In thesis 31, Pico uses *existentia* (his own preference) where Thomas uses *esse* to mark the real distinction between existence and essence in creatures. Thomas's *De ente et essentia* is the primary source. Pico's substitution of *existentia* for *esse* is noted as a terminological shift. (pp. 255–256)

**essentia / quiditas**: Essence / quiddity. For Thomas, essence and quiddity are roughly equivalent; their distinction from existence is the foundation of creaturely ontology. Pico preserves this but with his own vocabulary. (pp. 255–256, 267–270)

**ens / unum**: Being / one. Thesis 23 centers on the *transcendentals*. Thomas defines *unum* as *ens indivisum* and holds that it adds only *negatio divisionis* to *ens*. This is central to the *De ente et uno* (1490–91). (pp. 243–244)

**materia signata**: Signed or designated matter. Thomas's principle of individuation in composite substances: matter individuated by quantitative determination. Pico's thesis 26 uses this phrase precisely. (p. 248)

**materia prima / resolutio**: Prime matter / dissolution. Thesis 29 holds that substantial generation requires dissolution back to prime matter (*resolutio usque ad materiam primam*). *Resolutio* is a standard Aristotelian-scholastic method term (moving from composite effects back to material causes). (pp. 252–254)

**forma generatur per accidens**: Form is generated by accident. Thesis 25. Basis: Aristotle's distinction between form (which does not have independent existence) and composite (which is what is genuinely generated). (pp. 247–249)

**actus purus**: Pure act. Applies to God's essence in the beatific vision discussion (thesis 22): since divine essence is *actus purus*, it can function as the intelligible form through which the intellect understands. (p. 241)

**intelligibilis species**: Intelligible species. The form through which the intellect understands an object, distinguished from the sensible image (*species sensibilis*) and from *phantasma*. Thesis 22 centers on this, and thesis 39 treats *phantasma* as only a secondary instrument in its production. (pp. 239–245, 264–265)

**plurificatio**: Multiplication or plurification. Unusual Latin noun; Pico uses it in thesis 21 for the impossibility of multiple angels in one species. Thomas uses it in *De ente et essentia* 4. Edelheit suggests this may be its direct source. (pp. 239–240)

**condign merit / de condigno / de congruo**: Technical merit distinctions. Thesis 9. *Condign merit* implies proportional equality between act and reward; *congruous merit* is a lesser claim. Thomas distinguishes them in *Sentences* II, d. 27, q. 1, a. 3. (pp. 221–222)

**potentia Dei absoluta / ordinata**: God's absolute vs. ordained power. Thesis 8's key conceptual contrast: Aquinas holds grace necessary even under absolute power; Ockham holds it contingent. (pp. 219–221)

**secundum modum dicendi per se**: The modes of *per se* predication (from Aristotle's *Posterior Analytics*). Thesis 35 asserts that *homo est risibilis* is NOT in the second mode of *per se* predication (which would make it merely definitional), contra Ockham. (pp. 259–261)

**subiective**: Used in thesis 18 for the angel (*angelus beatior*): time is in the blessed angel in a subjective manner, meaning it is a real attribute of the individual subject, measured by its degree of participation in eternity. (pp. 234–235)

**aevum**: The temporal mode of spiritual beings, intermediate between eternity (God) and time (material things). Thesis 18: time exists subjectively in the more blessed angel. (pp. 234–235)

**phantasma**: The sensory image or mental picture retained from perception. Thesis 39: the *phantasma* is a secondary and instrumental, not principal, agent in the production of *intelligible species*. (pp. 264–265)

**conceptus interuniti**: Pico's own coinage for the categories as immediately implied by *ens* in thesis 30 — not of one thing, but referring to one thing. Not found in Thomas. (p. 254)

**actus entitativus positivus**: Pico's unique formulation in thesis 33 (matter has no positive entitative act). Emphasizes matter's purely passive nature. Not found as such in Thomas. (pp. 257–258)

**beatitudo / fruitio**: Beatitude / fruition. Thesis 12 and corollary: beatitude essentially in the intellect; *fruitio* (the will's enjoyment of its end) is not essentially beatitude. Thomas's nuanced account is sharpened into a firm anti-voluntarist claim. (pp. 224–227)

**tota libertas est in ratione**: Total liberty is in reason. Thesis 28. For Thomas, reason is the *first origin* (*prima origo*) of free choice but not its immediate cause; the will is the immediate cause. Pico's formulation drops this qualification. (pp. 250–252)

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Myth being tested**: The myth of Pico as anti-scholastic humanist — the idea that his scholasticism was superficial window-dressing on a fundamentally Neoplatonist or syncretist project. Chapter 7 systematically demolishes this by showing that 45 theses can each be traced to specific, technically precise passages across the full range of Thomas's output, and that Pico understood their argumentative contexts.

**Historiographical register**: Intellectual history grounded in close textual analysis. Edelheit positions himself against scholarship that treats Pico's scholastic theses as mere lists, insisting they require source-tracing to be intelligible. The approach follows the tradition of Farmer's edition and is in dialogue with Caroti's work on Pico's medieval sources in natural philosophy.

**Concord outcome**: Unusually for Part 2, this chapter reveals moments of *non-concord* as well as concord. Thesis 1 sides with Rome against the Greek Church. Several theses position Pico against Ockham. The concord impulse operates not between Plato and Aristotle here (that is the topic of *De ente et uno*) but between Pico's own eclecticism and the Thomistic corpus — Pico is a selective Thomist, not an opponent.

**Open historiographical question**: Edelheit flags at p. 273 that the pattern of Pico using Thomas against Ockham "deserves further research." This is the chapter's most productive live question for future scholarship.

**Depth assessment**: Edelheit's verdict is that Pico's Thomism is genuine but philosophical rather than theological. He masters the structural arguments but is not reliably sensitive to theological nuance. He is more interested in epistemology, metaphysics, and natural philosophy than in the fine points of sacramental theology or Christology.

## Relation to Pico Works

**900 Theses (1486)**: All 45 theses *secundum Thomam Aquinatem* analyzed. The primary focus is on the Thomistic *Sentences* commentary rather than the *Summa*, suggesting Pico worked with the full scholastic apparatus rather than later digests. Key theses for philosophical content:
- Thesis 8 (pp. 219–221): *potentia Dei absoluta* and grace; anti-Ockhamist.
- Thesis 12 (pp. 224–227): beatitude in the intellect; anti-voluntarist.
- Thesis 19 (pp. 236–237): no sin in will without defect in reason; anti-Ockhamist.
- Thesis 21 (pp. 239–240): no plurification of angels in one species; uses *De ente et essentia*.
- Thesis 23 (pp. 243–244): *unum* adds only *privatio divisionis* to *ens*; directly anticipates *De ente et uno*.
- Thesis 26 (p. 248): *materia signata* as principle of individuation.
- Thesis 28 (pp. 250–252): all liberty essentially in reason.
- Thesis 31 (pp. 255–256): real distinction of essence and existence in creatures; from *De ente et essentia*.
- Thesis 35 (pp. 259–261): *homo est risibilis* not in second mode *per se*; anti-Ockhamist.
- Thesis 39 (pp. 264–265): phantasm as secondary instrumental agent; Pico's own formulation.

**De ente et uno (1490–91)**: Explicitly prospected at p. 244, where thesis 23 is described as directly related to the conceptual tension (*ens/unum*) addressed in the later treatise. Edelheit calls this the center of Chapter 16. The 1486 thesis pre-figures the 1491 argument that *unum* and *ens* are convertible transcendentals, with *unum* adding only *negatio divisionis*.

**Heptaplus (1489)**: Not explicitly analyzed in this chapter. Chapter 7 is entirely about the *900 Theses*.

**Oration on the Dignity of Man (1486)**: Not analyzed as a primary text in this chapter; Edelheit treats the *Oration* primarily in Part 1 of the book.

- `being.md` / `unity.md`: Thesis 23 and its *De ente et uno* connection. The Avicenna–Averroës–Aristotle debate on what *unum* adds to *ens*; Thomas's position (*ens indivisum*, *negatio divisionis*); Pico's 1486 formulation as pre-*De ente et uno*.

- `predication.md`: Theses 30 (*ens* and the ten categories as *conceptus interuniti*), 35 (second mode *per se* predication), 23 and 24 (Ockham's analogy between *ens/unum* and *passio/subiectum*). The modes of *per se* predication.

- `act_and_potency.md`: Theses 31 (*esse*/*essentia* distinction via *actus purus* in God), 33 (matter as purely passive, no *actus entitativus positivus*), 43 (*materia sine forma* implies contradiction because matter's being presupposes form-as-act).

- `individuation.md` (create if not exists): Thesis 26 (*materia signata* as principle of individuation), thesis 21 (no plurification of angels in one species because no matter to individuate forms).

- `beatitude.md` (create if not exists): Thesis 12 (intellect vs. will in beatitude; Pico's intellectualism contra Ockham and Pecham; Thomas's nuanced position).

- `intellect_will.md` (create or check): Theses 12, 19, 28; the 1474 Ficino–Bandello debate cross-referenced by Edelheit.

- `grace_merit.md` (create if not exists): Theses 8, 9 on *potentia Dei absoluta*, *de condigno*, *gratia gratum faciens*; the anti-Ockhamist dimension.

- `creation.md`: Thesis 17 (*ministerium* vs. *auctoritas creationis*); thesis 33 (seminal reasons and passive matter).

- `angelology.md`: Theses 18 (*aevum* subjectively in the blessed angel), 21 (no plurification in one species).

- `ockham.md` (create or update): A substantial anti-Ockhamist sub-theme runs through theses 8, 12, 19, 35; Edelheit also notes potential Ockhamist context for theses 23–24 and 41. This chapter is the strongest textual evidence in the book for Pico's conscious positioning against Ockham.

**Open problems:**
1. Edelheit flags that the anti-Ockhamist use of Thomas in theses 8, 19, 35 "deserves further research" (p. 273). This is the chapter's most productive live question.
2. Whether Pico's distinctive coinages (*conceptus interuniti*, *actus entitativus positivus*, *plurificatio*) reflect a specific tradition, a single intermediate source, or Pico's own philosophical invention.
3. The relationship between the 1486 *Sentences*-based Thomism and the 1490–91 *De ente et uno*, which turns toward Platonic sources — what changed and why.

## Updates Existing Writing

- Any existing essay claiming Pico was primarily influenced by Ficino and Neoplatonism in his metaphysics should be nuanced: Chapter 7 shows the depth of his independent Thomism, especially in natural philosophy and epistemology.
- Any claim that Pico used the *Summa theologiae* as his primary Aquinas text should be corrected: Edelheit demonstrates the *Sentences* commentary is more heavily used (p. 214).
- Any essay treating *De ente et uno* as Pico's first engagement with the *ens/unum* question should note that thesis 23 of the *900 Theses* (1486) precedes and anticipates that work.
- No existing PicoDB essays appear to address the anti-Ockhamist dimension systematically; this is a gap.

## Uncertainties

1. **OCR/transcription issues in the source markdown**: Generally clean. Some minor ligature artifacts in the Latin block quotes (e.g., `⟨s⟩t` in footnote 55 for `est`). No major OCR problems affect the argument.

2. **Peter of Bergamo identification** (p. 215 n. 4): Edelheit notes uncertainty about whether the *Concordantiae* in Pico's library is Peter of Bergamo's or Gerard de Monte's *Tractatus ostendens concordiam S. Thome et venerabilis domini Alberti*. Minor bibliographic uncertainty.

3. **Thesis 18 and Pseudo-Dionysius**: Edelheit suggests Thomas's *Quaestiones quodlibetales* 5, q. 4 as source and names Pseudo-Dionysius as Thomas's background authority, but notes the relationship is implied rather than explicit in Pico's thesis.

4. **Thesis 30 terminology**: Edelheit acknowledges Pico's *conceptus interuniti* "is using his own terminology" and is not in Thomas — but does not identify a specific alternative source for this coinage.

5. **Completeness of anti-Ockhamist thesis identification**: Edelheit identifies theses 8, 19, 35 and possibly 23–24 and 41 as having anti-Ockhamist dimensions, but notes this pattern "deserves further research" — there may be more such theses.
