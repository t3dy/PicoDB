## Section Function

Chapter 17 is the final substantive chapter of Part 3, which traces scholastic reactions to Pico's philosophy across different universities and intellectual contexts. It closes the book's gallery of scholastic respondents — Vernia, Galgani, Securo, and others — by examining the most philosophically significant and chronologically extended reception: Pomponazzi's engagement with Pico's anti-astrology polemic in the *De incantationibus* (completed 1520, roughly a quarter-century after Pico's death). The chapter thus serves a dual function: it demonstrates that Pico's critical account of astrology was a live problem in the sixteenth-century scholastic world long after his death, and it shows that the scholastic response to Pico was not mere refutation but creative modification of shared philosophical frameworks. By placing Pomponazzi's position against Cassirer's influential but anachronistic reading of *De incantationibus* as "Enlightenment in scholastic garb," Edelheit clears the ground for a more historically accurate picture of the scholastic-humanist encounter. The "modification vs. rejection" antithesis named in the chapter title serves as the book's capstone analytical category: Pico and Pomponazzi represent the two principal possibilities for relating the scholastic scientific tradition to the evidence of occult and celestial phenomena.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

Edelheit opens by positioning Pomponazzi (1462–1525) as one of the most original voices in Renaissance Aristotelianism and a key figure in the development of Renaissance scholasticism, while noting that modern scholars — Cassirer prominently, but also Pine — have systematically misrepresented his work by importing anachronistic categories (Kantian rationalism, "Enlightenment," "naturalistic psychology," "radical") that obscure the sixteenth-century intellectual context. The chapter promises a focused correction: to read *De incantationibus* as a direct philosophical response to Pico's *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem*.

Edelheit's first move is historiographical. He follows Compagni's division of Pomponazzi's attitude toward Pico into two phases: an early, sporadic engagement in Paduan lectures on *De anima*, the *Quaestio de universalibus* (1503–04), and Bolognese lectures on *Metaphysics* XII (1511–12); and a second, post-1516 phase, after the publication of *De immortalitate animae*, where "Pico seems to be present as a constant point of reference, even if silent." The relationship between Pomponazzi and Pico's nephew Alberto Pio (with whom Pomponazzi engaged in private studies 1496–1499 and who had access to a rich library containing Pico's texts) is flagged as a question requiring care, though hard evidence for direct influence remains thin.

Before turning to astrology proper, Edelheit identifies one domain of genuine, though not provably direct, convergence: philosophical anthropology. Comparing passages from Pomponazzi's *De immortalitate animae* and the *Expositio super primo et secundo De partibus animalium* (1521–24) with Pico's *Oration*, Edelheit shows near-identical rhetoric — the human soul as microcosm capable of becoming gods, lions, wolves, fish, or plants; the human being as placed in the middle of the cosmic hierarchy; the free capacity to assume any nature — drawn on the same stock of biblical (Psalm 8:5–9), classical, patristic, and scholastic texts. Edelheit concludes that Pomponazzi probably read the *Oration* and followed its line of argumentation concerning self-determined human nature, using almost identical terminology with some variations. This similarity underscores by contrast that "this is not the case in regard to astrology."

The main analysis concerns *De incantationibus* (completed 28 July 1520, Bologna). The text originates as a response to queries from Pomponazzi's friend Ludovicus Panizza about whether people can be cured using solely words, incantations, or characters — either through demons or by attracting healing powers from the heavenly bodies. Pomponazzi notes that Aristotle denied demons and denied action at a distance, leaving no clear Peripatetic answer; he must therefore transcend the Aristotelian framework while retaining an "Aristotelian" perspective, offering not what Aristotle said but what he believes Aristotle would have said. Panizza reports that their mutual teacher Pietro Trapolino appealed to Albert the Great as an authority allowing words and characters to serve as instruments for attracting celestial powers.

The divergence from Pico's starting point is fundamental and Edelheit underlines it repeatedly. Pico's *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem* attacks "presaging astrology" (*astrologia divinatrix*) and its scientific foundations root-and-branch. Pomponazzi's starting point is medical and naturalistic: the possibility of healing through incantations, whether via demons or celestial attraction.

Edelheit traces Pomponazzi's argument through the chapters of *De incantationibus*:

In the third chapter, discussing medications that operate on the body, Pomponazzi mentions "almost infinite hidden qualities" (*fere infinitae virtutes occultae*) — magnets attracting iron, diamonds resisting that attraction, sapphires repelling cinnabar — cited from Albert the Great and Marsilio Ficino. Edelheit notes that this puts Pomponazzi "à jour" with the recentiores, including Pico, who in *Disputationes* III.XXIV had specifically argued that hidden qualities are not present in the heavens and that celestial bodies produce only heat and life-giving light. Pico had used magnets and the peony flower as his own examples, concluding that the only heavenly qualities affecting inferior regions are light and heat. Pomponazzi implicitly challenges this conclusion by embedding hidden qualities within a positive natural philosophy of celestial influence.

In the fourth chapter, Pomponazzi pursues a naturalistic reduction: phenomena attributed to demons can generally be explained through natural causes, and when no natural explanation is found, the most likely explanation is deception by an enchanter. In the fifth chapter, astrology first appears alongside medicine and natural philosophy as a legitimate discipline within a broader defense of the naturalistic mode. A crucial difficulty arises here — and both Pico and Pomponazzi engage it differently: Pomponazzi acknowledges that naturalistic reduction, pushed far enough, can be applied to biblical miracles and thus destroy the foundations of both Mosaic and Christian Law (citing Exodus 7–12 and Moses's struggle against Pharaoh's magicians as a case study). Pomponazzi resists this conclusion in the fourth and seventh chapters: he maintains that some biblical miracles — the raising of Lazarus, the healing of the man born blind, the feeding of five thousand from five loaves and two fish — cannot be reduced to any natural cause. This partial resistance to full naturalism places Pomponazzi "somewhat closer to Pico" on the theological limit-case and reflects a complexity missed by Cassirer and Pine.

Pico had addressed the religion-astrology conflict directly in *Disputationes* II.IV–V (on whether astrology is useful to religion, per Roger Bacon and Pierre d'Ailly; and on astrology's damage to Christian religion). Pico's argument: since all truth is in agreement with all other truth, astronomical truth must accord with theological truth; but since astrology is false, it cannot accord with theological truth. Furthermore, ascribing divine miracles to the movement of stars destroys religion's foundations, since the acts of the martyrs and Apostles exceed both human and demonic causation. Pomponazzi is willing to treat the magical mode as a true science — a branch of natural philosophy and astrology, as legitimate as medicine — precisely where Pico is "totally opposed to including astrology among the arts and sciences," associating it with fraud and incantation. Edelheit quotes Pico's explicit claim that Plato, Aristotle, and all the ancient philosophers denied celestial causation as astrologers understand it and refused to count astrology as an art or science (*Disputationes* I, p. 50).

Edelheit then presses on what makes Pomponazzi's account of astrology genuinely positive rather than merely naturalistic in a reductive sense. In the tenth chapter of *De incantationibus*, Pomponazzi openly rejects the anti-occultist reading of Aristotle and insists on examining cases individually. Through Aristotelian cosmology he finds that the superior and immaterial parts of the cosmos cannot effect inferior and material parts except through mediating bodies placed between eternity on one hand and generation-and-corruption on the other. The heavenly bodies are responsible for anything perfect in natural philosophy and are the total or partial, complete or regulatory causes of all operations in the inferior world. Crucially, Pomponazzi extends this: the heavenly bodies not only direct human lives but also predict future events and communicate with human beings in dreams and in wakefulness through the appearance of different figures, evidenced by Plutarch and "an infinite number of other authors." Every effect in the inferior world leads back to heaven, and from the practical knowledge of the heavenly bodies and their effects, fantastic and admirable things can be known and announced. This is, as Edelheit observes, "the point of view most remote from Pico."

On the problem of evil, Pomponazzi introduces the distinction between *malum culpae* (evil of sin) and *malum naturae* (evil of nature). God and the heavenly bodies cannot compel the will — if reason is free, no creature can move the will by force — and therefore they are not responsible for evil of sin. But the disposition of the heavenly bodies, together with God and the intelligences, is responsible for natural evil — defects that occur from time to time as a kind of natural necessity. This distinction is the philosophical pivot that allows Pomponazzi to preserve both celestial influence and human freedom, and it is strategically deployed just before his explicit criticism of Pico. Averroës had misunderstood astrology by believing that astrologers hold the heavenly bodies to compel humans to sin; but "certain contemporaries" (*quidam recentes*) make the same mistake as Averroës — either not understanding astrologers or, if they do, erring gravely. Their books contain nothing but arrogance and immodesty. The editor of the critical edition (Compagni) confirms that *recentes* is an explicit reference to Pico and *aliqui* an implicit reference to Lucio Bellanti. Edelheit identifies this passage (p. 142 of the *De incantationibus*) as "the clearest indication in the text that Pomponazzi's arguments are directed towards Pico."

Edelheit closes with his synthesis. In philosophical anthropology, Pico and Pomponazzi share strikingly similar views, reflecting to some extent the former's influence on the latter. In astrology, the two are diametrically opposed. Pico uses Aristotle and the Peripatetics to refute predicting astrology's scientific foundations entirely — rejection. Pomponazzi finds a proper place for astrology within Aristotelian cosmology, while admitting that discussing demons and occult phenomena explicitly does go beyond the strict Peripatetic framework — modification. The conclusion is categorical: "the activity of modifying the Aristotelian scientific tradition to embrace, or else to pay deference to the evidence of occult phenomena, is a distinctive feature of Pomponazzi's approach, while a total rejection of such evidence is characteristic of Pico's thinking. Modification and rejection are indeed the two possibilities in any approach towards scientific or philosophical traditions and they are presented by Pico and Pomponazzi in their competing accounts of astrology" (p. 531).

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: Pomponazzi (1462–1525) was a key figure in Renaissance Aristotelianism and scholasticism whose work has been systematically misrepresented by modern scholars including Cassirer and Pine, who imported anachronistic categories ("Enlightenment in scholastic garb," "naturalistic psychology," "radical philosopher") that obscure his sixteenth-century context (pp. 515–516).

**Claim 2**: Two phases in Pomponazzi's engagement with Pico can be identified: an early, sporadic phase in Paduan lectures and the *Quaestio de universalibus* (1503–04); and a second, more intensive phase after 1516 and the *De immortalitate animae*, where Pico functions as a "constant point of reference, even if silent" — Compagni's formulation (p. 517).

**Claim 3**: The 1496–1499 private studies between Pomponazzi and Alberto Pio (Pico's nephew), who possessed a rich library including Pico's texts, provided an opportunity for direct familiarity, but no hard textual evidence survives for direct influence during that period (p. 517).

**Claim 4**: Pomponazzi and Pico share almost identical rhetoric and terminology in their accounts of philosophical anthropology — the human soul as microcosm, the human being placed between mortality and immortality capable of ascending to gods or descending to beasts — drawing on the same biblical, classical, patristic, and scholastic sources (pp. 518–520).

**Claim 5**: On philosophical anthropology, Edelheit judges it "a cautious conclusion" that Pomponazzi read Pico's *Oration* and followed its line of argumentation, using near-identical terminology with variations, though this falls short of proof of direct influence (p. 520).

**Claim 6**: Pomponazzi's *De incantationibus* (completed 28 July 1520) originated as a response to queries from Ludovicus Panizza about healing by words, incantations, or characters, through demons or celestial powers — a starting point fundamentally different from Pico's attack on presaging astrology (*astrologia divinatrix*) (pp. 520–521).

**Claim 7**: Pomponazzi must transcend the Aristotelian framework in *De incantationibus* because Aristotle denied demons and denied action without contact, leaving no Peripatetic answer; he offers his own view as consistent with "the spirit if not the letter" of Aristotle's teaching (p. 521).

**Claim 8**: Pomponazzi explicitly recognizes Pico's argument about hidden qualities (Pico, *Disputationes* III.XXIV) and implicitly challenges it by embedding the discussion of *virtutes occultae* within a positive account of celestial influence, citing Albert the Great and Ficino (pp. 522–523).

**Claim 9**: Pico's *Disputationes* Book Two, Chapters IV–V argue that astrology cannot accord with theological truth and that ascribing divine miracles to stellar movements destroys religious foundations; these chapters are the direct Pican interlocutor for Pomponazzi's fifth-chapter treatment of occult practices and biblical miracles (pp. 523–525).

**Claim 10**: Pomponazzi is willing to treat magic and astrology as legitimate sciences — true branches of natural philosophy — and regards this "practical science" (*scientia factiva*) as not making anyone evil, since God and good angels possess it in the most perfect form (p. 525).

**Claim 11**: Pomponazzi's seemingly "naturalistic reduction" is not fully naturalistic: it is subjected to God and the good angels, which complicates Cassirer's and Pine's readings and places Pomponazzi closer to scholastic theological orthodoxy than those readings allow (pp. 525–527).

**Claim 12**: Through Aristotelian cosmological analysis in the tenth chapter of *De incantationibus*, Pomponazzi locates the heavenly bodies as total or partial causes of all operations in the inferior world, and affirms that they predict future events and communicate with humans in dreams and wakefulness — a position "most remote from Pico" (pp. 527–529).

**Claim 13**: Pomponazzi's *malum culpae*/*malum naturae* distinction allows him to affirm both celestial influence (heavenly bodies and intelligences are causes of natural evil) and human freedom (the will cannot be compelled by any creature or by the heavenly bodies in matters of sin) simultaneously (pp. 530–531).

**Claim 14**: The explicit citation of "certain contemporaries" (*quidam recentes*) attacking astrologers with "ornate words" and showing nothing but "arrogance and immodesty" is a direct criticism of Pico, confirmed as explicit by the editor Compagni; the additional reference to *aliqui* who claim the view was presented only "for entertainment" is an implicit reference to Lucio Bellanti (p. 531).

**Claim 15**: The fundamental opposition: Pico's strategy is total rejection of astrology's scientific foundations using Aristotle against celestial causation; Pomponazzi's strategy is modification of Aristotelianism to find a legitimate place for celestial influence, accepting what strict Peripateticism cannot. These are the two principal responses available to any philosopher confronting occult claims within a scholastic framework (p. 531).

## Technical Vocabulary

**astrologia divinatrix**: "Presaging astrology" / "predictive astrology." The specific target of Pico's *Disputationes*, distinguished from mathematical astronomy; the claim that celestial configurations predict contingent human and terrestrial events. Pico rejects it entirely. Pomponazzi does not use this exact phrase but his defense of astrology as a legitimate science implies a positive answer to what Pico denies (pp. 520–521).

**virtutes occultae** (*fere infinitae*): "Hidden (or occult) qualities" / "almost infinite hidden powers." In Pomponazzi (following Albert the Great and Ficino), real natural properties of things — magnets, diamonds, sapphires — that operate beyond the four primary qualities (hot, cold, wet, dry). Their presence or absence in celestial bodies is a key contested point: Pico denies them to the heavens; Pomponazzi affirms them indirectly (pp. 522–523).

**scientia factiva**: "Practical science" / "productive science." Pomponazzi's designation for magic (in the first mode) as a legitimate discipline — a branch of natural philosophy and astrology, analogous to medicine — that is good in itself (*in se bona*) and a perfection of the intellect (p. 525).

**malum culpae / malum naturae**: "Evil of sin" / "evil of nature." Pomponazzi's distinction, drawn from Pseudo-Dionysius and Augustine, that allows celestial bodies to be causes of natural defects (evil of nature) without being causes of moral evil (evil of sin), since the will cannot be compelled. This is his central philosophical mechanism for reconciling astrology with free will and theological orthodoxy, and it is the platform from which he criticizes Pico (pp. 530–531).

**causa deficiens / causa efficiens**: "Deficient cause" / "efficient cause." Drawn from Pseudo-Dionysius and Augustine: evil of sin has no efficient cause, only a deficient one (a lack or privation in the will), not a positive celestial determination. Used by Pomponazzi to preserve free will against astrological determinism while affirming celestial influence over natural processes (p. 531).

**simulacra**: "Images" / "likenesses." Used by Pomponazzi for what the heavenly bodies produce in human perception — the appearance of speaking gods or souls of the dead are in fact simulacra manufactured by celestial bodies operating on the sensitive faculty (p. 529).

**corpora coelestia**: "Heavenly bodies." The mediating physical agents in Aristotelian-Pomponazzian cosmology, placed between immaterial intelligences (eternal) and the terrestrial world of generation and corruption. They transmit form and influence downward and are the total or partial, direct or regulatory causes of all operations in the inferior world (pp. 527–529).

**intelligentiae**: "Intelligences." Immaterial movers of the celestial spheres in the Aristotelian-scholastic cosmological hierarchy, placed between God (first cause) and the heavenly bodies (instrumental causes). In Pomponazzi's positive cosmology, God, the intelligences, and the heavenly bodies together govern all inferior affairs; demons are therefore unnecessary (pp. 525, 528–529).

**recentes / quidam recentes**: "Certain recent [writers]." Pomponazzi's phrase for his contemporaries attacking astrology; confirmed by Compagni's editorial apparatus as an explicit reference to Pico della Mirandola (p. 531).

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Myth under test**: The myth that Pico della Mirandola was a uniquely isolated or unprecedented voice in attacking astrology, with no serious scholastic engagement. Edelheit's chapter shows that the most philosophically rigorous scholastic reader of the anti-astrology argument — Pomponazzi — engaged Pico systematically, and that his engagement constituted a sophisticated positive alternative, not mere refutation.

**Second myth under test**: The Cassirerian narrative that opposes Pomponazzi (as a proto-Enlightenment naturalist) against Pico (as a defender of spiritual human freedom against stellar determinism) as two poles of Renaissance thought — "In Pomponazzi's *De incantationibus* we have, in a sense, one pole of this movement; in Pico's polemic against astrology the other" (Cassirer, cited p. 516n4). Edelheit shows this polarity is misconceived: Pomponazzi is not a naturalist in the Enlightenment sense, since his cosmology is governed by God, the intelligences, and the heavenly bodies, and he explicitly exempts biblical miracles from naturalistic reduction; and Pico does not simply defend spiritual freedom but rather engages in a thoroughgoing scientific refutation of astrology's foundations using Peripatetic arguments.

**Historiographical register**: Part of the book's overarching project of rehabilitating Renaissance scholasticism against Whiggish narrative — showing that scholastics were not static but dynamically engaged with humanist challenges. Pomponazzi exemplifies the "modification" strategy: absorbing humanist concerns while reconfiguring the traditional framework.

**Concord outcome**: Unlike most chapters in Part 3, this one does not find a concord outcome between Pico and his scholastic interlocutor in the domain of astrology; the divergence is radical and explicitly acknowledged by Pomponazzi himself. The concord is instead found in philosophical anthropology — the shared account of human self-determination and the microcosm — suggesting that Pico's most lasting scholastic influence lay in anthropology rather than in his anti-astrology polemic.

**Scholarly significance**: By proving that Pomponazzi engaged Pico's *Disputationes* directly (via editorial apparatus showing implicit references at multiple pages of *De incantationibus*), Edelheit establishes a new terminus in the reception history of Pico's anti-astrology argument. He also complicates the reception picture by showing that the most sophisticated scholastic respondent did not simply "answer" Pico but developed an entirely different positive philosophy of celestial influence.

## Relation to Pico Works

**Primary text: *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem*, libri I–V, ed. Garin (Florence, 1946).**

The chapter focuses on the following specific passages and chapters:

- **Book I** (p. 50): Pico's claim that Plato, Aristotle, and the ancients all denied celestial causation as understood by astrologers and refused to include astrology among the arts or sciences, lumping it with geomancy, hydromancy, and incantations (*incantationes*). Pomponazzi's treatment of incantations and astrology as legitimate natural sciences is a direct inversion of this claim.

- **Book II, Chapter IV** (*Non esse astrologiam religioni utilem, quod Rogerius Bacon et Petrus Alliacensis existimarunt*, pp. 116–124): Pico argues that since all true things are in agreement, astronomical truth must accord with theological truth; but since astrology is false it cannot accord with theology. The heavens declare God's glory (Psalm 19:2), but astrology contradicts that glory. This is the positive theological anchor for Pico's refutation.

- **Book II, Chapter V** (*Quam noxia sit astrologia quamque pestifera christianae religioni*, pp. 124–134): Pico argues that impiety can arm itself against religion by attributing divine miracles — the deeds of the martyrs, the Apostles, even the resurrection of the dead — to stellar causation. The acts of the Apostles and martyrs exceed what either humans or demons can cause; to attribute them to stars is to destroy religion. Pico also refers to Moses versus Pharaoh's astrologers and closes with a paraphrase of Ecclesiastes 1:2: "Vanity of vanities is astrology, and any superstition is vanity." Pomponazzi's fifth chapter engages precisely this anti-naturalistic argument.

- **Book III, Chapter XXIV** (pp. 384–386): *Occultas vires caelestibus non inesse per quas occultas inferiorum rerum proprietates producant, sed calorem tantum lumenque vivificum.* Pico argues that hidden qualities are absent from the heavens; the only effects celestial bodies produce are heat and life-giving light. Uses magnets and peony flower as examples. This is directly contradicted by Pomponazzi's third chapter on *virtutes occultae* and his broader affirmation of celestial influence.

The editor Compagni's apparatus to *De incantationibus* (cited at p. 14n of the critical edition) provides a list of implicit references to Pico's arguments: pp. 39, 40, 45, 46, 58, 110, 118, 138, 139, 142 of the critical edition. This establishes a dense, if often silent, engagement with the *Disputationes* throughout *De incantationibus*.

**Secondary Pico text: *Oratio de hominis dignitate***: Engaged at the level of philosophical anthropology, not astrology. The account of human self-determination, the microcosm doctrine, and the capacity to ascend or descend the cosmic hierarchy appears nearly verbatim in Pomponazzi's *De immortalitate animae* and *De partibus animalium* commentary. Edelheit identifies this as convergent usage of a shared cultural stock (biblical, classical, patristic, scholastic) rather than certain direct influence.

## Uncertainties

1. **OCR/transcription quality of Pomponazzi Latin quotations**: The source markdown is a PDF-to-markdown conversion. Several Latin passages are long and complex; spot-checking against the critical edition (Compagni, Florence, 2011) would be needed for any publication use. Abbreviations and line-breaks from the original folio edition may have introduced errors.

2. **Page number mapping**: The source markdown uses "## Page NNN" headers that appear to indicate PDF page numbers (e.g., "## Page 527" corresponds to printed p. 516 in the book). The manuscript offset appears to be approximately –11 pages (PDF page 527 = printed page 516). Page citations in this artifact follow the printed page numbers as stated in the chapter's running headers, not the PDF page numbers.

3. **Compagni's identification of *recentes* = Pico, *aliqui* = Bellanti**: This identification comes from Compagni's editorial apparatus (nn. 101–102 in the critical edition) and is treated by Edelheit as authoritative. It should be flagged as editor's attribution, not explicit self-identification by Pomponazzi.

4. **Pomponazzi's knowledge of the *Disputationes* manuscript before 1496 publication**: The chapter does not address whether Pomponazzi could have known the *Disputationes* before its posthumous publication. The text was apparently circulating before Pico's death in 1494; the 1496–1499 studies with Alberto Pio are mentioned as a possible avenue of access.

5. **Scope of the "implicit references" in *De incantationibus***: Compagni's apparatus identifies implicit references to Pico at pp. 39, 40, 45, 46, 58, 110, 118, 138, 139, 142 of the critical edition, but Edelheit does not systematically address each one. A fuller treatment would require working through all of them.
