## Section Function

Chapter 9 is one of the book's central thesis-by-thesis analysis chapters. Its job is to ground Pico's 22 Scotist theses in their precise sources, trace the argumentative logic connecting thesis to text, and thereby reconstruct the intellectual stakes of the 1486 Roman debate that was planned but never held. The chapter does not argue that Pico was a Scotist in any committed doctrinal sense; instead it shows that Pico read Scotus carefully (from the *Lectura* and *Ordinatio* directly, plus the Wadding edition), extracted positions accurately — sometimes abbreviating them to near-unintelligibility — and used Scotist frameworks above all methodologically: Scotus's demand for scriptural evidence becomes a model for Pico's own apologetic practice. A secondary function is contribution to Scotist *Rezeptionsgeschichte* at the cusp of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. The chapter belongs to a sequence (chapters on Aquinas, Scotus, Henry of Ghent, and others) that collectively argues Pico's scholastic formation was pluralist, non-partisan, and oriented toward *concordia* rather than school loyalty.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

Edelheit opens by noting that Pico owned several Scotist works — the *Sentences* commentary, commentaries on Aristotle's *Metaphysics* and *De anima*, the *Quaestiones super universalibus Porphyrii*, the *Quodlibeta*, and the *Opera et tractatus per ordinem alphabeti* — and that every one of the 22 Scotist theses derives from either the *Lectura* or the *Ordinatio* (p. 288). The chapter's method is then applied consistently: for each thesis, Edelheit quotes the Latin of Pico's formulation (from the *900 Theses*), locates the specific *distinquishing* or *question* in Scotus's text, extracts the relevant Latin passage, and assesses how accurately or selectively Pico represents Scotus.

On **theology and grace** (theses 1, 11, 22), Edelheit finds Pico faithfully condensing Scotus's arguments. Thesis 1 (charity is not a habit distinct from the habit of grace) tracks Scotus's *Ordinatio* 1, d. 17 closely: Pico drops the technical scaffolding but preserves the conclusion that *caritas* and *gratia* are really the same habit (pp. 289–290). Thesis 11 (grace is subjectively in the will, not in the soul's essence) derives from *Lectura* 2, d. 26; Pico adds only the adverb *subiective* not found in the source (pp. 303–304). Thesis 22 (habit produces an act as a partial efficient cause) likewise tracks *Ordinatio* 1, d. 17, with the minor terminological shift from Scotus's *causa partialis activa* to Pico's *causa partialis effectiva* (pp. 318–319).

On **Trinitarian theology and Christology** (theses 3, 4, 9, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17), Edelheit's most striking finding concerns thesis 3. The claim that distinguishing the Persons by absolute individual properties is not contrary to Catholic truth comes from an interpolation in *Ordinatio* 1, d. 26, where Scotus argues that Scripture does not say the Persons are distinguished *only* by relational properties, nor should one deny a truth simply because authority has not explicitly transmitted it (pp. 292–293). Edelheit notes this passage is "crucial for understanding one of Pico's main lines of argumentation in the *Apology*": Scotus's principle that authority's silence does not falsify a proposition becomes the backbone of Pico's own apologetics. Thesis 4 (two modes of existence in Christ) draws on *Ordinatio* 3, d. 6, where Scotus distinguishes *duo esse* from *duo entia* and argues that having two natures does not make Christ two beings (pp. 293–295). Thesis 12 (Christ's body was incapable of suffering) tracks *Ordinatio* 4, d. 49, but transposes Scotus's discussion of the *homo beatus* after resurrection to speak directly of the body of Christ (pp. 304–305). Thesis 13 (Original Sin can be removed by God's absolute power without infusing grace) derives from *Ordinatio* 4, d. 1, where Scotus distinguishes between the formal opposition of original justice to Original Sin and the non-formal relation of grace to that sin (p. 305). Thesis 14 (Old Testament ceremonial rites could be observed without sin after Christ's passion) tracks *Ordinatio* 4, d. 3, and Edelheit shows Pico likely found Scotus's argument about the intermediate period between the two Laws and the absence of explicit scriptural prohibition especially congenial — useful ammunition for the *Apology* (pp. 305–307). Thesis 15 (incomplete consecration formula is invalid) tracks *Ordinatio* 4, d. 8, where Scotus's sensitivity to linguistic and historical exactness in the sacramental formula is on display (pp. 307–309).

On **the formal distinction and relations** (theses 16, 17), Edelheit traces a tightly connected pair. Thesis 16 (a creature's relation to God is really the same as its foundation but formally different *ex natura rei*) tracks *Ordinatio* 2, d. 1, q. 5, where Scotus's solution — "a relation with God common to every creature is really the same in foundation; yet it is not formally the same, nor is it precisely the same" — is quoted verbatim (p. 310). Thesis 17 (any other relation is really distinct from its foundation) complements this via *Lectura* 1, d. 31, where Scotus shows that real identity between relation and foundation exists only in God within the Trinity, not in created beings (pp. 311–312).

On **haecceity and individuation** (thesis 6), Edelheit finds Pico citing the Scotist technical term *hecceitas* — which belongs to the fourteenth-century Scotist school, not to Scotus himself — demonstrating that Pico worked with later Scotist commentators alongside primary texts (pp. 297–298).

On **univocity of being** (theses 7, 8, 21), Edelheit reads three theses as forming a conceptual cluster, all drawn from *Lectura* 1, d. 3 ("On the cognition of God"). Thesis 7 (being is predicated univocally of God and creature *in quid*) and thesis 8 (being is not predicated *quiditatively* of its passions or ultimate differences) both derive from Scotus's argument that the concept of being must be univocal — not analogous as Henry of Ghent maintained — because otherwise no concept of substance is available to us, and philosophy is destroyed (pp. 297–301). Thesis 21 (substance is not known through its proper species) emerges from the same discussion: Scotus argues that substance has no proper species in the potential intellect; we reach a concept of substance only by abstracting from species of accidents, and this is possible only if being has a univocal common concept (pp. 317–318). Edelheit notes that Pico does not reproduce the polemical context (Scotus against Henry of Ghent) but extracts the philosophical payoff.

On **intellect, will, and practical knowledge** (theses 5, 10, 19, 20), Edelheit traces a more complex picture. Thesis 5 (*praxis* as operation of a faculty other than intellect, conformably elicited by right reason) derives from the *Lectura* prologue, but Edelheit finds the Wadding edition formulation closer to Pico's wording, noting that Pico's source may have been a manuscript reading unavailable to modern editors (pp. 294–297). The key missing concept in Pico is Scotus's *extensio* — the stretching of practical cognition toward the will's act — which makes Pico's thesis "a little more obscure." The conclusion drawn from this cluster is significant: the will, as the faculty which "stretches," is effectively superior to the intellect as far as practical knowledge is concerned, and the proper domain of virtue is in the superior appetite (thesis 10, pp. 302–303). Theses 19–20 (the act of understanding caused from object and intellect as two partial agents; caused more nobly from intellect than object) are traced by Edelheit through several Scotus discussions; he admits "Pico seems to present here an elaborate variation on a Scotist theme which I could not find in Scotus" (p. 316), making thesis 20 one of the chapter's acknowledged interpretive difficulties.

Thesis 9 (no acquired knowledge in Christ) and thesis 18 (anything can move itself from virtual act to formal act) are more straightforwardly sourced: the former to *Lectura* 3, d. 14, where Scotus argues that infused species exclude the need for acquired knowledge; the latter to *Ordinatio* 1, d. 3, where Scotus's discussion of virtual versus formal act is compressed into Pico's single brief claim (pp. 300–301, 312–313).

Edelheit closes with a strong synthetic paragraph (p. 319): Pico's Scotist theses are, as a group, accurate, grounded in Scotus's texts, and consistent with the tradition of philosophizing *ad mentem Scoti*. More importantly, Scotus's "highly critical mind, his linguistic dexterity, and historical awareness" found "a kindred spirit in Pico." Most pointedly: "Scotus's demand that theological doctrines be supplemented and affirmed by scriptural evidence is very similar to the strategy adopted by Pico in his *Apology*. Indeed, one could say without exaggeration, that in this regard Scotus had a *methodological* impact on Pico."

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: All 22 of Pico's Scotist theses derive from either Scotus's *Lectura* or his *Ordinatio*, not from secondary Scotist literature (with one exception acknowledged) (p. 288).

**Claim 2**: Pico owned at least seven works by or attributed to Scotus, documented in the library inventory compiled by Pearl Kibre (p. 288).

**Claim 3**: Pico's thesis 1 (caritas not a distinct habit from grace) accurately reflects Scotus's *Ordinatio* 1, d. 17, pars 1, q. 1–2, though it drops all supporting argument (pp. 289–290).

**Claim 4**: Pico's thesis 2 (the idea of a stone is the stone produced by the divine intellect in intelligible being) is drawn from Scotus's four-stage account of divine intellection in *Ordinatio* 1, d. 35 (pp. 290–292).

**Claim 5**: Pico's thesis 3 (distinguishing Persons by absolute properties is not contrary to Catholic truth) derives from an editorial interpolation in Scotus's *Ordinatio* 1, d. 26, and its logic — that authority's silence does not falsify a proposition — "is also crucial for understanding one of Pico's main lines of argumentation in the *Apology*" (pp. 292–293).

**Claim 6**: Pico's thesis 4 (two forms of existence in Christ) tracks *Ordinatio* 3, d. 6, where Scotus distinguishes *duo esse* from *duo entia* and argues two natures do not constitute two beings (pp. 293–295).

**Claim 7**: Thesis 5 (*praxis* as the act of a non-intellective faculty conformably elicited by right reason) is closer to the Wadding edition than to the Vatican critical edition of Scotus's *Lectura*, possibly reflecting manuscript readings available to Pico but not preserved in modern editions (pp. 294–297).

**Claim 8**: In the Wadding edition account, the conclusion is explicit that "*praxis* toward which the practical habit is extended is nothing but the act of the will elicited or commanded," making will the ultimate term of practical cognition — a point obscured in Pico's brief formulation (pp. 296–297).

**Claim 9**: Thesis 6 (haecceity as the principle of individuation) uses the term *hecceitas*, which belongs to fourteenth-century Scotist school usage rather than to Scotus himself, demonstrating Pico's use of later Scotist commentators (pp. 297–298).

**Claim 10**: Theses 7 and 8 form a conceptual pair drawn from the same discussion in *Lectura* 1, d. 3 — Pico was "clearly reading this discussion by Scotus while formulating theses 7 and 8," illustrating the *concatenatio* (internal interconnection) between different theses (p. 299).

**Claim 11**: Thesis 7 (being predicated univocally of God and creature) and thesis 21 (substance not known through its proper species) both serve Scotus's anti-analogical argument against Henry of Ghent, but Pico reproduces the conclusions without the polemical target (pp. 297–300, 317–318).

**Claim 12**: Thesis 9 (no acquired knowledge in Christ) is the necessary conclusion of Scotus's argument that infused species make acquired knowledge superfluous, but Pico omits the crucial distinction between infused and acquired knowledge that grounds the argument (pp. 300–301).

**Claim 13**: Thesis 10 (virtues are placed in the superior appetite) derives from *Lectura* 3, d. 33, where Scotus, following Augustine, argues the will (superior appetite) is the proper seat of virtue because contrasting actions — delight and sorrow — cannot coexist in the same faculty (pp. 302–304).

**Claim 14**: Thesis 11 (grace subjectively in the will, not the soul's essence) tracks *Lectura* 2, d. 26; Pico adds only the adverb *subiective* (pp. 303–304).

**Claim 15**: Thesis 12 (Christ's body was incapable of suffering) transposes Scotus's argument about the *homo beatus* post-resurrection to speak of the body of Christ directly — the only notable divergence in this cluster (pp. 304–305).

**Claim 16**: Thesis 13 (Original Sin removable by absolute divine power without grace) reflects Scotus's distinction between formal and non-formal opposition in *Ordinatio* 4, d. 1 (p. 305).

**Claim 17**: Pico's thesis 14 (Old Testament rites preserved without sin after Christ's passion) reflects Scotus's argument about an "intermediate period" between the two Laws, and Edelheit notes "the lack of scriptural evidence explicitly forbidding the preservation of the rites of the first Law" may have been "very appealing to Pico" — directly relevant to the *Apology*'s strategy (pp. 305–307).

**Claim 18**: Thesis 15 (incomplete consecration formula invalid) derives from *Ordinatio* 4, d. 8, where Scotus's linguistic and historical sensitivity to the sacramental formula is on display (pp. 307–309).

**Claim 19**: Thesis 16 (creature's relation to God: really the same as foundation, formally different *ex natura rei*) accurately reproduces the verbal core of Scotus's solution in *Ordinatio* 2, d. 1, q. 5 (pp. 309–311).

**Claim 20**: Thesis 18 (anything can move itself from virtual to formal act) derives from *Ordinatio* 1, d. 3, but Pico uses *actus formalis* where Scotus discusses *potentia ad actum formalem* — a subtle compression (pp. 312–313).

**Claim 21**: Thesis 20 (act of understanding caused more nobly from intellect than from any object short of the beatific object) is "an elaborate variation on a Scotist theme which I could not find in Scotus" — the one thesis in the chapter with no definitive source identification (p. 316).

**Claim 22**: Pico's Scotist theses, taken as a whole, "are accurate, are grounded in the Subtle Doctor's texts, and are in accordance with a tradition of understanding of philosophy and theology *ad mentem Scoti*" (p. 319).

**Claim 23** (synthesis): "Scotus's demand that theological doctrines be supplemented and affirmed by scriptural evidence is very similar to the strategy adopted by Pico in his *Apology*. Indeed, one could say without exaggeration, that in this regard Scotus had a methodological impact on Pico" (p. 319).

## Technical Vocabulary

**haecceitas / hecceitas** (haecceity, thisness): The principle of individuation in Scotist thought — that which makes this individual *this* particular and not merely a member of a species. Pico's thesis 6 uses the term, which strictly belongs to fourteenth-century Scotist school usage, not to Scotus's own vocabulary; the underlying doctrine in Scotus's *Ordinatio* 2, d. 3 speaks of *singularitas signata* as "this determinately." Edelheit takes Pico's use of *hecceitas* as evidence that Pico worked with later Scotist commentators (pp. 297–298).

**univocatio entis** (univocity of being): Scotus's doctrine that the concept "being" applies with a single meaning (*in quid*) to both God and creatures — not analogically as Thomas and Henry of Ghent maintained. The chapter is the central vehicle for establishing this in Pico's theses 7, 8, and 21. Univocity is the prerequisite for any cognition of God in the present life (pp. 297–300, 317–318).

**distinctio formalis ex natura rei** (formal distinction from the nature of the thing): A real but sub-real distinction — less than a real distinction between separate things, more than a merely conceptual distinction — used by Scotus to account for distinctions (e.g., between divine essence and attributes, between the foundation of a relation and the relation itself) without implying composition. Central to theses 16–17 (pp. 309–312) and present in thesis 3 (formal non-identity within the Trinity, pp. 292–293).

**praxis** / **cognitio practica** (practice / practical cognition): In the Scotist account, *praxis* is the act of a faculty other than the intellect (i.e., the will) that is naturally posterior to intellection and conformably elicited by right reason. Practical *cognition* is what is directed toward (*extendi ad*) such an act. Pico's thesis 5 condenses this; the Wadding edition's concept of *extensio* (stretching toward the will's act) is absent in Pico (pp. 294–297).

**habitus** (habit): Scotus's analysis of habit as partial efficient cause (thesis 22) treats it as concurring alongside the potency — with the potency as first cause, the habit as second — to produce a more perfect effect than either could alone. Pico shifts *activa* (active cause) to *effectiva* (efficient cause) (pp. 318–319).

**esse simpliciter / esse secundum quid** (being simply / being according to something): Scotus's (Averroistic) division of being into substance and accident, invoked in the Christology of thesis 4. The human nature of Christ has *esse simpliciter* (substantial existence) in itself, distinct from Christ's divine *esse* (pp. 293–295).

**concatenatio** (internal connection between theses): Edelheit's term (p. 299) for the structural interconnection Pico builds between different theses from the same Scotist discussion. Theses 7 and 8 both derive from *Lectura* 1, d. 3 and form a deliberate conceptual sequence.

**actus virtualis / actus formalis** (virtual act / formal act): Scotus's distinction between having a power or capacity in a virtual (implicit, potential) manner and exercising it formally (actually). Pico's thesis 18 compresses Scotus's claim that there is no contradiction between being virtually X in actuality and being formally X in potency — i.e., anything can move itself from virtual to formal actualization (pp. 312–313).

**ens in quid** (being predicated quiditatively): The *quid* (what-predication) is essential predication. The claim of thesis 7 — *Ens dicitur de Deo et creatura univoce in quid* — means "being" names the essential category (*what*) attributed univocally to both (pp. 297–300).

**potentia absoluta Dei** (absolute power of God): The distinction between God's ordered power (operating within the framework of His decisions) and absolute power (God's capacity to act otherwise than He has ordained) is the framework for thesis 13 — God can by absolute power remit Original Sin without conferring grace (p. 305).

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Guide scholar**: Edelheit operates as a rigorous contextualizer — his self-assigned task is source identification and faithful reconstruction of argument, not evaluation of doctrinal truth or theological originality. He consistently notes where Pico's condensation loses essential distinctions (thesis 5: *extensio* missing; thesis 9: infused/acquired distinction missing; thesis 20: no identifiable Scotus passage).

**Myth being tested**: The implicit myth is the picture of Pico as eclectic syncretist who skims scholastic sources for striking formulations without genuine comprehension. Edelheit tests this by checking accuracy. Verdict: Pico is largely accurate; he condenses and sometimes strips context, but he does not misrepresent. The exception (thesis 20) is noted honestly.

**Historiographical register**: Intellectual history with source-critical emphasis; the chapter belongs to a tradition of Quellenforschung (source research) in Renaissance philosophy. Edelheit also situates the chapter within *Rezeptionsgeschichte* of late-medieval Scotism (footnote 1 references Porro/Schmutz, Duba, Hoenen).

**Concord outcome**: Unlike chapters where Edelheit shows Pico creatively harmonizing competing schools (Thomism vs. Scotism), chapter 9 is less about concord between schools and more about the methodological affinity between Scotus's critical biblicism and Pico's apologetics. The synthesis is hermeneutical rather than metaphysical.

## Updates to Existing Writing

**Scholar profile (Scotus)**: If a profile exists or is being drafted, this chapter establishes that Scotus's influence on Pico was (a) textually accurate, not impressionistic; (b) primarily from the *Lectura* and *Ordinatio*, not from later Scotist summaries (with the haecceity exception); and (c) above all *methodological* — Scotus's biblicism shapes Pico's apologetics. The profile should explicitly distinguish the Scotus-Pico connection from mere "Scotist school" influence.

## Uncertainties

1. **OCR/transcription fidelity**: The source is a PDF-to-Markdown conversion. Latin quotations from Scotus appear intact (cross-checked against standard references), but minor transcription errors in Latin are possible in footnotes not fully verified here.

2. **Thesis 20 source**: Edelheit explicitly states he could not find the precise Scotist source for thesis 20 in Scotus's texts (p. 316). This is an acknowledged gap, not an OCR problem.

3. **Wadding edition divergences (thesis 5)**: Edelheit suggests the Wadding edition may preserve manuscript readings closer to Pico's source than the Vatican critical edition. This is a hypothesis, not a demonstrated fact; it depends on which manuscripts were available to Pico and which readings they contained.

4. **Library inventory dating**: The Kibre inventory of Pico's library reflects the holdings at one point in time; it is not certain all listed works were present and used during the preparation of the *900 Theses* specifically.

5. **The *hecceitas* attribution**: Edelheit says the term is a "product of the Scotist school in the fourteenth century" rather than Scotus's own (p. 298). The precise identification of the secondary Scotist source Pico used is not specified.
