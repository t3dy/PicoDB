## Section Function

Chapter 6 is the culminating chapter of Howlett's argument, synthesizing the cosmological and metaphysical framework built across Chapters 1–5 into an account of the human soul's return to God. The chapter answers the question that the earlier material prepares: given Pico's universe of emanated worlds, synchronized Aristotelian, Platonic, and Kabbalistic structures, and astrological skepticism about external compulsion, what is the human being's ultimate vocation, and how does it achieve it?

The chapter performs two simultaneous tasks. First, it dismantles the modern reading of Pico as a proto-humanist celebrating self-determination and "human dignity" in a secular sense, insisting instead that the Oration's famous passage on human indeterminacy is a mystical and theological text about the possibility of ascent to God for exceptional philosophical individuals. Second, it reconstructs Pico's positive vision in detail: the anatomy of the human soul (body/spirit/soul; senses/reason/intellect; correspondence to the ten sefirot), the question of the soul's immortality, and two interlocking ascent programs — Diotima's Ladder from the *Commento* and Jacob's Ladder from the *Oration* and *Heptaplus* — which Howlett reads as complementary articulations of the same mystical itinerary. The chapter closes by distinguishing Pico's purely contemplative, inward, and ultimately death-directed asceticism from Ficino's more activist model of the returner who brings cosmological vision back into the world.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

**Mutability and Self-Fashioning.** Howlett opens by positioning the human being as the microcosm of a macrocosm: we contain *semina* — seeds of every kind of life — capable of actualizing any state of being from plant to angel. This positive anthropology is immediately subjected to a critical corrective. The famous Oration passage in which God tells Adam "We have made you neither of heaven nor of earth, neither mortal nor immortal" (p. 175) has been misread as a proclamation of secular self-determination. Howlett, following Copenhaver, argues it is a call to transcend the human world, not a celebration of it. She traces the dignitas tradition through Giannozzo Manetti (1452), Bartolommeo Facio (c. 1447), and Petrarch, finding Pico's text belongs to none of these as its primary genre. The "modern ring" Kristeller acknowledged but hedged (p. 172, n. 8) is, she argues, an anachronism: Pico is not discussing modern human dignity because, as Copenhaver notes, Kant invented that concept (p. 198, n. 16). Instead, Pico uses the trope of the indeterminate image (*indiscretae opus imaginis*) theologically: we have no fixed nature because we are called to become God, who is beyond all representation. Self-fashioning (*plastes et factor*) is not aesthetic self-creation but the theological-moral capacity to ascend or descend through the worlds by activating different seeds within us, enabled by God's grace. The Proteus and chameleon metaphors carry negative connotations in their classical sources (Aristotle, Plutarch), though Idel finds a positive Jewish parallel through Ibn al-Sid al-Batalyawsi and Alemanno (p. 172, n. 6).

Howlett grants limited literal force to Pico's language of becoming-animal or becoming-angel, noting that Plotinus in *Enneads* I.II and Kabbalistic sources (Bahir, Recanati's *Commentary on the Torah*) available through Flavius Mithridates' translations support actual transmigration of souls (p. 176). Pico's citation of Enoch's transformation into Metatron is one such literal case. But the dominant sense is moral-internal: becoming "brutish" means living by the senses, becoming "angelic" means purifying one's intellect. The mimetic process of emanation that forms the macrocosm is repeated in a second mimetic process in the human soul, reversing the scholastic formula *operari sequitur esse* — what we do determines what we become, not the other way around (p. 177).

**Our Anatomy.** Pico's anthropology is explicitly synthetic. The human soul is structured as a binary (eternal soul / corruptible body), a triad (body / soul / spirit after Ficino, but also intelligence / reason / senses), and a tetraktys (sensation, opinion, knowledge, contemplation). Most distinctively, in the *Conclusions* and *Disputations*, Pico maps the parts of the soul onto the ten sefirot: as a unified soul we are Keter, the Crown; our intellect corresponds to Chokmah (the second sefirah); our reason to Binah (the third); and so on downward through the faculties (p. 179, Conclusions 11 > 66). Abraham Abulafia uses the same correspondence.

The intelligence (*intelligenza*) is the highest faculty and corresponds to the angelic mind. Pico uses optics as his analogy: the intellect needs illumination just as the eye needs light, and "intelligible truth is light." Corporeal and intellectual vision are parallel: Moses, Paul, and the elect saw God's face with intellectual sight, which Pico identifies with what the theologians call intuitive cognition (*cognitio intuitiva*). Whether the intellect is active or passive — an Aristotelian problem with Averroist stakes — is left deliberately ambiguous. Howlett identifies three possibilities Pico may be holding simultaneously: (1) the intellect is active and abstracts upward in classical Aristotelian fashion; (2) the intellect is passive and must wait for illumination by a universal active intellect (quasi-Averroism), so God must reach down; or (3) the particular mind's upper level can be activated into an active intellect by God's grace, enabling it to abstract and ascend. This ambiguity is not a flaw but a deliberate syncretic bridge between Aristotelianism, Neoplatonism, and Kabbalistic noetics.

Connecting body and soul is *spiritus*, the luminous body described in the *Heptaplus* via Avicenna: just as every virtue of the heavens is conveyed by light, every power of the soul is transferred to the earthly body through the medium of this luminous spirit (p. 181). The rational soul in circular motion — reason joined to intellect — becomes "heaven"; the body is "earth"; spirit is the light that joins them.

**Immortality of the Soul.** The chapter addresses the Averroist problem directly: Aristotle is ambiguous about individual soul immortality; Alexander of Aphrodisias reads the *De anima* as leaving the soul mortal; Averroes removes all incorruptible elements from the individual soul. Soul immortality was not official Church doctrine until the Lateran Council (1512, denouncing Averroism) and the Council of Trent (1545). Ficino's defense of immortality in the *Theologia platonica* was a conscious anti-Averroist gesture following the Byzantine philosopher George Gemistus Pletho (Kristeller quoted at p. 181). Pico's problem is that his syncretic program requires bridging Averroism with the Platonic-Christian doctrine of immortality, but this argument, Howlett notes flatly, was "left unmade" by Pico (p. 181, citing Kristeller). In practice, Pico follows Ficino's deeply held belief in immortality: the soul, being incorporeal, is subject not to fate (which rules only temporal, corporeal things) but to providence, with the prospect of a divine destiny.

**Ascent.** The second half of the *Oration* is not an encomium on philosophy as such but on the mystical ascent of the soul through the union of philosophy, theology, and religious practice. Pico instructs his reader to "disdain the celestial" and fly to the "otherworldly court" near the most eminent Destiny (p. 183, Oration §§54–57). The cherubim, seraphim, and thrones mark stages: the cherub (intelligence/contemplation) allows movement toward the Godhead; the seraph (love/charity) burns with divine fire; the throne is associated with the active life. The soul that burns for God with the love of charity will become seraphic: "He who is a seraph, that is, a lover, is in God and God is in him; indeed, he and God are one" (*Oration* §61).

Henosis — union with the divine — requires both human ascent and divine descent: God's grace activates our will, providing the "motive power" without which the circuit cannot close. We cannot go, but can be "drawn" (*Heptaplus*, p. 151). Grace is described as "rays" that make humans and angels pleasing to God. Felicity (*foelicitas*) is this supernatural state received from God, identified with the Plotinian One, but also more specifically with Keter (rather than the Ein-Sof, about which Howlett is precise: the soul cleaves to Keter, not to what is beyond Keter, pp. 193–194). The ascent curriculum, following Iamblichus and Pseudo-Dionysius, runs: moral philosophy (catharsis/purification) → dialectics (clearing the darkness of reason) → natural philosophy (illumination) → theology → religious experience/theurgy leading to unification (p. 192). This maps onto the Pseudo-Dionysian *triplex via* of *purgatio*, *illuminatio*, and *unitio*.

Kabbalah enters the ascent at every level: Abulafia's Aristotelian Kabbalistic interpretation of Jacob's Ladder describes the Active Intellect "impregnating" human souls so that gematria and letter-manipulation can enable understanding (Binah). Rather than ascending to the Active Intellect (Chokmah), the mystic is infused with the divine presence (shekinah) when reaching Binah, and thereby "literally becomes the Agent Intellect" (Copenhaver quoted at p. 194). Idel argues both Pico and Alemanno believe the soul can cleave to the Ein-Sof; Howlett disagrees, reading Pico's language as pointing to cleaving to Keter as the First Father (p. 194).

**Love and Beauty.** The *Commento* provides the first ascent path: Diotima's Ladder from Plato's *Symposium*. Pico takes the six-stage structure seriously as a philosophical itinerary. Stage 1: loving corporeal beauty, recognizing the link between beauty and the good. Stage 2: forming an internal image of the beloved, more beautiful than the actual beloved through virtue. Stage 3: Aristotelian abstraction from particular beauty to the concept of corporeal beauty generally — "the last step reached by the soul dominated by the senses" (*Commento*, p. 96). Stage 4: the soul recognizes beauty as a universal rather than a particular, and that universal beauty derives from the soul's own "inner light and power" rather than from matter. Stage 5: ascending to the upper intellect, the soul finds the image of the Idea of beauty "in the mirror of its intellect," seeing the Heavenly Venus in her true but not complete guise. Stage 6: the soul ascends from the image to the Idea itself, where it may rest at the side of the First Father. This sixth step is as far as mortals may go; the seventh — the "sabbath" of heavenly love — is not available in the body (p. 186, *Commento*, p. 97).

Howlett identifies two key differences between Pico's and Ficino's Diotima's Ladder. First, Pico replaces Ficino's Platonic anamnesis (corporeal beauty reminding us of innate ideas) with an Aristotelian abstraction: beauty is abstracted from sensory particulars by reason, before the Platonic Idea appears as the endpoint. This is the syncretic bridge Jayne identifies (p. 204, n. 123). Second, and crucially, Pico alters Benivieni's poem so that the final stage reaches only "near" the uncreated Sun, not the Sun itself — the ascent falls one step short of full union with God. Three kinds of love correspond to three Venuses (earthly, celestial, heavenly) and to three types of desire (appetitive/bestial, rational/human, intellectual/angelic). Bodily beauty is caused by the visible heavens and animated by the rational soul; its "grace" (*gratia*) — a mysterious quality beyond the visible — is transfused by the soul into the earthly body. To mistake corporeal beauty for its own source is to descend to the animal; to read it correctly is to begin ascending.

Full ascent through love requires death. A temporary "first death" (ecstasy or trance) allows brief vision of the Heavenly Venus; permanent possession requires "the second death," the complete separation of soul from body, described in erotic terms as an "indissoluble knot" and a "kiss" (*Commento*, pp. 88–89). This death is the *mors osculi* — the "kiss of death" (Hebrew *mitat neshikah*, Italian *binsica*) — which Pico derives from Talmudic discussions of Song of Songs 1:2 and Maimonides' *Guide for the Perplexed*, and which he applies in both the *Commento* and the *Conclusions* (Conclusion 11 > 13: "Whoever operates in the Cabala without the mixture of anything extraneous, if he is long in the work, will die from *binsica*"). Gersonides' Commentary on Song of Songs provides the specific formulation; Andreatta reads the *mors osculi* as "exemplary" of the confluence of Aristotelian-Hebrew and Kabbalistic currents (p. 202, n. 182).

**Jacob's Ladder.** The second ascent path is explicitly Kabbalistic. Christ's sacrifice has "torn the veil" of the Holy of Holies and opened the way to the supracelestial world for all who follow. This is Pico's "seventh or sabbatical exposition" in the *Heptaplus*: an interpretative key returned to us through Christ, which allows the return (*felicity*) "granted to man" through Mosaic and Christian law. The ladder has specific rungs: becoming cherubim (contemplation, access to the angelic mind), then seraphim (proximity to God, the sphere of love). Pico follows here the pattern of Pseudo-Dionysius, Iamblichus, and Proclus more than the active Kabbalism of Alemanno. Copenhaver's formulation — that the *Oration* is "a mystic's manifesto for ascent, by way of magic and Kabbalah, to self-annihilating union with God" (p. 192) — captures Howlett's reading precisely.

**Ficino versus Pico.** The chapter's sharpest analytical move is the contrast with Ficino. Ficino's soul rides the chariot of the soul to the edge of the celestial sphere, glimpses the world of forms, and returns bearing creative Ideas (*blueprints*) that can be used to initiate *renovatio* — the transformation of the terrestrial world toward a new Golden Age (p. 191). Pico's ascent is one-directional: his Jacob's Ladder has angels going up and down, but Pico's emphasis is entirely on upward movement. The "descent" of higher beings into lower, Pico says in the *Commento*, is not a real descent but a drawing upward — the higher cannot truly go lower (p. 193). There is no return, no blueprint, no magico-practical transformation of the world. Magic appears briefly in the *Oration* as natural philosophy (theurgic magic, the *magus* as reader of the universe's sympathies), but by Chapter 6 Howlett judges Pico's actual engagement with operative magic "very limited, to the point where it simply seems like mysticism" (p. 196). The mystic takes the universe within him as he ascends, but does not return to transform it. Pico's goal is strictly contemplative, individual, and eschatological: "the solitary journey of the exceptional individual" (p. 197).

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: Pico's Oration passage on human indeterminacy is not a proclamation of human dignity in any modern sense but "a call to transcend the human world" (Copenhaver), misread by moderns as humanism (p. 172).

**Claim 2**: Pico's concept of self-fashioning (*plastes et factor*) depends on God's grace, not autonomous will; our freedom is "the motive power of grace" given to us, not generated by us (p. 175).

**Claim 3**: The human being is the "indeterminate image" (*indiscretae opus imaginis*) because it is "called to become God, Who is beyond all representation" — not indeterminate in the sense of mere contingency (p. 175).

**Claim 4**: Transmigration of the soul is both literally and symbolically available in Pico, drawing on Plotinus (*Enneads* I.II), Bahir, Recanati, and Pythagoreanism; but the dominant valence in Pico is moral-internal rather than literal physical reincarnation (pp. 176–177).

**Claim 5**: The mimetic process of self-fashioning reverses the scholastic formula *operari sequitur esse*; what we do constitutes what we become (p. 177).

**Claim 6**: Pico maps the parts of the soul onto the ten sefirot in *Conclusions* 11 > 66; the unified soul corresponds to Keter and the intellect to Chokmah (p. 179).

**Claim 7**: The status of the intellect as active or passive is deliberately ambiguous in Pico, serving as a syncretic bridge between Aristotelianism, Neoplatonism, and Kabbalistic noetics (pp. 179–180).

**Claim 8**: Soul immortality was not official Church doctrine at Pico's time; his argument bridging Averroism with immortality was "left unmade" (p. 181).

**Claim 9**: The second half of the Oration is an encomium on the mystical ascent of the soul through the union of philosophy, theology, and religious practice, not on philosophy as an intellectual discipline per se (p. 183).

**Claim 10**: Henosis in Pico requires divine grace to initiate; "we cannot go, but can be drawn" (*Heptaplus*, p. 151, quoted p. 183).

**Claim 11**: Pico's ascent curriculum follows Iamblichus' four-stage model: moral philosophy → dialectics → natural philosophy → theology, mapped onto the Pseudo-Dionysian *triplex via* (p. 193).

**Claim 12**: Pico's ultimate target in the Kabbalistic ascent is Keter, not the Ein-Sof; Howlett disagrees with Idel's claim that Pico believes the soul can cleave to the Ein-Sof (pp. 193–194).

**Claim 13**: Pico alters Benivieni's poem in the *Commento* so that the ascent through love reaches only "near" God (Keter as First Father), not God himself (p. 186).

**Claim 14**: Pico distinguishes three stages of love — bestial/appetitive, human/rational, angelic/intellectual — corresponding to three Venuses (earthly, celestial, heavenly) (p. 188).

**Claim 15**: The *mors osculi* (Kabbalistic "kiss of death") is the final stage of the Kabbalist's journey in both the *Commento* and *Conclusions*; it represents the soul's dissolution into intellectual ecstasy and emancipation from materiality (pp. 194–195).

**Claim 16**: Pico diverges from Ficino by refusing the return: the ascended soul does not come back bearing blueprints for *renovatio*. Pico's focus is entirely on ascent and permanent rest (pp. 191, 195–197).

**Claim 17**: Magic in Pico, after his Rome phase, becomes essentially synonymous with mysticism; it is not an operative technology for transforming the world (p. 196).

**Claim 18**: The *Oration*'s concept of "human dignity" is properly understood as a "mystical vocation" belonging to "exceptional individuals" capable of the contemplative ascent, not to humanity in general (p. 184).

## Technical Vocabulary

**binsica** (Italian, from Hebrew *mitat neshikah*, "death from a kiss"): Pico's Italian transcription of the Kabbalistic "kiss of death"; the soul's dissolution into intellectual ecstasy leading to permanent separation from the body; used in both *Commento* and *Conclusions* (p. 194).

**cherubim/cherub**: In Pico, the angelic order corresponding to intelligence and contemplation; model for the contemplative philosopher; represents the first stage of angelic proximity (p. 183).

**Ein-Sof**: Kabbalistic term for the infinite, hidden God beyond all sefirot; Howlett argues Pico's soul reaches Keter (the highest sefirah) rather than the Ein-Sof (p. 194).

**felicitas / foelicitas** (Latin, "felicity"): Pico's term for the supernatural state received from God at the completion of the ascent; identified with Plotinian henosis and with residence near Keter (p. 183).

**germina / semina** (Latin, "seeds/sprouts"): The seeds of all forms of life infused into the human being at birth (Oration §27); activated by moral choice to determine what kind of being one becomes (p. 172).

**gematria**: Jewish numerological hermeneutic; used by Abulafia and referenced by Pico as a method enabling ascent through understanding (Binah) (p. 194).

**grace (gratia)**: God's freely given power enabling human ascent; without it, the soul cannot "go in a circle and come back upon itself"; described as "rays" and as "motive power" (*Heptaplus* p. 151) (pp. 175, 183).

**henosis**: Greek term for union with the divine One; Pico's adaptation combines Plotinian metaphysics, Pseudo-Dionysian mysticism, and Kabbalistic imagery of cleaving to Keter; distinguished from noesis (pure intellectual vision) but the two overlap (pp. 183, 194–195).

**indiscretae opus imaginis** (Latin, "work of indeterminate/undecided image"): Howlett's and Copenhaver's translation of the Oration phrase describing the human being as having no fixed form; theologically motivated by the human's call to become God (p. 175).

**intelligentia / intellectus**: The highest faculty of the soul; corresponds to Chokmah in the sefirot; connected to the angelic mind; active or passive depending on the Aristotelian-Averroist debate; the faculty that enables divine vision (pp. 179–180).

**Jacob's Ladder**: Central Kabbalistic and biblical metaphor for the soul's ascent through stages from earth to God; angels ascending and descending; the primary image for Pico's mystical itinerary in the *Oration* and *Heptaplus* (pp. 184, 192–194).

**Keter**: Hebrew, "Crown"; first and highest sefirah; identified by Pico with God as "First Father" and ultimate resting-place of the soul after death; Howlett argues this is Pico's genuine endpoint rather than the Ein-Sof (pp. 183, 186, 194).

**mors osculi** (Latin, "death of the kiss"; Hebrew *mitat neshikah*): The "kiss of death" — the mystical union of the soul with God at the moment of death; drawn from Talmudic and Maimonidean sources; used by Pico as the culmination of both Diotima's Ladder and Jacob's Ladder (pp. 194–195).

**multipotentiality**: Howlett's term (via Trinkaus) for the Renaissance trope of human capacity to actualize any form of being; she reads it as the correct (limited) sense of Pico's "self-fashioning" (p. 172).

**noesis**: Greek for intellectual vision; touching the Active Intellect or Chokmah; Pico may hold both noesis (achievable in the body, temporarily) and henosis (requiring death) as endpoints, depending on the Kabbalistic reading (p. 194).

**operari sequitur esse** (Latin scholastic formula, "action follows being"): Pico's self-fashioning reverses this: what we do constitutes what we become (p. 177).

**plastes et factor** (Latin, "moulder and sculptor"): Pico's description of the human being in the Oration (§22); denotes the capacity for self-fashioning; Mahoney notes three near-synonymous terms Pico uses (p. 176).

**sefirot** (singular sefirah): The ten divine emanations in Kabbalistic cosmology; Pico maps the faculties of the soul onto them in *Conclusions* 11 > 66; the unified soul corresponds to Keter (p. 179).

**semina** — see germina above.

**shekinah**: Hebrew, divine presence; in Pico, the soul rises with the shekinah from Malkut to Keter; the divine presence infuses the soul reaching Binah (pp. 183, 194).

**spiritus**: Latin, the luminous pneumatic body connecting soul and gross body; corresponds to the Avicennan vehicle conveying celestial virtue to earth; Ficino's triad of body/spiritus/soul (p. 181).

**temurah**: Hebrew letter-permutation technique; used by Abulafia in conjunction with gematria for mystical ascent (p. 194).

**triplex via** (Latin, "threefold path"): Pseudo-Dionysius's three stages of mystical ascent: *purgatio* (purification), *illuminatio* (illumination through contemplation), *unitio* or *perfectio* (union with God) (p. 193).

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Primary thesis being tested**: The "self-fashioning" thesis (Greenblatt / Burckhardt-Garin tradition) as applied to Pico's Oration. Howlett joins Copenhaver in decisively rejecting this reading. The Oration is not a Renaissance manifesto for secular self-determination; it is a mystical programme for exceptional contemplatives seeking union with God.

**Myth being dismantled**: The "Renaissance Man" / "dignity of man" reading — the idea that Pico is celebrating humankind's creative autonomy and universal potential. Howlett traces this misreading through Burckhardt → Garin (who sidelined Kabbalah and foregrounded Pseudo-Dionysius) → Italian fascist scholarship (Gentile) → Anglo-American existentialist readings → popular imagination.

**Historiographical register**: Revisionist/corrective. Howlett positions herself with Copenhaver's 2019 monograph against the Burckhardt-Garin consensus. She is less combative than Copenhaver but more systematic: she works through the anthropological, psychological, and soteriological elements in sequence to reconstruct the positive vision underneath the dismantled myth.

**Concord outcome**: The chapter shows that Pico's three pillars (Platonism, Aristotelianism, Kabbalah) are not merely juxtaposed but structurally synthesized in the ascent program: the Aristotelian active intellect provides the noetic mechanism; Platonic henosis provides the unitive goal; Kabbalah provides the interpretative key (sefirot, mors osculi, shekinah) that makes the journey concrete. The synthesis is held together by Pseudo-Dionysian Christian mysticism as the explicit pattern. The concord remains incomplete (the Averroist immortality bridge was never built) but the aspiration is coherent and consistent across all Pico's major works.

**Key interpretive move**: Howlett distinguishes Pico from Ficino not at the level of sources (both use Plotinus, Plato, Pseudo-Dionysius) but at the level of goal: Ficino's soul returns to transform the world; Pico's soul ascends and does not return. This makes Pico's position structurally more "monastic" and less "humanist" than Ficino's, despite sharing a Florentine milieu and many textual sources.

## Relation to Pico Works

**Oration on the Dignity of Man** is the primary text of the chapter. Howlett works through it systematically: §§17–23 (human indeterminacy, self-fashioning, transmigration); §§37–42 (moral becoming, symbolic animality/angelhood); §§54–62 (seraph/cherub/throne; goal of burning in divine love); §§70–82 (Jacob's Ladder passage; ascent curriculum: moral philosophy/dialectics/natural philosophy/theology); §92 (Platonic frenzies and rapture); §97 (study of philosophy as contemplation of death); §§214–233 (theurgic magic).

**Heptaplus** is the secondary text, providing the most sustained philosophical anthropology: God's speech to Adam (the "seeds of all life" passage, pp. 120–21); the soul-body-spirit anatomy (pp. 118–21); the triads of unity (p. 139); the soul as "bond and union" of three worlds (p. 134); the fourth book (Christ as human, and as our book, pp. 125–26); the "motive power of grace" passage (p. 151–52).

**Commento sopra una canzone de amore** provides the love-and-beauty ascent (Diotima's Ladder), discussed in greatest detail in the "Love and Beauty" section: the six stages of the ladder (pp. 96–99); the mors osculi and "second death" (pp. 86–89); grace transfused by the soul into the earthly body (pp. 94, 98–99); the kiss as mutual soul-transfer (p. 150).

**Conclusiones nongentae** supplies the mors osculi as Kabbalistic conclusion (Conclusion 11 > 13) and the soul-sefirot mapping (Conclusion 11 > 66) and the circular motion of the soul when assimilated to the intellect (23.6).

**De ente et uno** provides a brief citation on self-knowledge and the brutish (p. 118).

**Sonetti** (Sonnet 32) illustrates the ascetic flight from earthly love in Pico's lyric poetry.

## Uncertainties

1. **OCR quality**: The source file is a PDF-to-Markdown conversion. Latin quotations appear accurate in spot-checks but diacritics and ligatures in Pico's Latin may have been lost. The long Latin quotations (Oration §§22, 30, 42, 71–72, 74, 82, 97) appear complete in the notes section (lines 9094–9563) and can be verified against the Opera Omnia artifact.

2. **Footnote-to-page correlation**: The Markdown conversion uses `## Page N` headers, which Howlett's page numbering begins at 171 (rendered as Markdown pages 179+). The page offset is approximately +8 pages from the book's pagination to the Markdown page counter. Claims have been cited to Howlett's stated book pages (171–208) throughout.

3. **Idel citation at p. 194**: Howlett cites Idel (1988, 206–7) for the claim that both Pico and Alemanno believe the soul can cleave to the Ein-Sof, then disputes it. The 1988 reference appears to be *Kabbalah: New Perspectives*. The specific passage was not directly verifiable in this session.

4. **Aasdalen citation (p. 203, n. 117)**: Refers to "two fourteenth century commentators, Dino del Garbo and Giles of Rome" on Cavalcanti's original poem — a detail about the tradition Pico is writing against that may be worth verifying for accuracy.

5. **"Psychic" at p. 172 (line 7888)**: Howlett uses "psychic" in a technical sense ("the psychic equivalent of that being") meaning soul-like/pertaining to the soul (*psyche*), not supernatural perception. The OCR appears correct; the usage reflects Plotinian terminology.
