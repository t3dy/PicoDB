## Section Function

Chapter 5 is the cosmological centerpiece of Howlett's book. Having established Pico's three "pillars" (Aristotelianism, Kabbalism, Platonism) in earlier chapters, this chapter tests their concordance by mapping the full structure of Pico's universe — from the hidden God down through the angelic mind, celestial world, and natural world. The chapter's argumentative function is twofold: (1) to show that Pico's cosmology is a structurally original synthesis rather than a simple eclectic mix, primarily Ficinian Platonism inflected by Proclus and revised at every level by Kabbalistic and Aristotelian pressures; (2) to expose the fundamental tensions that concordance generates — between apophatic and kataphatic theology, between emanation and creation, between fate and free will — as productive contradictions rather than failures. This sets up Chapter 6 ("The Way Home"), which addresses the question of ascent and human transformation that this cosmology makes possible.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

Howlett opens by using Ficino's Christian Plotinian universe as the baseline from which Pico's distinctiveness can be measured. The basic Ficinian tripartite structure — angelic mind (supracelestial), celestial world (anima mundi), and subcelestial nature — derives from Plotinus's three hypostases (intellect, soul, nature). Pico adopts this scaffold but amplifies it through Proclus, producing a universe of triadic repetition: three worlds each internally divided into triads, a structure "reminiscent of the triads of the gnostic aeons and Pythagoras' triangle" (p. 137). The universe is also radically interconnected — whatever is in any one world is, in some mode, present in each other world — a principle Pico derives from the Heptaplus and which grounds his "science of allegorical interpretation": the early Fathers could represent one thing by another because trained in the hidden alliances of all nature (p. 138). The Heptaplus itself anatomizes Genesis 1–2 as a theophanic cosmic code, and the seven days of creation map onto the three worlds: the intelligible/angelic world is eternal and unchanging; the celestial is compounded of light and darkness, stable in life but variable in activity; the sublunary is the world of darkness, water, alternation, corruption (pp. 138–39). The seventh day of rest figures creation's circular return to the One.

**The Question of God.** Pico's God is structurally twofold, a duality that Howlett unpacks as the Kabbalistic distinction between the Ein-Sof and Keter. The Ein-Sof is the hidden God of apophatic theology: infinite, self-enclosed, utterly removed — "the abstract and uncommunicated unity" of the Conclusions (p. 140). Keter is the partially revealed divine, the crown on the head of God, through which the creative/emanatory chain begins on the Tree of Life. Keter participates in the Ein-Sof's hiddenness but is also the "beginning of all" (the Father, the numerical "one," the "coordinated unity" containing plenitude). Howlett reads Pico's discussions in *On Being and the One* and the *Conclusions* as oscillating between these two registers without always marking the transition clearly, which produces apparent contradictions: God described as "above being" (Ein-Sof) and simultaneously as "being itself" (Keter, or even Aristotle's First Cause as the causal source of being). The distinction between causal, formal, and participatory being is the key: God as First Cause contains being causally (not formally), which is why God can be said to have being without having formal being — a point Pico makes explicitly against Ficino at *Commentary* 11–12 (p. 143). Pico's argument in *On Being and the One* that Plato and Aristotle agree that the One contains being is thus read as referring to Keter (the plenitude, the "coordinated unity") rather than to the Ein-Sof, and Pico's reading of the *Parmenides* as a "dialectical exercise" rather than doctrine is described as a "weak parrying of an inconvenient text" (p. 143).

**God's Love.** Ficino's universe is held together by the circuitus spiritualis, a circle of spirit connecting God's love for creation and creation's desire for God. Pico significantly revises both terms of this exchange. God's love for his creatures is pure volition, not desire: God is self-sufficient, lacking nothing, and the love attributed to God in Pico's account is not the Platonic eros for beauty but rather an act of pure giving (*Commentary* 33, p. 147). Our love for God is more complex: Pico defines beauty as a "proper mixture and just proportion" arising from imperfection and compositeness, meaning nothing simple can be beautiful, and therefore "there is no beauty in God" (*Commentary* 39, pp. 147–48). Beauty is a subspecies of the good, not its co-equal (contra Ficino). The good, which is God, is the object of a natural desire or appetite that operates prior to cognition: we do not need to know God in order to desire God, because all creatures naturally desire what makes them happy, and that is ultimately God. This explains the compatibility of apophatic theology with the devotional life: we can love God without being able to say what God is. Love is above intellect (*Conclusions* 5 > 6: "Love is said by Orpheus to be without eyes, because it is above the intellect," p. 148). Crucially, beauty — since it requires compositeness — can only arise in the angelic mind and below, not in the hidden God. Divine beauty therefore resides in the first emanated world.

**The Angelic Mind.** When the First Cause acts (in Kabbalistic terms: Keter enters the void), the first hypostasis is created — the supracelestial or intellectual world, the angelic mind. In Kabbalistic terms, this world corresponds to the first triad of sefirot: Keter (throne), Hokmah (incomprehensible wisdom), and Binah (understanding that gives wisdom form). Howlett identifies this first triad with the Christian Trinity: Father (alef), Son (bet), Holy Ghost. Within this triad, the relationship between Keter and Hokmah is especially significant. Hokmah — divine wisdom — is associated with "beginning" throughout early Kabbalism. Pico's *Conclusions* 28.25 equates "bereshit" (in the beginning) with "in wisdom" (*b'Hokmah*), and *Heptaplus* explicitly identifies "the Son of God, who is the beginning through which all things were made" with "the wisdom of the Father" (p. 150). Howlett surveys Alemanno, Nahmanides, Recanati, the Targum Yerushalmi, and the Bahir to establish the range of opinion Pico had access to: Hokmah as the "beginning of all that is attainable," Keter as before the beginning (the "alef" preceding reshit) but causally participating in it. The angelic mind is simultaneously: the Platonic world of forms; the home of formal being; the realm of seraphim, cherubim, and thrones; the domain of the Metatron; the locus of the higher shekinah; the site where love is born.

**The Active Intellect.** The chapter's most sustained philosophical argument concerns Aristotle's active intellect and its assimilation into Pico's Platonism. In Aristotle's *De Anima* III.5, the active intellect (intellectus agens) recognizes what is common across particular things, abstrating universals from particulars by "illuminating" the possible/passive intellect. Alexander of Aphrodisias argued that this active intellect was universal and external to the individual. Augustine drew a correspondence between the world of Platonic forms and the angelic sphere, linking the ontological (forms having being) with the epistemological (intellect knowing universals). Ficino's Christian Platonism maintains this link: the world of forms is the mind of God, which is also Aristotle's agent intellect. Averroes went further, arguing that both the active and the possible intellects are universal and unified, abolishing any personal intellect — a position followed at Padua, rejected by Aquinas. Howlett's claim is that Pico locates the active intellect in the angelic mind, specifically at or near Hokmah (p. 152), and that his use of the Ein-Sof concentrates much of the dynamism of the One into the angelic mind or its proximate region. Most persuasively, Hokmah is Aristotle's Active Intellect (p. 152). The ideas are in the angelic mind as formal being; they are reflected downward having participatory being in the next step. "Every intellect is full of them" from angelic mind to individual soul (*Commentary* 43, p. 153).

**Populating the Angelic Mind.** The angelic mind is populated by: (1) Christ, present above the angels (*Heptaplus* 115); (2) the Metatron, the transfigured Enoch, who in Kabbalistic literature (Abulafia's Commentary on Maimonides, via Idel) functions as a synonym for the active intellect — "the thing that is actualizing our intellect from its potentiality," the "Angel of the Face," "the divine Spirit," "the divine presence" (pp. 155–56); (3) three ranks of three groups of nine orders of angels — in the angelic mind proper: thrones, cherubim, seraphim; in the celestial world: principalities, archangels, angels. Angels have a "double imperfection": they are not being itself (only participate in being) and they are not intelligence itself (only happen to understand, *Heptaplus* 108, p. 157). The Metatron's dual valence — as both the firstborn Son of God and the ascended mystic Enoch — is especially significant for Howlett: it implies a "double intellectual sonship," whereby the cosmic Agent Intellect and the mystic who has actualized his intellect are functionally equivalent, anticipating the Oration's claim that the intellectual man can be an angel (Idel, pp. 155–56). The connection between Christ and Enoch-as-Metatron was not unique to Pico: Mercurio da Correggio also called himself Son of God and Enoch at this period (p. 156).

**The Celestial and Natural Worlds.** The second world is the celestial sphere: seven planets, eighth sphere of fixed stars, ninth (primum mobile), and tenth (empyrean heaven), which "presides like the commander of an army over nine heavenly spheres" in imitation of God (*Heptaplus* 78). Pico correlates these ten spheres with the ten sefirot (*Conclusions* 11 > 48), citing the Jewish philosopher Isaac ben Solomon Israeli for the identification of the empyrean with the sapphire throne of Ezekiel's Merkavah vision (p. 158). Planetary triads repeat the Platonic structure: Uranus = God, Saturn = first mind, Jupiter = world-soul. The celestial world is full of soul rather than being. Eight rational souls plus a ninth (the world-soul, represented as Calliope) inhabit the nine active levels; the nine Muses allegorize the planetary souls. Daemons follow the planets. The third world — the natural or sublunary — is everything below the moon, divided into nine levels after prime matter: three categories of inanimate bodies, three of vegetable nature (grasses, shrubs, trees), three of sensitive souls. Saturn and his three sons (Jupiter, Neptune, Pluto) allegorize the sublunary triad: Neptune rules earth/generation (not the sea) because generation is symbolized by water's flux. Each element has causal being in the celestial world, formal being in the natural world, and participated being in the subterranean. The relationship is Platonic: our world mirrors the angelic mind and celestial world "as perfect as its nature allows" (*Commentary* 17, p. 160).

**Astrology and Fate.** The chapter closes by addressing celestial influence and free will. Pico does not simply reject astrology but distinguishes its legitimate (astronomical-scientific) from its illegitimate (divinatory, deterministic) uses. The *Heptaplus* rejects astrologers who read individual fates from the stars; the *Disputations* mocks both allegorizing astrologers and Kabbalists who read Hebrew letters in the stars. Pico's position: signs in the stars are signs of divine order, not predictors of sublunary events. The celestial world is the world of fate (as opposed to the angelic mind, which is the world of divine providence), but fate pertains to bodies, not souls. The deeper issue is that a strictly deterministic reading of the connected triadic universe eliminates free will entirely — the very problem Chapter 6 addresses. Pico's universe is therefore not "modern" in the sense of asserting autonomous self-determination; rather it is a place of baroque necessity overlaid by the possibility, through grace, of ascent beyond fate itself (pp. 161–63).

Howlett concludes the chapter by presenting the productive space opened by the tensions within Pico's concordance. The questions it raises — can the hidden God act? Is the One in apophatic or kataphatic theology? Does the divine extend to all worlds or only the angelic mind? Can emanation and creation coexist? — are not failures of the project but the conditions for an intense and ongoing philosophical conversation (p. 163). Pico believed the answers were already there, embedded in the structure of his universe, which is a ladder built from the combination of philosophy, theology, and religious practice: "a puzzle, but also a ladder" (p. 163).

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: Pico's cosmology is primarily Christian Platonic but uses concordance at critical points with Aristotelianism and Kabbalism, with Ficino's universe as the useful starting point for identifying Pico's differences (p. 135).

**Claim 2**: Pico emphasizes Proclus more than Ficino does, resulting in a "baroque" triadic structure — three worlds each internally subdivided into triads — reminiscent of gnostic aeons and Pythagorean geometry (pp. 136–37).

**Claim 3**: The Heptaplus anatomizes Genesis 1–2 as a "theophanic cosmic code" in which Moses' text is both allegory and mathematical revelation readable only by the philosophically initiated (p. 137).

**Claim 4**: Pico's God is twofold: an apophatic hidden God (Ein-Sof) and a partially revealed active divine (Keter); this duality underlies Kabbalistic cosmology and explains the oscillation in Pico's texts between calling God "above being" and calling God "being itself" (pp. 139–41).

**Claim 5**: The *Conclusions* passage identifying the Ein-Sof as the "abstract and uncommunicated unity" versus Keter as "coordinated unity" is the key to understanding Pico's apparently contradictory theological statements (p. 140).

**Claim 6**: Pico's distinction between causal, formal, and participatory being — a distinction he uses against Ficino, drawing on Aquinas (per Lohr) — resolves the God/being question: God contains being causally but not formally (p. 143).

**Claim 7**: Pico's reading of the *Parmenides* as merely a "dialectical exercise" is a "weak parrying of an inconvenient text" that nonetheless forms part of a consistent argument for equating the One with plenitude (Keter) rather than nought (p. 143).

**Claim 8**: God's love in Pico is not the Platonic eros for beauty but pure volitional giving; God is self-sufficient and the circuitus spiritualis of Ficino is substantially revised (pp. 146–47).

**Claim 9**: Beauty is a subspecies of the good (contra Ficino), and beauty requires compositeness; therefore there is "no beauty in God," and beauty first appears in the angelic mind (pp. 147–48).

**Claim 10**: Love is above intellect in Pico (*Conclusions* 5 > 6), meaning we can desire God — and apophatic theology is possible — without cognitive knowledge of God (p. 148).

**Claim 11**: Hokmah is most persuasively identified as Aristotle's Active Intellect in Pico's system (p. 152).

**Claim 12**: The Metatron in Abulafia's Kabbalistic tradition is functionally a synonym for the active intellect, and Pico's identification of the Metatron with the intellectual man-made-angel in the *Oration* likely has an "ecstatic Kabbalistic source" (Idel, pp. 155–56).

**Claim 13**: Angels have a "double imperfection": they are not being itself (only participate in being) and are not intelligence itself (only happen to understand), making God's active presence necessary for genuine illumination (*Heptaplus* 108, p. 157).

**Claim 14**: The ten spheres of the celestial world correspond to the ten sefirot (*Conclusions* 11 > 48); Pico derives this correspondence from Isaac Israeli (p. 158).

**Claim 15**: The celestial world is the domain of fate; the angelic mind is the domain of divine providence; fate acts on bodies, providence on souls — a distinction that preserves a space for free will and ascent (p. 161).

**Claim 16**: Pico is not a "modern" in the sense of asserting autonomous individual self-determination; the *Disputations*' attack on judicial astrology is not about individual freedom but about the limits of fate's hold on souls (pp. 162–63).

**Claim 17**: The tensions generated by Pico's project of concordance — between apophatic and kataphatic theology, between emanation and creation, between fate and free will — are productive spaces for interrogation rather than failures (p. 163).

## Technical Vocabulary

**Ein-Sof** (Hebrew: "without end/limit"): The absolutely hidden God in Kabbalism; self-enclosed, retracted, incapable of description; Howlett's term for Pico's "abstract and uncommunicated unity" and God of apophatic theology. Corresponds to the Plotinian One as above being (pp. 139–44).

**Keter** (Hebrew: "crown"): First sefirah; partially hidden, partially revealed; the beginning of the creative/emanatory chain on the Tree of Life; the Father (alef/av); the "coordinated unity" or plenitude; linked to the Merkavah throne; active principle in the void. Howlett's term for Pico's "One that contains being" (pp. 140–44, 150–51).

**Hokmah** (Hebrew: "wisdom"): Second sefirah; the incomprehensible wisdom of God; identified with "beginning" (reshit) and with Christ as Son; equated by Howlett with Aristotle's Active Intellect in Pico's system; the limit of attainable ascent for some Kabbalists (pp. 150–52).

**Binah** (Hebrew: "understanding"): Third sefirah; gives form to wisdom; with Keter and Hokmah forms the first triad = Trinity and top of the angelic mind (pp. 149–50).

**Bereshit / Reshit** (Hebrew: "in the beginning"): First word of Genesis; Pico's *Conclusions* 28.25 equates it with "in wisdom" (*b'Hokmah*); the "beginning" that is both Hokmah and the starting point of creation (pp. 149–50).

**Merkavah** (Hebrew: "chariot"): Jewish mystical tradition of the divine chariot-throne of Ezekiel; associated with Keter as the throne of God; the first triad of sefirot is the Merkavah; the empyrean heaven compared to the sapphire throne (pp. 149, 158).

**Metatron**: Highest angel in Kabbalistic tradition; identified with the transfigured Enoch and as a synonym for the Active Intellect (Abulafia); prefiguring of Christ; "prince of the world," "divine presence," universal spirit (pp. 155–56).

**Shekinah** (Hebrew: "divine presence"): Higher shekinah in the angelic mind (Hokmah/Binah triad); lower shekinah in Malkut (kingdom); associated with the 'atarah prayer-ascent; related to divine presence in the world (pp. 149–50).

**Intellectus agens / active intellect**: Aristotle's *De Anima* III.5; the intellect that recognizes universals in particulars by illumination; in Alexander of Aphrodisias made external and universal; in Averroes both active and possible intellects are universal; in Pico's concordance, located in the angelic mind (Hokmah) and equated with the divine wisdom that illuminates (pp. 153–54).

**Intellectus possibilis / possible intellect**: The receptive intellect that takes in information from the senses; in Averroes also universal and unified; in Aquinas, individual (p. 153).

**Causal being / formal being / participatory being** (esse causale / esse formale / esse participativum): Howlett's reading of Pico's tripartite ontology. God contains all things in the mode of causal being; the angelic mind contains formal being; the rational soul/celestial world has participatory being. Key to resolving the God/being contradiction and to Pico's critique of Ficino (pp. 143–44, 153).

**Circuitus spiritualis**: Ficino's "circle of spirit" — God's love flowing down, creation's desire returning up; Pico substantially revises this by denying that God has desire or eros for beauty (pp. 146–47).

**Hyle / prime matter**: Formless matter at the bottom of the emanatory chain; "truly not-being" (neither has being nor is Ein-Sof's nought); potentiality without form; the "brooding darkness" over the deep; "crude matter devoid of form, capable of taking on all forms" (pp. 144–45).

**Primum mobile**: Ninth celestial sphere, "first moved," apprehended by reason not sense; corresponds to the second sefirah (Hokmah) in Pico's *Conclusions* planetary mapping (p. 158).

**Empyrean heaven**: Tenth sphere, fixed and unmoving, presiding over the nine active spheres; corresponds to the first sefirah (Keter); allegorized as the sapphire throne of Ezekiel (pp. 158).

**Anagogical allegory**: Upward-leading reading of scripture and nature; Heptaplus deploys it to read Genesis as a cosmic code; allegory allows the mind to perceive interconnectedness across all worlds (pp. 137–38).

**Concordia discors / discordant concord**: The structural principle of Pico's universe; things are distinct yet interwoven: "a friendly enmity and a concordant discord" — Pico's definition of beauty (*Commentary* 39), extended to the universe's ontological architecture (pp. 138, 148).

**Felicity**: The return of each thing to its beginning; the highest good and ultimate end; available only to the angelic and rational worlds as "intelligible circles"; angels can be raised to it by God's grace, but they cannot ascend to it on their own (*Heptaplus* 150–51, pp. 157–58).

**Providence / fate**: Providence governs souls through the angelic mind; fate governs bodies through the celestial world; the distinction preserves space for free will in Pico's deterministic universe (p. 161).

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Guide scholars**: Howlett's Chapter 5 operates within the tradition of synthetic intellectual history of Renaissance Neoplatonism (Kristeller, Garin, Trinkaus, Copenhaver, Farmer). She uses systematic philosophical analysis to read across Pico's texts, treating them as constituting a single though incomplete system.

**Myth being tested**: The chapter explicitly challenges the myth of Pico as "one of the first moderns" — the narrative that Pico's *Oration* asserts autonomous individual self-determination and that the *Disputations* is a foundational document for the scientific method and the assertion of free will against religious superstition. Howlett rejects both readings (pp. 162–63) without denying the texts' importance.

**Historiographical register**: The chapter belongs to what might be called "contextual revisionism" — using the full range of Pico's sources (Kabbalistic, Aristotelian, Neoplatonic) to produce a more accurate internal map of his cosmology, rather than reading it through a single tradition. The revision of Ficino-Pico relations is a running thread: Pico is not Ficino's disciple but a critic who attacks Ficino explicitly (*Commentary* 34: "how many errors our Marsilio committed"), especially on love, beauty, and being.

**Concord outcome**: Howlett's conclusion is that Pico's concordance is a coherent project that generates genuine philosophical tensions. These tensions are not resolved but held open as the chapter ends — the resolution comes only in Chapter 6 (the question of ascent and the way home). Concordance in Howlett's framing is not simply eclectic syncretism but a structured encounter between traditions that forces each to reveal its limits.

## Uncertainties

1. **OCR quality**: The source file shows occasional formatting artifacts (spaces within words, stray characters). No significant content corruption detected in this chapter, but passages with large Latin quotations (especially from the *Conclusions* and *Heptaplus*) should be verified against the original Latin texts when cited for publication.

2. **Page breaks within the chapter**: The source file marks page breaks as "## Page N" headings. Page numbering in the source (pp. 144–178 in the PDF) maps as: "## Page 144" = p. 136 of the book. Readers should note the PDF pagination offset: the book's pp. 135–170 correspond to source markers "## Page 143" through approximately "## Page 178."

3. **"Isaac" identification**: At p. 158, Howlett cites "the Jewish philosopher, Isaac" from *Heptaplus*. The footnote (n. 120) identifies this as "probably Isaac ben Solomon Israeli, a North African Jew of the ninth century." This is a scholarly consensus identification, not a certainty.

4. **Mercurio da Correggio**: Mentioned briefly (p. 156) as a contemporary of Pico who also called himself Son of God and Enoch. No further context given. This figure's relationship to Pico's circle is not elaborated in the chapter and would require additional research.

5. **Alemanno's influence**: Howlett notes that Johanan Alemanno "worked with and potentially influenced Pico's understanding of Kabbalism" (p. 150). The hedge "potentially" is significant — this is an ongoing scholarly debate not resolved here.
