## Section Function

Chapter 3 is the philosophical architecture chapter of the book. Chapters 1–2 established biographical framing and the book's revisionary premise (dismantling the "myth" of Ficino's Platonic Academy as the natural home for Pico). Chapter 3 constructs in their place a positive account of what Pico actually was: a "christo-syncretist" whose intellectual foundations are three-pillared — Aristotelianism, Platonism, and Kabbalism — with a further important fifth strand, humanism, treated as a milieu Pico inhabited without belonging to. The chapter serves the book's master argument that Pico's allegiances cannot be resolved to a single tradition, and that the received debate — "was he an Aristotelian or Platonist?" — is a category error produced by reading him through frameworks external to his own project. Having cleared the Platonic Academy framework in Chapter 2, Chapter 3 puts in its place the three-pillar model that will govern all subsequent analysis of specific works (Chapters 4–7). The chapter also establishes Howlett's key claim about *prisca theologia* and mathematical-mysticism as the connective tissue between all three pillars, and sets up the Ficino-Pico antagonism as a disagreement of *mission* rather than *sources*.

## Exhaustive Argument Summary

Howlett opens the chapter by announcing Pico's fundamental character: he is a syncretist whose goal is to disclose the "deep structure" of the universe where philosophy and theology meet, and to reveal within it a pathway home to God. This pathway is irreducibly Christian, but since Christ's revelation came after the earliest communications of this deep structure, its Christian character was not apparent to those who went before. Pico's syncretism therefore also functions as a rectification — of what appeared pagan or Jewish — producing what Howlett calls a "christo-syncretism" (citing Sudduth): a philosophical outlook made up of classical, scholastic, and other religious sources, suffused with Christian sensibility and oriented toward mystical experience (p. 53–54).

The three traditions that rapidly emerge as the foundations of this deep structure are Platonism, Aristotelianism, and Jewish Kabbalism. Howlett assigns each a structural function: Kabbalism provides the skeleton — the Tree of Life or divine lightning flash — the pathway and ladder from earth to divinity. Aristotelianism, especially its logic and theory of causality, provides the primary mode of argumentation, applied both to the act of concordance and to the description of deep structure. Platonism provides the "divine something" (*to theion*, quoted from the Oration) that links the more scientific Aristotelianism to religious revelation. Christianity, especially through Pseudo-Dionysius, is the living spirit binding everything together (p. 55). Howlett notes that the henotic process — the noetic union of the human intellect with the divine — is specifically linked to the Aristotelian Active Intellect, making Pico's mysticism partly an Aristotelian proposition (p. 55).

Having established this tripartite model, Howlett confronts the received problem of allegiance. The modern critical debate from Garin (1937) onward has framed Pico as primarily Aristotelian (Garin) or primarily Platonist (Kristeller), or as evolving between positions (Valcke), or as essentially inconsistent (Safa). Howlett argues all these framings miss the point: Pico's interest was genuine across all positions, and his education provided depth in each. He was Aristotelian through scholastic and Averroist training in Ferrara and Padua, Platonist through his relationship with Ficino, and Kabbalist through instruction from Elijah del Medigo and others. What looks like inconsistency is structural: the concordist method deliberately moves between traditions, using each for what it does best, with Christianity as the organizing principle (pp. 56–58).

On Aristotelianism specifically, Howlett reconstructs the late fifteenth-century landscape Pico inhabited. She distinguishes three strands: (1) Averroism, in its Latin form, which had by the fifteenth century largely decoupled Aristotelian philosophy from theology and thereby heightened the division between reason and faith, making "Averroism" synonymous with irreligion or atheism in polemical usage (pp. 59); (2) scholasticism and its two branches, the *via antiqua* (realism, associated with Aquinas) and *via moderna* (nominalism, Ockhamism), which had become increasingly arcane and which Pico tended to navigate toward Aquinas while remaining sensitive to Parisian nominalism (p. 60); and (3) neo-Aristotelianism or the humanist reform of Aristotelianism led by Ermolao Barbaro and his circle, who used philological methods to return to the original Greek texts and early peripatetic commentators (Simplicius, Themistius). This third strand, associated with the University of Padua and the new print culture of the Veneto (Manutius Press), was transforming Aristotelian studies as Pico studied there (pp. 60–62).

Pico's personal network in Aristotelianism included Barbaro himself, Nicoletto Vernia (who taught Pico, and whom Pico may have influenced toward neo-Aristotelianism), and Agostino Nifo (a student-colleague who used a Simplicius commentary on *De anima* apparently found by Pico). But the most important figure is Elijah del Medigo (c. 1460–1497), the Cretan Jewish Averroist, who served as teacher, translator, treatise-writer, and reading-list advisor. Del Medigo translated Arabic Aristotelian texts from Hebrew for Pico, wrote at Pico's request two treatises on Averroes' doctrine of the unity of the intellect and on the conjunction of the human being with the separate intellect, and may have guided the formation of Pico's Kabbalistic library. Crucially, del Medigo's Averroism was Jewish-Arabic in character — oriented toward resolving dichotomies between reason and faith, not toward their opposition — and this allowed Pico to see Aristotle as part of a chain of thinking that could lead eventually to religious experience (pp. 62–64).

On Platonism, Howlett's key argument is that the received narrative of the Platonic Academy — in which Pico figures as Ficino's heir-apparent — obscures a decade-long sustained antagonism between the two philosophers. She identifies three major polemical ruptures. First, Pico's *Commentary on a Canzone by Benivieni* (c. 1485–86) was written explicitly to correct and contradict Ficino's *Commentary on Plato's Symposium* (republished as *De Amore* in 1484), with Pico asserting a "purer" and more directly Plotinian reading than Ficino's. Ficino later claimed after Pico's death that Pico had "renounced" this work — in effect suppressing the record of conflict (p. 65). Second, Pico's *On Being and the One* (1491) contradicted Ficino's orthodox Neoplatonic position that the One is above Being (derived from the *Parmenides* via Proclus), arguing instead that God is both singular and Being and that Plato and Aristotle agree on this. Ficino responded by writing his *Commentary on Plato's Parmenides* (circulated 1492–94, published 1496), which proceeds section by section to refute Pico (pp. 66, van Haelen 2009). Third, Pico's unfinished *Disputations against Astrology* indirectly attacks Ficino's astrological magic as expounded in *De vita coelitus comparanda* (1489), though Howlett notes the complexity of Pico's own position: he employs "true astrology" (*vera astrologia*) linking planets to the sefirot of the Tree of Life and endorses Pythagorean-Kabbalistic mathematical mysticism, while rejecting Ficino's sympathetic magic and divinatory astrology (pp. 67–69). She acknowledges the unresolved question of whether this inconsistency reflects genuine intellectual tension, a specific political message about divinatory prophecy, or the editorial interference of Gianfrancesco Pico (p. 69).

Howlett argues that without the Academy framework, the differences between Ficino and Pico become intelligible as a disagreement of *mission*. Ficino's mission is *renovatio* — using Platonized Christianity to return the world to a Golden Age through a community of learned souls under his patronage and guidance. Pico's mission is exceptionalist and individualizing: the ascent of a singular philosopher-mystic to henosis, with no envisioned renovatio or social dimension. Pico "begins with an interest in changing the world" through the Rome disputation but ultimately retreats to the contemplative life; "there is no discussion of return to establish a renovatio" (p. 72).

Yet despite their antagonism, Howlett identifies three important similarities. Both Ficino and Pico use Aristotle in their argumentation: Ficino employs Aristotelian-Thomistic analogy of being and act/potency distinctions even in his *Platonic Theology*. Both draw on the same specific strands of late Platonism — Proclus (Pico cites him more than any other thinker in the *Conclusions*, 100 of 900 theses) and Plotinus, making both thinkers fundamentally "Neoplatonic" despite the revival of Plato. And both pursue an "originalism," a drive back to the oldest and most authentic sources, which they share with humanists and neo-Aristotelians alike. Pico's paradox is that he claims greater originalism than Ficino (asserting a more direct Plotinian reading) while simultaneously making heavy use of Proclus (pp. 69–71).

The section on humanism treats this tradition as a milieu Pico was inside without being definable within it. Howlett takes Angelo Poliziano as the key figure: a close personal friend of Pico, who also struggled between philological and philosophical identities, and whose *Lamia* (1490) engages the same dichotomy. "Humanism" in Pico's moment is irreducibly plural: it includes the *studia humanitatis*, the philological return to original texts, civic orientation, and a spirit of Renaissance rebirth. Pico had humanist inclinations (interest in rhetoric, translation, original texts) but his focus was the contemplative rather than the active life, and his works were not oriented to humanist education. His "mix is better expressed in terms of his 'uniqueness' or 'exceptionalism' and what we call his 'syncretism'" (p. 74).

The chapter's final two sections address syncretism and *prisca theologia* as the framework holding all three pillars together. Pico's "concordism" — a term associated also with Cardinal Bessarion — involves a claim to have access to the key unlocking all traditions' underlying truth. Howlett emphasizes its *hierarchical* character (following De Boer): resolution in Pico's concordism always leans toward one argument over another, with Christianity as the ultimate organizing principle (p. 76). Pico practices what Safa (2001) calls an "exclusive syncretism": he already knows the underlying truth toward which he is drawing everything. The method in the *Conclusions* moves from the widest point of the philosophical funnel (fifteenth-century scholasticism) backward through the Arabs, Greeks, Chaldeans, Egyptians, and finally the Kabbalists as ancient Hebrews, whose revelation from Moses is the origin of the deep structure (pp. 77–78).

The *prisca theologia* (shared with Ficino, who uses it for renovatio) is distinguished by Pico's emphasis on Pythagoras, his Kabbalistic fourth strand, and the elitism of the hidden mystery: only those capable of reading numbers, symbols, and hieroglyphs can access what the *prisca* transmitted. Pico explicitly privileges private revelation over public communication: the *Oration* tells us he wished to demonstrate "not so much that I know many things as that I know things that many do not know" (p. 78). The *Conclusions* structure reflects mathematical-philosophical-mysticism as a governing logic: the largest numerical block in the work belongs to Proclus, Kabbalism, Aquinas, and Averroes; and the *philosophia nova* section is organized around numerology and Kabbalistic structure. The climax of this pillar is Kabbalism proper: not merely gematria but the full Tree of Life structure that provides a detailed "skeleton" to the Platonic emanations with resonances of Proclan theology and Aristotelianism, and which constitutes Pico's personal "pathway home to the divine" (pp. 79–80).

## Claims and Subclaims

**Claim 1**: Pico's syncretism is a "christo-syncretism" — its outcome is always Christian, using classical, scholastic, and other religious traditions as building blocks toward a Christian theological philosophy (p. 53–54).

**Claim 2**: Kabbalism provides the structural skeleton — the Tree of Life and divine lightning flash — that gives detailed form to Platonic emanations, with the ladder between earth and divinity as the central operative image (p. 55).

**Claim 3**: Aristotelianism, specifically Aristotelian logic and theory of causality, provides the primary mode of argumentation, applied both to the act of concordance and to the description of deep structure (p. 55).

**Claim 4**: Platonism supplies "that divine something" (Pico's phrase, *Oration*, p. 190 Latin) that links the scientific Aristotelianism to religious revelation; the henotic process in Pico is specifically a noetic union with the Aristotelian Active Intellect (p. 55).

**Claim 5**: The received debate over whether Pico was primarily Aristotelian or primarily Platonist is a category error; Pico was genuinely interested in all positions and his education provided real depth in each (p. 56).

**Claim 6**: Latin Averroism by the fifteenth century had become synonymous with irreligion or atheism, defined by its opposition to theology rather than by any engagement with faith and reason (p. 59, citing Schmitt 1984).

**Claim 7**: Pico tended toward Aquinas and the via antiqua but remained sensitive to the via moderna debates in Paris (p. 60, citing Trinkaus 1994, Dulles 1941, Farmer 1998, Valcke 2005).

**Claim 8**: Ermolao Barbaro represents a "humanist reform of Aristotelianism" centering on philological study and the early peripatetic commentators Simplicius and Themistius; Pico was his famous sparring partner in the 1485 letter-exchange (p. 61, citing Kraye 2008, Lohr 1999, Mahoney 1997).

**Claim 9**: Elijah del Medigo is the strongest intellectual influence of the Paduans on Pico; he provided teaching, translation of Arabic Aristotelian texts from Hebrew, treatises on Averroes' doctrine of intellect, and probable guidance for Pico's Kabbalistic library (pp. 62–64).

**Claim 10**: Del Medigo's Jewish-Arabic Averroism — oriented toward resolving reason/faith dichotomies — allowed Pico to see Aristotle as compatible with a pathway toward religious experience, unlike the irreligious Latin Averroism of Padua (p. 63).

**Claim 11**: The "Platonic Academy" framework, in which Pico is Ficino's heir-apparent, is a narrative partly constructed by Ficino after Pico's death and obscures a decade-long sustained antagonism between the two thinkers (pp. 63–65).

**Claim 12**: Pico's Commentary (c. 1485–86) was written to contradict Ficino's *De Amore*, asserting a more direct Plotinian reading; Ficino subsequently claimed Pico "renounced" the work, suppressing the record of disagreement (p. 65).

**Claim 13**: Pico's *On Being and the One* (1491) contradicts Ficino's orthodox Neoplatonic position on the One and Being; Ficino responded with his *Commentary on Plato's Parmenides* (circulated 1492–94), proceeding section by section to refute Pico (p. 66, citing van Haelen 2009).

**Claim 14**: The *Disputations* indirectly attack Ficino's astrological magic, but Pico's own position is inconsistent: he endorses *vera astrologia* linking planets to sefirot and Pythagorean-Kabbalistic numerology while rejecting divinatory and sympathetic astrology (pp. 67–69, citing Rutkin 2004).

**Claim 15**: Ficino and Pico's disagreement is fundamentally one of *mission*: Ficino pursues renovatio (social, communal, Golden Age); Pico pursues exceptionalist, individual, contemplative henosis with no vision of social return (p. 69, 72).

**Claim 16**: Both Ficino and Pico are fundamentally "Neoplatonic" despite the Platonic revival: their inherited Platonism was already Neoplatonic, filtered through Plotinus, Christianity, and Jewish-Arabic reinterpretation (p. 70).

**Claim 17**: Pico cites Proclus more than any other thinker in the *Conclusions* (100 of 900 theses); he is also more intensively Plotinian than Ficino at this period, using *Enneads* before Ficino's translation (p. 70, citing Farmer 1998, Allen 1997).

**Claim 18**: Pico was not a humanist: his focus was the contemplative rather than the active life and his works were not oriented to humanist education; his position is better described by his exceptionalism and syncretism (p. 74, citing Copenhaver 2019).

**Claim 19**: Pico's concordism is hierarchical (De Boer 2009): resolution leans toward one argument over another, with Christianity always as the ultimate organizing principle; this makes it an "exclusive syncretism" (Safa 2001) rather than a neutral pluralism (pp. 76–77).

**Claim 20**: The *Conclusions* structure is organized as a chronological/philosophical funnel from the widest point (fifteenth-century scholasticism) backward to the Kabbalists as the original recipients of divine revelation through Moses (p. 77–78, citing Farmer 1998).

**Claim 21**: Pico and Ficino share the *prisca theologia* but use it differently: Ficino uses it for renovatio; Pico uses it for private revelation and esoteric individualism; Pico emphasizes Pythagoras and Kabbalism where Ficino emphasizes Hermes Trismegistus and Zoroaster (pp. 78–79).

**Claim 22**: The *philosophia nova* in the *Conclusions* is primarily Kabbalistic and mathematical; its climax, the promise to prove God's existence "by the way of numbers," must be read within a Kabbalistic-Pythagorean mathematical philosophy (p. 79, citing Farmer 1998).

## Technical Vocabulary

**Christo-syncretism** (Sudduth): Howlett's borrowed term for Pico's synthesis — not a neutral eclecticism but a Christian-centred concordism using classical and other traditions as propaedeutic to Christian revelation (p. 53).

**Three pillars** (*tres columnae*, Howlett's analogy): Aristotelianism, Platonism, Kabbalism as the three structural foundations of Pico's deep structure (p. 55).

**Deep structure**: Howlett's term for the underlying ontological-theological architecture of the universe that Pico's project aims to reveal; not a surface eclecticism but access to an original stratum of divine truth (pp. 53–54).

**Concordism** / **Concordia**: the project of bringing Aristotle and Plato into agreement; associated with Cardinal Bessarion; Pico extends this to all of philosophy and theology (p. 75). Source: Pico's letter to Baptista Mantuano (1490) on "Concordiam Platonis et Aristotelis" (*Opera* 1:358–59).

**Exclusive syncretism** (Safa 2001): Pico's concordism is not pluralist but directed: he already knows the truth toward which he is drawing all traditions (p. 77).

**Via antiqua** / **Via moderna**: the two scholastic strands — realism (Aquinas) and nominalism (Ockham); Pico tended toward the via antiqua (p. 60).

**Averroism** (Latin): by the 15th century, a label for Aristotelianism disconnected from or opposed to theology; centered at Padua; associated with irreligion or even atheism (pp. 59, 80).

**Jewish-Arabic Averroism**: the strand of Averroist rationalism represented by del Medigo, oriented toward reconciling reason and faith within Judaism rather than separating them; crucially different from Latin Averroism (pp. 62–63).

**Neo-Aristotelianism**: the humanist reform of Aristotelianism led by Barbaro, using philological methods to return to original Greek texts and early peripatetic commentators (Simplicius, Themistius); represented the cutting edge at Padua in Pico's time (p. 61).

**Henosis** (Gr. ἕνωσις): mystical union with the One/God; Howlett identifies this as Pico's ultimate goal and the organizing principle distinguishing his project from Ficino's renovatio (pp. 54–55, 72).

**Noetic** (adj., from Gr. *nous*, intellect): pertaining to intellect; Pico's henotic process is also noetic — the union is an intellectual union with the divine Active Intellect (p. 55).

**Prisca theologia** / **Prisca magia**: the chain of "ancient theologians" (Moses, Pythagoras, Plato, Hermes Trismegistus, Zoroaster, Orpheus) understood to have received and transmitted divine wisdom from the origin; for Pico, Kabbalism is included; used differently from Ficino (pp. 77–79).

**Renovatio**: Ficino's mission term — the renewal of the world into a Platonic-Christian Golden Age; explicitly absent from Pico's project (pp. 72–73).

**To theion** (Gr., "the divine something"): Pico's phrase from the *Oration* for the distinctive mark of Platonism that elevates it above Aristotelianism (p. 56, *Oration* 210–11, 190 Latin).

**Vera astrologia** (Lat., "true astrology"): Pico's term in his Kabbalistic conclusions for the correspondence between planets and sefirot; distinguished from divinatory astrology (p. 69, citing Rutkin 2004).

**Gematria**: Kabbalistic numerological method; Pico links it to Pythagorean mathematical philosophy as part of his sacred-mathematical-mysticism framework (pp. 63, 79).

**Kataphatic / Apophatic theology**: positive (descriptive) vs. negative (via negativa) approaches to theology; Pico's synthesis combines both traditions, linking Latin kataphatic emphasis with Byzantine apophatic tradition (Pseudo-Dionysius) (p. 54).

**Philosophia nova**: the "new philosophy" section of the *Conclusions* (theses 7–7a); Howlett identifies it as fundamentally Kabbalistic and mathematical-mystical (p. 79).

## Scholarly Values Overlay

**Myth being tested**: The chapter systematically dismantles two interrelated myths. The first is the "Platonic Academy" myth — that Pico was essentially Ficino's closest disciple and can be read as a Ficinian Platonist with added complications. The second is the allegiance-simplification myth — that Pico was "primarily" Aristotelian (Garin) or "primarily" Platonist (Kristeller's emphasis on Ficino's influence). Both myths produce distortions: the first forces Pico's disagreements with Ficino to be explained away as misunderstandings or provocations rather than substantive philosophical antagonism; the second prevents recognition of the Aristotelian and Kabbalistic dimensions as genuinely constitutive of Pico's vision rather than ornamental.

**Historiographical register**: Howlett positions herself between two scholarly camps she calls the "Ficino scholars" (who subsume Pico into Ficino's world) and the "Pico Studies" scholars (Farmer, Valcke, etc., who sideline Ficino almost completely). Her middle path — taking both the Ficino relationship and Pico's independence seriously — is framed as correcting distortions on both sides. She is also in conversation with Copenhaver's 2019 *Magic and the Dignity of Man*, accepting his demythologization of the Oration while extending it into the philosophical foundations chapter.

**Concord outcome**: The chapter itself is "concord" in Howlett's sense — it draws together the strands (Aristotelian, Platonic, humanist) into a single architectural model without eliminating their distinctiveness. The payoff is the three-pillar model that enables subsequent chapters on Kabbalism (Ch. 4), the ascent of intellect (Ch. 5), the Conclusions (Ch. 6), and the Oration/Heptaplus/De ente (Ch. 7) to operate without needing to re-establish Pico's basic orientation.

**Interventions against prior scholarship**: Garin's 1937 scholastic-Aristotelian emphasis; the twentieth-century Platonic Academy consensus (della Torre 1902 through Kristeller); Walker's reading of the *Disputations* as primarily concerned with freedom of will (Howlett following Craven: the target is Ficino's magic); any reading that posits a simple evolution or conversion narrative (Valcke's sequence from Aristotelianism to Platonism and back).

## Relation to Pico Works

**Oration on the Dignity of Man**: Opening motto cited for the claim to have studied "all masters of philosophy." Phrases on *to theion* (Platonism as "most sacred of all philosophies"), on the *prisca magia* and Pythagorean number, and on elite esoteric knowledge ("things that many do not know") are used to establish Pico's intellectual self-presentation (pp. 55–56, 78, citing 2012 ed. pp. 180, 190, 195, 208, 267).

**900 Conclusions**: Structural analysis is central. Howlett uses Farmer's (1998) tabulation to establish the proportional weight of traditions: Proclus leads individual sections (100 theses), followed by Kabbalism, Aquinas, Averroes. The *philosophia nova* section (7–7a) is Kabbalistic-mathematical in character. Conclusion 1.1 ("There is no natural or divine question in which Aristotle and Plato do not agree in meaning and substance, though in words they seem to disagree") is cited as the programmatic statement of concordism (p. 77, citing 1998 ed. 364–65).

**Commentary on a Canzone by Benivieni** (c. 1485–86): Central to the Ficino antagonism. Its attack on Ficino's *De Amore*, the claim to a purer Plotinian reading, and Ficino's post-mortem "cover-up" (claiming Pico "renounced" the work) are all analyzed (pp. 65, 73, 78–79).

**On Being and the One** (*De ente et uno*, 1491): Key exhibit for the Ficino antagonism and for Pico's philosophical originalism. Pico argues Plato and Aristotle agree that God is both the One and Being; he dismisses *Parmenides* as a rhetorical exercise to achieve this concordance. Howlett presents this as Pico's last completed manuscript and a key step toward the planned *Concordia of Plato and Aristotle* (pp. 66–67, 71).

**Disputations against Astrology** (*Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem*, unfinished): Used to establish the third major Ficino conflict and to probe the inconsistency of Pico's position on "science" and magic (pp. 67–69).

**Heptaplus**: Referenced as working with *Timaeus* throughout Pico's career; also for discussions of human nature that conflict with Ficino (pp. 69–70).

**Letters**: Three letters are given close attention — the letter to Ficino on alternating Plato-Aristotle studies; the 1484 letter to Barbaro on concordance; and the 1490 letter to Baptista Mantuano confirming work on the *Concordia* (pp. 56–57, 61, notes 11–13).

## Uncertainties

1. **Authorship of Disputations**: Howlett repeatedly flags the unresolved question of whether parts of the *Disputations* were edited or composed by Gianfrancesco Pico rather than Giovanni (p. 69, p. 232 note 79). This makes claims about Pico's position on astrology using this text methodologically uncertain. The essay does not resolve this and neither does this chapter.

2. **Del Medigo and Kabbalism**: Howlett notes that del Medigo's Averroism probably pointed Pico *away* from more Platonic forms of Kabbalism (note 47, citing Mahoney and Andreatta 2014). The source of Pico's Platonic-inflected Kabbalism remains unidentified. This is a genuine open research question.

3. **Ficino's claim about the "Platonic Academy"**: Howlett cites della Torre (1902) as the origin of the Academy as a scholarly norm and Hankins (1991, 2009) as having contested it, but she does not fully resolve the question of Ficino's own use of "Academia" in his letters. See her 2016 book pp. 17–19 for fuller treatment.

4. **Pico's access to Greek texts**: The claim that Pico used Plotinus' *Enneads* in 1486 before Ficino had published a translation (p. 57) implies access to a manuscript; the exact textual conduit is not discussed here and may depend on scholarship not cited in this chapter.

5. **OCR**: The source Markdown file is a PDF conversion. The chapter text reads cleanly in this passage with no evident major OCR errors in the main body. One bracketed ambiguity: at p. 57 ("Pico's use of Plotinus' Enneads. In 1486, for example, occurred before Ficino had published a translation") — the sentence structure is awkward, possibly a conversion artifact, but the meaning is clear from context.
