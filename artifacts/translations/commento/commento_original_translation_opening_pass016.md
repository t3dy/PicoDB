## Title

Commentary by the most illustrious lord Count Giovanni Pico della Mirandola on a canzone of love composed by Girolamo Benivieni, citizen of Florence, according to the mind and opinion of the Platonists.

## Blasius Bonacursius to His Most Beloved Friend Girolamo Benivieni

Most beloved Girolamo, all the students of our own times, and those who will come after us, ought certainly to mourn continually the untimely death of that admirable young man, Giovanni Pico, Prince of Mirandola. Considering his many excellent virtues, they should recognize how great a loss they have suffered, especially with respect to the benefit they could have hoped and promised themselves from his life.

This loss has been common not only to those who desire philosophical things, and especially Platonic things, but also to all lovers and professors of sacred Scripture. For by several most excellent works, which he had not yet brought to light, he had already satisfied the former in great part, and he was now seeking, with the greatest usefulness and glory for the Christian religion, to satisfy the latter. To this the unexpected and tearful event of his death opposed itself.

Yet I believe that the aforementioned students and men of letters bear this patiently, considering that Almighty God does nothing except with the greatest mystery and divine providence. I believe that this providence has now put it into the minds of our printers to publish, through their art, the learned and elegant commentary of the aforesaid Prince on a learned and graceful canzone of yours, composed on divine love according to the mind and opinion of the Platonists. Thus this work too will not remain, together with many other works of his, neglected and forgotten by men; and all men of letters and lovers of his name will have the opportunity to enjoy it and take from it the fruit that they cannot take from those other works of his that have not been brought to light.

Since I had in my possession a copy of the said canzone and commentary, and since certain printers asked me for it with great insistence, I was for some time uncertain whether I ought to grant it or not. On one side, I was held back by knowing how contrary the publication of such a work would be to your mind and to that of the author, for reasons known to you. On the other, I was urged by the insistence of the printers, the desire of many, and the usefulness and advantage that seemed to me likely to result from such publication.

Therefore, finally overcome by the encouragement and prayers of friends, I preferred, though with some burden upon myself in your eyes, to provide the aforesaid printers with a copy of this transcript of mine, even outside your will, rather than, by keeping it back, to defraud the desire of many. I thought especially that my fault ought to be more excusable insofar as a private offense is less grave than a public one, since this publication, besides the common good, also has for its end the private good of a friend.

For since I knew of several transcripts of the said work, which are read in various places and circulated through many hands, I judged it entirely impossible that it would not one day be published in the same way. That would necessarily have brought far greater displeasure to you and to all lovers of the Count's writings, since such transcripts are imperfect and full of many errors, from which I believe this copy of mine has been purged, if not entirely, at least for the most part.

And if in this matter you still recognize some fault in me, may all those who read the commentary excuse me before you and before the happy memory of its author. Besides love, which knows neither bridle nor law, they will be able, unless my judgment deceives me, easily to recognize from this work that if the Count had had occasion to write on love in a Christian manner, as was his intention, he would have done so with as much greater success as truly divine doctrine surpasses and excels the doctrine of Plato and of all other philosophers. Farewell.

## Girolamo Benivieni, Citizen of Florence, to the Reader

Giovanni Pico, Prince of Mirandola, a man truly admirable in every respect, once read, as happens among friends, a canzone of mine. In it, invited by the most delightful reading of the learned commentaries of our Marsilio Ficino on Plato's *Symposium*, I had compressed into a few verses what Marsilio describes with the greatest elegance over many pages. It pleased Pico to illuminate my poem with an interpretation no less learned and elegant than abundant. He was moved, I believe, not so much by the merits of the thing itself as by a tender and singular affection, beyond all belief, which he always had for me and for my writings.

But afterward, when we reconsidered the canzone and the commentary, and when the spirit and fervor that had led me to compose it and him to interpret it had already partly diminished, some shadow of doubt arose in our minds. Was it fitting for a professor of the law of Christ, when wishing to treat of love, especially celestial and divine love, to treat it as a Platonist and not as a Christian? We thought it good, therefore, to suspend publication of the work, at least until we could see whether, by some revision, it might become Christian rather than Platonic.

Not long after this decision came the untimely death of Giovanni Pico, harmful and tearful beyond every other calamity of these times. Because of its unexpected arrival, I remained almost without sense, full of confusion and weariness with the things of the world, and thought to leave this canzone and commentary, together with many other verses of mine, to the discretion of dust and to suppress it forever.

Although I observed this decision up to the present time, the zeal and desire of others to bring this work to light have nevertheless been stronger than my scruple and diligence in withholding it. Since it had already come into the power of certain more curious persons, perhaps by the indulgence and permission of those with whom the original draft was kept together with the other books and commentaries of Giovanni Pico, it was first given into the hands of our printers and put into production by them before I had scarcely any notice of it.

Since I could not honorably resist this, and since on the other hand I did not recognize in it, that is, in the publication of such a work, any fault of mine, I thought, as the saying goes, to let it go for nature's benefit. Trusting especially in the prudence, goodness, and learning of those who persuaded me to do so, I now pray the reader that in every place where this canzone or commentary, following the doctrine of Plato, departs in any way from Christian truth, the authority of Christ and of his saints may prevail more with him, together with the irrefutable reasons brought forward by our theologians, especially the angelic doctor Saint Thomas Aquinas, than the opinion of a pagan man.

Let him excuse our error, if indeed it can be called error simply to report, without any approval, the opinion of another, even if it is not true. Let him excuse it, I say, by the inscription or title placed before the canzone and commentary, in which it is openly stated that we wish to treat of love not according to Catholic truth, but according to the mind and opinion of the Platonists.

In carrying this out, even if beyond the aforementioned error, if error it is, there may be many others, nevertheless this good and this usefulness cannot be taken away from the work: students of Plato and of his doctrine, if they read this commentary attentively, will find in it many lights by means of which the eye of their intention may more easily, and perhaps with another kind of gaze, penetrate to the inner marrow of some of the more remote meanings of so great a philosopher.

## Translator's Notes

- `professore della legge di Cristo`: rendered as "professor of the law of Christ," preserving the confessional and juridical force rather than softening it to "Christian."
- `secondo la mente e opinione de' Platonici`: rendered consistently as "according to the mind and opinion of the Platonists," because this phrase is the work's protective frame.
- Benivieni's preface is crucial evidence for the Ficino/Pico problem: he explicitly says the poem was prompted by Ficino's *Symposium* commentary, then marks anxiety over whether Platonic love can be made Christian.
- Bonacursius frames publication as both preservation and a partial betrayal: the text is made public despite Benivieni's and Pico's earlier hesitation.
