## Core Concept

Jayne helps PicoDB separate three things that are too easily collapsed:

1. Ficino's broad role as translator and architect of Renaissance Platonism.
2. Ficino's more specific role as the fountainhead of Renaissance love Platonism.
3. The mediated travel of Ficinian love Platonism through Pico, Benivieni, Italian love treatises, French poetry, English reception, and later translators.

This matters because Pico's relation to Ficino is not merely intellectual dependence followed by rebellion. Pico is one of the mediators through whom Ficinian Platonism becomes portable, literary, and controversial. The *Commento* is therefore both inside Ficino's orbit and against Ficino's authority.

## Reading Rule

When a text or scholar says "Ficinian influence," ask:

- Is the influence direct reading of Ficino?
- Is it mediated through Pico, Benivieni, Bembo, Castiglione, French poetry, Stanley, or later translation?
- Does it involve love and beauty specifically, or another Platonism such as cosmology, politics, Dionysian theology, or metaphysics?
- Does the receiving author adopt, depaganize, correct, popularize, or attack Ficino?
- Is the evidence a quotation, a marginal note, a parallel, a translation, a review claim, or a later reception narrative?

## Application to Pico

The *Commento* should now be described as a rival commentary machine. Ficino's *De amore* is a commentary on Plato's *Symposium* that becomes Ficino's own doctrine of love. Pico's *Commento* is a commentary on Benivieni's canzone that becomes Pico's poetic theology, love metaphysics, and critique of Ficino's *De amore*. Both works exceed their stated occasions.

Jayne's reception model also clarifies Pico's later importance. In England, according to Jayne, Pico and other mediators often carried Ficinian or post-Ficinian Platonism more effectively than Ficino's own works. Pico is therefore not just a younger Florentine thinker reacting to Ficino; he is a transformer of Ficino's intellectual afterlife.
