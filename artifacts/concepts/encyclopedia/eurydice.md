# Eurydice

Eurydice appears in Pico della Mirandola's writings in a single but philosophically dense locus: the allegorical commentary on the myth of Orpheus in the *Commento sopra una canzona d'amore* (c. 1486), where she functions as the figure who carries the entire weight of Pico's argument about the conditions of genuine philosophical love and the soul's access to intelligible beauty. She is not a goddess, not a cosmic principle with independent theological standing in Pico's system, and she does not appear in the *900 Conclusions*, the *Oration*, or the other major works where Pico deploys his full syncretic apparatus. But the significance of the Eurydice allegory in the *Commento* is entirely disproportionate to its brevity: in the few pages Pico devotes to the myth, he argues against Ficino's reading of the *Symposium*, defines the philosophical conditions of soul's proper ascent, and works out the implications of his own more severe account of what genuine love of beauty requires. To understand what Eurydice means in Pico's philosophy is therefore to understand the *Commento* in its most pointed and distinctive aspect.

In the ancient mythological tradition, Eurydice is Orpheus's wife. She is bitten by a serpent — in Virgil's *Georgics* IV while fleeing the attentions of the shepherd Aristaeus — and drawn down to Hades. Orpheus, the legendary singer who could move rivers and trees with his music, descends after her, charms Persephone and Hades with his lyre, and is granted permission to bring Eurydice back to the upper world on one condition: that he not look back at her during the ascent. He fails to observe the condition, looks back, and loses her for the second and final time. The story appears in Plato's *Symposium* at 179D, where Phaedrus tells it as part of his praise of love: Orpheus, he says, was thought a coward by the gods of the underworld because he came down still alive, still carrying his lyre, and therefore was shown only a phantom of Eurydice rather than the real woman. The gods' punishment for his insufficiency was to have him killed later by women. Plato's version of the story is thus already colored by a philosophical judgment: Orpheus lacked the courage or the commitment that genuine love requires.

Ficino chose, at the corresponding point in his *De amore* (1.4), not to offer an allegorical interpretation. He noted the Orpheus story and passed over it, declining to inquire into its meaning. This silence is significant: it represents a deliberate choice to leave the mythological material undigested philosophically, either because Ficino did not yet have a satisfactory reading or because he did not regard the story as central to his argument. Pico seizes on the opportunity. He provides in the *Commento* what he presents as the philosophically correct interpretation of the myth — one that, characteristically, is more demanding and more severe than anything Ficino had suggested, and that turns out to be a pointed implicit critique of the tendencies in Ficino's love theory that Pico finds insufficiently rigorous.

In Pico's allegorical reading, as Michael Allen has analyzed in "Eurydice in Hades: Florentine Platonism and an Orphic Mystery" (Allen 2012, 19–40), Eurydice signifies the intelligible beauty that is the proper formal object of philosophical love. She is not simply "beauty" in a generic sense but the specific intelligible beauty that the philosophically prepared soul can encounter — the *bellezza intelligibile* associated with what Pico calls the celestial Venus, the beauty that subsists in the angelic intellect, in the Ideas as they exist in the first mind created by God. Eurydice is the soul's genuine beloved, its true object of philosophical *amor*, what it is actually seeking when it seeks beauty at all. Her death and descent into the underworld represent, on this reading, the initial condition of the soul that has not yet found its proper object: beauty in its full intelligible form is not immediately available to the soul in its embodied condition, not because beauty does not exist but because the embodied soul's cognitive faculties — its imagination, its passive reason, its dependence on sensible perception — prevent it from apprehending intelligible beauty directly. Eurydice dwells in the underworld from the soul's perspective because the faculties required to perceive her are, in the embodied soul, submerged beneath the operations of sensory cognition.

Orpheus's descent, on this reading, is the soul's deliberate entry into the realm of lower cognition in pursuit of beauty — a figure of the philosopher's decision to direct attention downward, toward the understanding of embodied existence and the claims of the sensible world, rather than immediately upward, toward the purely intellectual. Pico grants the descent a degree of philosophical legitimacy: the soul is not simply going astray when it engages with the sensible world, and Orpheus's motivation — love — is not in itself misguided. The problem lies in the conditions of the descent. Orpheus goes down alive. He carries with him the functions of imagination and what Pico, drawing on the scholastic Aristotelian tradition, calls passive reason: the cognitive operations that process sensible information, generate mental images, and constitute the intermediate zone between pure intellectual vision and bodily sensation. He is, in the technical Neoplatonic vocabulary, a soul still attached to the *pneumatic vehicle*, the subtle semi-material envelope through which the soul's lower operations are conducted. This means that what Orpheus encounters in the underworld — what he takes for Eurydice and attempts to bring back — is not Eurydice herself but a *simulacrum*, a phantom of intelligible beauty generated by his own imagination operating on the traces of sensible experience. The condition the gods impose — do not look back — is simply the translation into narrative form of what genuine philosophical vision requires: the total suspension of the imaginative and passive-rational faculties that would generate a false Eurydice in place of the real one.

When Orpheus looks back, he loses Eurydice for the final time — or rather, he reveals that he never possessed her in the first place. The look back is not simply a failure of willpower but the external sign of an interior condition: a soul still attached to the lower faculties cannot help but turn back toward them, because the cognitive operations available to such a soul are precisely the ones that generate the image of beauty rather than beauty itself. The loss of Eurydice at this moment is therefore not an external punishment imposed by the gods but a philosophical consequence built into the situation from the beginning. A soul that has not undergone the philosophical death that would detach it from imagination and passive reason cannot apprehend intelligible beauty directly; it will always, when it attempts to ascend with beauty, find that it is carrying a phantom rather than the real thing, and the phantom will dissolve at the moment when the attachment to the lower faculties becomes visible as a looking back.

Pico's term for the philosophical death that Orpheus failed to undergo is the *mors osculi*, the death by kiss, drawn from Kabbalistic sources where it describes the mode of death attributed to the Hebrew patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, Aaron, Miriam — who died, according to the rabbinic tradition, not by illness or violence but by a kiss, *binsica*, a union with the divine so complete that the soul separated from the body without struggle. In the *Commento*, Pico identifies this Kabbalistic *mors* with the philosophical death that Platonic ascent requires: the withdrawal of all cognitive functions from the body into a state of pure intellectual rapture in which the soul, still nominally embodied, operates exclusively through the intellectuality proper to the angelic level of being. Only in this state can the soul encounter Eurydice — only when the imagination and passive reason are entirely suspended can intelligible beauty appear without the mediation of sensible images. Orpheus's failure is, in these terms, his failure to achieve the *mors osculi* before undertaking his descent: he went down into Hades as a man, not as a philosopher who had died to the sensible, and the result was that he found beauty's phantom rather than beauty herself.

The contrast between Pico's interpretation and Ficino's broader position on love and beauty is philosophically fundamental. Ficino, in the *De amore*, had presented the soul's response to sensible beauty as the legitimate beginning of a genuine philosophical ascent. Beauty is distributed through all levels of being — divine, angelic, celestial, corporeal — and the beautiful body genuinely participates in the divine beauty that shines through it. The soul that loves a beautiful body is responding, however dimly, to the divine beauty refracted through sensible form, and its task is to be led by this response upward rather than away from sensible beauty altogether. Ficino's account is cosmologically generous: it finds genuine philosophical value even in the lowest rung of the ladder of love. Pico's Eurydice allegory implies a direct challenge to this generosity. The soul that enters the underworld of lower cognition carrying its imagination — that attempts the ascent to beauty while still relying on sensible perception and imaginative processing — will not find Eurydice but her simulacrum, and will lose even that when the attachment to the lower faculties becomes manifest. The conditions of genuine vision, for Pico, are more demanding than Ficino acknowledges: not a gradual purification through the levels of beauty but a prior death to the sensible that clears the way for intellectual vision as such.

The connection between the Eurydice allegory and Pico's three-Venus hierarchy is direct. In the *Commento*, Pico distinguishes among three Venuses corresponding to three kinds of beauty and three kinds of love. The celestial or Uranian Venus represents the beauty of the angelic intellect — the Ideas in their intelligible ordering — and is the proper object of the highest philosophical love. The intermediate Venus governs the rational soul's love of beauty as reflected in human form and in the order of rational existence. The lower or generative Venus presides over the beauty operative in the natural world of generation and corruption. These three Venuses do not represent three equally legitimate starting points for a graduated ascent; they represent three levels of metaphysical reality with correspondingly different relations to intelligible beauty as such. Eurydice, as the figure of intelligible beauty in its proper form, belongs to the celestial Venus. What the soul risks mistaking for Eurydice — the beauty perceived through imagination and sensible cognition — belongs to the lower registers of the hierarchy: it is at best an image of beauty, at worst a confusion of beauty's trace with beauty itself. The Orpheus myth stages, in narrative form, exactly this risk of confusion and its consequences.

Several related interpretive threads connect the Eurydice allegory to the broader landscape of Pico's philosophical commitments. The allegory's insistence on the necessity of dying to imagination and passive reason before genuine intellectual vision becomes possible reflects Pico's Neoplatonic anthropology, derived from Proclus and from the reading of Plotinus that he conducted partly through Ficino's translations and partly through direct engagement with the texts. Plotinus's account of the soul's ascent in *Ennead* I.6 already distinguishes between the beauty apprehended by the eye of the body and the beauty apprehended by the soul's own proper vision; the soul recognizes beauty because it is of the same nature as the intelligible forms that constitute beauty, and it achieves its proper vision of beauty by a withdrawal from the sensible analogous to what Pico calls the *mors osculi*. Pico sharpens this Plotinian account by insisting that the withdrawal must be total: not a relative turning away from the sensible but an absolute suspension of the cognitive faculties through which the sensible is processed.

The Kabbalistic resonance of the Eurydice allegory is also worth noting, even though Pico does not make it explicit in this passage of the *Commento*. The *mors osculi*, the death by kiss that Pico presents as the philosophical preparation that Orpheus lacked, is a Kabbalistic concept — *binsica* in Hebrew, the form of death attributed to the patriarchs who were wholly absorbed into the divine. Pico's identification of this Kabbalistic *mors* with the philosophical condition of genuine intellectual vision is one of the characteristic moves of his syncretic method: a figure from Jewish mysticism is decoded as encoding the same truth that the Platonic tradition states discursively. The patriarchs who died of kissing were, in Pico's reading, the successful philosophers who achieved what Orpheus could not: they entered the underworld of embodied existence not carrying their imagination and passive reason but having first died to those faculties, and they brought back not Eurydice's phantom but Eurydice herself. Moses, who died *binsica* at the end of his life, is in this context the figure who succeeded where Orpheus failed — a convergence of Kabbalistic legend and Platonic allegory that is distinctively Piconian.

The art-historical resonance of the Eurydice figure also deserves mention, though Pico himself does not pursue it in this direction. The *Commento*'s allegory of Orpheus and Eurydice circulated in the sixteenth century partly through Gabriel Chappuys's partial French translation, and some of the *Commento*'s mythological allegories — particularly those involving Venus — influenced the interpretation of Florentine Neoplatonic art, including works by Botticelli and Michelangelo. Whether the specific Eurydice allegory shaped any particular artistic program is uncertain. What is clear is that Pico's reading of the myth — with its insistence on the intelligible beauty that the soul seeks and loses through its attachment to the sensible — articulates one of the most rigorous accounts of philosophical *eros* in the Renaissance literature, one that reflects his characteristic refusal to be satisfied with the more accommodating syntheses that Ficino's greater rhetorical generosity allowed.

The Eurydice of Pico's *Commento* is ultimately a figure for what philosophy promises and what philosophy requires: the intelligible beauty that is the soul's genuine object, present but inaccessible to the soul that approaches it without the prior preparation of philosophical death to the sensible. She is lost not because the philosopher does not love her but because the philosopher does not yet know how to love rightly — not because he lacks eros but because his eros is still contaminated by the lower faculties that generate phantoms rather than realities. Her loss, repeated at the moment of Orpheus's backward glance, is the allegory of the soul's recurrent failure to achieve what it seeks, a failure built into the conditions of embodied existence and curable only by the demanding philosophical and spiritual practice that culminates in the *mors osculi*. In this sense, the Eurydice of the *Commento* is not merely a mythological figure but a philosophical concept in narrative form: the name Pico gives to the goal of the Platonic ascent described in the *Oration*, what the soul is seeking when it seeks to transform itself from the vegetative through the rational to the angelic level of being.
