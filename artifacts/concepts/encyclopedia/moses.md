# Moses

Moses — in Hebrew משה, *Moshe*, in Latin *Moyses* — is not a marginal presence in Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's intellectual world but one of its organizing centers. He functions simultaneously as the divinely inspired author of the most important text Pico ever commented on, as the paradigmatic figure of apophatic mystical ascent, as the originary recipient of the esoteric oral tradition that Pico identified with Kabbalah, and as the privileged position-marker in the hierarchy of ancient wisdom that structures Pico's understanding of the *prisca theologia*. In the *Heptaplus* (1489), the *Oration* (1486), the *900 Conclusions* (1486), and the *Apology* (1487), Moses appears in each of these registers, and the roles are not merely additive: they reinforce one another in ways that make Moses the indispensable theological and hermeneutical figure without whom Pico's entire synthetic project would lose its foundation.

The most extensive and literal engagement with Moses in Pico's corpus is the *Heptaplus*, the seven-exposition commentary on the first seventeen verses of Genesis (the account of the six days of creation, *bereshit* through the Sabbath) published in Florence in late 1489. The title — from the Greek *heptaplus*, sevenfold — signals the method: each of the seven expositions reads the same passage of Mosaic text through a different ontological lens, revealing in succession the doctrines of the elemental world, the celestial world, the angelic world, the human world, the interrelation of all worlds, the correspondence of the other worlds to man, and finally the beatitude that is the end of human life. This sevenfold structure itself derives, as Crofton Black has demonstrated through a careful philological study, from the Kabbalistic tradition of the forty-nine gates of understanding (*Sha'arei Binah*), which Pico's sources associate with Moses: Moses, according to those texts, had ascended through forty-nine of the fifty gates of understanding, and his knowledge of the Genesis text concealed and encoded the wisdom of those forty-nine gates (Black 2006, 225–232). The *Heptaplus* is structured as a demonstration of this concealment, and Moses is therefore not only the historical author of the commentary's text but the authority whose arcane knowledge the commentary labors to disclose.

Pico's prefatory justification for the *Heptaplus* — the first of its two proems — is a sustained argument for the necessity of esotericism in the reading of sacred texts. The argument is that Moses, as a divinely inspired legislator who wrote for a mixed audience of the educated and the uneducated, the philosophically capable and the philosophically incapable, was obliged to write in a way that encoded different meanings at different levels of the text, so that the vulgar reader would receive the story of creation as a narrative of origins while the philosophical reader would find in the same words a complete account of the structure of being, the nature of the angels, the constitution of the soul, and the path to beatitude. This strategy of esoteric writing — the concealment of philosophical truth in mythological or historical narrative — is, Pico argues, characteristic of all the greatest ancient writers: Plato, Pythagoras, Zoroaster, Orpheus, and the Kabbalists all write esoterically, and all for the same reason. But Moses is, for Pico, the supreme practitioner of the esoteric mode, because his text is divinely inspired rather than merely humanly wise, and because the secret he conceals is therefore more complete and more certain than any human philosophical discovery.

The claim that Moses wrote esoterically is not new with Pico; it is a standard position in the tradition of allegorical biblical interpretation from Philo Judaeus and Origen through Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite to the medieval Kabbalists. What is distinctive in Pico's *Heptaplus* is the combination of this claim with the specific philosophical framework of three worlds (angelic, celestial, elemental) and the specific hermeneutical technique of reading all seven days of creation as simultaneously encoding all three worlds and man. Black's study of the *Heptaplus* establishes that this framework is drawn in significant part from Kabbalistic sources, particularly the tradition of the three worlds (*'olamot*) that Pico encountered in the translations of Flavius Mithridates, and that the sevenfold structure of the commentary reflects the Kabbalistic importance of the number seven as the number of the Sabbath and of the lower sefirot (Black 2006, 221–226). Moses is therefore positioned as the originator of this Kabbalistic-philosophical framework, the author who encoded it in the creation narrative, and Pico as the interpreter who decodes it — a relationship of author and commentator that also enacts the larger claim of the *prisca theologia*: that the ancient wisdom and the biblical revelation are not two different things but the same truth in different modes.

The relationship of Moses to the *prisca theologia* hierarchy requires careful navigation, because Pico's position is more complex than a simple assimilation of Moses to the line of ancient sages. In the standard Ficinian account of the *prisca theologia*, the chain of ancient wisdom runs from Zoroaster through Hermes Trismegistus, Orpheus, Pythagoras, and Philolaus to Plato, with each figure handing on the same primordial wisdom in a different form. Moses does not figure prominently in Ficino's version of this chain. In Pico's version, however, Moses occupies a qualitatively different position from all the other ancient sages. He is not one wisdom-transmitter among many but the recipient of the most direct divine revelation that has ever occurred. As the narrative of Exodus records (Exod. 33–34), Moses ascended Sinai into the dark cloud where God was, and received not merely the tablets of the Law but a direct vision of the divine — or, in the apophatic tradition that Pseudo-Dionysius drew from this narrative, an experience of the divine darkness (*gnophos*) that constitutes the highest form of union available to a creature. No other figure in Pico's intellectual world, not even Plato, is the recipient of this kind of direct vertical revelation. Moses is therefore the most authoritative single figure in the *prisca theologia*, not because he is the oldest but because his mode of access to divine truth was the most immediate.

The *Oration* (composed in late 1486 as the introductory address for the projected Roman disputation) engages Moses in a register that is both biographical and protreptic. In the famous passage where Pico describes Moses ascending Sinai to receive the Law, the ascent functions as the paradigm of what the philosophical life is: a movement upward through the hierarchy of being, progressively leaving behind the lower faculties and lower objects, until the soul stands in the divine presence. Sophia Howlett's reading of the *Oration* in her study of Pico's philosophical program (Howlett 2021) emphasizes that the figure of Moses in the *Oration* is not primarily a historical or theological datum but a model for philosophical aspiration: the soul that practices philosophy in the way Pico recommends is enacting a version of the Mosaic ascent, rising through moral purification (the Mosaic law), through philosophical contemplation (the vision of the burning bush, the encounter with the divine name), toward the final union in the divine darkness that is the summit of the ascent program. Moses is the prototype of the *Oratio*'s philosopher, whose ascent Pico describes as the progressive transformation of the self — from the vegetative level through the rational to the angelic and finally beyond the angelic to the union with the One that transcends all categories.

The Pseudo-Dionysian mediation of the Moses figure is crucial for understanding how Pico uses it in the ascent context. Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, in the *Mystical Theology* — a short but enormously influential text whose first chapter describes Moses ascending Sinai into the divine darkness — had interpreted the Mosaic ascent as the paradigmatic account of apophatic union: Moses enters the darkness not despite his vision but through the progressive abandonment of all positive knowledge, all sensory content, all conceptual determination, until he encounters the divine in its absolute simplicity, which no concept can grasp and no name can name. This Dionysian reading of Moses's Sinai ascent was the authoritative account of mystical union in the Christian Neoplatonic tradition, and it shaped the account of the highest stage of ascent in Pico's *Oration* as well as the treatment of the seventh exposition in the *Heptaplus*, where the final chapter describes the soul's arrival at the *felicitas* — the beatitude — that is the end of the entire journey. Black's analysis of the *Heptaplus* shows how closely Pico follows Pseudo-Dionysius's epistemological framework, particularly in the account of how biblical exegesis functions as a form of intellectual ascent, guiding the soul progressively from the sensory content of the creation narrative toward the purely intelligible content that the narrative conceals (Black 2006, 391–410).

The third and perhaps most philosophically consequential role Moses plays in Pico's thought is as the originary Kabbalist — the figure who received not only the written Torah (*Torah she-bichtav*, the Pentateuch) but the oral Torah (*Torah she-be'al peh*), transmitted unwritten from generation to generation, and whose esoteric dimension constitutes the Kabbalistic tradition that Pico treated as the most direct access to divine truth available to any human tradition. This identification appears with remarkable clarity in the *Apology* (1487), Pico's defense of the condemned theses, where he explains the nature of Kabbalah to readers who, he notes, may never have encountered the term without hostility. Kabbalah, he writes, is not the name of a person (as some had supposed) but of a tradition: the secret interpretation of the divine Law that Moses received on Sinai as a companion to the written text, transmitted orally through an unbroken chain of sages, and constituting the hidden wisdom that explains the true meaning of the Law and confirms the deepest truths of Christian theology. The crucial point, as Chaim Wirszubski's monumental philological study of Pico's encounter with Jewish mysticism demonstrates (Wirszubski 1989), is that Pico's understanding of this tradition depended entirely on the Latin translations of Hebrew Kabbalistic texts made for him by Flavius Mithridates between May and November 1486 — some 3,500 surviving pages in the Vatican manuscripts, representing a substantial corpus of Kabbalistic literature made available in a Latin that was simultaneously faithful to the Hebrew and systematically Christianized by Mithridates's own interpolations.

The *900 Conclusions* contain two sets of Kabbalistic theses. The first set, presented *secundum secretam doctrinam sapientum Hebraeorum Cabalistarum* ("according to the secret doctrine of the wise Hebrew Kabbalists"), presents doctrines drawn from Pico's Kabbalistic reading and attributed to the tradition as a whole. The second set, *secundum opinionem propriam* ("according to his own opinion"), presents Pico's own interpretations and syntheses of Kabbalistic material, including the Christological applications that were among the most controversial elements of the Roman affair. Central to both sets is the presupposition that the tradition they draw on derives from Moses's oral revelation at Sinai: the Kabbalists are not individual philosophers who developed their doctrines from rational inquiry but the transmitters of a Mosaic secret. Wirszubski's analysis of the sources of the first set of Kabbalistic theses demonstrates that Pico's principal source was Menahem Recanati's commentary on the Pentateuch (available to Pico in Mithridates's lost Latin translation), and that Recanati himself had been a major conduit for the Zohar's doctrines (Wirszubski 1989, 53–56). The chain from Moses through the oral Torah through Recanati through Mithridates to Pico is a long and technically mediated one, but Pico's theological claim is that its ultimate authority derives from the original Sinaitic revelation.

The *Heptaplus*'s first proem is the fullest expression of this claim in Pico's published works. Pico argues there — drawing on precedents from Origen, Clement of Alexandria, and the Neoplatonic commentators as well as from Kabbalistic sources available to him — that the great teachers of every tradition have always concealed their deepest wisdom from the uninitiated. Moses was no different, and in his case the concealment was doubly authorized: by the divine prohibition against disclosing certain truths (*secretum*) and by the pedagogical obligation to write for an audience of varied capacities. The argument then proceeds through three successive formulations, developed across the *Commento*, the *Apology*, and the *Heptaplus*, that Black traces and analyzes as a progressive refinement: the first formulation, in the *Commento*, draws primarily on Platonic and Neoplatonic precedents for esoteric writing; the second, in the *Apology*, introduces the Kabbalistic tradition as the privileged case of Mosaic esotericism; the third, in the *Heptaplus*, synthesizes all the traditions and makes Moses the supreme esoteric author whose sevenfold text contains the entirety of philosophical and theological wisdom (Black 2006, 97–148). The trajectory is from a general philosophical defense of esotericism to a specific claim about Moses as the most profound esoteric author in human history.

What the *Heptaplus* also discloses, in a way that the *Conclusions* and *Oration* leave implicit, is the extent to which Moses functions for Pico as the authority that validates the entire enterprise of philosophical syncretism. If Moses knew everything — the structure of the three worlds, the nature of the angels, the constitution of the human soul, the path to beatitude — then the Platonic, Aristotelian, Neoplatonic, and Kabbalistic traditions that Pico synthesizes are not rivals but variants of a single wisdom that Moses already possessed. Pico's task as commentator is not to adjudicate among competing philosophical schools but to demonstrate their convergence with what Moses already knew. This is why Moses is not simply a figure in Pico's intellectual world but its ultimate warrant: the agreement of Plato and Averroes and the Kabbalists on any given point is evidence not merely of philosophical coherence but of their shared descent from the same original revelation. The philosopher as Pico imagines him — ascending through the disciplines, purifying his intellect, reaching progressively toward the divine — is recapitulating Moses's ascent of Sinai, and the wisdom he achieves is the wisdom Moses received at its summit.

The reader of PicoDB should bear in mind the significant gap between Pico's Moses and the historical figure of Moses as Pico might have reconstructed him from critical philological inquiry, had such inquiry been available. Pico did not know, and could not have known, that the Kabbalistic tradition he treated as Mosaic in origin was substantially a medieval development, composed in Spain, France, and Provence between the twelfth and fourteenth centuries. Wirszubski's research establishes that even Pico's access to this medieval tradition was filtered through Mithridates's systematically Christianized translations, so that the Mosaic esotericism Pico describes in the *Apology* and the *Heptaplus* was itself a composite construction: partly genuine Kabbalistic doctrine, partly Mithridates's Christological interpolations, and partly Pico's own synthetic readings and applications. The Moses who appears in Pico's works is therefore an intellectual construction assembled from multiple layers of tradition, translation, and allegoresis — a figure whose authority is enormous precisely because it is constitutively beyond critical verification, located at the origin of a tradition whose authenticity Pico took entirely on faith.
