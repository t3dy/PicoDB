Abu Ali al-Husayn ibn Sina (980–1037 CE), known to the Latin Middle Ages and Renaissance as Avicenna, was the most architecturally ambitious philosopher of the Islamic world and the figure whose metaphysics, psychology, and theory of the intellect most deeply penetrated the scholastic tradition before the full weight of Averroes came to supplant him in the later thirteenth century. Born in Bukhara in Khorasan and educated with extraordinary precocity in medicine, logic, mathematics, and philosophy, Ibn Sina synthesised Aristotelian logic and natural philosophy with Neoplatonic metaphysics and developed an account of the soul and its cognitive capacities that remained a reference point — simultaneously a resource and a contested authority — in university philosophy and theology from the translation movement at Toledo onward. His *Kitab al-Shifa'* (Book of Healing), translated into Latin as the *Liber Sufficientiae*, and his *Kitab al-Najat* (Book of Salvation) provided the Latin West with accounts of psychology, logic, and metaphysics that scholastics from Albert the Great through Duns Scotus engaged, disputed, and in many cases adopted in modified form. By the late fifteenth century, when Giovanni Pico della Mirandola assembled his *900 Conclusions* for the projected disputation of 1487, Avicenna's work was available in Latin in multiple partial translations, and his *Liber De anima* had been in print since 1485 in the Pavia edition.

The twelve theses *secundum Avicennam* in the *900 Conclusions* occupy section eight in Pico's Arab taxonomy, between Averroes's forty-one and al-Farabi's eleven. Farmer's critical edition notes that "The *Oration* and *Apology* speak of 'something divine and Platonic' in Avicenna, and Pico's theses 'according to his own opinion' endorse a number of Avicenna's most characteristic doctrines" (Farmer 1998, 264). At the same time, Pico maintains critical distance: in *De ente et uno* 8 he complains that Avicenna "in many places interpolated the philosophy of Aristotle, leading to the great wars fought by him with Averroes." Farmer further notes that many of the Avicennan theses were drawn second-hand from Averroes's attacks on Avicenna and from the epitomes and commentaries on Arabic philosophy that Pico had commissioned from Elijah Delmedigo. This characteristic method — engaging with a thinker partly through the polemical mirror of his opponents — does not diminish Avicenna's importance to Pico's synthesis; it rather attests to how thoroughly Avicennan ideas had become the common currency of the debates within which Pico operated.

The Avicennan theses in the *Conclusiones* engage primarily with logic and natural philosophy. Thesis 8.1 introduces the genus of composite syllogisms beyond Aristotle's categorical and hypothetical forms; 8.2 concerns the derivation of negative conclusions; 8.3 proposes that celestial matter is of the same kind as the matter of inferior things — a claim opposed to the Averroean thesis 7.9 and later to Pico's own position. Thesis 8.6 — "the essence of an object is composed of its own matter and form" — belongs to the series on the essence/existence distinction that runs through the Averroean and scholastic sections of the text. The note in Farmer's edition makes clear that this is opposed to the Averroean thesis 7.21, so that the juxtaposition of Avicennan and Averroean positions on essence and existence is a deliberate structural feature of the historical part of the text, staging a controversy that Pico attempts to resolve in his own theses at 3>11: "In created beings, the same thing is called existence in respect to its actuality and essence in respect to its determinacy" (Farmer 1998, 510). This reconciling move, Pico's own *distinctio inadequationis*, is one of the examples of his *philosophia nova* working precisely at the point where Avicennan and Averroean positions seem irreconcilable.

The metaphysical contribution of Avicenna that Pico draws upon beyond the explicit theses is the distinction between essence (*mahiyya*, *quidditas*) and existence (*wujud*, *esse*) as constitutive of all created beings. For Avicenna, God alone is the *Necessary Existent* (*wajib al-wujud*), the being in whom essence and existence are identical; every other being is *possible* (*mumkin al-wujud*), contingent in the sense that it receives its existence from without, from a cause that ultimately traces back to the First Cause. This distinction, which entered Latin scholasticism in part through Avicenna's translations and became one of the central metaphysical debates of the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries — was it a merely logical distinction, a formal distinction, or a real distinction? — provided Pico with resources for his philosophical theology in *De ente et uno* and in the theological section of the *Conclusiones*. Edelheit observes that the vocabulary of necessity and possibility in Pico's discussion of the relation between God and creatures maps onto the scholastic distinction between *ens a se* (being from itself) and *ens ab alio* (being from another), a distinction whose Arabic articulation passes through Avicenna's necessary/possible dyad before it reaches the Latin scholastics (Edelheit 2008, 157). The *Apology*'s careful navigation of the line between what can be demonstrated philosophically about God's nature and what must be received by faith draws on this same framework of modal distinctions.

Avicenna's psychology, and in particular his theory of intellectual conjunction, is the most philosophically charged element of his inheritance for Pico's project. Black's study situates Avicenna within the three-way debate — al-Farabi, Avicenna, Averroes — over the nature and operation of the Active Intellect and the possibility of human conjunction with it. Avicenna differs from al-Farabi in that his hierarchy of intellectual states — from material intellect, through intellect *in habitu* and intellect *in effectu* to acquired intellect — is not a sequence of stages that only exceptional individuals traverse but a structure that underlies every act of thought: "his hierarchy of intellectual states ... is a necessary condition of any act of thought; every thought entails a fleeting conjunction between the potential and active intellects" (Black 2006, 181). Nonetheless, Avicenna also describes a state in which the potential intellect is brought into such proximity with the Active Intellect that it can receive all intelligibles either immediately or almost immediately; this is the highest level of attainment, and it is explicitly equated with prophecy. The Latin translation of the *Liber De anima* states: "It is possible that some man's soul, because it is clear and cohering with intellectual principles, may be so inspired as to take fire from the disposition to receive all questions from the Active Intellect, either immediately or almost immediately ... And this is one mode of prophecy, which is the highest of the prophetic powers. Hence it is rightly called the holy power, since it is the highest grade among all human powers" (Avicenna, *Liber De anima*, ed. Van Riet, 153, cited in Black 2006, 181 n. 15).

This doctrine of prophecy as the culmination of intellectual conjunction, the highest *ittissal* (*continuatio* in Latin renderings), is directly relevant to the account of ascent in the *Oration*. When Pico describes the highest stage of the philosopher's ascent — seraphic union, the transformation of the intellect through love into the divine fire — he is drawing on a cluster of sources that includes Dionysian angelology, Kabbalistic devekut (adhesion), and the Avicennan model of *ittissal*. The imaginative faculty in Avicenna's psychology, which in prophets is so perfectly configured that it can receive intelligibles from the Active Intellect directly and communicate them in sensible form to the people, maps onto Pico's account of how the philosopher-prophet in the *Oration* moves from the dialectical ascent through the angelic hierarchies toward a mode of illuminated knowing that is simultaneously intellectual and communicable. Avicenna's prophetic psychology is thus one of the conceptual ancestors of the Picoesque figure of the *vir philosophus* who is also a legislator, a prophet, and ultimately a seraph.

The floating man (*homo suspenso* or *l'homme volant* as later readers named the thought experiment, though Avicenna himself in the Latin context uses the term *homo in vacuo*) is the most epistemologically striking of Avicenna's contributions to the tradition. Avicenna asks us to imagine a man created fully formed but suspended in air, without any sensory input from outside and without even the pressure of his own limbs against each other. Would such a man know of his own existence? Avicenna's answer is affirmative: the soul's awareness of itself is not derived from bodily sensation but is an immediate self-acquaintance that the soul possesses by its own nature. The soul, in other words, knows that it is before it knows anything about what it is or where it is, and this original self-luminosity of the soul is the foundation for Avicenna's claim that the soul is a substance distinct in essence from the body and not merely a function or property of it. This argument circulated widely in the Latin scholastic tradition and was invoked by thinkers working on the soul's self-knowledge and its independence from matter. Black's study identifies the floating man's relevance to Pico's account of self-knowledge in the *Heptaplus*: the soul's knowledge of itself as an intellectual substance that can be separated from the body and can ascend through the intellectual world is precisely the kind of self-knowledge that underlies the entire programme of the *Oration* and *Heptaplus* (Black 2006, 157 n. 99). Pico's God in the *Oration* tells Adam that he has been given no fixed nature or place precisely because man's nature is that of a being who can determine his own nature — and this capacity for self-determination presupposes the kind of soul-body relation that Avicenna's argument specifies: not a form inseparable from matter but a substance that can exist in diverse relations to matter, up to and including the relation of radical independence.

Pico's ambivalence toward Avicenna — the praise for something "divine and Platonic" in him, alongside the complaint in *De ente et uno* that he "interpolated" Aristotle — reflects a genuine tension in the Avicennan legacy. Where Averroes accused Avicenna of departing from Aristotle by introducing Platonic elements into his psychology (above all by making the Active Intellect a separate cosmic substance rather than a function of the individual soul), the scholastic tradition had in many respects found Avicenna more congenial than Averroes precisely because his account of the soul's substantive independence from the body was easier to reconcile with the Christian doctrine of personal immortality. Thomas Aquinas attacked Averroes far more aggressively than he attacked Avicenna, and the tradition of "Augustinianism avicennizant" identified by Etienne Gilson — a medieval synthesis of Augustinian illuminationism and Avicennan intellect theory, traceable in thinkers from Philip the Chancellor through Roger Bacon and Bonaventure — shows how deeply Avicenna had been absorbed into the Christian philosophical mainstream before the full Averroist reaction gathered force. Ficino's comment, quoted by Black, that "Avicenna" was among those who interpreted Aristotle with piety, alongside Theophrastus, Themistius, Porphyry, Simplicius, and Plethon, confirms that for the Florentine Platonist circle Avicenna's psychology occupied a position of qualified authority on the proper side of the intellect controversy — not a heterodox Averroist but a philosophically sophisticated interpreter who had managed to preserve something of the Platonic heritage within Aristotelian form (Black 2006, 189 n. 42).

Pico situates Avicenna at the centre of the contested terrain over the intellect's relation to felicity (*felicitas*) that is one of the dominant concerns of his *Conclusiones*. In the third conclusion according to Averroes, Pico states that "the ultimate felicity of man is when the active intellect is joined to the potential intellect as its form," and complains that the Latins have utterly corrupted this doctrine; in the seventh conclusion according to Plotinus, he states that ultimate felicity is when the particular intellect is "fully joined to the first and total intellect." These two formulations bracket Avicenna's position — the Averroist version, which Pico endorses with modifications, and the Neoplatonic version, which he also endorses — while the Avicennan version provides the intermediary framework in which the *ittissal* is not a permanent ontological unification (as in Averroes's strongest account) but a dynamic and repeatable cognitive event in which the prepared mind receives illuminations from the separate Active Intellect. This dynamism suits Pico's purposes: in the *Oration*, the ascent is not a single event but a series of transformations, and the model that best accounts for a *gradual* approach to a *permanent* summit is precisely the Avicennan account of progressive conjunction, culminating in the prophetic state where illumination becomes continuous.

The Avicennan background of the *Oration*'s ascent programme has been confirmed by the Pirtea chapter on self-knowledge, illumination, and natural magic in Pico's esotericism, which shows that the *cognitive* dimension of Pico's ascent — the claim that philosophical preparation transforms the soul's mode of knowing, not merely its moral dispositions — draws directly on Avicennan and related Arabic accounts of intellection. The seraphic union at the culmination of the *Oration*'s ascent programme can be read as an Avicennan *ittissal* reframed in Christian-Kabbalistic dress: the soul that has traversed the philosophical curriculum, practiced the Mosaic ethical disciplines, and achieved the Pauline theological illumination finds itself in the condition that Avicenna identified as the highest state of the prophetic intellect, now identified with the angel of divine love. What the Arabic philosophical tradition calls *ittissal* or *continuatio*, Pico calls seraphic union; what Avicenna calls the *virtus sancta* (holy power), Pico calls the fire of the seraphim. The terminological translation is deliberate: it is precisely the point of Pico's syncretic method to demonstrate that these are not competing descriptions but converging approaches to a single philosophical and theological truth.
