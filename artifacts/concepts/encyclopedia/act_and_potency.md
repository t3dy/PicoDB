The distinction between act (*actus*, from Greek *energeia*, ἐνέργεια) and potency (*potentia*, from Greek *dynamis*, δύναμις) is one of Aristotle's most fundamental conceptual contributions, introduced primarily in the *Metaphysics* and the *Physics* to explain both natural change and the ultimate structure of being. In its Aristotelian form, the distinction serves several functions simultaneously: it explains how change is possible without collapsing into either the Eleatic denial of change or the Heraclitean dissolution of stable being; it articulates the hylomorphic composition of ordinary substances (matter as potency, form as act); and it grounds the Aristotelian theology of the *Metaphysics* Lambda, where God is identified as pure actuality (*actus purus*) without any admixture of potentiality. When this framework is appropriated by Thomas Aquinas and then encountered by Pico della Mirandola, it becomes the conceptual vocabulary through which Pico both engages and, in the *Oration*, distinctively transforms scholastic anthropology.

In Aristotle's original account, potency (*dynamis*) names a capacity or power — the wood's capacity to become a table, the acorn's capacity to become an oak, the student's capacity to learn geometry. Actuality (*energeia*) names the realization or exercise of that capacity. Change, on this account, is the actualization of a potentiality: the transition from being potentially F to being actually F. Matter, in Aristotle's hylomorphic analysis of natural substances, is characterized as pure potentiality: prime matter in itself has no determinate qualities; it is only the capacity to receive any form whatsoever. Form, by contrast, is the actuality of matter: what makes this mass of bronze an actual statue rather than a mere capacity for statuehood. A natural substance, then, is always a compound of matter and form, potency and act, capacity and realization. The hierarchy of actuality runs from the relatively passive and potential at one extreme to the fully actual and self-sufficient at the other, with God at the limit: *actus purus*, pure act, pure actuality, the self-thinking thought (*noesis noeseos*) that moves the cosmos without being moved by anything.

Aquinas appropriates the act-potency framework and extends it from physics and hylomorphic analysis into the most fundamental register of metaphysics. For Aquinas, the composition of act and potency governs not only the relation of form to matter within material substances but also the relation of existence (*esse*) to essence (*essentia*) in every creature. Essence functions as a kind of potency for existence: it is the capacity to be, the "what-it-is" of a thing that determines what kind of being the thing would be. Existence is the act that actualizes that capacity, that makes the essence "stand outside nothing." God alone, as *ipsum esse subsistens* (subsistent existence itself), has no composition of esse and essentia: God's essence *is* God's existence, which is why God is pure act without potency of any kind. Every creature, by contrast, is a composition: it has an essence that is in itself indeterminate with respect to whether it will exist, and an existence that is received, limited, and particularized by that essence. The act-potency vocabulary here makes the real distinction between esse and essentia intelligible, and this distinction is itself the metaphysical foundation of the participation structure developed in the Thomistic tradition: creatures participate in existence (*esse*) because their essence is only the potency for it, while God *is* existence, unparticipated and unlimited.

This double use of act and potency — in physics, as form actuating matter; in fundamental metaphysics, as esse actualizing essentia — gives the vocabulary extraordinary range. When Pico encounters it, it is a living framework both in Aristotelian natural philosophy and in Thomistic theology. In his scholastic studies at Padua and later in Paris, Pico would have encountered act-potency as the basic grammar of philosophical analysis. In *De ente et uno*, as Salas (2014) shows, the Thomistic formulation of esse-essentia composition governs Pico's metaphysics of participation: *ens* (being, concretely signified) participates in *esse* (existence, abstractly signified) in the manner of potency receiving act. The luminous thing participates in light; the being participates in existence. The framework is present but mostly presupposed rather than thematically developed in *De ente et uno* itself, since Pico's focus there is on the convertibility of being and unity rather than on hylomorphic or act-potency analysis as such.

The most philosophically striking use of the act-potency vocabulary in Pico occurs in the *Oration on the Dignity of Man*, and it is here that his distinctive transformation of the doctrine becomes visible. The *Oration* opens with the famous speech of God to Adam, delivered in Pico's reconstruction at the moment of the creation of the human being. The speech declares: "We have made you neither of heaven nor of earth, neither mortal nor immortal, so that you may, as the free and extraordinary shaper of yourself, fashion yourself in whatever form you prefer." Adam receives no fixed nature, no determinate form, no assigned station in the hierarchy of being. While every other creature — from minerals through plants and animals to celestial intelligences — has its being determined by its created nature (its *forma*), Adam is given not a determinate form but the power (*opus*) to take on any form through his own choice and self-cultivation.

The philosophical question that the *Oration*'s anthropology forces is: what exactly is the ontological status of this indeterminate Adam in terms of act and potency? In standard Aristotelian hylomorphic analysis, potentiality is always the potentiality *for* a specific actuality: the bronze is potentially the statue but not potentially anything whatsoever. Pure, undetermined potentiality — potentiality with no specification toward any particular actuality — is prime matter, and prime matter in itself cannot exist: it always already receives some form that determines it as a specific kind of thing. The student who can potentially learn geometry is not purely indeterminate; she is a specific human being with a determinate nature, who has, in addition to that nature, the capacity to acquire a particular skill. Adam's situation in the *Oration*, as Pico describes it, seems to go beyond anything that standard act-potency analysis can accommodate: he is not the potentiality for some specific actuality but the indeterminate power to actualize any form whatsoever.

Howlett (2021) captures this precisely when she notes that Pico's Adam is "an indeterminate image" — a being that "has no image because he or she is called to become God, Who is beyond all representation." Human beings share every state with every other being because there is nothing specifically their own: "At length, the Master Creator decreed that the creature to whom He had been unable to give anything wholly his own should share in common whatever belonged to every other being." This "sharing in common" is distinct from participation in the technical Neoplatonic or Thomistic sense: it is not that Adam is a lower-grade instance of the properties he instantiates, but that he can become, through moral and intellectual discipline, whatever he chooses. Howlett comments that this represents "a reversal of the scholastic operari sequitur esse" — the scholastic principle that activity follows from being, that what a thing does is a function of what it is. For Pico's Adam, being follows from activity: what Adam becomes is a result of what he chooses to do and be.

The language of the *Oration* hovers between an Aristotelian vocabulary and its transformation. When Pico writes that Adam can "degenerate into the lower forms of life, which are brutish" or be "reborn into the higher orders, those that are divine," the terms "degenerate" and "be reborn" carry connotations of formal change — actual transitions from one kind of being to another — that go beyond mere metaphorical self-fashioning. The *Oration* also draws on Pythagorean themes of metempsychosis, as when Pico writes that "according to Pythagoreans, wicked men are deformed into brutes and, if Empedocles is to be believed, into plants as well." Howlett acknowledges the ambiguity: Pico may mean literal formal transformation (transmigration of the soul), or he may mean an internal change in which the soul takes on a "brutish" character while remaining numerically identical. The act-potency vocabulary does not clearly settle this question, but it shapes it: to become a beast in the full sense would be to become actually what previously existed only potentially — to actualize the beastly possibility latent in human indeterminacy.

What Pico does with the act-potency framework in the *Oration* is to relocate indeterminacy from matter — where Aristotle had placed it — to the highest level of the created hierarchy. For Aristotle, what is indeterminate and formless is matter, the lowest and most passive aspect of the cosmos. Form is what confers actuality, determination, and dignity. Pico inverts this valuation: Adam's indeterminacy is not a deficiency of form but the ground of his dignity. Because Adam has no fixed form, he can take on any form; his indeterminacy is a superabundance of potentiality rather than a privation of actuality. This revaluation is arguably made possible by Pico's engagement with the Kabbalistic tradition, where the hidden Ein Sof — which Howlett (2021) describes as "indeterminate being" as opposed to the "active being we might see within creation" — is precisely the most excellent and most real aspect of divinity. The indeterminate human mirrors the indeterminate divine, and Adam's power of self-determination is a creaturely analogue to God's own self-sufficiency.

The act-potency vocabulary also governs Pico's account of the soul's ascent program in the *Oration* and in the *Heptaplus*. As Howlett traces, the soul begins in a condition of multiplicity and dispersal, divided among the faculties of sensation, opinion, knowledge, and contemplation. The ascending path — through moral purification, dialectical inquiry, philosophy, and finally mystical contemplation — is a process of progressive actualization: the soul actualizes capacities that were initially only potential. The rational part of the soul, its highest faculty, is at first only potentially directed toward the divine intellect; the ascent actualizes this potential. Howlett cites Abulafia's Commentary to the Guide for the Perplexed (mediated through Pico's Kabbalistic sources) to note that the active intellect is described as "the thing that is actualizing our intellect from its potentiality" — language that maps the Aristotelian act-potency framework directly onto the Kabbalistic conception of the angelic intellect's role in human intellectual perfection. The mystic who fully actualizes her intellectual potential "becomes also a son," achieving what the sources call "a double intellectual sonship: that of the cosmic, who is the Agent Intellect, and an angel and son… and that of the mystic, who became a son by becoming an intellect in actu." Act (*actu*, in actuality) is the endpoint of the spiritual journey.

In the scholastic sources that Pico had studied most intensively — Thomas Aquinas, Duns Scotus, Averroes, and their commentators — the act-potency distinction served as the fundamental tool for analyzing composition and change. Pico's use of this vocabulary in the *Oration* is not a rejection of the scholastic framework but a creative extension of it. He takes what the scholastics had primarily used to analyze the composition of natural substances and the difference between God's pure actuality and creaturely composition, and he deploys it to illuminate the unique anthropological situation of the human being: a creature whose essence is, uniquely, not a determinate form but an indeterminate power of self-determination. The result is a philosophical anthropology that is simultaneously scholastic in vocabulary, Platonic in its hierarchy of ascent, and Kabbalistic in its valorization of the hidden and indeterminate as the source of creative power.

Edelheit (2008) situates this anthropological move within Pico's broader theological method, which he characterizes as a form of humanist theology that neither simply accepts scholastic formulas nor simply overturns them but works through them creatively toward theological conclusions that draw on multiple traditions. The act-potency vocabulary in the *Oration* is not a technical philosophical apparatus deployed for its own sake but a set of conceptual tools pressed into the service of a theological vision: the dignity of the human being as a creature made in the image of God's own creative and self-determining power. What Aristotle had conceived as the structure of natural change, Aquinas had extended into the metaphysics of divine and creaturely being, and Pico transforms into an account of human freedom and the possibility of spiritual ascent. In each transformation, the vocabulary of act and potency serves as the thread of continuity, even as the philosophical conclusions diverge.
