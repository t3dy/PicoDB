Zoroaster — the Greek transliteration of the Avestan *Zarathustra*, the prophet-founder of Persian religion — appears in Pico della Mirandola's work in a form radically different from the historical figure. The Zoroaster of the *900 Conclusions* and the *Oration* is not the religious reformer of the ancient Iranian steppe but a prisca theologus, an ancient sage whose writings were believed to contain a philosophical theology concordant with Platonic metaphysics, Jewish Kabbalah, and Christian revelation. Understanding how this transformation came about, and what specific texts Pico believed he was reading when he read "Zoroaster," is essential to understanding Pico's position within the broader project of Renaissance concordism.

The texts in question were the *Chaldean Oracles*, a collection of hexameter verses preserved in fragments embedded in the works of late Platonic philosophers, above all Proclus and the eleventh-century Byzantine scholar Michael Psellus. In the Renaissance, these verses were attributed to Zoroaster — more precisely, to a tradition of Chaldean wisdom that Zoroaster was believed to have founded and that was transmitted through a chain of commentators including Julian the Theurgist, Porphyry, Iamblichus, and Proclus. The attribution was credible to Renaissance readers because it was ancient: Plato had spoken respectfully of Persian wisdom, later Platonists had identified Zoroaster as a forerunner of their own tradition, and the *Oracles* themselves spoke a language of cosmic hierarchy and divine fire that resonated with Neoplatonic metaphysics. Only modern scholarship has established what Casaubon would later demonstrate for the *Corpus Hermeticum*: the *Chaldean Oracles* are almost certainly a product of the second century CE, composed in Greek under the influence of Middle Platonism and Gnostic thought, with no authentic connection to ancient Persian religion.

In the structure of Pico's *prisca theologia*, Zoroaster typically occupies the first position in the genealogical chain — the earliest recoverable link. While Ficino's version of the sequence sometimes begins with Zoroaster and sometimes with Hermes Trismegistus, Pico's version consistently places the Chaldean sage at the origin of the wisdom tradition, followed by Orpheus, Pythagoras, and Plato. This placement reflects the Renaissance understanding of Chaldea — ancient Mesopotamia and Persia — as the most ancient locus of human wisdom after the Hebrew revelation. Zoroaster is the pagan counterpart to Moses, a sage who received a primordial theology that was subsequently transmitted, partially corrupted, and partially preserved through the Greek philosophical tradition. In the *Oration on the Dignity of Man*, Pico invokes Zoroaster alongside Plato's *Timaeus* and the Kabbalistic tradition when he sketches the philosophical genealogy that his proposed disputation would synthesize.

The *900 Conclusions* contain a section titled "Conclusions on Zoroaster and his Chaldean commentators" (section 8), comprising fifteen theses. Pico announced in a letter to Ficino written in the summer of 1486 that he possessed not merely the Greek fragments of the *Chaldean Oracles* known to his contemporaries but an original "Chaldean" text — complete and uncorrupted (*integra et absoluta*) where the Greek version was "full of faults and mutilated" (*mendosa et mutila*) (Farmer 1998, 13). This claim excited considerable interest among Renaissance classicists from the fifteenth through the seventeenth centuries, though modern scholars have largely overlooked it. Farmer argues that the "Chaldean" text Pico claimed to possess was almost certainly a forgery, and that Flavius Mithridates — Pico's first tutor in Hebrew, Aramaic, and related languages, and a figure whose reputation for textual fraud was well established — probably played a role in producing it (Farmer 1998, 13, 38–39).

The theses *secundum Zoroastrem et suos interpretes Chaldeos* that Pico derived from these sources reveal what he understood the Chaldean tradition to teach. The cosmology is Neoplatonic in structure: a supreme Father or paternal intellect generates a hierarchy of divine principles that descend through celestial spheres to the material world, sustained by a divine fire (*pyr*) that permeates all levels of reality. Pico's Zoroastrian theses place special emphasis on the triadic structure of divine reality — Father, Hecate-like intermediary, and the cosmic Demiurge — a triad that Pico, like Proclus and Iamblichus before him, read as a philosophical precursor to the Christian Trinity. The connection to theurgy is also present: the *Chaldean Oracles* prescribed specific ritual practices — theurgical rites involving fire, prayer, and contemplation — for the soul's ascent through the cosmic hierarchy to reunion with the divine source. Pico inherits this theurgical dimension through his engagement with Iamblichus and Proclus, both of whom treated the *Oracles* as the authoritative text of theurgical philosophy.

The position of Zoroaster in Pico's hierarchy of legitimate magic is significant and stands in telling contrast to the treatment of Hermes Trismegistus. In the *Oration* and *Apology*, Pico provides lists of ancient magicians whose traditions he claims as the sources of natural magic; these lists include Zoroaster alongside Pythagoras, Plato, Empedocles, Democritus, Plotinus, and others. Zoroaster is thus named as a practitioner of legitimate natural magic — the kind of magic that operates through knowledge of natural and divine powers rather than through demonic invocation. This is precisely the characterization that Pico denies to Hermes Trismegistus, and the asymmetry is philosophically significant. Farmer connects it to the broader structure of Pico's project: Pico was building a synthesis centered on Kabbalah and the Chaldean tradition, in which Zoroaster and the Hebrew Kabbalists shared an authoritative position, while the Hermetic tradition (which Pico closely associated with Ficino) was treated with reserve.

The concordist ambition that drives Pico's treatment of Zoroaster is visible in the way the Chaldean theses interact with the other sections of the *900 Conclusions*. Pico's syncretic strategy required showing that Zoroastrian theosophy, Orphic theology, Pythagorean mathematics, Platonic metaphysics, and Jewish Kabbalah all expressed the same truths under different symbolic vocabularies. The fusion he attempts at thesis 8>1, where Zoroastrian doctrine is connected to a method of metaphysical division that appears also in his own "philosophical conclusions" (5>15), illustrates this program: a Chaldean sage and a Florentine philosopher of 1486 are shown to be drawing on the same underlying reality. More spectacularly, Pico argues in the *Oration* that Zoroaster had predicted Christ's Incarnation — a claim he supported by reference to the Chaldean texts he believed he possessed, written in a language he represented as more ancient and more complete than the Greek.

The concordist argument required not only that Zoroaster agree with the other ancient sages but that he be compatible with Moses — that Chaldean and Hebrew wisdom, despite their apparent differences of cultural origin, expressed the same primordial revelation. This requirement shaped the way Pico read the *Chaldean Oracles*. The triadic structure of Chaldean divine reality could be mapped onto the Kabbalistic sefirot; the theurgical ascent through the spheres could be read as a parallel to the Kabbalistic ascent through the four worlds; the divine fire (*pyr*) that animated the cosmic hierarchy could be identified with aspects of divine activity described in Hebrew mystical texts. Pico's theses *secundum Zoroastrem* are thus not simple expositions of what the Chaldean tradition taught but highly synthetic constructions in which Chaldean vocabulary has been used to express a system that is simultaneously Neoplatonic, Kabbalistic, and proto-Christian.

The later history of Pico's engagement with Zoroaster is complicated by his posthumous *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem*, the unfinished anti-astrological work that Pico left at his death and that was edited and published by his nephew Gianfrancesco Pico under the influence of Savonarola. In the *Disputationes*, Zoroaster is treated with striking contempt: "Who was more powerful in antiquity in astrology, in magic, and in all superstition than Zoroaster? And yet Zoroaster was killed in battle by Ninus, the first king of Assyria, who was neither an astrologer nor magician" (quoted in Farmer 1998, 144). This reversal — from privileged ancient sage to exemplar of superstition — represents either a dramatic intellectual about-face by Pico in his final years or (as Farmer argues) evidence of posthumous tampering by the Savonarolan editors who shaped the published text of the *Disputationes* to align with Savonarola's anti-astrological and anti-magical agenda. The question of whether the later Pico genuinely abandoned his earlier Zoroastrian commitments remains unresolved, but the weight of evidence supports Farmer's suspicion that the most extreme anti-syncretic passages in the *Disputationes* reflect editorial intervention rather than Pico's own mature thought.

For the intellectual world of 1486, when the *900 Conclusions* were published, Zoroaster functioned as a necessary anchor for the *prisca theologia* program. Without a primordial sage who predated the Greeks and who could be shown to have taught the same doctrines as Plato, Kabbalah, and Christianity, the entire edifice of concordist synthesis would lack its historical foundation. Zoroaster provided that foundation precisely because the *Chaldean Oracles* — read through Proclus and understood through Platonic categories — offered a cosmological theology rich enough to bear the interpretive weight Pico placed on it. That the *Oracles* were almost certainly composed in the second century CE, in the same late antique milieu that produced the Neoplatonism through which they were being read, is an irony that Pico could not have perceived. He believed he was recovering the original Chaldean source from which all subsequent wisdom had derived; in reality, he was reading a sophisticated philosophical poem that was itself a creative synthesis of earlier traditions, produced by authors as engaged in concordist thinking as he was himself.

The scholarly tradition that has most illuminated Pico's Zoroastrianism runs from Wilhelm Kroll's early critical edition of the *Chaldean Oracles* (1894) through Hans Lewy's systematic study (1956) to the more recent work of Ruth Majercik (1989) on the *Oracles* as a document in their own right. For the Renaissance reception specifically, Farmer's *Syncretism in the West* remains the essential guide, offering both a comprehensive edition of the theses themselves and an analytical framework for understanding how Pico's syncretic method transformed whatever sources he had before him. The theses *secundum Zoroastrem et suos interpretes Chaldeos* cannot be read as reliable reports of what the *Chaldean Oracles* actually say; they can only be read as evidence of how Pico, in the remarkable intellectual crucible of Florence in 1486, constructed a Zoroaster adequate to the demands of his own philosophical project.
