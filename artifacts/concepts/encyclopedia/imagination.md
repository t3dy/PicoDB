Imagination (*imaginatio*, φαντασία) is, in Pico's philosophical anthropology, a double-faced faculty: both the necessary mediating condition of ordinary cognition and the primary obstacle to the soul's ascent toward purely intellectual apprehension. In the *Commento sopra una Canzone de Amore* (ca. 1486), Pico deploys a striking allegorical interpretation of the Orpheus myth that places imagination at the center of his account of how the soul fails to recover the intelligible truth it seeks. That allegory, which Allen has shown to be Pico's most self-consciously original contribution to the tradition of Orphic commentary (Allen 2017, 143–47), crystallizes a set of concerns about the psychology of intellectual ascent that runs throughout Pico's writings.

The philosophical background is Aristotle's account of *phantasia* in the *De anima* III.3. For Aristotle, imagination is the faculty that produces *phantasmata* — mental images derived from sensory experience — which persist after the original sensation has ceased and which serve as the necessary material for intellectual cognition. The intellect, on Aristotle's account, cannot think without images: "the soul never thinks without a *phantasma*" (*De anima* 431a16). This relationship between imagination and intellect is not a defect but a structural feature of human cognition as Aristotle understood it: we are embodied beings, and our thinking is therefore necessarily mediated through the imagery that derives from our embodied, sensory engagement with the world. The *phantasma* is not identical to the sensory impression (*aisthema*) from which it derives; it is a more processed, flexible mental representation that can be recombined, generalized, and made available to the active intellect for abstraction.

The Arabic philosophical tradition developed this Aristotelian psychology into a more systematic account of what came to be called the "internal senses" (*sensus interni*). Avicenna's psychology, as transmitted through his *Kitab al-Nafs* (the psychological section of the *Shifa*, known in Latin as the *De anima*), distinguished five internal senses: common sense (*sensus communis*), imagination in the sense of *khayāl* (a storehouse of sensory forms), the compositive imagination or *mutakhayyila* (which combines and rearranges stored images), the estimative faculty (*wahm*), and memory. This five-part scheme systematized the terrain between raw sensation and pure intellection, making imagination a central node in a complex hierarchy of cognitive powers. The Avicennan psychology was enormously influential in the Latin scholastic tradition, and it formed part of the background that Pico inherited both directly and through his Paduan education under Elia del Medigo and his Parisian study of scholastic philosophy (Akopyan 2021, 8).

For the Neoplatonic tradition, imagination poses a more acute problem than it does for Aristotle, because Neoplatonism is committed to a strong account of intellectual ascent in which the soul must progressively detach itself from sensory and imaginative attachment in order to rise toward the purely intellectual and ultimately toward the One. In Plotinus, *phantasia* is a faculty of the lower soul that keeps it anchored to the sensory world by providing a constant supply of images. The Neoplatonic program of ascent requires that the soul withdraw its attention from these images and turn toward the purely intelligible; imagination is the faculty that resists this withdrawal. Iamblichus and Proclus elaborated similar accounts, and Marsilio Ficino's Renaissance Platonism inherited this understanding of imagination as both necessary for ordinary cognition and problematic for the soul's higher aspirations. In Ficino's framework, the *spiritus* — the subtle pneumatic vapor mediating between body and rational soul — carries the images of the imagination, and the proper management of these images through music, solar contemplation, and other therapeutic practices is part of the regimen that keeps the contemplative life spiritually healthy (Allen 2017, 2422–53).

It is against this background that Pico's treatment of imagination in the *Commento* becomes philosophically legible. The passage in question concerns Benivieni's fourth stanza, and the interpretive occasion is provided by Plato's account in the *Symposium* (179A–D) of Orpheus's failure to recover Eurydice from Hades. In Plato's version, Phaedrus accuses Orpheus of cowardice — of refusing to die for love, of trying "to scheme his way into Hades" while still alive rather than surrendering himself entirely. The consequence is that Orpheus sees not the true Eurydice but only her wraith (*eidolon*), an image or ghost of the real woman. Ficino had registered this passage in his *De amore* (1.4) without venturing an allegorical reading, and Pico seizes on this apparent oversight with an air of proud discovery: "Although this meaning is subtle and profound, it is nevertheless so consistent with the facts that it seems to me almost a wonder that neither Marsilio nor anyone else has understood it on the basis of Plato's text. My conscience is my witness, that the first time I ever read the Symposium, I had no sooner finished reading Plato's words in this passage than this interpretation came into my mind" (*Commento*, in Allen 2017, 145).

Pico's reading maps the Orpheus myth onto the psychology of intellectual ascent. Orpheus, in Pico's allegory, represents the intellect that seeks the intelligible Ideas but refuses to make the complete sacrificial surrender that genuine intellectual apprehension requires. Because Orpheus retained the functions of imagination and discursive reason — because he went to Hades alive, with his cognitive machinery still operative — he was unable to see the true Eurydice, the Idea of Beauty in its full intelligible reality. What he saw instead was "only some fantasm or likeness of them, shining either in the passible intellect or in the imagination" (Allen 2017, 145). The wraith of Eurydice is precisely the imaginative image of intelligible beauty: a copy mediated through the lower faculties, still structured by sensory form, not the reality itself apprehended in its pure intelligibility.

The philosophical claim embedded in this allegory is demanding. For Pico, the true apprehension of the intelligible Ideas requires not merely concentrated attention but what he calls a "death" — a severance from the lower cognitive faculties, including imagination, that is so complete it constitutes a transformation of the knower's mode of being. He distinguishes two such deaths in the following section of the *Commento*. The "first death" is the amatory ecstasy in which the soul separates from the body to the extent that all its lower powers cease to function (save the vegetative, which maintains the body's minimal life), while the intellect "talks face to face" with the heavenly Venus, the Idea of Beauty, and feeds upon her image. The "second death" is the total surrender in which the body drops entirely away and the soul joins with the heavenly Venus "in an intimate embrace" — what the Kabbalists call *binsica*, death from the kiss, exemplified for Pico by the deaths of Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, Aaron, and Miriam. Orpheus, by contrast, attempted intellectual ascent without any such death. His imagination remained active, and as long as imagination remained active, what he grasped was only the shadow of what he sought (Allen 2017, 145–46).

This allegory has a precise technical implication for Pico's psychology. Imagination (*imaginatio*, *fantasia*) is not a defective or pathological faculty; it is the normal operation of the embodied mind. But it is by nature oriented toward sensory forms, toward the likenesses of things rather than the things themselves. Even when it reaches toward intelligible realities — even when the philosopher concentrates his attention on the Ideas — imagination delivers not the Ideas themselves but their reflections in the sensible or quasi-sensible medium of the *phantasma*. The Platonic philosopher who takes himself to be grasping the Ideas while still relying on imagination is, on Pico's account, in precisely Orpheus's position: looking at the ghost of Eurydice and mistaking it for the woman herself. The ascent program of the *Oration on the Dignity of Man* — moving up from the vegetative life through the animal, the celestial, the cherubic, and ultimately the seraphic — requires at each stage a genuine relinquishment of the cognitive mode appropriate to the lower level. Imagination belongs to the animal level; to genuinely ascend beyond it, one must cease to rely on imaginative cognition.

The magical dimension of imagination in Ficino's system provides a contrasting context for Pico's more austere treatment. In Ficino's *De vita coelitus comparanda* (the third book of the *De vita triplici*, 1489), imagination plays a central role in natural magic. The magician who seeks to draw down favorable celestial influences must first form appropriate images in his own imagination — images charged with the qualities of the celestial body whose influence he seeks to capture. The *spiritus animalis*, the subtle pneumatic medium of the soul's lower operations, is the vehicle for these images, and when properly charged by concentrated imaginative intention it can transmit its influence outward through the *spiritus mundi*, the world-soul's analogous subtle medium, to attract celestial virtue into material objects (Allen 2017, 2422–53). Copenhaver has analyzed this magical use of imagination and *spiritus* in Ficino with particular precision, showing how it depends on a specific interpretation of Plotinus's *Enneads* 4.3–5 and on the theory of *logoi spermatikoi* or seminal reasons embedded in the world soul (Copenhaver 1984).

Pico is notably more cautious about this magical dimension of imagination than Ficino. The *900 Conclusions* include theses on natural magic (*magia naturalis*) and on the Kabbalah that situate Pico within the tradition that Ficino had cultivated, but the *Commento*'s Orphic allegory points in a different direction: for Pico, the philosophically serious question about imagination is not how to use it more effectively in magical operations but how to transcend it in the pursuit of genuine intellectual knowledge. In the *Oration*, the ascent program is framed as a matter of moral and intellectual choice — the soul chooses its own nature — not as a matter of attracting celestial influences through imaginative technique. Pico's anthropology is, as Allen observes, more Socratic than Neoplatonic in this respect: it emphasizes the soul's autonomy and inner decision over its embeddedness in cosmic sympathies and celestial channels (Allen 2017, 730–31).

The *Apology* (*Apologia conclusionum suarum*, 1487), written to defend the condemned theses before the papal commission, contains Pico's most sustained attempt to clarify the relationship between natural magic and demonic magic, and to insist that natural magic (*magia naturalis*) operates through legitimate natural causes rather than through demonic invocation. This distinction is important for the status of imagination: demonic magic involves the imagination precisely in its susceptibility to external spiritual influence — the imagination is the faculty through which the demon can manipulate the soul, because it operates with sensory-type images and is therefore accessible to beings that operate through sensory-type media. Natural magic, by contrast, is supposed to work through the natural causal powers of things without requiring this imaginative susceptibility. Pico's argument in the *Apology* thus requires him to draw a line between the legitimate use of natural correspondences and the illegitimate engagement of imagination with demonic forces — a line that is philosophically difficult to draw within the Ficinian framework, where imagination and spiritus are precisely the media through which celestial and quasi-celestial influences operate.

In the *Heptaplus* (1489), Pico's allegorical commentary on the Mosaic account of creation, the faculty of imagination appears implicitly in the tripartite cosmological scheme that maps the three worlds — sublunary, celestial, and supercelestial — onto the three parts of the human being — body, soul, and intellect. The faculties associated with the middle world (the celestial) include precisely those that mediate between pure intellection and embodied sensation: imagination among them. Biblical exegesis in the *Heptaplus* deploys a fourfold hermeneutic that culminates in the anagogical sense, the sense of the soul's ascent toward God; and the anagogical reading consistently points beyond the imaginative grasp that characterizes the literal and allegorical levels toward a mode of apprehension that leaves images behind.

The *De ente et uno* (*On Being and Unity*, 1491–92) does not treat imagination directly, but its philosophical argument — that Plato and Aristotle agree on the identity of Being and the One, contra the Neoplatonic tradition — has indirect implications for the psychology of imagination. By rejecting the Neoplatonic hierarchy in which the One stands above Being, Pico removes the most demanding requirement of the Neoplatonic ascent program: the need to ascend beyond all cognitive content, including the content of intellectual apprehension, into the darkness of union with the pure One. If Being and the One are identical, then the fully intellectual apprehension of Being is already the highest available mode of knowing — the demand to transcend imagination does not require the hyperintellectual apophasis of Neoplatonism's highest stages, but simply the transition from sensory-imaginative to genuinely intellectual cognition. This is, in a sense, a less severe but more philosophically coherent account of what the Orpheus allegory had already suggested.

For scholars of PicoDB, imagination serves as a key connector between Pico's philosophical psychology, his allegorical hermeneutics, his attitude toward natural magic, and his relationship to Ficino's system. Allen's "Eurydice in Hades" essay (Allen 2017, essay 16) is the essential point of entry for the Orphic allegory in the *Commento*. Copenhaver's analysis of the magical role of imagination and *spiritus* in Ficino establishes the contrasting backdrop against which Pico's more restrained position can be assessed. The concept connects directly to the entries on ascent, love (*amore*), intellect, natural magic, and the allegorical method in the *Commento*.
