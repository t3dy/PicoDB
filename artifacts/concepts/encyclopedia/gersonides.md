Levi ben Gershom (1288–1344 CE), known in the medieval Latin West as Gersonides and in the Jewish tradition by the Hebrew acronym *RaLBaG* (Rabbi Levi ben Gershom), was a Provencal Jewish philosopher, mathematician, astronomer, and biblical exegete who represents the most systematic attempt within medieval Jewish thought to reconcile rigorous Aristotelian philosophy with the commitments of Jewish theology. Born in Bagnols-sur-Cèze in the Languedoc region, Gersonides spent his life in southern France and was in contact with the papal court at Avignon, which commissioned astronomical work from him. His major philosophical work, *Milhamot ha-Shem* (The Wars of the Lord, completed around 1329), engages the hardest problems in medieval Aristotelian philosophy — the nature of the intellect and individual immortality, divine knowledge of particulars and divine providence, creation ex nihilo, the nature of prophecy, and the movements of the celestial spheres — with a rigor and a willingness to follow arguments to uncomfortable conclusions that set him apart from his Jewish contemporaries. Gersonides was an Aristotelian rationalist in the tradition inaugurated for Jewish philosophy by Maimonides, but he pushed beyond Maimonides at several points and arrived at positions that generated substantial controversy within later Jewish thought.

Pico della Mirandola knew of Gersonides through multiple channels. The most direct evidence comes from the *900 Conclusions* of 1486, which include no separate section of theses dedicated to Gersonides, but which engage his positions through the broader Arabic and Jewish philosophical traditions that the *Conclusions* map. Copenhaver, in his authoritative recent study of the *Oration* and its intellectual world, notes that Pico "did not claim to be a sage, however, like Maimonides or Gersonides — Rabbi Levi ben Gerson, Ralbag. Neither Ralbag nor Rambam was a Kabbalist: both were Aristotelian rationalists, and Gersonides was a naturalist as well. All the Jewish sages known to Pico — Kabbalists or not, philosophers or not — had secrets for him to find" (Copenhaver 2019, 353–54). This characterization is precise and important: Gersonides figures in Pico's intellectual world not primarily as a Kabbalistic source but as a philosophical one, a representative of the Maimonidean rationalist tradition that Pico needed to engage even as he moved toward positions that went well beyond anything that tradition endorsed. The conjunction of Gersonides with Maimonides identifies them as the two authoritative Jewish Aristotelians, and the point that some of Gersonides' works existed in medieval Latin versions — Copenhaver notes that "like some of Ralbag's books" they were available in Latin alongside the *Guide for the Perplexed* — suggests that Pico may have had access to at least some of Gersonides' thought without relying entirely on intermediaries.

The library inventory of Pico documents the scope of what was available to him. Howlett (2021, 200–201), synthesizing the evidence from Andreatta's 2014 study of Pico's library, notes that Pico owned Gersonides' commentary on the Song of Songs, together with six further works: the Commentary on the Torah, the Commentary on Job, the Commentary on Proverbs, possibly the *Sefer ha-bekkesh ha-yashar*, and the astronomical treatise *Sefer ha-tekhunah*. This is a substantial collection, which indicates that Pico's engagement with Gersonides extended well beyond a passing acquaintance. The commentary on the Song of Songs is particularly interesting in the light of a specific passage of Pico's thought: Howlett demonstrates that the passage in the *Oration* describing the "death from a kiss" (*binsica*) — the ecstatic intellectual death in which the soul so unites itself to separated substances that it abandons the body — derives in part from Gersonides' Commentary on the Song of Songs, "deriving in turn from various Talmudic (Mishnah and Midrash) discussions of the Song of Songs 1:2" (Howlett 2021, 208, citing Andreatta 2014, 190–91). In Gersonides' reading of the Song of Songs, God reaches down to kiss human intellect in an active divine reaching toward the human — an image that combines the Maimonidean account of ecstatic intellectual death with a distinctively Gersonidean emphasis on the active movement of the divine. The relevance of this to Pico's celebrated *Oration* passage on death from a kiss shows how Gersonides functioned for Pico not merely as a philosophical authority to be catalogued but as a live exegetical resource for the elaboration of his own positions.

Gersonides' most distinctive and historically controversial philosophical position concerns the intellect and individual immortality. The problem is generated by the Averroist account of the Active Intellect, which Averroes had argued is a single, universal intellect shared by all humanity, not individuated by the bodies it informs. If this is correct, the question arises of whether there can be any individual immortality at all: if individual human intellects are merely modes of a single universal intellect, what survives the death of the individual body? Gersonides' response to this problem in *Milhamot ha-Shem* is philosophically sophisticated. He accepted the Averroist doctrine of the single Active Intellect as a correct account of the principle through which individual human intellects are actualized, but argued for a distinctive account of the acquired intellect (*ha-sekhel ha-nikneh*) — the body of organized knowledge that the individual intellect acquires through philosophical activity over a lifetime. For Gersonides, the acquired intellect constitutes the genuinely individual achievement of philosophical activity, and it is this individuated body of intellectual achievement, not the common material intellect, that survives death and constitutes individual immortality. The position attempts to preserve both the Averroist account of the Active Intellect (which Gersonides regarded as philosophically demonstrable) and Jewish commitment to individual survival after death, holding the two together by locating immortality in acquired rather than material intellect.

This position placed Gersonides in direct controversy with those Jewish thinkers who accepted Averroist monopsychism as logically entailing the denial of individual immortality, and with those who rejected Averroism entirely in favor of Maimonides' more theologically cautious approach. It also generated the distinctive charge, registered by subsequent readers, that Gersonides' naturalism led him to subordinate certain theological commitments to philosophical demonstration. His account of divine providence and divine knowledge of particulars is the most contested instance: Gersonides argued, against both Maimonides and the Kabbalists, that God's knowledge does not extend to individual contingent particulars but only to the general laws and orderly patterns of nature. This was a position that troubled his Jewish contemporaries and that later Jewish philosophers, including Isaac Abravanel, argued against at length. The debate between Abravanel and Gersonides over the interpretation of *tohu* and *bohu* (the primordial void and formlessness of Genesis 1:2) illustrates the scope of the controversy: Gersonides read these terms through an Aristotelian hylomorphic lens (with *tohu* representing form and *bohu* matter in a famous reversal that Abravanel found philosophically flawed), while Abravanel rejected this reading as inconsistent both with the text and with Plotinian metaphysics (Ogren 2014, 140–41, in the Supplements volume on Abravanel, Alemanno, and Pico).

For Pico, the Gersonidean position on the intellect offered one answer — a carefully argued and philosophically serious one — to the question of how individual intellectual achievement relates to the common principles through which intellectual activity is possible. The broader Arabic and Jewish intellect debate, of which Gersonides' account is a major episode, provides the philosophical context for one strand of Pico's own thinking about intellectual ascent and ultimate felicity. The *Heptaplus* of 1489, Pico's elaborately structured interpretation of the six days of creation, treats the intellective world as the highest of the three created worlds and describes the ultimate human felicity as the soul's return to this intellectual world — a return that, in Pico's account, depends on the individual soul's having actualized its intellectual potential during earthly life. Though the Heptaplus is saturated with Kabbalistic material rather than with Gersonidean philosophy, the underlying concern with the conditions under which the individual intellect can achieve genuine and lasting actualization connects Pico's program to the debates in which Gersonides had been a central participant.

Gersonides' biblical commentaries, particularly the Pentateuch commentary and the Commentary on Genesis, contributed to the broader tradition of Jewish philosophical exegesis that Pico encountered through several channels, including Recanati's *Commentary on the Torah* (which was Mithridates' primary Kabbalistic translation source for the *Conclusions*). As an interpreter of Genesis, Gersonides offers a rigorously philosophical account of creation that reads the six days and the opening verses of Genesis through an Aristotelian lens. The hylomorphic interpretation of *tohu* and *bohu* mentioned above is one example; his reading of the creative acts as the successive actualizations of form in matter is another. This tradition of philosophical Genesis commentary stands in the background of Pico's *Heptaplus*, though the *Heptaplus* itself operates more through Kabbalistic than through Aristotelian categories. Black (2006) has shown how deeply the *Heptaplus* is indebted to Jewish exegetical traditions that Pico had encountered through Mithridates' translations; the Gersonidean strand of that tradition is present less in direct quotation than in the general intellectual environment that made sophisticated philosophical interpretation of the Genesis narrative normal and authoritative within Jewish learning.

The status of Gersonides within Pico's syncretic program is therefore complex and not reducible to a simple characterization. He is not primarily a Kabbalistic source for Pico in the way that Recanati or Abraham Abulafia are; Copenhaver is right that Gersonides was an Aristotelian rationalist and not a mystic. But he is also not simply one more Averroist whose positions Pico catalogues alongside the Arabic thinkers. He represents, rather, the application of rigorous Aristotelian reasoning to specifically Jewish theological problems — the immortality of the individual soul, divine knowledge, and the interpretation of scripture — and it is in this capacity that Pico found him useful. The acquired intellect doctrine, the philosophical reading of Genesis, and the "kiss of death" passage in the Song of Songs commentary all point to a thinker whose engagement with the questions that mattered most to Pico — the conditions of individual intellectual perfection, the metaphysics of creation, and the ascent of the soul — was more serious and more directly relevant to Pico's own concerns than the simple category of "Jewish Aristotelian" might suggest. Howlett's analysis of the *binsica* passage demonstrates that Gersonides' influence on the *Oration* extends to one of its most celebrated passages, and that Pico was reading him with the kind of interpretive attention that characterizes his engagement with the thinkers he found genuinely illuminating.
