The concept of *ens* (being) occupies the center of Pico della Mirandola's most technically demanding philosophical work, the *De ente et uno* (On Being and Unity), composed in 1491 and the only surviving fragment of the ambitious *Concordia Platonis et Aristotelis* (Concord of Plato and Aristotle) that Pico had projected but death prevented him from completing. The treatise's occasion was personal and humanist in the best sense: Angelo Poliziano, the great philologist and Pico's closest friend, had been present at a conversation between Pico and Lorenzo de' Medici, in which Lorenzo argued — taking the Platonist side — that the One (*unum*) was superior to and prior to Being (*ens*). Poliziano, unconvinced, turned to Pico and asked him to set down, in brief compass, the resolution to this ancient quarrel. Pico's response, addressed to Poliziano in a preface of characteristic warmth, is the *De ente et uno*: a short treatise of nine surviving chapters, deploying Aristotelian metaphysics, Thomistic participation theory, and careful philological reading of the Platonic dialogues to argue that being and unity are convertible — the same thing viewed from different angles — and that neither is hierarchically prior to the other.

The philosophical stakes of this position are high, and they set Pico directly against the dominant tendency of Florentine Neoplatonism as represented by Marsilio Ficino. For Ficino, following Plotinus and Proclus, the One (*to hen*) stands absolutely above Intellect (*nous*) and Being (*to on*): the first hypostasis of the Plotinian emanative hierarchy is utterly simple, ineffable, and beyond rational determination. Being, on this account, belongs to the second hypostasis — Intellect — and the One is accordingly *supra-ens*, prior to and more universal than being. This is the position Pico's *De ente et uno* engages and refutes, though the work is diplomatic in its silence about Ficino by name. Ficino, for his part, was not diplomatic: commenting on the *Parmenides*, he exclaimed that he wished "that marvelous youth [*mirandus iuvenis*]" had carefully considered his arguments before advancing a position "contrary to the opinion of all the Platonists," as Allen notes (Allen 2017, essay IX). The second Ficino-Pico controversy, as Allen has analyzed it, hinges precisely on whether the *Parmenides* is a metaphysical revelation of divine hierarchies or a dialectical exercise in logical method.

Pico's strategy in *De ente et uno* unfolds in two principal movements. The first is hermeneutic: Pico argues that Plato himself never asserted the priority of the One over Being as a metaphysical doctrine. Reading the *Parmenides*, Pico insists that the whole dialogue is "nothing else than a sort of exercise in dialectic" (*nihil aliud est, quam dialectica quaedam exercitatio*), and that the famous first hypothesis — "if the one is, what follows?" — cannot be read as a direct disclosure of the divine nature. The Neoplatonists, Plotinus and Proclus above all, have mistaken a logical exercise for a theological revelation. The *Sophist*, by contrast, actually suggests the *equivalence* of being and unity: Plato argues there that "to say something is to say some one thing," such that "not-one" and "nothing" are equivalent, and consequently one and something are equal — a conclusion that, in Pico's reading, confirms the Aristotelian convertibility thesis rather than the Plotinian hierarchy.

The second movement is systematic: Pico deploys Aristotelian and Thomistic resources to show that being and unity share the same extension and are therefore real convertibles, differing only in the mode of consideration (*ratione*). As Aristotle repeatedly argues in the *Metaphysics* (book 4 and elsewhere), "one man" and "man" are the same thing; being and unity are co-extensive and reciprocal, because a thing cannot be one without being, and cannot be without being one. Indivision — which is what unity means at its most fundamental — is inseparable from determinate actuality, which is what being means. Salas has shown in detail how Pico's argument in chapters three and four of *De ente et uno* appropriates Thomas Aquinas's distinction between *ens* and *esse*: *ens* (being as the concrete subject) participates in *esse* (being as the abstract act of existing), and this participation structure allows Pico to make room within his convertibility thesis for the Neoplatonic intuition that something stands "above being" in eminence, without conceding the full Plotinian priority of the One (Salas 2014, 351–77). The key move appears in chapter four: Pico distinguishes between "concrete" and "abstract" names. *Ens* is concrete — it names what participates in *esse*. *Esse* is abstract — it names what things participate in. God, as the plenitude of being (*plenitudo entis*), is not himself a being who participates in esse; rather, God *is* esse itself. For this reason one can say, with Dionysius the Areopagite, that God is "above being" — not because he falls short of being, but because he surpasses every finite mode of participation in it. The One (*unum*) and Being (*ens*), on this reading, are identical in scope; and at the summit of reality, the two coincide in God, who is simultaneously *plenissima entitas, individua unitas, solidissima veritas, beatissima bonitas* — the Latin phrasing of the *Opera Omnia* text — the scholastic transcendentals (being, unity, truth, goodness) all converging without remainder in the divine simplicity.

The relationship of this position to Aquinas is central for understanding what kind of philosopher Pico is in this text. As Victor Salas has argued at length, *De ente et uno* is substantially a Thomistic enterprise: Pico's distinction of *esse* and *essentia*, his use of participation, and his account of the transcendentals as convertibles all follow the framework Thomas developed in the *De ente et essentia* and the commentary on Boethius's *De hebdomadibus*. Pico's library contained multiple works of Aquinas, and the *De ente et uno* deploys Thomistic vocabulary with evident familiarity. At the same time, Salas notes a significant dissonance: Thomas's theory of analogy — the doctrine that being is said of God and creatures neither univocally nor equivocally but analogically — provides the precise instrument for holding together the Creator-creature relationship in a way that neither flattens difference into identity nor severs connection into equivocation. Pico's account, while structurally Thomistic, slackens the analogical tension in a direction that risks eliding the difference between the absolute actuality of divine *esse* and the participated *esse* of finite beings (Salas 2014, 162). Whether this is a deliberate philosophical choice or an incompleteness left by premature death — the *De ente et uno* was planned to be a much larger work with seven books — is a question the single surviving book cannot answer.

The Aristotelian dimension of *De ente et uno* is equally important. Pico is not simply a Thomist; he is engaged in what was to be a life's work of reconciling Plato and Aristotle, two philosophical traditions that fifteenth-century Italian intellectuals had set against each other with increasing polemical intensity. The controversy had erupted in a series of exchanges between Greek scholars at the Council of Florence (1438–45) — Gemistus Pletho defending Plato, George of Trebizond attacking him, Cardinal Bessarion attempting mediation — and continued through the later Quattrocento in works that Pico knew well. Against this background, the *De ente et uno* argues that the convertibility of being and unity, properly understood, is a position common to Plato and Aristotle: neither philosopher subordinated one to the other. The subordination is the innovation of Plotinus and his successors, who misread the *Parmenides* as metaphysical doctrine and derived from it a hierarchy of hypostases in which the One sits above Intellect-qua-Being. Pico's philological reading — treating the *Parmenides* as dialectical exercise — is designed to show that this Neoplatonic reading, however influential, is not Platonic in any strict sense.

Copenhaver has argued that this dimension of *De ente et uno* reveals Pico as a genuine philosopher engaging in technical metaphysics, not merely an eclectic synthesizer: the treatise demonstrates careful argument, precise use of terms, and willingness to take a definite and unpopular side against the most authoritative Platonist of his generation (Copenhaver 2019, passim). The incompleteness of the work — the single surviving book out of a planned seven — has tended to distort assessments of Pico's capacities, making it appear a sketch rather than an achievement. But even in its truncated form, *De ente et uno* works through the problem systematically and with genuine philosophical control.

Edelheit has emphasized the theological implications of Pico's position. If Being and the One are convertible, then the first principle of reality — identified by Pico with God — is not ineffable and supra-rational in the Plotinian sense, but is the *plenitudo entis*, the fullness of being, accessible in principle to rational inquiry even if its depths exceed human comprehension. This is a position much closer to Aquinas's doctrine of *esse* as the act of all acts (*actus omnium actuum*) than to Ficino's mystical Neoplatonism, in which the One's transcendence of being places it beyond any positive rational determination. For Edelheit, this matters enormously for Pico's "humanist theology": the convertibility of being and unity allows Pico to affirm that genuine philosophical inquiry into being is also, properly ordered, an inquiry into God — not a mystical bypass of reason but its highest fulfillment (Edelheit 2008, passim). God is the fullness of being, not its transcendent negation; positive theology and negative theology are both available and their relationship is one of complementary approaches to the same divine reality, not a hierarchy in which negative theology wholly supersedes the positive.

The text's argument also engages Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, a figure whose authority bore equally on both sides of the dispute. Pico reads Dionysius carefully: while Dionysius does speak of God as beyond being and beyond naming, he also affirms God's self-identification to Moses in Exodus 3:14 — "I am who am" (*ego sum qui sum*) — as a disclosure of genuine being. Pico's reading is that Dionysius's negative theology operates on a different logical register from his positive: to deny being of God is not to exclude God from reality but to signal that God's manner of being infinitely exceeds what we mean when we predicate being of finite things. This reading draws on the same logic as the distinction between *ens* (concrete) and *esse* (abstract): God, as pure *esse*, exceeds the category of *ens* precisely because he is being in the most absolute and underived sense.

The *De ente et uno* was followed by objections from the Aristotelian philosopher Antonio Cittadini, to which Pico replied — and these exchanges survive alongside the main text in the 1519 edition, indicating that the controversy was resuming when death intervened. The treatise thus had an immediate polemical life that its incomplete state might otherwise obscure. In the larger architecture of Pico's thought, *De ente et uno* stands as the systematic philosophical foundation for the concordist project that animated the *900 Conclusions* and the projected but unwritten *Concord of Plato and Aristotle*: before one can argue that Plato and Aristotle agree, one must show that their most fundamental metaphysical commitments — about what it means to be — are compatible. This is what *De ente et uno* attempts, and its central argument is that the apparent disagreement, the quarrel over whether the One transcends Being, is the invention of later interpreters who read their own metaphysics into dialogues that do not warrant it.
