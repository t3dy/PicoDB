# Prisca Theologia

The phrase *prisca theologia* — ancient theology, or the theology of the ancients — names one of the most consequential ideas in fifteenth-century Florentine intellectual culture, and one of the most historically misleading. Its logic is simple: certain ancient sages, writing before Plato and in some cases before Moses, were the recipients of divine wisdom — a primordial revelation that constitutes the common source of all authentic religious and philosophical truth. This wisdom was transmitted across the ages, passing from one inspired sage to another, expressed in different vocabularies and symbolic forms but always pointing toward the same divine realities. When Christian theology, Platonic philosophy, ancient poetry, and esoteric cosmology appear to teach the same things, the explanation is genealogical: they derive from the same original source, the divine wisdom granted to humanity at the dawn of history.

The term and the idea are associated above all with Marsilio Ficino, whose translations and philosophical syntheses dominated the intellectual life of the Florentine Platonic Academy in the decades before Pico's arrival. Ficino's account of the *prisca theologia* arranged its principal witnesses in a chain beginning with Zoroaster, through Hermes Trismegistus, Orpheus, Pythagoras, and Philolaus, culminating in Plato, whose philosophy Ficino read as the culmination and clarification of an inherited wisdom ultimately compatible with, and in crucial respects anticipatory of, Christian theology. For Ficino, this chain of transmission was confirmed by the texts attributed to these figures: the *Corpus Hermeticum*, a collection of Greek dialogues attributed to the legendary Egyptian sage Hermes Trismegistus; the *Chaldean Oracles*, attributed to Zoroaster or his followers; and the collection of hexameters known as the Orphic Hymns. These texts circulated with the authority of deep antiquity. Ficino translated the *Corpus Hermeticum* into Latin in 1463 at Cosimo de' Medici's urgent request, completing it before he turned to Plato — a telling index of how highly he and his patron valued Hermetic authority.

The scholarly problem that all subsequent readers of the *prisca theologia* tradition must confront is that the antiquity these texts claimed is a fiction. The *Corpus Hermeticum* is not the work of an ancient Egyptian sage: it is a collection of late antique Greek texts, composed between roughly 100 and 300 CE in an environment of Platonizing religious philosophy, almost certainly in Alexandria. The *Chaldean Oracles* are second-century compositions, probably from the circle of Julian the Theurgist. The Orphic poems are similarly late antique productions, not the works of a legendary singer who preceded Homer. When Isaac Casaubon demonstrated the late date of the Hermetic texts by internal analysis in 1614, he exposed the fundamental anachronism on which the *prisca theologia* framework rested. The supposed ancient theologians were not ancient at all; their apparent anticipation of Platonic and Christian doctrine was the result of composition in a Platonized cultural environment, not of independent prophetic insight. The authority conferred by antiquity was simply a mistake about chronology. This does not mean the texts are philosophically worthless, but it does mean that they cannot perform the function the Renaissance assigned them: they cannot serve as independent witnesses to a primordial truth that the later traditions corroborate.

Pico inherits the *prisca theologia* framework from Ficino but transforms it in ways that reveal his distinctive intellectual priorities. The historical section of the 900 *Conclusiones* is organized as a survey of what Pico calls the *gentes* — the nations of thinkers — arranged, as Farmer demonstrates, in reverse historical order. The Latin scholastics appear first, then the Arabs, then the Greeks, then the Chaldeans, then the Egyptians, and finally the Hebrew Kabbalist sages. This sequence is not chronological but cosmological: it moves from the most fragmented and derivative wisdom (the disputatious moderns) back toward the most unified and primordial (the Kabbalists, whose secret tradition reaches back to Moses on Sinai). The arrangement encodes Pico's claim that the earliest witnesses are the most authoritative, and that the trajectory of intellectual history has been one of progressive corruption and fragmentation from a divine original.

But Pico's list of *gentes* diverges significantly from Ficino's. The most consequential divergence is the inclusion of Hebrew Kabbalah as a privileged witness to ancient wisdom — a witness that Ficino had largely ignored. For Pico, the Kabbalists are not simply one source among many in the ancient theology's chain of transmission; they are, in crucial respects, its most important witnesses, because they possess not a philosophical or poetic approximation of the primordial revelation but the revealed interpretation of it. Pico's definition of Kabbalah in the *Apology* — as the secret interpretation of the Law received by Moses on Sinai alongside the written Torah, transmitted orally across the generations — makes the Kabbalistic tradition the oldest and most direct custodian of divine wisdom. Moses received not only the Ten Commandments but the hidden key to their interpretation; that key was transmitted without interruption down to the Kabbalists of Pico's own day. On this account, Kabbalah is not merely ancient theology: it is the *original* theology, the tradition from which all others derive or with which all others, when correctly read, must ultimately agree.

Wirszubski's philological reconstruction of Pico's Kabbalistic sources — particularly in *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989) — demonstrated that the Kabbalah available to Pico was filtered through the translations of Flavius Mithridates, a Sicilian convert who produced some 3,500 surviving pages of Latin versions in the crucial months of 1486. Those translations were systematically Christianized: Trinitarian references were interpolated where the Hebrew originals contained nothing of the kind; the symbol Ze (the sefirah Yesod, also called Justus and Redeemer) was loaded with Christological suggestion; Abulafia's mystical commentary was revised so that its author appeared to be disclosing Christian secrets. The effect was to produce a Kabbalah that already looked like *prisca theologia* in Pico's sense — a tradition that, read in the Mithridates translations, confirmed the Trinity, the Incarnation, and the Messiahship of Jesus. When Pico declared in the *Oration* that he had found in the books of Kabbalah "not so much the Jewish religion as the Christian," he was partly reading what Mithridates had put there. The *prisca theologia* argument was thus reinforced, in Pico's case, by a philological contamination he could not detect.

The conspicuous absence from Pico's lists of privileged ancient witnesses is Hermes Trismegistus. Farmer observes that when Pico enumerates the ancient magicians whose authority he invokes for his magical theses, the name of Hermes does not appear. The list includes Pythagoras, Plato, Zoroaster, Plotinus, and Roger Bacon — but not Ficino's central figure in the ancient theology, the supposed Egyptian sage who allegedly disclosed the mysteries of creation in language that mirrored both Platonic and biblical cosmogony. This omission is deliberate. Pico's silence about Hermes is a signal that he is positioning his *prisca theologia* against Ficino's Hermetic emphasis — that he regards Kabbalistic testimony as more authoritative, more direct, and more philosophically usable than the Hermetic texts that Ficino had made the centerpiece of his synthesis. The *Corpus Hermeticum* is not absent from Pico's intellectual world — he had read it — but it is not the privileged witness to primordial wisdom that it is for Ficino. That role belongs, in Pico's version, to Moses and the Kabbalistic tradition that Moses initiated.

Pico articulates the logic of the *prisca theologia* most explicitly in the *Oration*, where he surveys the content of the historical section of the *Conclusiones* and explains why the traditions he has assembled should be studied alongside Scripture and scholastic theology. The wisdom of the Chaldeans, the theology of Orpheus, the doctrines of Pythagoras, the philosophy of Plato — all are presented as different languages for a single truth, and the soul that ascends through philosophical study will encounter, at each level of the hierarchy, the same realities expressed in the appropriate vocabulary of that level. The *prisca theologia* is thus not merely a claim about intellectual history; it is a claim about the structure of reality itself. If all authentic wisdom traditions point toward the same truth, the explanation is that they are all oriented toward the same divinely ordered cosmos, and that the cosmos has a single structure that any sufficiently penetrating inquiry will eventually discover. Pico's syncretic method and his *prisca theologia* support each other: the method assumes that traditions agree, and the ancient theology explains why they agree.

Brian Copenhaver's work on Pico's natural magic and Hermetic tradition has been particularly attentive to the question of how Pico's *prisca theologia* relates to his magical practice. The magician who operates through natural magic, as Pico defines it, is drawing on the same principle that underlies the historical section of the *Conclusiones*: the structural correspondences (*sympatheia*) among levels of reality that make it possible for terrestrial operations to be affected by celestial causes. The *prisca theologia* provides the magician's authority — the ancient sages understood these correspondences because they had access to the primordial wisdom — while natural magic provides a practical application of the same correlative metaphysics. Copenhaver's analysis emphasizes that Pico's magic cannot be understood without his cosmology, and his cosmology cannot be understood without the *prisca theologia* that presents it as a recovered ancient truth rather than a modern speculation.

The enduring historiographical challenge posed by the *prisca theologia* is to take seriously a set of arguments built on a false premise. The texts Pico and Ficino treated as ancient witnesses are not ancient; the chain of transmission they traced is a fiction. Yet the intellectual structure erected on this foundation — the correlative metaphysics, the hierarchical cosmology, the program of concordance among traditions — has a philosophical interest independent of its genealogical assumptions. Scholars have rightly insisted that Pico's thought cannot be reduced to the error about chronology; but they must equally insist that the error about chronology is not trivial, because it was precisely the supposed antiquity of these texts that gave them their authority. The *prisca theologia* is a Renaissance myth in the precise sense: a narrative about origins that structures present practice without being verifiable — and, as Casaubon eventually demonstrated, that is not true.
