Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274) is one of the few medieval authorities who appears explicitly and consistently across the full range of Pico's writings — not merely as a target for philosophical critique or as a name in a list of historical sources, but as a genuine intellectual resource whose specific doctrines and distinctions Pico deploys for his own purposes. The standard account of Pico as a syncretic Platonist who subordinated scholastic theology to humanist philosophy risks obscuring the depth of his engagement with Thomism, an engagement that was productive precisely because it operated on scholastic theology's own terms. Amos Edelheit's study of Ficino, Pico, and Savonarola makes this relationship legible by arguing that Pico's *Apology* develops a "humanist theology" whose central structural distinction — between *opinio* (philosophical opinion) and *fides* (revealed faith) — is a specifically Thomistic move turned against the Thomistic establishment (Edelheit 2008, 279–367).

The *opinio/fides* distinction is the theological key to understanding Pico's entire project, and its debt to Aquinas is both direct and dialectically sophisticated. Thomas, building on the faculty psychology inherited from Aristotle, had argued that the act of faith (*credere*) is an act of the intellect directed by the will toward a truth that exceeds the intellect's capacity for demonstration; faith therefore belongs to a different epistemic register from both knowledge (*scientia*) and opinion (*opinio*) (Aquinas, *Summa theologiae* IIa IIae q. 4, a. 2). What the Thomistic school had subsequently done, in Edelheit's account, was to blur this distinction by identifying the *doctrinae* of the Church's Doctors — above all Thomas himself — with the content of faith, such that to err against the *opinion* of Thomas was treated as effectively equivalent to erring against the *fides* itself. This was precisely the charge leveled against Pico by the papal commission: his theses were condemned not simply because they contradicted articles of the Creed but because they deviated from positions that Thomistic theology had come to regard as authoritative.

Pico's response in the *Apology* is to invoke the original Thomistic distinction against this identification. The argument runs as follows: to err regarding Thomas's *opinion* (*opinio Thomae*) is not the same as erring regarding the faith (*errare in fide*). This is the formula that Edelheit identifies as the thesis structuring Pico's entire defense: *quia errare in opinione Thomae, non est errare in fide* (Edelheit 2008, 286). By reasserting the gap between theological opinion and revealed doctrine — a gap that Aquinas himself had theorized — Pico creates the philosophical space necessary to defend his most controversial theses. If a proposition belongs to the domain of *opinio* rather than *fides*, it cannot be heresy to hold it even if it contradicts the authorized teaching of the Doctors. The *Apology* then argues, case by case, that each of the condemned theses is a matter of theological *opinio* subject to philosophical dispute, not an article of faith whose denial constitutes heresy.

This move is enabled by, and could not function without, a Thomistic understanding of the epistemological hierarchy governing Christian intellectual life. Pico draws on Thomas's own analysis of the relations among demonstration, dialectical opinion, and faith to show that the papal commission has confused distinct epistemic levels. He also draws on Thomas's teaching on the voluntarism of heresy — that heresy is a sin of the will, not merely an intellectual error — to argue in the *Apology*'s fifth question (*Q8*) that his condemned propositions, even if erroneous, do not constitute formal heresy because heresy requires a deliberate act of the will against faith (Copenhaver 2022, 200–228). This argument directly deploys Thomistic moral theology to contest the commission's juridical finding. Brian Copenhaver has analyzed these arguments in exhaustive detail, showing that Pico's position in Q8 on the voluntarism of heresy draws on Aquinas via a complex series of scholastic intermediaries, including Robert Holcot, and that the Apology's argument is philosophically coherent even when its strategy is politically unwise (Copenhaver 2022, 212–218).

The relationship between Pico and Thomism is further complicated by his *900 Conclusions*, which include a substantial section of theses *secundum Thomam* — propositions that Pico identifies as following, in his judgment, from Thomas's positions. The presence of these theses is significant because it demonstrates that Pico's engagement with Aquinas is not purely defensive or polemical; he actively draws on Thomistic doctrines as philosophical resources in his constructive work. The theses *secundum Thomam* in the *Conclusions* cover questions in metaphysics, psychology, and theology, and they are placed alongside theses from Scotus, Averroes, Albertus Magnus, and others in a systematic display of scholastic philosophical positions that Pico intends to harmonize. The concord project that characterizes Pico's entire philosophical program extends to the scholastic tradition as well: his aim is not to refute Thomas but to identify the positions where Thomas is right, and to show how those positions connect with positions from other traditions.

One area where Thomistic doctrine and Pico's own philosophical commitments converge is the question of the transcendentals — the *unum*, *bonum*, and *verum* that are said to be convertible with *ens* (being) in scholastic ontology. Thomas Aquinas, following Aristotle, held that the One and Being are mutually convertible: everything that is, is one, and everything that is one, is. This is the Aristotelian position against which the Neoplatonic tradition — and Ficino's Platonism — pits the claim that the One transcends Being in Plato's *Parmenides*. Pico's *De ente et uno* argues precisely for the convertibility of the One and Being, and in doing so it aligns with the Thomistic and Aristotelian tradition against the Neoplatonic reading of Plato. Although Pico reaches this position through a reading of Proclus rather than directly from Aristotle, and although his path runs through a revisionary reading of the *Parmenides*, the conclusion he reaches is structurally consistent with Aquinas's own metaphysics of the transcendentals (Howlett 2021, 193–211). The concord between Plato and Aristotle that *De ente et uno* demonstrates is therefore also, implicitly, a concord between the best of Platonic philosophy and the best of Thomistic scholasticism.

Pico's attitude toward Thomas differs importantly from Ficino's. Ficino's relationship to scholasticism is largely one of avoidance: the *Platonic Theology* has occasional echoes of Thomas's *Summa contra Gentiles*, but these are largely hidden — Ficino does not cite Thomas explicitly as an authority in philosophical theology (Edelheit 2008, 314). Ficino's project is to *replace* scholastic theology with a Platonic alternative, to show that Neoplatonic philosophy provides a richer and truer account of the soul's ascent to God than the scholastic framework can offer. Pico's project is different in kind: he does not propose to replace scholastic theology but to reform it from within, using its own conceptual resources — including Thomistic resources — to demonstrate the inadequacy of certain Thomistic conclusions and the necessity of revisiting the relationship between *opinio* and *fides*. This is why the *Apology* reads, as Copenhaver observes, not like humanist philosophy but like scholastic theology — because it is scholastic theology, deployed with the sophistication of someone who has mastered the tradition he is criticizing (Copenhaver 2022, 7–8).

The specific scholastic resources Pico draws on in the *Apology* extend well beyond Aquinas to include Duns Scotus, William of Ockham, Henry of Ghent, Jean Quidort, Pierre de la Palud, Jean Cabrol, and other medieval authorities. Copenhaver has shown that much of the *Apology*'s metaphysical argument — particularly in Q6 on the eucharist — was drawn almost verbatim from Cabrol's *Defense of the Theology of Thomas Aquinas*, a Thomistic compilation that Pico owned (Copenhaver 2022, 23–24). This detail is revealing: Pico uses a defense of Thomism to construct his argument against the Thomistic commission's finding. The Thomistic tradition is thus both the framework within which Pico defends himself and the source of the distinctions he uses to challenge the commission's conclusions.

The eucharistic theses raise the most technically demanding questions about Pico's relationship to Thomism. Thomas had defended the doctrine of transubstantiation — the complete conversion of the bread's substance into the body of Christ — as the philosophically adequate account of the Real Presence. Pico's Q6 thesis proposes that one can hold to sacramental reality (*veritas sacramenti*) without the bread's substance undergoing conversion (*conversio*), by positing a *suppositatio* — a formal-logical mode of presence in which the Word of God takes on or "supposits" the bread's substance — and without eliminating the *paneitas* (bread-ness, the essential character of bread as bread). This is not a rejection of transubstantiation but an attempt to give a philosophically more precise account of its possibility, drawing on the Scotist tradition's concept of *substentatio* and the medieval logical doctrine of supposition (Copenhaver 2022, 165–175). The papal commission condemned this thesis as contradicting the received Thomistic account of the eucharist. Pico's response is that the thesis is a statement *de possibili* (about possibility), not *de sic esse* (about how things actually are), and therefore does not contradict the dogma but explores its philosophical conditions.

It would be anachronistic to read Pico as engaged in a straightforwardly anti-Thomistic project. The commission's representative who most directly opposed Pico — Pedro Garcia, a Spanish Dominican and rigorous Thomist whose extended rebuttal of the *Apology* appeared in 1489 — represents the Thomistic tradition at its most defensive and institutionally entrenched. But the Thomism that Pico actually engages in his own arguments is more supple, more internally differentiated, and more capable of yielding the distinctions he needs than Garcia's version allows. Edelheit's key insight is that Pico's *Apology* represents not an attack on scholastic theology from a humanist exterior but a humanist reform of theology that operates through scholastic methods and categories, using Thomistic distinctions to free philosophical inquiry from what Pico saw as the illegitimate conflation of theological opinion with revealed doctrine (Edelheit 2008, 284–290).

The historiographical consequence of this analysis is that the familiar picture of Renaissance Platonism as a movement away from scholasticism toward a freer, more humanistic philosophy needs to be substantially qualified in Pico's case. Pico is not more Platonist and less scholastic than Ficino — he is, in significant respects, more scholastic and more technically engaged with the medieval tradition than Ficino ever was. The depth of his engagement with Aquinas is not incidental to his project; it is constitutive of it. The *opinio/fides* distinction, the analysis of heresy as a voluntary act, the doctrine of the convertibility of the transcendentals, the eucharistic metaphysics of supposition and *substentatio* — all of these reflect a serious and technically sophisticated engagement with the Thomistic tradition, one that uses that tradition's own intellectual resources to argue for a more historically differentiated and philosophically rigorous account of Christian doctrine than the commission's scholastic theology was prepared to permit.

The question of what Pico learned from Aquinas and what he rejected requires more textual study than has yet been devoted to it. Copenhaver's philological analysis of the *Apology* identifies dozens of specific passages where Pico's argument derives from Thomistic sources, sometimes acknowledged and sometimes hidden behind Cabrol's compilations (Copenhaver 2022, ch. 2). Future scholarship on *De ente et uno* in the context of Thomistic metaphysics of the transcendentals, and on the *Conclusions*' section *secundum Thomam* in relation to Thomas's actual texts, would substantially enrich our understanding of how deeply and in what precise ways Pico drew on the Angelic Doctor's philosophy. What is already clear is that Thomas Aquinas is not merely an institutional obstacle for Pico — not the face of the orthodoxy he resists — but a philosophical authority whose distinctions he needs, whose arguments he has mastered, and whose system he seeks to reform rather than to replace.
