# Venus

Venus — *Aphrodite* in Greek, *venerem* in the Latin philosophical tradition, and carrying certain structural resonances with the female divine figures of the Kabbalistic *sefirot* — is among the philosophically richest and most contentious mythological figures in Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's thought. She is the protagonist of the most technically ambitious allegorization in the *Commento sopra una canzona d'amore* (c. 1486), and the central figure through whom Pico articulates his departures from Ficino's account of love, beauty, and the soul's ascent. To understand Pico's Venus is to understand the metaphysical architecture of the *Commento*; to understand how she differs from Ficino's Venus is to understand the nature and stakes of the most significant philosophical dispute within Florentine Neoplatonism.

The classical foundation for the philosophical discussion of Venus is the opening speech of Pausanias in Plato's *Symposium* (180C–185C). Pausanias distinguishes between two Aphrodites and, correspondingly, two kinds of Eros or Love. The Heavenly Aphrodite (*Aphrodite Ourania*), daughter of Ouranos born without a mother from the sea-foam that gathered around the severed genitals of Ouranos — the origin myth narrated in Hesiod's *Theogony* — is associated with a love that is oriented toward the soul rather than the body, toward virtue and philosophy rather than physical pleasure. The Common Aphrodite (*Aphrodite Pandemos*), daughter of Zeus and Dione, is associated with love as it is ordinarily practiced, mixed in its orientation between soul and body, and not especially elevated in its objects. Pausanias's point is political and ethical rather than metaphysical: he wants to distinguish the kind of eros that benefits the beloved by attending to the beloved's soul from the kind that uses the beloved for physical gratification. But the distinction between two Aphrodites provides the organizing framework for all subsequent philosophical discussions of Venus.

Ficino's treatment of the two Venuses in the *De amore* — his Latin commentary on the *Symposium* composed around 1469 — gives the distinction its Renaissance philosophical form and its most influential Neoplatonic elaboration. For Ficino, the Heavenly Venus (*Venus Coelestis*) dwells in the Angelic Mind (*Mens Angelica*): she is the intelligible beauty, the splendor of the Good as it shines through the first and highest intelligible creature, and the love she inspires is the contemplative love of the philosopher for the divine. The Common or Popular Venus (*Venus Vulgaris*, *Venus Mundana*) dwells in the World Soul: she governs the production of forms in the sensible world, presides over the generative processes of nature, and the love she inspires is the love of beautiful bodies and souls at the level of ordinary human experience. This is already a dignified account of the lower Venus: she governs a genuine dimension of reality (the natural world, the procession of the Ideas into matter), and the love she inspires, though not as elevated as the contemplative love of the heavenly Venus, is not mere sensuality. Ficino argues that sensible beauty, because it participates in divine beauty through the chain of the World Soul, is genuinely beautiful: the soul that responds to a beautiful face is responding, however obscurely, to the divine beauty that shines through that face. The lower Venus is therefore legitimate, and her love is the first rung of the ladder that leads ultimately to union with the divine.

Pico's *Commento* radically restructures this scheme. His most consequential innovation is the introduction of a third Venus — what Allen (2017, 81–113, the essay "The Birth Day of Venus") has identified as the organizing metaphysical contribution of the *Commento* — and his redistribution of the Platonic distinction across a tripartite rather than bipartite scheme. For Pico, the three Venuses correspond to three levels of the metaphysical hierarchy and three modes of beauty: (1) the celestial Venus (*Venus Coelestis*), identified with the Idea of Beauty as it exists in the angelic intellect — the first and most perfect instance of beauty, beauty in its formal and proper being; (2) an intermediate Venus, associated with the World Soul (*anima mundi*) — the beauty that the World Soul possesses insofar as it has been formed by the intelligible Ideas, and which functions as the principle of beauty in the celestial world; and (3) the earthly or generative Venus (*Venus Vulgaris*, *Venus Generativa*), the principle of corporeal beauty and the power that presides over the generation of forms in the elementary world. Each of the three Venuses generates a corresponding Eros or Love: the love that moves the angelic intellect toward God in the contemplation of the divine Ideas; the love that moves the celestial spheres; and the generative desire that moves bodies toward one another and produces the perpetual procession of natural forms.

The most philosophically significant aspect of Pico's tripartite scheme is the sharply diminished status it assigns to sensible beauty and the love it inspires. For Ficino, the lower Venus and her love, while less elevated than the heavenly, are nonetheless genuine responses to genuine beauty — because beauty is distributed through all levels of being and the lower participates in the higher. For Pico, the situation is more severe. He defines beauty — in the technically precise analysis of *Commento* 2.8 — as *una amica inimicizia, ovvero una discorde concordia* ("a friendly enmity, or a harmonious discord"): the property that results when diverse things are ordered into a concordant whole. This definition, drawn in part from Heraclitean and Empedoclean sources and in part from the Neoplatonic analysis of how the Ideas constitute a unified multiplicity in the angelic intellect, locates the primary instance of beauty in the angelic world, where the Ideas first achieve the form of a diversified unity. Beauty in the sensible world — the beauty of a face, a body, a flower — is not beauty in the proper sense but only a participated image (*vestigio*, *immagine*) of real beauty: it is the trace left in matter by the procession of intelligible form through the World Soul and into the elemental world, and while this trace can point the attentive soul toward its source, it does not itself constitute genuine beauty.

The consequence, as Allen documents through close reading of the *Commento*, is that genuine *amor* — love in the philosophically serious sense — has only one proper object: the celestial Venus, the Idea of Beauty in the angelic intellect. The love that is oriented toward the intermediate Venus (beauty at the level of the World Soul) is love at a lower register; the love that is oriented toward the generative Venus (the beauty of bodies) is not properly love (*amor*) at all but a counterfeit — desire masquerading as philosophy. This is a more austere position than Ficino's, and it is expressed most sharply in Pico's explicit polemic against Ficino in the unexpurgated text of the *Commento*: Allen notes that Pico attacks Marsilio by name, faults his logic and acuity, and takes specific issue with Ficino's views on "the meaning of Plato's two Venuses" and "the metaphysical status of beauty and love" (Allen, *Italica* 1986, 189–190). The points of disagreement are not peripheral but go to the heart of both thinkers' accounts of the philosophical life.

The birth of the celestial Venus is the subject of Pico's most technically elaborate mythological allegoresis in the *Commento*, developed most fully in books two and three. Pico follows the *Theogony* myth in which Venus arises from the sea-foam that gathers around the severed genitals of Ouranos after his castration by Kronos: Venus is born from the sea, from the mingling of the celestial members (Ouranos) with the water (matter, the potential of the angelic intellect). In Pico's allegorization, the castration of Ouranos signifies the separation of the divine Ideas from God's causal containment of them: the Ideas in God have causal being but not yet formal being; they become formally existent — they become the Ideas as the angelic intellect possesses them — when they are, so to speak, separated from the absolute simplicity of God and planted in the substance of the angelic mind. This planting is the birth of the celestial Venus: the first moment at which beauty exists as a formal property of an intelligible composite being. The sea from which Venus arises is the still-unformed substance of the angelic intellect — its *hyle noete*, intelligible matter — which receives the influx of the divine Ideas and, in receiving them, becomes beautiful.

This birth is simultaneous with the birth of Love (*Eros*, *Amor*), narrated in Plato's *Symposium* 203B–C by Diotima. Love is born in the gardens of Jupiter on the natal day of Venus, when Poros (Plenty) lay with Penia (Poverty). Pico's allegorization identifies Poros with the plenitude of the Ideas as they first enter the angelic intellect, and Penia with the still-imperfect, deficient state of the angelic intellect before it has organized those Ideas into the full concordance of a unified multiplicity. Beauty (Venus) is born when the Ideas first enter Mind, but at this moment she is still an infant, imperfect, because the ordering of the Ideas within the intellectual substance is not yet complete. Love (*Eros*) arises as Mind's desire to possess the Ideas in their perfect form — its appetite for the complete concordance that would constitute full intelligible beauty. Mind then turns toward God, the Ideas are perfected within it, and Venus grows from an infant into the mature and fully realized goddess: beauty reaches its perfection when the angelic intellect achieves its full intelligible unity through the circular movement of procession from and return to its divine source. As Allen shows (2017, 150–152), this account makes beauty and love co-originate and mutually implicating: beauty causes love by presenting itself as an object of desire, and love causes beauty to achieve its perfection by drawing Mind back to God.

The second Venus — the intermediate one, associated with the World Soul — is philosophically important but receives less sustained attention in the *Commento* than either the celestial or the generative Venus. She functions primarily as the principle that transmits beauty from the angelic intellect to the celestial realm: the World Soul, insofar as it has been formed and illuminated by the Ideas of the angelic intellect, possesses a participated beauty that is real but derivative, a beauty mediated by the hierarchical procession rather than constituted in its own right. The love she generates is the love that moves the celestial spheres in their eternal circuits — the astronomical love that Plato describes in the *Timaeus* as the soul of the world organizing and animating the heavens. This is the love that Ficino had also associated with the World Soul, but Pico's intermediate Venus is, structurally, more clearly delimited as a mediating rather than a constituting principle: she does not generate beauty from herself but transmits it from above.

The generative or vulgar Venus (*Venus Vulgaris*, *Venus Generativa*) is the most ambivalent of the three in Pico's treatment. She is real — she is a genuine principle in the cosmic hierarchy — but the love she inspires is the love that mistakes a participated image for genuine beauty, the counterfeit that mimics genuine *amor* without being it. Pico does not condemn the generative Venus in simple moral terms: she governs the processes of natural procreation and the perpetual renewal of corporeal forms, and these are genuine goods in their own order. What he insists on is that the love oriented toward the generative Venus — the love of bodies for one another, the love of beautiful sensible things — does not constitute the ascending movement toward genuine beauty but rather a lateral movement within the sensible realm that, without proper philosophical guidance, terminates in the sensible and fails to generate genuine ascent. Sears Jayne's translation of the *Commento* and his introduction document how this position generates the sharp distinction between *amore vulgare* (common or vulgar love) and *amore celeste* (celestial love) that structures the second and third books of the *Commento* (Jayne 1984, introduction, passim).

The difference between Pico's three-Venus scheme and Ficino's two-Venus scheme has sometimes been minimized in the scholarly literature, on the grounds that both thinkers distinguish higher from lower love and assign the highest philosophical status to the love of intelligible beauty. But Allen's scholarship insists on the sharpness of the difference, and the insistence is warranted. For Ficino, the two Venuses are both genuinely Venuses — both are genuine principles of beauty, and the lower Venus is a legitimate first rung on the ladder of ascent because she governs a genuine participated beauty. For Pico, the celestial Venus alone is the Venus properly speaking: she is beauty in its proper being, and the love oriented toward her is love in its proper philosophical sense. The intermediate and generative Venuses are real but derivative, and the love oriented toward them is real but impure — genuinely love only insofar as it is genuinely oriented toward beauty, and beauty only insofar as it participates in the celestial beauty of the angelic intellect. Pico's scheme is not simply a refinement of Ficino's but a structural alteration that changes the philosophical significance of every love at every level: what Ficino assigns genuine philosophical dignity, Pico either promotes (the celestial love) or demotes (everything that falls short of the celestial).

The Kabbalistic dimension of Pico's Venus scheme is a matter on which modern scholarship has been careful to maintain a distinction between structural analogy and direct identification. There are female divine figures in the Kabbalistic system of the *sefirot* that bear some resemblance to Pico's Venus hierarchy. *Binah* — Understanding, the third *sefirah*, often called the Divine Mother or the upper *Shekhinah* — is the womb of the divine world, the source from which the lower *sefirot* flow, and the level at which the divine intellect achieves its self-determination as a structured whole. *Malkhut* — Kingdom, the tenth and final *sefirah*, identified with the lower *Shekhinah* — is the divine Presence as it dwells in the world, the feminine principle that receives and transmits the divine influx from above and mediates it to the created realm. Between them, *Tiferet* (Beauty, the sixth *sefirah*) unites and harmonizes the other *sefirot* in the middle of the cosmic tree. These structural homologies — a triadic structure involving an upper feminine principle of intelligible beauty, a mediating principle of cosmic beauty, and a lower principle that transmits divine presence to the world — do suggest a convergence with Pico's three-Venus scheme that Pico himself would have found philosophically significant.

Chaim Wirszubski's careful philological reconstruction of Pico's Kabbalistic sources, however, cautions against identifying the *sefirot* and the Venuses too directly (Wirszubski 1989, passim). Pico's access to Kabbalistic texts was mediated through the translations of Flavius Mithridates, which introduced systematic distortions and Christianizing overlays; the structural homologies between Kabbalistic and Neoplatonic hierarchies were therefore partly the product of translation rather than discovery. Pico reads Kabbalah in a framework already shaped by Neoplatonic categories — the triadic hierarchy of Plotinus's three hypostases (*to Hen*, *Nous*, *Psyche*) provides the structural template through which the *sefirot* are interpreted — and the convergence between the Venus hierarchy and the feminine *sefirot* may be partly an artifact of this template. What Pico does draw from the Kabbalistic sources is confirmation that the philosophical truth about beauty and love, which he has identified through allegoresis of the *Symposium* and the Orphic tradition, is also encoded in the Kabbalistic tradition — that the celestial Venus of the *Commento* names a principle that Moses and the Kabbalists also knew, however differently they named it.

The reception of Pico's Venus scheme illustrates the characteristic fate of his most technically demanding positions. The *Commento* reached wider circulation through the 1519 Giunti edition of Benivieni's poems — in a form expurgated of the direct attacks on Ficino — and through the French translation attributed to Claude Chappuys (c. 1544–1548), which mediated it to French and, eventually, English Renaissance readers. By the time Pico's Venus reached Edmund Spenser, George Chapman, or the Italian literary tradition of the *Canzonieri*, it had been substantially refracted through Ficino's more generous account: the sharp distinction between the three Venuses, and above all Pico's insistence that only the celestial Venus is genuinely Venus, had been smoothed into a vaguer hierarchy in which multiple forms of love retain philosophical dignity. What the later tradition received was not Pico's Venus but a Pico filtered through Ficino — a more accommodating doctrine in which bodily beauty retains at least a trace of philosophical legitimacy. Recovering what Pico actually argued about Venus — the severity of his tripartite hierarchy, the restricted scope of genuine *amor*, the denial that sensible beauty constitutes a legitimate first rung of ascent — remains a task that Sears Jayne's translation and Michael Allen's scholarship have made possible for modern readers, even as the broader cultural history of Renaissance Neoplatonism continues to obscure it.
