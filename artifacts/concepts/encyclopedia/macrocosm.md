# Macrocosm

The *macrocosmos* — the great world, the structured totality of created being — functions in Pico della Mirandola's thought primarily as the encompassing framework within which the human microcosm operates, ascends, and finds its proper end. Unlike later cosmological speculation driven by the new astronomy, Pico works within a geocentric, hierarchically ordered universe inherited from the Aristotelian and Neoplatonic traditions and transmitted through medieval scholastic philosophy. What is distinctive about his treatment of this inheritance is not a departure from its cosmological picture but a sustained philosophical pressure on the question of what that picture implies about the relationship between celestial causality and human freedom — a pressure exercised most explicitly in the *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinicatricem* and most architecturally in the *Heptaplus*. At the same time, Pico's encounter with Kabbalah introduces a competing cosmological scheme — the Kabbalistic four worlds — that creates both productive convergences and genuine tensions with the Neoplatonic three-world model he had already adopted.

The cosmological picture Pico inherits is Ptolemaic and Aristotelian: a geocentric universe composed of concentric spheres arranged hierarchically around an immobile Earth. The seven classical planets occupy the seven innermost spheres (Moon, Mercury, Venus, Sun, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn in ascending order); beyond them lies the sphere of the fixed stars (*firmamentum*); beyond that, the *primum mobile*, the outermost moving sphere that communicates motion to all those below it; and finally, at the outer limit, the *caelum empyreum* (empyrean heaven), the immobile and fiery heaven that is the abode of God and the angels. Some medieval and Renaissance cosmologists posited an additional crystalline sphere between the firmament and the *primum mobile*, making eleven spheres in all; Pico adopts the ten-sphere model, as Black's analysis of the *Heptaplus* shows, not primarily on astronomical grounds but because the ten-sphere structure allows him to generate a numerologically elegant symmetry: God and nine ranks of angels in the angelic world, the empyrean and nine heavenly spheres in the celestial world, prime matter and nine categories of corruptible forms in the elemental world (Black 2006, 150–52; *Heptaplus* 2, proem, Garin, 190).

Each of the planetary spheres, on the inherited model, is governed by an intelligence — a separate, immaterial substance that moves its sphere and thereby transmits its quality downward to the sublunary world. The sun governs heat and light and life; Saturn governs melancholy, deep contemplation, and time; Jupiter governs justice, moderation, and political order; Mars governs conflict and martial courage; Venus governs love and generation; Mercury governs reason, eloquence, and commerce; the Moon governs growth, change, and the tides of sublunary life. These qualities descend through the *spiritus mundi* — the world spirit permeating the cosmos — to affect the sublunary world in ways that can be read from the configuration of the heavens at any given moment: this is the foundational cosmological claim of astrological theory.

What distinguishes Pico's cosmological picture from a simple adoption of Aristotelian-Ptolemaic orthodoxy is the three-world structure he elaborates most fully in the *Heptaplus*. The second proem articulates a philosophical taxonomy of the cosmos that groups the physical spheres into three ontological orders rather than analyzing them individually. The *mundus angelicus vel intellectualis* (angelic or intellectual world), the *mundus caelestis* (celestial world), and the *mundus elementaris* (sublunary or elemental world) are described in terms of qualities rather than locations: the angelic world is characterized by light, fire, perpetual stability, and life without vicissitude; the elemental world is its opposite — darkness, watery instability, the alternation of generation and corruption; and the celestial world is intermediate, composed of light and shadow, unchanging in its operation but changing in its motion (H. P2, Garin, 184–86; Black 2006, 148–50). This threefold structure is not a simple mapping of the Aristotelian spheres onto a Neoplatonic hierarchy, though it owes something to both traditions. As Black demonstrates, the Neoplatonic hypostases (One, Mind, Soul) do not map cleanly onto Pico's three worlds, and the available Neoplatonic models of cosmic structure (from Plotinus, Proclus, Macrobius) each differ from his scheme in important ways (Black 2006, 152–57). Pico attributes the three-world model simply to "antiquity" (*tres mundos figurat antiquitas*), treating it as a common inheritance rather than the property of any single school.

The *Commento sopra una canzone d'amore* (c. 1485–86) provides an earlier and explicitly Platonic formulation of the same structure. There Pico writes: "The Platonists divide all created things into three levels, of which there are two extremes. Under one are included all corporeal and visible things, such as the heavens, the elements, plants, animals and everything composed of the elements. Under the other is understood everything which is invisible, and not only incorporeal, but entirely free and separate from any body. This is properly called intellectual nature, and by our theologians is called angelic nature. In between the two extremes is a median nature which, although incorporeal, and invisible and immortal, is nonetheless a mover of bodies and is tied to this function. This is called the rational soul, which comes after the angelic and before the corporeal, and is subject to the former and in charge of the latter" (Garin, 463; translated Black 2006, 152). Here the middle term in the three-world hierarchy is the rational soul rather than the celestial spheres, reflecting the *Commento*'s psychological rather than cosmological focus: in this register, the three worlds correspond to the three ontological levels in the Neoplatonic tradition of hypostases.

By the time of the *Heptaplus*, the same structure has been transposed from a primarily psychological to a primarily cosmological register, and the middle term has shifted from the rational soul to the celestial spheres. This shift is significant because it changes what the macrocosm is doing philosophically. In the *Commento*, the three-world structure explains the soul's intermediate position in the cosmic hierarchy; in the *Heptaplus*, the same structure becomes the organizing principle for seven simultaneous readings of the Genesis creation narrative. Each of the seven expositions reads the six days through one of seven ontological lenses, but the fundamental structure underlying all seven is the three-world cosmological model: the first exposition treats the elemental world (*De mundo elementari*), the second the celestial world (*De mundo caelesti*), the third the angelic world (*De mundo angelico et invisibili*), and the fourth the human world (*De mundo humano*) understood as the microcosmic synthesis of the preceding three. The fifth and sixth expositions then treat the correspondence and interconnection of all the worlds, before the seventh reaches for the eschatological goal of the entire program.

One of the philosophically central questions about the macrocosm in Pico's period is whether it is animate — whether it possesses a World Soul. Plato's *Timaeus* affirmed that the cosmos was a living animal, endowed with soul and intelligence by the Demiurge, and this affirmation was central to the Neoplatonic tradition from Plotinus onward. Ficino, in the *Theologia Platonica*, affirmed a World Soul as a real cosmological entity and built his theory of natural magic around it: the *spiritus mundi* (world spirit) is the body of the World Soul, and celestial influences operate through it. Pico's position on the World Soul is, characteristically, more cautious than Ficino's, and his caution has doctrinal motivation. A World Soul that is genuinely animate — that thinks, desires, and acts purposively — creates theological difficulties for a Christian philosopher, because it can easily appear to elevate the created cosmos to a status that encroaches on the creator's sovereignty. More concretely, a World Soul with real causal powers operating deterministically through celestial influences supports the astrological program that Pico wishes to resist.

The *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinicatricem* — Pico's unfinished final work, probably composed between 1493 and 1494 and published posthumously by Gianfrancesco Pico — is the most sustained philosophical attack on astrological theory in the Renaissance. The central philosophical target of the *Disputationes* is causal determinism: the claim that celestial bodies, by virtue of their superior position in the cosmic hierarchy and their governance by intelligences, exercise an overwhelming causal power over sublunary events, including human actions and human fate. Pico's counter-argument operates on several levels simultaneously. At the cosmological level, he attacks the intelligibility of the mechanisms by which celestial influence is supposed to operate: how exactly does the configuration of distant spheres produce specific effects in sublunary matter? The *spiritus mundi* and the doctrine of cosmic sympathy are too vague to constitute a genuine causal account. At the epistemological level, he argues that astrologers cannot in principle achieve the precision of observation their predictions require. At the philosophical level, he insists that the operations of human reason and will cannot be determined by celestial configurations, because reason and will belong to a level of being that transcends the material and semi-material mechanisms through which celestial influence operates.

What Pico does not do in the *Disputationes* is deny that the heavens exert any influence on the sublunary world. Natural sympathies between celestial bodies and sublunary things are real: the sun genuinely causes warmth and growth, the moon genuinely affects tides and biological rhythms, and the correspondences between planets and particular metals, plants, and humors that underlie astrological symbolism are grounded in real natural connections. The *Disputationes* distinguishes between this generic celestial influence — which Pico accepts as real and as the foundation of natural medicine and natural magic — and the specific, predictive, deterministic claims of horoscopic astrology, which he rejects as epistemologically unwarranted and philosophically inconsistent with human freedom (Copenhaver 2019, 456–60). The macrocosm for Pico is a real and causally organized structure; what it is not is an astrological machine that programs the course of individual human lives.

The Kabbalistic macrocosm introduces a quite different cosmological scheme with which Pico's three-world model must be harmonized. The Kabbalistic tradition, as it reaches Pico through Mithridates's translations and through sources like Recanati and Abulafia, posits not three but four worlds: *Atziluth* (the world of emanation, the divine realm of the sefirot), *Beriah* (the world of creation, the realm of the throne and the higher angels), *Yetzirah* (the world of formation, the realm of the lower angels and celestial spheres), and *Assiyah* (the world of action, the material world). These four worlds represent an emanationist cascade from the divine through increasingly remote levels of being, analogous to but not identical with the Neoplatonic descent from the One through Mind and Soul to Matter. The relationship between the Kabbalistic four-world scheme and the Neoplatonic three-world scheme is one of the architecturally central problems of Pico's syncretic program, and the *Heptaplus*'s proposed solution is elegant if not entirely consistent. Pico identifies man as a "fourth world" in addition to the three cosmological worlds — a world that contains the other three within itself in microcosmic form. This quarternary structure rhymes with the Kabbalistic four worlds while preserving the Neoplatonic three-world cosmology as the framework for reading Genesis. Wirszubski demonstrates that the analogical likenings of the Tabernacle, man, and the world to the three-world structure in the *Heptaplus* are drawn from Kabbalistic sources that already operate with a three-plus-one structure, and that Pico's treatment is directly influenced by these sources even where he does not cite them by name (Wirszubski 1989, 246–50).

The most important single passage for understanding how Pico maps Kabbalistic onto Neoplatonic cosmology is the dedicatory epistle to the *Heptaplus* itself, where Pico describes the three sections of the Mosaic Tabernacle as images of the three worlds: the court (*atrium*), accessible to the people, corresponds to the elemental world; the outer sanctuary (*sanctum*), with its seven-branched candlestick representing the seven planets, corresponds to the celestial world; and the holy of holies (*sanctum sanctorum*), with the Cherubim at the ark, corresponds to the angelic world. Man, as the microcosmic fourth world, is then added as the completion of the scheme. As Wirszubski traces, this four-part structure of tabernacle / cosmos / man is already present in the Kabbalistic texts Pico knew, and what Pico contributes is the integration of this Kabbalistic cosmological symbolism with the Neoplatonic ontological hierarchy and with the Christian doctrine of creation as disclosed in Genesis.

The relationship between the Kabbalistic *Atziluth* and the Neoplatonic *mundus angelicus* deserves particular attention. The Neoplatonic angelic world is a world of pure intelligence — beings who know immediately, without the discursive reasoning required by embodied intellects. The Kabbalistic *Atziluth* is the divine realm itself, the world of the sefirot, which are not created beings but aspects or attributes of the divine nature itself, identified with the Kabbalistic *Ein Sof* (the Infinite). Mapping these onto one another — as Pico implicitly does when he identifies the Kabbalistic macrocosm with the Neoplatonic-Christian macrocosm — requires either a radical distinction between the angelic world (created intelligences) and the sefirotic world (uncreated divine attributes), or a theology of emanation in which the sefirot are themselves the first created beings. Pico's position on this question is not explicitly worked out in his extant writings, but the pattern of his Kabbalistic theses suggests that he leans toward reading the sefirot as the highest created beings — instruments of divine creative action rather than aspects of the divine nature itself. This reading, contested among Kabbalists but possible within certain schools of thought, allows the sefirotic world to be mapped onto the Neoplatonic angelic world without requiring either a collapse of the distinction between God and creation or a subordination of the Christian *Trinitas* to the Kabbalistic *Ein Sof*.

What the macrocosm is, finally, in Pico's account is a structured totality of created being organized in three hierarchical registers — elemental, celestial, angelic — each governed by real causal powers flowing from higher to lower, each level reflecting the others in its own mode, and all three united by the divine creative act that the Genesis narrative records and that the *Heptaplus* labors to interpret. It is a world that the human being can read because the human being contains it in miniature; a world whose inner connections natural magic can exploit because those connections are real causal sympathies, not arbitrary correlations; a world whose celestial influences the wise philosopher can navigate because those influences, while real, are not deterministic; and a world whose three-plus-one structure converges, for Pico, with the Kabbalistic four worlds as evidence that the philosophers of every tradition, reading different books, have been reading the same great text.
