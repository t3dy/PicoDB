# Penia

Penia (Πενία), Poverty or Need, is the mythological mother of Eros in the speech of Diotima recounted in Plato's *Symposium* (203b–e), and in the philosophical allegory that Marsilio Ficino and Giovanni Pico della Mirandola derive from that speech, she represents the material, deficient, desiring aspect of love — the soul's condition of lacking what it most deeply needs, the constitutive emptiness that makes erotic aspiration possible. Her counterpart and the father of Eros is Poros (Resource), and the love born from their union carries both her poverty and his resourcefulness as permanent structural features. For Pico, writing in the *Commento sopra una canzona d'amore* (c. 1486), the allegorization of Penia opens onto a set of questions about the ontological condition of the embodied soul, the nature of genuine philosophical desire, and the distinction between the celestial and the earthly forms of Eros that constitute the core of his disagreement with Ficino's account of love.

The Platonic narrative is stark in its characterization of Penia. On the day of Aphrodite's birth, Penia came to the feast of the gods as a suppliant — not as a participant but as a beggar, existing at the margins of divine abundance. She was barred from the feast itself and had no share in its pleasures. Seeing the drunken Poros asleep in the garden of Zeus, she decided to lie with him, not out of desire for him as an individual but in order to remedy her poverty: she would conceive a child who might carry within him the resources she herself lacked. The child, Eros, therefore inherits from her poverty, roughness, shoelessness, homelessness, and the habit of sleeping in doorways and on hard ground rather than in soft beds. Eros is never wealthy, never beautiful, never comfortable; like his mother, he is always on the outside of the abundance he seeks. The defining feature of Penia in the myth is this constitutive lack — she is the figure of need that cannot satisfy itself from its own resources, that must look outward to what it lacks in order to survive.

The philosophical tradition following the *Symposium* read Penia as an allegory of the soul's condition in embodiment. The soul, on Platonic and Neoplatonic analysis, comes from the intelligible world and is in some sense native to it; in embodiment, it finds itself separated from its origin, deprived of the immediate vision of the Ideas that constituted its original richness, and therefore in a condition structurally analogous to Penia's at the divine feast. It is present in a world of abundance — the material world, like the feast, is full of beautiful things — but excluded from the real nourishment, which is intelligible rather than sensory. From this Penia-condition arises the soul's desire: it wants what it lacks, and what it lacks is intelligible beauty, the vision of the Ideas, the contemplative union with the Good. Love, in the Platonic tradition that runs from Plato through Plotinus to Ficino and Pico, is the expression of this structural lack — the yearning of the embodied soul for its intelligible home.

Ficino's handling of the Penia allegory in the *De amore* (*Commentary on Plato's Symposium*, c. 1469, printed 1484) tends toward cosmological generosity. For Ficino, Penia names the lack or deprivation (*indigentia*) that characterizes all lower levels of being relative to their origin: matter relative to form, soul relative to intellect, the finite creature relative to the infinite God. This deprivation generates love — Eros as the cosmological force that drives everything in creation back toward its source. The Penia-condition is therefore distributed across all levels of being, and at each level it generates a corresponding love oriented upward. Even bodily love — the love of beautiful bodies — is, on Ficino's account, a genuine expression of the Penia-condition of embodied souls: they lack the beauty they desire, and their desire moves them, however indirectly and perhaps erroneously, toward the divine beauty they are ultimately seeking. The erotic experience of a beautiful person is, for Ficino, a genuine if confused recognition of the divine beauty that shines through sensible form.

Pico's *Commento* both inherits and revises this analysis in ways that sharpen the philosophical stakes. As Michael Allen has documented through his study of the Ficino-Pico relationship (Allen 2017), and as Sears Jayne's translation and commentary on the *Commento* (Jayne 1984) makes available in English, Pico's allegory assigns Penia a more specific and structurally precise role than Ficino's more diffuse account allows. In Pico's analysis, Penia represents not merely lack in general but the specifically material, body-bound, sensory condition of the soul — the condition of a being that, finding itself in the elemental world, is oriented primarily downward or outward toward sensory satisfaction rather than upward toward intellectual vision. The Penia-component of love is the component that looks toward the material remediation of lack: toward the beautiful body that might satisfy the eye, the beautiful voice that might satisfy the ear, the beautiful person whose presence might fill the emptiness temporarily. It is the component that, if it predominates, keeps love attached to the sensible world and prevents the upward turn toward intelligible beauty.

This distinction between the Poros and Penia components of love maps directly onto Pico's famous distinction among three Venuses and three kinds of love, which is the architectural center of the *Commento*'s second and third books. The celestial or Uranian Venus presides over the intellectual world, the realm of the angelic intellect and the divine Ideas. The lower or generative Venus presides over the production of beautiful forms in the sensible world, the domain of the world soul. Love in its highest form — genuine philosophical love, Platonic *amor* in the strict sense — is love in which the Poros-component predominates: the upward striving, idea-seeking, intellectual orientation that takes the beauty of the angelic intellect as its formal object. Love that corresponds to the lower Venus is love in which the Penia-component predominates: desire oriented toward the sensible, toward the beauty of particular bodies and particular faces, toward the material remediation of felt lack. Pico does not quite call this latter love, but he treats it as a counterfeit of love — the confusion of genuine erotic aspiration with the mere appetite for sensory satisfaction.

The philosophical severity of this position is one of the clearest points at which Pico's *Commento* departs from Ficino's more accommodating account. For Ficino, even the love of beautiful bodies participates genuinely in the erotic economy of the cosmos: the *splendor boni*, the splendor of the Good that is beauty, shines through sensible things, and the soul that responds to that splendor in the most imperfect sensible form is responding to it genuinely, however dimly. The first rung of the Platonic ladder is real even if it is the lowest rung. Pico denies this generosity. For Pico, as Allen demonstrates through a careful reading of *Commento* 2.8 and the surrounding chapters, beauty in the proper sense is an intelligible property — the "friendly enmity and harmonious discord" (*un'amica inimicizia, ovvero una discorde concordia*) that characterizes the ordering of intelligibles in the first mind. Sensible beauty is at best a participated image of this property, an echo or reflection rather than the thing itself; and the love that responds to sensible beauty is not genuine love but its image. The soul in the grip of what Pico calls purely Penia-driven love — love that seeks its object among beautiful bodies — is a soul that has confused the echo with the source, the image with the original. It is not that such love is morally wrong (though Pico is not indifferent to moral questions); it is that it is metaphysically mistaken, oriented toward an object that cannot sustain it.

The condition of the embodied soul is therefore essentially Penia-like in Pico's account. The soul in the body is materially situated, sensory in its immediate faculties, deprived of the direct intellectual vision that constitutes its proper activity. Its condition is that of Penia at the feast: present in a world full of beautiful things, but excluded from the real nourishment. The task of the philosophical life is to awaken the Poros-inheritance that the soul also carries — the resource, the intellectual capacity, the upward-striving energy — and to direct it toward its proper object, intelligible beauty. The *Commento*'s account of philosophical ascent is therefore the story of how the soul, beginning from the Penia-condition of embodiment, activates its Poros-resources and progressively orients itself toward the celestial Venus and the intellectual beauty she embodies. This story is narrated in the *Commento*'s treatment of the Platonic allegories — particularly the Orpheus and Eurydice episode and the *mors osculi* or death by kiss — which Pico reads as accounts of the different outcomes that await souls that succeed or fail in this reorientation.

The Orpheus allegory is particularly instructive for understanding what Penia-dominated love means in Pico's terms. Orpheus, as Pico reads the story in the *Commento*'s glosses on Benivieni's poem, failed to recover the true Eurydice because he refused to die to the sensible world entirely: he descended to the underworld still attached to his imaginative and sensory faculties, still partially in the grip of the Penia-condition, still unable to release his grip on the sensible image of Eurydice and seek her purely intelligible reality. The result was that he found not Eurydice herself but a phantom — the shadow of the beloved that the soul attached to the sensory produces instead of the genuine intelligible object. Penia-love, the love that cannot relinquish the sensory image, is Orphic love in Pico's typology: it generates a phantom instead of reality, and it loses even the phantom in the end. Genuine philosophical love requires a total death to the Penia-condition — a severance of the soul from the sensory faculties that is so complete that the soul can receive the intelligible beloved directly, without the mediation of image or imagination.

The culmination of this analysis in the *Commento* is the concept of the *mors osculi* or death by kiss, which Pico draws from the Kabbalistic tradition through the sources made available to him by his Hebrew studies. In the *Commento*'s account of the Song of Songs verse "Kiss me with the kisses of thy mouth" (*Canticles* 1:2), Pico connects the Platonic account of the soul's union with intelligible beauty to the Kabbalistic tradition that the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, Aaron, and Miriam — died *binsica*, by kissing, in a state of intellectual rapture in which the soul was so absorbed in its union with the divine that it was separated from the body not by violence but by the intensity of contemplative love. This is the ultimate overcoming of the Penia-condition: not merely the partial awakening of the Poros-inheritance, but the total absorption of the soul into the intelligible world, the complete resolution of the Penia-Poros tension on the side of Poros, the soul's final arrival at the abundance that Penia always lacked. The *mors osculi* is the death of Penia in the subject who dies it.

Pico's analysis of the Penia figure has implications beyond the *Commento* itself. In the *Oration* (also composed in 1486), the account of the soul's capacity for transformation — the famous passage in which Pico describes humanity as a being of no fixed nature, placed at the center of the world with the freedom to descend to the vegetative level or ascend to the angelic — is structurally dependent on the same analysis. The soul's Penia-condition is what makes it susceptible to degradation: a soul that cannot rouse its Poros-inheritance, that remains stuck in the material, sensory, needy condition of the embodied, will descend rather than ascend. The ascent program of the *Oration*, which moves through moral purification, philosophical discipline, and finally mystical union, is the program by which the soul progressively overcomes its Penia-condition and realizes its Poros-inheritance. The connection between the *Commento*'s love theory and the *Oration*'s anthropology is therefore direct: the three-Venus hierarchy of the *Commento* and the levels of ascent in the *Oration* are complementary accounts of the same philosophical project, viewed from the perspectives of love and of nature respectively.

For readers approaching the *Commento* through the translations and commentaries that shaped its early modern reception, Penia is often reduced to a generalized symbol of lack or need, read in isolation from the precise metaphysical role she plays in Pico's allegory. Jayne's work on the transmission of the *Commento* through French intermediaries and into the English Renaissance (Jayne 1984, introduction) documents how the Ficino-Pico distinction was flattened in transmission: the sharp contrast between Penia-love (love of the sensible, love that mistakes the image for the original) and Poros-love (love of the intelligible, love that presses through to the real object) became a more generalized account of love's aspiration toward beauty, losing the metaphysical precision that constituted Pico's distinctive contribution. Recovering that precision — understanding Penia not as a vague symbol of human limitation but as the specific figure of the soul's material situatedness and its tendency to mistake sensory satisfaction for genuine erotic fulfillment — is what the *Commento* requires of its careful readers, and what the PicoDB entry on Penia attempts to restore.
