Moses ben Maimon (1138–1204 CE), known by the Hebrew acronym RaMBaM and in the Latin West as Moses Aegyptius or Maimonides, was the greatest Jewish philosopher of the medieval period and the author of the *Moreh Nevuchim* (*Dalala al-Ha'irin* in the Arabic original, *Dux neutrorum* or *Dux perplexorum* in Latin translation), the foundational text of Jewish rationalist philosophy. Born in Cordoba and forced by the Almohad persecution into a long journey across the Mediterranean — through North Africa, the Land of Israel, and finally Egypt, where he died as court physician to the sultan — Maimonides synthesised the philosophical heritage of Aristotle, al-Farabi, and Avicenna with the demands of Jewish law and scriptural interpretation, producing a philosophical theology that would define the parameters of Jewish intellectual culture for centuries. The *Guide for the Perplexed*, composed in Arabic around 1190 and translated into Hebrew by Samuel ibn Tibbon in 1204, circulated in Latin from the thirteenth century onward in several partial and complete translations, and two manuscript copies of the Latin text were listed among Pico's holdings in the earlier of his two library inventories (Black 2006, 227). By 1486, when Pico completed the *900 Conclusions*, the *Dux neutrorum* was one of the most significant texts in his Jewish philosophical dossier, and it would remain a reference point through the *Heptaplus* (1489) and *Apology* (1487).

Pico groups Maimonides in the *900 Conclusions* under the Arabic "nation" of thinkers, assigning him the designation "Moses the Egyptian" (*Moses Aegyptius*) and giving him three theses, section twelve in the Arab taxonomy — fewer than al-Farabi's eleven and Avicenna's twelve, but the very fact of inclusion in this section is, as Farmer notes, "of historical interest": the most famous Jewish philosopher in history is classed with the Arab philosophers rather than with the Hebrew Kabbalists or biblical exegetes (Farmer 1998, 276). The three Maimonidean theses are drawn from the *Guide*. Thesis 12.1, that "the demonstration in the eighth book of the *Physics*, brought forward by Aristotle to prove the existence of the first mover, first proves something particular," derives from *Guide* II, introduction, proposition 25. Thesis 12.2 — "the science of metaphysics is not one science" — reflects the *Guide*'s careful demarcation of the boundaries of metaphysical knowledge, its insistence that the single name "metaphysics" covers distinct inquiries that do not form a unified demonstrative science (Guide 2.2, as noted in Farmer's apparatus). Thesis 12.3, that "the simplicity and total immateriality of the first cannot be demonstrated through the efficient but only through the final causality of motion," likewise derives from the *Guide*'s argument about how the existence and attributes of God can and cannot be demonstrated (Guide 2.1). These three theses together reflect the *Guide*'s most distinctive philosophical contribution to the tradition: its insistence on the limits of human demonstrative reason when applied to the divine, and its corresponding advocacy of what we now call negative theology.

The *Guide for the Perplexed* opens with a systematic examination of the equivocal terms (*mushtarak*, *aequivoca*) applied to God in Scripture — terms like "form," "place," "sitting," "descending," and all the anthropomorphic predicates — and argues that every apparently positive attribute of God must be understood either as an attribute of God's action (not his essence) or as a negation of some limitation. God is not "living" in the sense we apply to created beings; rather, the word "living" when applied to God means the negation of death and of the kind of dependence on matter that makes death possible. God is not "powerful" in the sense we apply to human agents; rather, the application of "power" to God negates the kind of impotence or limited capacity that characterises created powers. This via negativa (*derek ha-shelilah*, the path of negation) is, for Maimonides, not a defeat of theological language but its highest form: to know that God is *not* corporeal, *not* multiple, *not* subject to time or change is genuine knowledge, while the pretence that positive attributes can adequately represent God's nature leads to idolatry. The *Guide* II.1 provides the twenty-five propositions and corollary from which Maimonides argues for the existence, incorporeality, and unity of God — precisely the territory from which Pico's Maimonidean theses 12.1 and 12.3 are drawn.

For Pico, Maimonides serves two distinct but interrelated purposes, one philosophical and one apologetic. The philosophical purpose is to establish that Jewish tradition at its highest intellectual level had arrived at conclusions compatible with Christian philosophical theology: a rigorous negative theology regarding the divine essence, a doctrine of divine simplicity (God's attributes are identical with God's essence, not a plurality of distinct properties), and a view of creation as dependent entirely on God's will and power. Pico's Kabbalistic thesis 11>63 — one of the theses according to his own opinion from the Kabbalistic section — reads: "Just as Aristotle concealed the more divine philosophy, which the ancient philosophers had veiled under fables and apologues, beneath the face of philosophical speculation and the brevity of words, so Rabbi Moses the Egyptian, in the book called by the Latins *Dux neutrorum*, while appearing through the superficial bark of words to walk with philosophy, embraces through the hidden intelligences of the deeper sense the mysteries of the Cabala" (*Conclusions*, Conc. cabalisticae... sec. opinionem propriam, 63; cited in Black 2006, 227). This remarkable thesis — which Farmer's note flags by observing that in it Pico claims Kabbalistic mysteries lie hidden beneath Maimonides's philosophical prose — is the most explicit statement of Pico's reading strategy for the *Guide*. Maimonides is not merely a philosophical theologian who happens to be Jewish; he is a crypto-Kabbalist who has encoded the deepest secrets of the Jewish mystical tradition behind a philosophical veil precisely analogous to the philosophical veil behind which Aristotle (according to a related Picoesque claim) concealed Neoplatonic wisdom.

This reading of Maimonides as crypto-Kabbalist is in direct contradiction with everything Maimonides actually said. The *Guide* is not merely non-Kabbalistic; it is implicitly anti-Kabbalistic in its strict negative theology, its insistence on divine simplicity and unity, and its rejection of any account of the divine that would introduce multiplicity into the Godhead. The doctrine of the sefirot, the ten emanatory powers through which the Kabbalistic tradition articulates the structure of divinity, is precisely the kind of theology that Maimonides's negative theology is designed to exclude. If the sefirot are divine attributes or powers distinct from God's essence, they threaten divine simplicity; if they are God himself, their multiplicity threatens divine unity. Wirszubski's landmark study *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989) documents with great precision how the distortion of Maimonides into a Kabbalistic authority was accomplished, and who was responsible. The chapter "Maimonides: Kabbalist in Philosophical Guise" in Wirszubski's second part (devoted to the translations of Flavius Mithridates) shows that the principal mechanism was the *Liber Redemptionis*, a Kabbalistic commentary on the thirty-six main secrets of the *Guide of the Perplexed* written by an Abulafian author and translated for Pico by Mithridates. This commentary presented Maimonides's philosophical text as a surface beneath which Kabbalistic secrets lay concealed, and the translator's own interpolations reinforced this interpretation. Wirszubski establishes that the translations of Mithridates are "shot through" with interpolations — some born of translation difficulties, some personal, some doctrinal — and that many of these doctrinal interpolations systematically slant the Kabbalistic texts toward Christian readings, toward a version of Kabbalism in which Maimonidean rationalism and Abulafian prophetic mysticism are presented as fundamentally compatible (Wirszubski 1989, 69–100).

Pico could not easily have known the extent to which Mithridates had distorted his sources. His Hebrew was functional but not sufficient for the kind of close textual comparison that would have revealed the translator's systematic interventions. Wirszubski's most important finding is that many of the features of Pico's reading of Maimonides that seem most original — including the central claim that the *Guide* is a Kabbalistic text in philosophical disguise — were not Pico's creative inventions but rather Mithridates's distortions presented to Pico as authentic. The Abulafian tradition had its own version of this claim: Abulafia genuinely believed that the *Guide* contained hidden Kabbalistic secrets and wrote his *Sitrei Torah* ("Secrets of the Torah") as a commentary that drew out these secrets. But this was an Abulafian interpretation of Maimonides, not an authentic account of Maimonides's own intentions. The translation that Mithridates titled *De secretis legis* (from the Abulafian *Sitrei Torah*) presented this Abulafian reading as if it were Maimonidean doctrine, and Pico absorbed this distorted Maimonides as part of his Kabbalistic curriculum.

Black's treatment of the role of Maimonides in Pico's account of *felicitas* and the intellect's ascent provides a further dimension. In the parable of the king's palace at the end of the *Guide*, Maimonides describes the graduated approach to knowledge of God in terms of proximity to a sovereign whose face one has not yet seen. Those outside the city are the unbelievers; those inside with their backs to the palace are those whose speculation rests on false principles; those who have found the wall but not the gate are those who have correct beliefs without philosophical foundations; those who have entered the hall are those who have studied the natural sciences; those who are near the king but cannot see him are those who have understood metaphysics. The one who is "able to prove what can be proved" and has "ascertained the truth as far as is possible" has entered the palace and is with the king (*Guide* III.51; Black 2006, 200–201). This account of a hierarchical ascent toward divine knowledge through the study of natural and metaphysical sciences — an ascent that leads to a contemplative beatitude described as proximity to and ultimately vision of the divine king — provided Pico with a major Maimonidean precedent for his own programme of philosophical ascent in the *Oration* and *Heptaplus*. The *Guide*'s account of the "true perfection" of man, which "consists in the acquisition of the rational virtues — the conception of intelligibles, which teach true opinions concerning the divine things" (Guide III.54), is one of the Jewish philosophical loci most directly relevant to Pico's *felicitas* programme.

In the *Apology* (1487), Pico invokes Maimonidean authority in a context that reveals his pragmatic as well as philosophical interest in the *Guide*. The *Apology*'s defence of the thirteenth of Pico's condemned theses — concerning the magic of divine names — draws partly on Maimonidean distinctions between the proper worship of God through his names and the illicit invocation of demonic powers. Maimonides in the *Guide* had distinguished between the true sage who worships God by contemplating and combining his proper names, and the idolater who invokes demonic powers through the names of foreign deities. Pico's argument that his theses about divine-name Kabbalah do not constitute demonic magic but rather the highest form of contemplative worship — addressing God by his proper names in the spirit of philosophical understanding — echoes this Maimonidean framework while recasting it through Kabbalistic lens. The *Liber Redemptionis* passage quoted by Wirszubski, in which the author states that "the great meditation on God according to his due — greater than any prayer and fasting and outcry — when this religion or cult of intellectual meditation on God and on his properties and works and on his names is followed, this in truth is the true religion owed to God by every sage ... nor can you know his names or have true knowledge of him except through the combination of letters" (Wirszubski 1989, 86–87), fuses Maimonidean rationalism with Abulafian letter-mysticism in precisely the synthesis that Pico's magic theses require. Whether Pico recognised the fusion as a distortion or accepted it as authentic Maimonidean doctrine is a question the surviving evidence does not resolve with certainty; what is clear is that Mithridates's version of Maimonides provided Pico with exactly the apologetic resource he needed.

The status of Maimonides as simultaneously a rationalist philosopher and a (crypto-)mystic in Pico's reading also illuminates the broader structure of the *900 Conclusions*. Pico places Maimonides among the Arabs rather than among the Hebrew Kabbalists because, from Pico's perspective, the *Guide* represents the highest philosophical articulation of Jewish tradition — a text that operates in the register of demonstration and argument rather than the register of symbolic mysticism. But the Kabbalistic thesis 11>63 insists that the philosophical surface of the *Guide* conceals a mystical depth. Maimonides is therefore simultaneously available as a philosophical source (for the three Maimonidean theses in section twelve, which draw on the *Guide*'s demonstrative arguments about God's existence and simplicity) and as a confirmatory mystical source (for the claim that Jewish philosophical tradition at its summit points toward Kabbalistic wisdom). This double deployment — Maimonides as rational philosopher and Maimonides as veiled Kabbalist — is one of the most characteristic strategies of Pico's syncretic method, and it depends on the mediation of Mithridates.

From the historian of ideas perspective, the Maimonidean thread in Pico's thought raises questions that scholarship is still resolving. Black's study of the *Heptaplus* traces the influence of the *Guide*'s approach to biblical hermeneutics on Pico's seven-level exegesis of Genesis: where Maimonides argued that the opening of Genesis encodes philosophical truths about the structure of the cosmos in allegorical form, Pico's *Heptaplus* generalises this insight into a comprehensive system of multiple literal-senses in which the entire first chapter of Genesis is shown to contain, simultaneously, accounts of the elementary, celestial, and intelligible worlds and of the human being as the microcosm of all three. One of Pico's copies of the *Guide* was originally bound with Philo's *Quaestiones in Genesim* — a physical conjunction suggesting that Pico saw the *Guide* and Philo's allegorical Genesis commentary as belonging to a single tradition of philosophical Scripture-reading (Black 2006, 228 n. 101). The *Heptaplus* develops a hermeneutics that is in this respect more Maimonidean than Abulafian: it seeks the philosophical content hidden in the scriptural text rather than the mystical transformations effected by the letters of the text. Pico's ability to hold both modes simultaneously — the Maimonidean philosophical-allegorical and the Abulafian prophetic-mystical — is one measure of the ambition and instability of his syncretic enterprise.

The encounter with Maimonides thus occupies a distinctive place in Pico's intellectual biography. More than al-Farabi and Avicenna, whose reception by Pico was primarily through the channel of Latin scholasticism, Maimonides was available to Pico as a figure who had wrestled with many of the same hermeneutical and theological problems that Pico faced: how to reconcile philosophical reason with revealed scripture, how to interpret the most apparently anti-philosophical passages of scripture in a way that preserves both philosophical integrity and scriptural authority, how to distinguish between what must be believed on faith and what can or must be demonstrated by reason. The *Guide*'s introduction states its aim as explaining equivocal terms in scripture and illuminating "parables too obscure" in the prophetic books. Pico's various programmes — the concordia of philosophy and theology in the *Oration*, the sevenfold exegesis of the *Heptaplus*, the negative-theological reflections on the *Apology*'s theses about God's names — are all in their different ways responses to the same problematic that generated the *Guide*. That Pico's Maimonides was partly a Mithridatean construction does not diminish this structural affinity; it rather shows how productively a distorted text can function when it arrives at exactly the moment when its reader most needs it.
