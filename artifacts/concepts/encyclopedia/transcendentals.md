The scholastic doctrine of the transcendentals (*transcendentalia*) holds that certain properties apply to every being whatsoever, transcending the Aristotelian categories and the boundaries of any genus or species. Unlike predicates that belong to a genus — like "animal" or "rational" — the transcendentals are said of being as such: every being without restriction is one, true, and good, and these properties are all "convertible" (*convertuntur*) with being, meaning that they have exactly the same extension even while differing in their conceptual content. In Pico della Mirandola's *De ente et uno*, the transcendental framework is not peripheral decoration but the structural armature of the treatise's central argument: that Plato and Aristotle genuinely agree about the relation of being and unity, and that the Neoplatonic tradition's apparent evidence to the contrary rests on a misreading that the transcendental doctrine can correct.

The historical origins of the doctrine lie in Aristotle's *Metaphysics*, where the relation between being (*to on*) and unity (*to hen*) is treated in book four. Aristotle's claim there is direct: being and unity are the same (*tauton*), and "one man" and "man" say the same thing. This is the locus classicus for what would become the Scholastic thesis of their convertibility. But Aristotle's point is not a merely linguistic one; he is making a metaphysical claim about the coextension of the concepts. Whatever is, is one; whatever is one, is. Similarly, truth and goodness were gradually incorporated into the list: truth names being as it stands in relation to an intellect that knows it, goodness names being as it stands in relation to an appetite or will that desires it. These relational additions are due in large part to Aquinas's systematic formulation in the *De veritate* (q. 1, a. 1), where he argues that being (*ens*) is the first concept of the intellect — the concept that is presupposed by every other act of understanding — while truth (*verum*) adds to being its relation to a knowing intellect, and goodness (*bonum*) adds its relation to an appetitive power. The addition of *aliquid* (something, a thing, marking the individuation of a being from every other being) rounds out the classical scholastic list that Pico inherits.

What the transcendental doctrine asserts, technically, is real identity combined with conceptual or "notional" distinction (*distinctio rationis cum fundamento in re*). Being, unity, truth, and goodness are not four different things: they are one reality considered under different aspects. A being is one insofar as it is undivided in itself; it is true insofar as it is knowable and corresponds to an intellect; it is good insofar as it is desirable or perfective of an appetite. These aspects are genuinely different as conceptual perspectives, but they pick out no different real features of the being in question. Because the transcendentals co-extend perfectly with being — because every being without exception has them all and nothing lacking any of them could be a being — they are said to "convert" with one another: *ens et unum convertuntur*, being and unity convert; *ens et verum convertuntur*, being and truth convert; and so forth.

Pico's *De ente et uno* (1491) was composed as a letter to Angelo Poliziano and was intended as the opening installment of a larger concord project, a *Symphonia Platonis et Aristotelis* that was never completed before his death in 1494. The immediate polemical target of *De ente et uno* is the Neoplatonic tradition as Pico encountered it in Marsilio Ficino: the view, derived ultimately from Plotinus and systematized by Proclus, that the One (*to hen*) transcends being (*to on*) and is therefore "prior," "simpler," and "more common" than being. Pico's argument in the opening of the treatise, as Edelheit (2008) reconstructs it, acknowledges the Neoplatonic claim on its own terms: the Neoplatonists say that unity is "simpler" (*simplicius*) than being because being, which Plotinus identifies with *nous* (intellect), involves the duality of knower and known, while the One transcends this duality. They also say unity is "more common" (*communius*) because unity extends not only to everything that being does but also to God (who is nonbeing in a transcending sense) and even to prime matter. The apparent superiority of unity over being seems, on this reading, well established.

Salas (2014) provides the most careful analysis of what Pico's response to this claim actually does philosophically. Pico's strategy is twofold. First, he argues that the Platonic dialogues most cited in support of the Neoplatonic position — the *Parmenides* and the *Sophist* — do not in fact convey dogmatic metaphysical teachings about the superiority of unity over being. The Parmenides, Pico insists, is an exercise in dialectic (*dialectica quaedam exercitatio*), not a statement of Platonic doctrine. Pico found support for this reading in the skeptical Academy's non-dogmatic interpretation of the Platonic dialogues — a reading traceable through Cicero — though its application to the Parmenides was contested by Ficino, who insisted that the dialogue contains genuine Platonic theology. Edelheit shows that Pico's hermeneutic move here is influenced by Academic probabilism: the Parmenides cannot be the basis for a firm metaphysical doctrine because Plato's dialogues are not the vehicle of doctrinal assertion in the first place.

The second and more philosophically original prong of Pico's argument brings the transcendental doctrine directly to bear. Pico distinguishes two senses of "being" (*ens*): first, being in the broad sense of anything that is not nothing, standing outside nihility (*extra nihilum*); second, being in the sense of a concrete entity, something that *has* being rather than being being itself. In the first sense, being and unity must convert: whatever is not nothing, is one, and whatever is one, is; anything failing either condition falls into nonbeing. The Aristotelian identity of being and unity at this level is, Pico argues, what Aristotle actually maintains in *Metaphysics* IV, and it should not be confused with the Neoplatonic claim that unity in some more eminent sense transcends being.

The second sense of being opens onto the metaphysics of participation. Using the distinction between abstract names (*abstracta*) — heat, light, humanity — and concrete names (*concreta*) — hot, luminous, man — Pico establishes that concrete things are not their properties from themselves but possess those properties by participating in the abstract principles. Being (*ens*), signified concretely, is a thing that has or participates in existence (*esse*), which is signified abstractly. God, as the totality of existence — *totius esse plenitudo*, the fullness of all existence — is not a being in this concrete sense but is being itself, from itself (*a se*), unparticipated. In this sense one can say, with the Neoplatonists, that God is "above being" (*supra ens*), above unity, above truth, above goodness — not because God lacks these perfections but because God is those perfections in their unparticipated, unqualified form, while creatures are beings, ones, trues, and goods only through participation.

The transcendental framework here performs a double service. On one hand, it establishes the convertibility of being and unity at the level of created things: every being is one, every unity is a being, and neither term has greater extension than the other in the created order. This is Pico's Aristotelian and Thomistic point, directed against the Neoplatonic inflation of unity. On the other hand, the logic of participation allows Pico to accommodate the Neoplatonic insight that God is "above being" without conceding that unity is genuinely superior to being: God is above the participated order as a whole, not above being in some sense that privileges unity. Pico's rhetorical strategy, which Edelheit describes as characteristic of his broader theological method, is to find a framework capacious enough to accommodate apparently contradictory claims by situating them at different levels or in different semantic registers.

Victor Salas (2014) points out a significant dissonance in Pico's use of the Thomistic framework. Thomas Aquinas carefully preserves what Salas calls an "analogical community" between God and creatures: even though God is ipsum esse subsistens and no creature is its own esse, there remains a relation of "dissimilar similarity" between creator and creature on account of the causal imitation involved in creation. God communicates esse to creatures as a participated act, and this communication establishes both the difference (no creature equals its cause) and the similarity (every creature imitates its cause). For Thomas, this analogical balance — not univocity, not equivocity, but analogy — is what prevents transcendental predication from collapsing into either pantheism or agnosticism. Pico, in Salas's reading, "slackens" this tension in favor of radical difference. In chapter five of *De ente et uno*, Pico conducts an ascending negation that affirms God as being itself, truth itself, unity itself, and goodness itself, only to then negate even these affirmations: God is above being, above truth, above the one. The endpoint — "for the first time we know him in some way when we are altogether ignorant of him" — is Pseudo-Dionysian apophasis. The transcendentals are thus vehicles not of a stable analogical community but of a progressive stripping-away that leads beyond the convertible transcendentals into unintelligible darkness.

Whether this is a failure of philosophical nerve or a deliberate theological move is one of the genuinely open questions in *De ente et uno* scholarship. Edelheit (2008) suggests that Pico is here following what he calls a "probabilist" or Academic method: philosophical claims, including transcendental ones, function as probable rather than demonstrably certain truths, and their ultimate trajectory is toward a theological certitude that transcends philosophical probability. The transcendentals mark the limit of positive philosophical predication about God; beyond that limit lies revealed theology and, beyond that, the mystical ascent described by Pseudo-Dionysius and coded by the Kabbalah into the progressive ascent through the sefirot. The convertibility of being and unity is thus, in Pico's hands, both a firm scholastic thesis and a provisional resting point in an ascent that moves beyond it.

It is important to note the historical context of the Ficino-Pico dispute that gives *De ente et uno* its polemical edge. Ficino, in his commentary on Plato's Parmenides, had attacked the younger Pico — playing with Pico's surname — for maintaining a position "contrary to the opinion of all the Platonists" (cited in Salas 2014). Ficino's commitment to the Plotinian elevation of the One over being was not merely philosophical but cosmological: for Ficino, the Platonic-Neoplatonic hierarchy of the One, Intellect, and Soul required the strict transcendence of the One over being, and any collapse of this distinction would flatten the metaphysical landscape on which the entire Platonic project of intellectual ascent depended. Pico's response, as Michael Allen (2020) shows in his detailed reconstruction of the "second Ficino-Pico controversy," was to argue that Ficino's identification of the Neoplatonic Academici with Plato himself was a mistake: the position that unity transcends being belongs to Speusippus, Xenocrates, and the dogmatic successors of Plato, not to Plato himself, who nowhere in the dialogues asserts such a metaphysical doctrine. The transcendental convertibility of being and unity is thus, for Pico, a Platonic position as well as an Aristotelian and Thomistic one — Plato, properly read, is a proto-scholastic on this question.

The practical stakes of the doctrine for Pico's *Apology* and his defense of the condemned theses are also significant. Several of Pico's 900 Conclusions touched on metaphysical questions — about the relation of being and unity, about whether God is beyond being, about participation and the transcendentals — that his examiners found theologically suspicious. Edelheit shows that in the *Apology*, Pico defends the orthodoxy of these theses by anchoring them firmly within the Thomistic scholastic framework: the claim that God is above being (*supra ens*) as the unparticipated source of existence is not a heterodox Neoplatonic claim but a rigorous application of the Thomistic real distinction between esse and essentia. The transcendentals provide the vocabulary for this defense: by showing that "being," "unity," "truth," and "goodness" are convertible and co-extensive at the level of creatures, while God is the unparticipated source of all of them, Pico maintains both the scholastic convertibility thesis and the apophatic theological tradition within a single framework. What might look like contradictory positions — affirming that being and unity convert, and also affirming that God is above being — turn out to be positions at different levels of the same analysis, and the transcendental framework is what makes their compatibility visible.
