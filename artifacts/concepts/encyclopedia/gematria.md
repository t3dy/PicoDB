Gematria (גמטריה) is the Kabbalistic interpretive method of assigning numerical values to Hebrew letters and using those values to discover hidden correspondences between words, phrases, and concepts. The Hebrew alphabet assigns a fixed numerical value to each of its twenty-two letters: aleph (א) = 1, beth (ב) = 2, gimel (ג) = 3, and so on up to yod (י) = 10, then kaph (כ) = 20, lamed (ל) = 30, continuing through the hundreds, with tav (ת) = 400. Every Hebrew word therefore has a numerical sum, and any two words or phrases sharing the same sum are taken to share a hidden connection that reveals underlying truth concealed in the surface of the scriptural text. Gematria is enumerated among the thirty-two hermeneutical paths of wisdom described in the *Sefer Yetzirah* (Book of Creation), the ancient cosmological text that was foundational to almost all subsequent Kabbalistic speculation. Within the Kabbalistic tradition more broadly, gematria belongs to a family of related interpretive techniques — including *notarikon* (the reading of each letter of a word as an initial for another word) and *temurah* (letter substitution by various cipher schemes such as *atbash*) — all of which treat the Hebrew text as a code whose numerical and formal properties disclose divine meaning inaccessible to literal reading.

Pico della Mirandola encountered gematria through the Kabbalistic translations made for him by Flavius Mithridates in the summer of 1486, and he deployed it in the *900 Conclusions* as one of the principal methodological instruments by which Kabbalistic argument achieves doctrinal force. Wirszubski observes that the translations of Mithridates are "shot through with Hebrew words and phrases" precisely because the isopsephic equations at the heart of gematria — *isopsephy* being the Greek equivalent of the Hebrew technique, calculating the same correspondence by letter-values — are essentially untranslatable without retaining the Hebrew (Wirszubski 1989, 7). A Latin translation can state the conclusion of a gematric argument — that two concepts are secretly connected — but cannot show the argumentative mechanism without displaying the Hebrew letters. Mithridates was aware of this limitation. In one marginal note he writes directly to Pico: "These are great things, but it is impossible that you should understand them except in the proper Hebrew letters and words" (Cod. Chigi, fol. 75r).

The specific gematriot that appear in Pico's *Conclusions* were documented by Wirszubski through careful comparison of the theses with the Latin translations and with the underlying Hebrew originals. Several of Pico's most significant Kabbalistic theses *secundum opinionem propriam* — the theses expressing Pico's own conclusions rather than those attributed to Kabbalistic tradition — depend on gematric equations for their argumentative core. These are not purely formal exercises: they serve Pico's overarching apologetic program, which is to demonstrate that Kabbalah, when rightly interpreted, confirms and illuminates Christian doctrine. Farmer's study (1998) describes this as one of the primary mechanisms of Pico's broader concordist project: gematria provides what might be called an objective structural warrant for the *occultae concatenationes* — the hidden connections that Pico believed underlay all philosophical and theological traditions. If two Hebrew terms share a numerical total, that equivalence is not a human convention but a property inscribed in the divine language, a real rather than arbitrary connection between the referents of those terms.

The most celebrated gematric equation in the *Conclusions* is the equivalence of *Mashiach* (מָשִׁיחַ, Messiah) with *Nahash* (נָחָשׁ, serpent). The numerical value of *Mashiach* (mem = 40, shin = 300, yod = 10, het = 8) is 358. The numerical value of *Nahash* (nun = 50, het = 8, shin = 300) is likewise 358. For Pico, this numerical identity is not an accident. It confirms what he reads as a prefigurative relationship between the bronze serpent Moses erected in the wilderness (Numbers 21:8–9), the sight of which healed the Israelites bitten by fiery serpents, and Christ, whose crucifixion is the ultimate healing of humanity's condition. Pico's Kabbalistic thesis on this point develops the same typological interpretation that John 3:14 advances — "as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of Man be lifted up" — but grounds it not in typological exegesis alone but in the internal numerical structure of the Hebrew language. The gematria thus constitutes, in Pico's system, a second level of evidence, one that operates within Jewish textual tradition itself rather than being imposed from outside by Christian interpretation. The argument is: if Kabbalah's own interpretive methods — methods that the rabbinic tradition itself endorses — reveal this equivalence, then Kabbalah itself points toward Christ.

A second significant equation is the equivalence of *Elohim* (אֱלֹהִים, the divine name usually translated as God) with *ha-teva* (הַטֶּבַע, nature). The numerical value of *Elohim* (aleph = 1, lamed = 30, he = 5, yod = 10, mem = 40) is 86. The numerical value of *ha-teva* (he = 5, tet = 9, bet = 2, ayin = 70) is likewise 86. This equation had a long and consequential afterlife in Jewish philosophical thought — it was taken up by later thinkers to suggest that "God" and "nature" are the same reality under different descriptions, a reading with obvious resonances with Spinoza — but for Pico the significance is somewhat different. The equation underscores the divine immanence in the natural world: God's presence pervades the structure of nature so thoroughly that the divine name and the name of nature share a single numerical identity. This supports Pico's broader vision of a philosophical theology in which the divine is accessible through multiple levels of reality — natural, celestial, and angelic — rather than being withdrawn into pure transcendence.

The technical vocabulary of gematria as it appears in the *Conclusions* and in the translations of Mithridates reveals how the translations themselves mediated and partially transformed the technique. Wirszubski demonstrates that Mithridates often includes Hebrew words and phrases in the Latin translation specifically when they are the subjects of isopsephic equations, because there is no way to convey the argument without them (Wirszubski 1989, 7–8). A striking example from the *Liber Redemptionis* (Cod. Chigi, fol. 332r–v) shows Mithridates working out a complex gematric demonstration in which the three divine names YHWH (יהוה, numerical value 26), Adonai (אֲדֹנָי, numerical value 65), and Elohim (אֱלֹהִים, numerical value 86) combine to a total of 177, which equals the numerical value of three further phrases all meaning "cause of being" or "cause of the world." The translation reproduces the entire demonstration in Latin, embedding the Hebrew numerals and even providing the raw letter-values, because only in this form can the reader follow the argument. This passage illustrates a characteristic of gematric reasoning that distinguishes it sharply from mere symbolism: it claims to be not merely associative but demonstrative, proceeding by arithmetic that any reader can verify. The *occultae concatenationes* are hidden but not arbitrary; once disclosed by the computation, they compel acknowledgment.

A peculiar episode that Wirszubski documents shows how Mithridates himself deployed gematria for purposes of self-promotion and playful mockery, inserting equations into the translated texts in which his own name appears. At one point in a translation of Abulafia's *De Secretis Legis* (Cod. Vat. Ebr. 190, fol. 415v), Mithridates writes a marginal note noting that the numerical value of a particular Hebrew phrase equals the numerical value of his own name "Mithridates," adding the ambiguous remark "the air will be foul where he is" — a possible self-deprecating joke about the equation between his name and a less-than-dignified concept. Wirszubski interprets this note as evidence that Mithridates used the gematric framework playfully and sometimes subversively, perhaps equating his own name with divine names in passages that Pico could not easily decode without Hebrew competency (Wirszubski 1989, 115–16). This playful dimension of the mock mysteries (*Quodlibet in quolibet*, "anything in anything," as Wirszubski titles Chapter 8) does not undermine the serious uses of gematria in the *Conclusions*, but it does indicate that the medium Pico was working with was not a neutral conveyance of Kabbalistic doctrine. Mithridates' translations are performances as well as translations, and the persona of the translator is woven into the text in ways that Pico could only partially perceive.

For Pico's philosophical program, gematria represents the most direct form of his concordist method. The claim of the *Conclusions* as a whole is that all philosophical and theological traditions, rightly understood, converge on a single truth. The method for demonstrating this convergence is what Farmer describes as Pico's "correlative system": the identification of structural parallels and hidden connections across traditions, with each connection serving as evidence of the underlying unity. Gematria is the paradigm case of this procedure in its Kabbalistic form, because the numerical equivalences it discovers are not proposed by the interpreter but revealed by the properties of the divine language itself. Hebrew, in the Kabbalistic tradition, is not a human invention but the original language of creation: the same divine speech that brought the world into being is encoded in the letters and numbers of Scripture. Gematria therefore does not impose a Christian meaning on a Jewish text; it discloses a meaning that was always already present in the text's structure, waiting for an interpreter with the right hermeneutical tools to uncover it.

This claim — that gematria is revelatory rather than merely interpretive — is what gives Pico's Kabbalistic theses their particular bite in the context of the *Apology*. When Pope Innocent VIII's commission condemned thirteen of Pico's theses in early 1487, the Kabbalistic thesis Q5 was among them: Pico had argued that "there is no science that makes us more certain of Christ's divinity than magic and Kabbalah." The commissioners found this scandalous and apparently incomprehensible, as Copenhaver emphasizes — the thesis was "utterly unintelligible" to Pico's judges, who lacked the technical knowledge to evaluate it (Copenhaver 2022, 15). But the structure of the argument is precisely gematric in character: Kabbalah's own internal procedures, when applied to the Hebrew names for Messiah, Jesus, and related terms, produce numerical equivalences that, on Pico's reading, confirm Christian doctrine from within the Jewish tradition. The thesis is not simply an assertion that Kabbalah is compatible with Christianity; it is a claim about the epistemic status of the gematric method, which Pico treats as yielding a form of certainty superior to merely rational argument.

The question of whether Pico actually performed gematric computations himself or simply accepted the conclusions presented in Mithridates' translations is difficult to resolve. Wirszubski's evidence suggests that Pico's knowledge of Hebrew, acquired hastily in the summer of 1486, was sufficient to follow a gematric demonstration when the Hebrew words were provided, but not sufficient to conduct independent searches for relevant equations in the biblical text (Wirszubski 1989, 4–9). The theses that deploy gematria are in most cases directly derived from specific passages in the translated texts, where Mithridates had already identified the equivalences. This does not mean Pico was passive: the *Conclusions* show him selecting, combining, and redirecting the equations he found in his sources toward new argumentative purposes, especially the Christological ones that the translations had already encouraged through their interpolations. The *occultae concatenationes* that gematria reveals were, for Pico, genuine discoveries about the structure of reality — discoveries that Mithridates had mediated, manipulated, and in some measure fabricated, but which Pico received in good faith as ancient Kabbalistic wisdom.

The broader significance of gematria in Pico's project is methodological as much as doctrinal. By including gematria among the tools of philosophical inquiry, Pico implicitly claims that the numerical properties of the divine language belong to the same order of evidence as logical demonstration or natural philosophical argument. This is a fundamental departure from the Aristotelian and scholastic epistemic framework, which recognizes no revelatory function for linguistic form as such. The claim has affinities with the Neoplatonic tradition, in which number is a metaphysical principle and mathematical structures are real properties of the cosmos rather than human abstractions. For Pico, gematria is the point where Kabbalistic hermeneutics and Pythagorean-Platonic number theory meet: both traditions insist that numerical structure is not conventional but ontological, that the ratios and equations discoverable in language and in the cosmos alike are signatures of divine reason inscribed in creation. The *900 Conclusions* as a whole can be read as an attempt to show that this convergence between Jewish Kabbalistic number-hermeneutics and Platonic-Pythagorean number-metaphysics is itself evidence of the unity of all wisdom — and gematria is one of the primary exhibits in that demonstration.
