In February 1487, within weeks of the *900 Conclusions* being printed in Rome in December 1486, Pope Innocent VIII convened a commission of sixteen theologians, lawyers, and papal officials to examine the controversial propositions that had caused immediate uproar in Rome. By mid-March — moving, as Brian Copenhaver observes, with lightning speed by Vatican standards — the commission had rendered its verdict, finding thirteen of Pico's nine hundred propositions to be theologically problematic, with some deemed outright heretical, others scandalous, and still others erroneous (Copenhaver 2022, 7–8). These thirteen condemned theses are the biographical, theological, and philosophical crux of the entire crisis of 1487, and they determine the structure of the *Apology*, Pico's lengthy self-defense in which he devotes one "Question" — Q1 through Q13 — to defending each condemned proposition in turn. Understanding the condemned theses requires attention to their content, the logic of the commission's objections, and the nature of Pico's responses, as analyzed in the most detailed recent treatments by Copenhaver in *Pico della Mirandola on Trial* (2022) and *Magic and the Dignity of Man* (2019), and by S. A. Farmer in his edition of the *Conclusions* (1998).

The thirteen theses are not a coherent philosophical system but a disparate set of propositions drawn from different parts of the *Conclusions*, reflecting the papal commission's selection process rather than any internal logic of Pico's project. As Copenhaver notes, the pattern of all but two of the condemned theses follows the *quaestio* form of medieval scholastic commentary and disputation — they are framed as technical claims in metaphysics, sacramental theology, Christology, and moral theology (Copenhaver 2022, 15). The two exceptions are Q5, on Kabbalah, which is formally and substantively alien to the scholastic *quaestio* format, and Q7, on Origen, which takes the form of philological polemic rather than metaphysical argumentation. This means that the condemned theses, viewed together, reveal something important about what the papal commission understood Pico to be doing: eleven of the thirteen are scholastic theological propositions that the commission judged by the criteria of Thomistic orthodoxy, while only two reach into the exotic traditions — Jewish mysticism and patristic theology — that Pico's broader syncretic program engages.

The thirteen Questions of the *Apology* address topics in the following order, which corresponds roughly to the commission's order of inquiry: Christ's descent into Hell (Q1), original sin (Q2), worship (Q3), the incarnation (Q4), Kabbalah (Q5), eucharist I (Q6), Origen (Q7), the nature of heresy and belief (Q8), eucharist II (Q9), eucharist III (Q10), miracles (Q11), the divine nature (Q12), and the soul (Q13). The clustering of three eucharistic theses (Q6, Q9, Q10) is striking and reflects a pattern that Copenhaver traces back to the long and contested history of eucharistic controversy since Berengar of Tours in the eleventh century — a tradition whose accumulated technical apparatus Pico had mastered and in which he had proposed novel solutions that his judges found inadmissible (Copenhaver 2022, 15–29).

The eucharistic theses reveal Pico at his most technically ambitious as a scholastic philosopher. The Q6 thesis proposes that without bread's substance undergoing conversion (*conversio*) into Christ's body, and without eliminating *paneitas* — bread-ness, the formal character of bread — Christ's body can nonetheless be present on the altar in keeping with sacramental reality (*veritas sacramenti*), through the mechanism of *suppositatio*, a formal-logical mode of assumption or "suppositing" by the Word. This terminology derives from a tradition of medieval logical and metaphysical analysis — the doctrine of *suppositum* and its variants — that was central to Thomist and Scotist treatments of the incarnation and the eucharist. Copenhaver devotes an entire chapter to unpacking what the "suppo-words" (*suppositum*, *suppositare*, *suppositatio*, *substentatio*) mean in the context of Pico's Q6, demonstrating that Pico is engaging in a philosophically coherent, if bold, extension of existing scholastic analysis of sacramental presence (Copenhaver 2022, 70–96). The Q9 thesis concerns the metaphysics of accidents existing without their substance after consecration — a classic problem in eucharistic theology, since after the bread's substance becomes Christ's body, the observable accidents of bread (its color, weight, taste) must somehow persist — and Pico's proposal involves a specific reading of what it means for an accident to "exist-in" (*inexistere*) its subject. The Q10 thesis addresses the words of consecration (*hoc est enim corpus meum*), arguing that the demonstrative pronoun *hoc* is to be taken materially rather than as signifying — a claim in medieval supposition theory that the commission condemned as scandalous and contrary to common opinion.

The papal commission's approach to the eucharistic theses was driven by the formula *ex vi verborum* — "from the meaning of the words" — which the commissioners cited in four of their verdicts, indicating that they were condemning Pico's propositions as stated, without attending to the contextual qualifications and philosophical distinctions he claimed they required (Copenhaver 2022, 8). Pico's consistent complaint in the *Apology* is that the commission ignored his testimony when he explained what he meant by the condemned words; the documents of the trial, as Copenhaver reconstructs them, confirm that this was indeed the pattern of the proceedings — the verdict was effectively determined before the hearings concluded (Copenhaver 2022, 8).

The theses on the incarnation (Q4) are the thread that connects the eucharistic theses to the other theological topics. Pico's Q4 thesis concerns the possibility of *suppositatio* — the Word of God taking on or "suppositing" a created nature — and it is the logical foundation for his eucharistic proposals, since the same mechanism that he uses to explain how the divine Word assumes human nature in the incarnation he also applies to explain how Christ's body might be present in the sacrament without the bread undergoing substantial conversion. The commission condemned this thesis as suggesting heresy about the incarnation, reading Pico's statement about *what is possible* (*de possibili*) as a claim about *how things actually are* (*de sic esse*). Pico's central argument throughout the *Apology* is that this reading misidentifies the modal status of his propositions: he is exploring the logical possibility space of Christian doctrine, not denying any article of the creed.

The Q1 thesis, concerning Christ's descent into Hell (the *descensus ad inferos* of the Creed), raises the metaphysical problem of how Christ, whose body was in the tomb and whose soul was the bodiless divine spirit, could be present *in a place* — Hell being understood as a spatial location, or rather a complex of locations (Purgatory, Limbo, Hell proper) in the theological geography of the late medieval world. The question of how a bodiless spirit can be "in" a place was technically demanding, since location was generally understood as a predicate of bodies. Pico proposes a solution using the theory of *suppositatio* — the same family of concepts that governs his eucharistic proposals — which the commission found inadmissible. The Harrowing of Hell was, Copenhaver notes, the most open to attack of all the condemned propositions, precisely because the metaphysical puzzle it raised was genuine and the traditional scholastic solutions were themselves philosophically contested (Copenhaver 2022, 209).

Q5, on Kabbalah and magic, stands apart from the other condemned theses both in form and in philosophical register, and it has attracted more modern attention than any of the others. The thesis, which Copenhaver identifies as "T2M9" in his taxonomy of the *Conclusions*, reads: "There is no knowledge that gives us more certainty of Christ's divinity than magic and Kabbalah" (*Nulla est scientia quae nos magis certificet de divinitate Christi, quam magia et Cabala*) (Copenhaver 2019, 406). This proposition condenses the entire syncretic program of the *Conclusions* — the claim that Jewish Kabbalah and natural philosophy (*magia naturalis*) provide confirmatory evidence for Christian doctrine — into a single dramatic assertion. The commission read it as claiming that occult knowledge is more epistemically certain than Christian theology — a clear offense against the hierarchy of the disciplines and the authority of revealed doctrine. The proposition was incomprehensible to the commission in another sense as well: the commissioners, as Pico scornfully noted in the *Apology*, did not know what Kabbalah meant and had no access to the Kabbalistic sources in Hebrew and Aramaic that would have allowed them to evaluate Pico's claim (Copenhaver 2019, 406–410).

Pico's defense of T2M9 in the *Apology* is, as Copenhaver acknowledges, a piece of "sophistry" employing the logic of disjunctive hypotheticals: he restates the thesis as a conditional whose antecedent contains a disjunction, so that the consequent (certainty of Christ's divinity) holds if any one of the disjuncts (magic, Kabbalah, or some other knowledge) is true (Copenhaver 2019, 406–407). The logical move is correct but evasive, as Copenhaver notes. The deeper philosophical intent of T2M9, however, is not primarily epistemological but apologetic: Pico is arguing that from within the traditions of Jewish mysticism and natural philosophy, one can *demonstrate* — rather than merely assert on the basis of authority — the divinity of Christ. This is an apologetic claim, not a claim about the superiority of magic over theology. From Pico's perspective, T2M9 is not a threat to Christian theology but a supplement to it: it expands the range of traditions from which confirmatory arguments for Christian doctrine can be drawn.

The broader context of T2M9 is established by its surrounding theses in the *Conclusions*. The immediately preceding proposition (T2M8) states that "Christ's miracles are the most certain evidence of his divinity not because of what was done but because of how it was done" — referring to the manner of miraculous action as a signature of divine power. T2M9 then claims that magic and Kabbalah provide a different route to the same certainty, not through miracle but through philosophical analysis of the divine names and natural powers. Together, these theses constitute a miniature theory of apologetics: there are multiple independent routes to certainty about Christ's divinity, and the fact that one route runs through traditions the commission regards as suspect does not disqualify it as a route (Copenhaver 2019, 406–410).

S. A. Farmer's analysis of the *Conclusions* provides an essential complement to Copenhaver's work on the *Apology*. Farmer argues that the condemnation of 1487 is the biographical event that forces the structure of the *Conclusions*' entire reception history — it defines what Pico's project was seen as, and what it was defended as (Farmer 1998). The thirteen condemned theses were not, on Farmer's account, randomly selected: they represent the commission's attempt to identify the most dangerous proposals in a work whose theological radicalism was systematic rather than incidental. The commission's selection reveals their reading: they were most alarmed by the eucharistic metaphysics (which touched the most sensitive area of late-medieval sacramental theology), by the Kabbalah thesis (which seemed to enthrone occult knowledge above Christian doctrine), and by the proposals on incarnation and hell-descent (which raised the possibility that Pico had a non-standard theory of divine embodiment).

Pico's response to the condemnation was not capitulation but escalation. The *Apology*, which he produced in great haste in the spring and summer of 1487, is an aggressive document — combative, sometimes insulting to its intended audience, and philosophically demanding in ways that the papal commission was not equipped to evaluate. Copenhaver's reconstruction of the trial proceedings shows that Pico appeared before the commission to defend his theses and did not merely submit; his exchanges with individual commissioners were heated, involving accusations of incompetence and at least one memorable exchange in which Pico challenged a commissioner who cited a dubious historical authority with the words "your little packet can go straight to Hell since no one trusts it" (Copenhaver 2022, 9–10). The *Apology* was published on May 31, 1487, before the process was concluded, which led Innocent VIII to announce a further inquiry; on July 31, 1487, Pico swore to accept the pope's future decision; on August 4, 1487, the pope formally condemned the entire *Conclusions* and prohibited their publication and circulation under pain of excommunication (Edelheit 2008, 286–287).

The intellectual significance of the condemned theses extends beyond the biographical drama of the trial. Copenhaver's *Pico della Mirandola on Trial* demonstrates that the *Apology*'s engagement with the condemned theses reveals a Pico who is thoroughly scholastic in his philosophical formation — not a humanist philosopher writing in the Petrarchan mode, but a late medieval thinker whose intellectual resources are Aquinas, Scotus, Ockham, Henry of Ghent, and dozens of other scholastic authorities, deployed with extraordinary technical competence in service of a genuinely novel philosophical theology (Copenhaver 2022, 360–371). The condemned theses are thus not evidence of a failed humanist project but evidence of a thoroughly medieval project that proved too philosophically bold — too creative in its application of scholastic categories — for the institutional guardians of orthodoxy to accommodate.

The *Apology*'s rhetorical and philosophical strategy, as both Copenhaver and Edelheit have analyzed it, involves a double movement: using the framework of those who condemned the theses to argue against the condemnation. Pico draws on Thomistic distinctions to show that the commission has confused the level of *opinio* with the level of *fides*; he draws on Scotist metaphysics to argue for the philosophical legitimacy of his eucharistic proposals; he draws on medieval logical theory to defend his treatment of the words of consecration. Each condemned thesis becomes an occasion to demonstrate that the scholastic tradition, properly understood, contains the resources for a more philosophically sophisticated account of Christian doctrine than the commission's rigid Thomism permits. The *Apology* is not, in the end, an apology in the modern sense of the word — an expression of regret or submission — but an apologia in the ancient sense: a philosophical defense before a tribunal, making the case that the defendant's positions are not only defensible but represent a more rigorous engagement with the tradition than the prosecution's theology has achieved.

The legacy of the condemned theses was paradoxically generative. The condemnation forced Pico to articulate his philosophical theology with a clarity and completeness that the compressed form of the *Conclusions* did not require; the *Apology* is a far more detailed and philosophically revealing text than any of his other works, precisely because the legal-theological context demanded explicitness. Gianfrancesco Pico, Giovanni's nephew and first biographer, insisted that the thirteen theses had been misunderstood and wrongly condemned, and his insistence shaped the subsequent reception of his uncle as a philosopher unjustly persecuted for philosophical boldness (Copenhaver 2022, 274). Whether that assessment is historically accurate or posthumous myth-making is itself a question the scholarship has not fully resolved. What is clear is that the condemned theses — as a specific set of propositions in a specific historical trial — remain the most productive entry point for understanding what Pico's philosophical theology actually claimed, how scholastic thought worked in late fifteenth-century Italy, and where the boundaries of theological toleration lay in the years before the Reformation transformed those questions into something neither Pico nor his judges would have recognized.
