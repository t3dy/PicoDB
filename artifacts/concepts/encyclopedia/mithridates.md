Flavius Mithridates — baptized name of a Jewish convert to Christianity who was probably born in Sicily around the middle of the fifteenth century and died sometime around 1489 — is the single most important human intermediary between Giovanni Pico della Mirandola and the world of Jewish mysticism. Without him, Pico's Kabbalistic project as it actually exists would have been impossible: the *900 Conclusions* of 1486 would contain no Kabbalistic section, the *Oration* would reach no Kabbalistic climax, and the entire program of Christian Kabbalah that Pico launched would lack its primary textual foundation. But Mithridates was not a neutral conduit. Chaim Wirszubski's monograph *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989), the indispensable scholarly reckoning with this relationship, demonstrates that Mithridates translated not only Kabbalistic texts but his own theological convictions into them — inserting Christianizing interpolations that manufactured convergences between Jewish mysticism and Christian doctrine, and thereby producing for Pico a version of Kabbalah that was, in important respects, already Christian before Pico read it.

The man's origins are uncertain. His baptized name, Flavius Mithridates, is a Latinized Roman name that he appears to have adopted on conversion and used in his scholarly career. His Jewish identity before baptism was probably Shmuel ibn Farisol or, by Umberto Cassuto's conjecture (supported by gematric evidence in Mithridates' own translations), Samuel — *Shmuel* in Hebrew. He was from Sicily, and Sicilian Jewish communities had been subject to intense conversion pressure and periodic persecution throughout the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries. Mithridates shows up in several contexts before his association with Pico: he delivered a famous Good Friday sermon before Pope Sixtus IV in 1481, demonstrating remarkable erudition in Hebrew, Aramaic, Arabic, and Latin, and he composed a *Sermo de Passione Domini* that survives and has been studied by Wirszubski. He also appears to have had a complex and troubled relationship with the papal court, with his patron the Cardinal of Benevento (Giovanni Battista Cibo, who became Innocent VIII), whom he later pilloried savagely in the margins of his translations.

His association with Pico is dateable with reasonable precision to the summer and autumn of 1486. Pico's letter to Ficino of early autumn 1486, written from La Fratta, describes his intensive study of Hebrew: having spent "a full month, days and nights, in vigil over the Hebrew language," he was now turning to Arabic and Chaldean. The letter to an unknown friend, dated November 10, 1486, from Fratta — one of the most revealing documents in the Pico epistolary and now preserved in translation in the PicoDB primary texts — provides a vivid account of Mithridates' relationship with Pico. Pico writes that he is reading the unknown friend's letter to Mithridates, which he was unable to deliver because of Mithridates' absence. He discusses the question of Hebrew and Chaldean script that the correspondent had raised, invoking both Mithridates' authority and the evidence of the Kabbalistic commentaries on the *Sefer Yetzirah* (the "book that the ancient father Abraham published on the Creation"), and he reports a remarkable episode: "As for the Chaldean alphabet you ask for — you could not have gotten it even from Mithridates; nothing through him, even though you can use me to obtain it from him who can do all things. For he refused in every way to teach me the Chaldean language unless I first swore — indeed, with a sworn formula — never to transmit it to anyone. Our Girolamo Benivieni can testify to this: he happened to be present while Mithridates was teaching me, and the furious man threw him bodily out of the house."

This episode is a window into the peculiar dynamics of the relationship. Mithridates was simultaneously Pico's paid translator and teacher, his cultural broker between the Christian humanist world and the world of medieval Jewish learning, and a man with his own agenda, his own resentments, and his own theological investments. The oath of secrecy that Mithridates demanded before teaching Chaldean suggests that he understood his role as a mediator of esoteric knowledge — and that he was careful about the terms on which he transmitted it. The spectacle of Benivieni being physically ejected from the house while Pico was receiving instruction gives the collaboration an almost furtive intensity: this was not a standard Latin-lesson but a transmission of guarded secrets between a former rabbi and an ambitious young count.

The scale of what Mithridates produced for Pico is staggering. Wirszubski identifies four manuscripts in the Vatican Library as Mithridates' autograph translations: Codices Vaticani Ebraici 189, 190, 191, and Codex Chigi A.VI.190. In their present state of preservation these manuscripts amount to nearly 3,500 pages in folio. Wirszubski estimates losses at well over 2,000 additional pages; a whole large manuscript described by Jacques Gaffarel in the seventeenth century has not been recovered. The extant corpus represents a translation enterprise roughly the size of Justinian's *Digest* — and it was produced in a matter of months, probably between May and November 1486, with Mithridates working "two gatherings a day" (according to one of his marginal notes). The texts translated include works by Eleazar of Worms, Abraham Abulafia (the *Liber Redemptionis*, the *De Secretis Legis*, the *Summa Brevis Cabale*), Joseph Gicatilla (*Portae Iustitiae* = *Sha'are Sedeq*), Rabbi Azriel of Gerona (*Quaestiones super Decem Numerationibus*), the anonymous *Bahir*, the *Liber de Radicibus seu Terminis Cabalae* (*Sefer ha-Sorasim*), and Menahem Recanati's *Commentary on the Pentateuch* (now lost in translation but described by Gaffarel). Each of these texts represents a different strand or period of Kabbalistic development; together they gave Pico the widest panorama of Kabbalistic material that any non-Jew had ever possessed in translated form.

Wirszubski's central methodological contribution is the systematic comparison of Mithridates' Latin translations with the surviving Hebrew originals. This comparison reveals, with scholarly precision that is difficult to contest, that Mithridates interpolated his own material into the texts at many points. Some interpolations are personal — comments addressed to Pico in the second person, marginal notes about Mithridates' own life, his enmity with Innocent VIII, his sexual preoccupations with a young companion. The tone of these personal notes ranges from warmly collegial ("O Pice") to savagely satirical (Innocent VIII is repeatedly referred to as "Malfecta" — a corruption of "Melfitensis," his former title as Cardinal of Benevento — and is likened to an ass unworthy of the secrets being translated). Other interpolations are doctrinal, and these are the most consequential for the history of Christian Kabbalah.

The doctrinal interpolations follow a consistent pattern: they insert Christian theological categories — *trinitas*, the figure of Christ, the identity of *Ze* with Jesus, the equation of Maimonides with a Kabbalist, the suggestion that the apostles were familiar with Kabbalah — into texts where the Hebrew originals contain no such references. In the *Summa Brevis Cabalae* (a letter by Abulafia translated in Cod. Vat. Ebr. 190, fol. 125v), Mithridates inserts the remark that "there are Christians who are founded on this science imperfectly, although their founders knew it perfectly" — a sentence with no counterpart in the Hebrew manuscript tradition of Abulafia's epistle, which Wirszubski was able to verify against multiple surviving Hebrew copies. In the translation of Abulafia's *De Secretis Legis*, Mithridates interpolates the Cusan principle "quodlibet est in quolibet" (every thing is in every other thing) — derived from Nicholas of Cusa's *De Docta Ignorantia*, Chapter V — into a passage of Abulafia's explanation of his letter-combinatory method, thereby manufacturing a convergence between Jewish ecstatic Kabbalah and one of the most significant Christian theological-philosophical texts of the fifteenth century (Wirszubski 1989, 100–108).

The case of the Hebrew pronoun *ze* (זה, "this/he") illustrates the subtlety and cumulative effect of Mithridates' interventions. In Kabbalistic texts, particularly in Gicatilla's *Portae Iustitiae*, *ze* functions as a symbolic name for *Yesod*, the ninth sefirah. In the Latin translation, Mithridates systematically renders or leaves untranslated *ze* in ways that make it function as a symbol pointing beyond Yesod toward a Christological referent — exploiting the phonetic proximity of *ze* to *Jesu* and the long tradition, traceable back to the *Pugio Fidei* of Raymundus Martini, of reading *ze* as a prefiguration of Christ. Pico's twenty-first Kabbalistic thesis *secundum opinionem propriam* draws directly on this reading: "Whoever will combine the saying of the Kabbalists that the numeration called just and redeemer is also called *Ze*, with the saying of the Talmudists that Isaac went like *Ze* carrying his cross, will see that what was prefigured in Isaac was fulfilled in Christ, who was the true God sold for silver." The Kabbalistic text Pico has read was already a Christianized text before he encountered it.

The most elaborate example of Mithridates' interventions concerns his treatment of Abulafia's commentary on Maimonides' *Guide of the Perplexed*, translated as the *Liber Redemptionis* (Cod. Chigi). Wirszubski shows that Mithridates transformed Abulafia's text by systematic interpolation from a work in which Abulafia discusses Maimonidean philosophy in mystical-Kabbalistic terms into a work in which Maimonides himself appears to be the source of the Kabbalistic interpretations — as though Maimonides were a secret Kabbalist who revealed his esoteric teaching to disciples. The interpolations in the *De Secretis Legis* (Cod. Vat. Ebr. 190) make the same argument more explicitly: they insert the claim "quod etiam in libro More Cabala est" ("that even in the Book of the Guide there is Kabbalah"), a statement flatly absent from the oldest Hebrew manuscripts of Abulafia's text. Pico's sixty-third Kabbalistic thesis *secundum opinionem propriam* reflects this interpolated image directly: "Just as Aristotle concealed the more divine philosophy which the ancient philosophers veiled in fables and apologues, beneath the face of philosophical speculation and the brevity of his words, so Rabbi Moses the Egyptian in the book called by the Latins *Dux Neutrorum* [Guide of the Perplexed], while through the superficial bark of words he seems to walk with the philosophers, through the latent intelligences of the profound meaning he embraces the mysteries of Kabbalah."

Wirszubski also documents the gematric jokes that Mithridates embedded in his translations — the equation of Mithridates' own Hebrew name (Shmuel, numerically equivalent to *Adonai Nikra*, "he is called YHVH") with the divine Tetragrammaton, and the equation of Pico's name (Giovanni = Yohanan, numerically equivalent through the substitution cipher *atbash* to *Adonai Elohim*). These appear in the Gicatilla translation (Cod. Chigi, fol. 44r): "Post *xem* [*Shem*] vere aliquid et ad Tiphereth... ad similitudinem Adonai Elohim scriptorum per has literas ΠΙ*?Κ ΓΠΓΡן (*Mithridatis et Pici*)." The Tetragrammaton belongs to Mithridates; Pico gets only *Adonai Elohim* — a distinction Wirszubski notes with dry irony: "*proximus sed longo intervallo*." Later Mithridates demotes Pico even from *Elohim* to *ish* (man), on the grounds that Pico could not stay away from women — a reference to the notorious Arezzo elopement of May 1486 with Margherita, the wife of Giuliano Mariotto de' Medici. The fact that Mithridates was translating during and immediately after this scandal is recorded in the manuscripts themselves: references to Pico's love affair are interspersed throughout the translations as chronological markers, enabling Wirszubski to date the composition of the manuscripts with considerable precision.

The letter to Ermolao Barbaro of 30 May 1492, preserved in the 1557 Opera Omnia and now translated in PicoDB, provides another document of Pico's engagement with Hebrew learning mediated through "a certain Sicilian Jew" (*Siculus quidam Hebraeus* — that is, Mithridates, though this letter appears to describe a later encounter with another individual bringing Hebrew volumes). Pico writes that he has been "immersed without ceasing for a full week, day and night" in Hebrew volumes brought by a man who intends to leave within twenty days, leaving Pico barely enough time to work through them all. The urgency of the timeline — the translator present in person, the manuscripts flowing — recapitulates the dynamics that Wirszubski documents for the 1486 campaign of translation: Mithridates was physically present with Pico, translating and teaching simultaneously, in a compressed and intense collaboration.

Giulio Busi's subsequent work, including the Einaudi volume *Giovanni Pico della Mirandola: Mito, magia, Qabbalah* (2014, co-authored with Raphael Ebgi), builds on Wirszubski's foundation while extending it to consider Pico's engagement with Kabbalah as more than purely a textual matter — as a form of operative knowledge that Pico was genuinely attempting to inhabit. But Busi's more recent critical editions and studies at the Freie Universität Berlin (the Kabbalistic manuscripts project) also deepen the Wirszubskian picture by making the Mithridates translations available in critical editions that allow further comparison with the Hebrew originals. The historiographical implications of this ongoing work remain significant: virtually every claim Pico makes about what Kabbalah "teaches" concerning Christian doctrine must be evaluated against the question of whether the teaching was present in the Hebrew text or was manufactured in the act of translation.

The figure of Mithridates thus raises what is perhaps the most fundamental historiographical question in Pico studies: to what extent did Pico encounter Jewish mysticism at all, as opposed to a Christianized simulacrum of Jewish mysticism produced by a convert with theological investments and a talent for interpolation? Wirszubski's answer is carefully calibrated. He does not reduce Pico's project to mere appropriation of manufactured convergences. He acknowledges that Pico was capable of original thought within the Kabbalistic framework, that his synthesis of sefirot-doctrine with Platonic and scholastic philosophy was genuinely his own contribution, and that his bipartite division of Kabbalah into *scientia sephiroth* and *scientia semot* reflects a real engagement with Abulafia's text even if filtered through translation. But he insists, and the manuscript evidence supports him, that the specific Christological claims Pico extracts from Kabbalistic sources — above all the insertion of *shin* into the Tetragrammaton to produce *Yeheshua*, the equation of *Ze* with Christ, the Trinity in the ten *sefirot* — were enabled by Mithridates' interventions rather than by anything present in authentic Kabbalistic tradition.

For the purposes of PicoDB, Mithridates functions not merely as a biographical entry but as a methodological warning: any claim Pico makes about Kabbalistic sources must be traced, wherever possible, through the known translations to the identified Hebrew originals. This is the methodology Wirszubski pioneered, and it remains the gold standard for Pico Kabbalah scholarship. The figure of Mithridates stands at the threshold between Pico's published Kabbalistic theses and the Hebrew texts that ostensibly underlie them — a threshold that, on close inspection, turns out to be far more permeable, and more theologially loaded, than it appears.
