The Tetragrammaton — the four-letter divine name יהוה (*Yod He Vav He*, YHVH) — stands at the theological and philological center of Pico della Mirandola's Kabbalistic project. No other single concept in Pico's engagement with Hebrew mysticism carries more weight, generates more controversy, or connects more directly to the larger history of Christian Kabbalah than his interpretation of this name. To read what Pico says about the Tetragrammaton is to encounter the most audacious and technically elaborate claim in the *900 Conclusions* of 1486: that Kabbalistic letter-doctrine, properly applied, demonstrates from within the Hebrew mystical tradition itself that the divine name encodes the name of Jesus.

In Jewish tradition, the Tetragrammaton is the most sacred of all divine names — so sacred that it is not pronounced in synagogue reading, where it is systematically replaced by *Adonai* (אדני, "Lord"). In the Temple period, the High Priest pronounced it once a year on Yom Kippur in the innermost sanctuary. The name itself communicates the divine essence more directly than any other designation: where names like *El* or *Elohim* describe God's power and *Shaddai* his sufficiency, YHVH — typically interpreted in connection with the verb *hayah* (to be) — designates the self-subsistent, uncaused being that is the ground of all existence. The linguistic philosophy this entails is not an accident but the foundation of an entire Kabbalistic ontology.

Within the theosophical Kabbalah that flowered in thirteenth-century Castile and that furnished most of Pico's identifiable sources, the Tetragrammaton is primarily associated with the sixth *sefirah*, *Tiferet* (Beauty or Glory), the central node of the sefirotic system and the principle of divine mercy that mediates between the higher triad and the lower emanations. Yet this identification does not exhaust the name's significance. Kabbalistic literature from the *Bahir* and the *Sefer Yetzirah* through the *Zohar* and the writings of Joseph Gicatilla establishes a dense network of correspondences in which the four letters of the Tetragrammaton map onto multiple structural divisions of reality simultaneously. *Yod* corresponds to *Hokmah* (Wisdom, the second sefirah) and to the world of *Atziluth* (Emanation); the first *He* to *Binah* (Understanding) and the world of *Beriah* (Creation); *Vav* to the six lower sefirot from *Hesed* through *Yesod* and to the world of *Yetzirah* (Formation); the final *He* to *Malkhut* (Kingdom/Shekhinah) and the world of *Assiyah* (Action). The four letters are therefore not merely a name but a structural diagram of the entire process of divine emanation and its four-fold ontological stratification. This is the context that gives Pico's use of the Tetragrammaton its full weight: he is not simply invoking a sacred word but engaging a philosophical system in which the structure of the divine name is isomorphic with the structure of being.

Gicatilla's *Sha'are Orah* (Gates of Light), translated for Pico by Mithridates and surviving in the Codex Chigi A.VI.190, provides the most sustained account of this mapping available to Pico. Gicatilla's text proceeds systematically from the tenth *sefirah* upward, showing at each stage how the proper divine name associated with each *sefirah* grants access to that aspect of the divine, and how improper or ignorant address — calling on God by a name inappropriate to the aspect being invoked — is spiritually inefficacious. The climax of the ascent is reached when the practitioner is able to address *Tiferet* by its proper name, the Tetragrammaton, and thereby enter the innermost gate. Gicatilla writes that "the secret of this holy gate, which Jacob merited to grasp, is the secret of the tenth numeration, which is called *Shekinah*" — but the ultimate destination is the Tetragrammaton, YHVH, through which all the *sefirot* are united in their source.

Pico's sixth Kabbalistic thesis *secundum opinionem propriam* (according to his own opinion) applies this architecture to the Christian Trinity: "Whoever is profound in the science of Kabbalah can understand that the three great four-lettered names of God which appear in the secrets of the Kabbalists ought to be attributed to the three persons of the Trinity by a wonderful appropriation, so that the name Ehyeh (אהיה) belongs to the Father, the name YHVH (יהוה) to the Son, and the name Adonai (אדני) to the Holy Spirit." The hierarchical assignment of Ehyeh to the Father corresponds to the standard equation of the first *sefirah*, *Keter* (Crown), with the highest and most hidden aspect of divinity. The assignment of the Tetragrammaton to the Son, associating YHVH with *Tiferet*, exploits the widespread Kabbalistic identification of *Tiferet* as a mediating, masculine principle — the son of *Hokmah* and *Binah*, the bridegroom of the Shekhinah. Wirszubski (1989, 167) analyzes the structure of this thesis carefully and notes that what makes it possible within Pico's system is the prior equation of the Tetragrammaton with the second person of the Trinity, an equation without which his symbolic use of letter-combination as a vehicle of Trinitarian interpretation would be inconceivable.

The most technically charged and historiographically consequential of all Pico's Tetragrammatic theses is the fourteenth: "By the letter *Shin* (ש) which mediates in the name of Jesus, it is Kabbalistically signified that the world rested perfectly, as in its perfection, when *Yod* was joined with *Vav*, which was done in Christ who was the true Son of God and man." This thesis encodes the argument that inserting the Hebrew letter *shin* into the Tetragrammaton — between the first *He* and *Vav* — transforms YHVH (יהוה) into YHŠVH (יהשוה), a form vocalizable as *Yeheshua*, the Hebrew name of Jesus (ישוע). The letter *shin*, which in Jewish mystical tradition represents the primordial fire and the divine breath (*ruah*), thereby becomes the operative element that, when inserted into the divine name, transforms the unpronounceable name of God into the pronounceable name of the Messiah. This is not Pico's invention: the identification of *Yeheshua* as the Tetragrammaton expanded by *shin* appears in earlier converso literature and in Mithridates' own *Sermo de Passione Domini*. But Pico gives it its most precise and most philosophically serious formulation.

The fifteenth thesis reinforces the argument: "By the name *Yod He Vav He*, which is the ineffable name, which the Kabbalists say will be the name of the Messiah, it is clearly known that he will be God, Son of God, made man by the Holy Spirit, and that after him the Paraclete will descend upon men for the perfection of the human race." The talmudic tradition that the Messiah will bear the name YHVH — derived from Jeremiah 23:6, where the righteous king is called "YHVH our righteousness" (*Yahveh tzidkenu*) — is here read as a Kabbalistic proof of Christ's divine nature: if the Messiah's name is the Tetragrammaton, and the Tetragrammaton in Kabbalistic mapping designates the Son, then the Messiah is the divine Son incarnate. The argument moves from a talmudic aggadah through the Kabbalistic sefirot-name correspondences to a Christological conclusion. As Wirszubski observes (1989, 161–68), this pattern of reasoning — using existing Jewish texts and methods but infusing them with Christian theological conclusions — is the defining characteristic of Pico's Christian Kabbalah, and the Tetragrammaton is its master example.

The condemned theses of 1487 included several touching directly on divine names and their Christological implications. Innocent VIII's bull of 4 August 1487 condemned seven theses outright as heretical and six as suspected; among the most contested was the Kabbalistic claim that magic and Kabbalah provide certainty about Christ's divinity. The *Apology*, composed in self-defense, devotes considerable space to explaining what Pico means by addressing God by the Tetragrammaton and related names. He insists that the operation of divine names is prayer in its highest form — the High Priest's pronunciation of the ineffable name in the Temple was itself an act of *operatio cabbalistica*, a sacred invocation that accomplished effects in the divine order. The distinction between legitimate invocation of divine names and condemned demonic magic is, for Pico, the distinction between names that are proper to God himself and names that belong to fallen spirits.

Wirszubski's detailed source study establishes that the Christological reading of the Tetragrammaton that Pico presents was not derived directly from authentic Kabbalistic sources but was transmitted through Mithridates' translations, which systematically interpolated Christianizing glosses. The transliteration of *ze* (זה) as a symbol of the ninth sefirah *Yesod* was used by Mithridates to suggest Christological readings: since *ze* could be read as pointing to the figure who carries his cross, a Christian reading of the Kabbalistic symbol as a prefiguration of Christ was built into the translation before Pico ever encountered the text. Mithridates also interpolated the word *trinitas* into passages where the Hebrew originals contain only the ten-fold unity of the *sefirot*, and he arranged isopsephic equations — calculating the numerical values of Hebrew letter-combinations — in ways designed to demonstrate that Pico and Mithridates himself bore the names of divine entities. The equation of Mithridates' Hebrew name (understood as Samuel, *Shmuel*) with the numerical value of *Adonai-Nikra* ("he is called YHVH"), and the equation of Pico's name (*Yohanan*) with the numerical value of *Adonai-Elohim*, appears in the margins of the Gicatilla translation (Cod. Chigi, fol. 44r) as a kind of Kabbalistic joke — but also as an index of how deeply Mithridates saturated the translations with name-numerological readings.

The significance of the Tetragrammaton in Pico extends beyond the specific theses of 1486 into the broader architecture of his encyclopedic project. The *Oration*, written as the proem for the projected disputation, places Kabbalah at the culminating stage of the philosopher's ascent: having passed through philosophy and the ancillary disciplines, the investigator must still — to complete the journey — ascend through Kabbalistic knowledge of the divine names. The Tetragrammaton is the summit of this hierarchy, the name above all names, and its Kabbalistic interpretation is what gives the *Oration*'s rhetoric of ascent its most dramatic content. The philosopher does not merely contemplate the divine name from outside; he enters into its structure and, through the Kabbalistic operation on its letters, participates in the divine architecture it designates.

The historiographical stakes of Pico's Tetragrammaton doctrine extend far beyond his own writings. Johannes Reuchlin's *De Verbo Mirifico* (1494) and *De Arte Cabalistica* (1517) — the foundational texts of early modern Christian Kabbalah — take up Pico's attribution of the Tetragrammaton to the Son and his reading of *Yeheshua* as the expanded divine name, developing them into a systematic philosophical program. Guillaume Postel, Pietro Galatino, and later figures in the sixteenth-century tradition of *Cabala Hebraeorum* all work within the framework that Pico's Tetragrammaton exegesis established. The trajectory from Pico through Reuchlin to the broader early modern Christian Kabbalistic tradition is one of the most consequential instances of the transmission of Kabbalistic ideas into European intellectual culture, and the Tetragrammaton — as the name that, once decoded by Kabbalistic method, was held to yield the name of Jesus — is the hinge on which that transmission turns.

For PicoDB, tracking this concept requires distinguishing between at least three levels of evidence: what the authentic Hebrew Kabbalistic sources actually say about the Tetragrammaton (established by comparing Pico's theses with the identified Hebrew originals of his sources); what Mithridates' translations said — including the interpolations that are demonstrably not present in the Hebrew (established by Wirszubski's manuscript work); and what Pico himself concludes and argues across the *900 Conclusions*, the *Apology*, and the *Oration*. The gap between these three levels is not incidental but constitutive: Pico's Christian reading of the Tetragrammaton was enabled by translations that had already been rendered Christian before he read them.
