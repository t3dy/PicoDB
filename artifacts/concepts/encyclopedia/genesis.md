# Genesis

The first book of the Bible — known in Hebrew as *Bereshit* (בְּרֵאשִׁית), from the opening word meaning "in the beginning," and in Greek as *Genesis* (Γένεσις), from the title given in the Septuagint — is the primary textual object that organizes Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's *Heptaplus* of 1489. It is not, however, Genesis as a whole that the *Heptaplus* expounds. The work is a commentary on a single verse — *bereshit bara Elohim et hashamayim ve'et ha'aretz* (Genesis 1:1), "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth" — and on the account of the six days of creation that follows from Genesis 1:2 through Genesis 1:27. The choice to concentrate on the hexaemeron, the six-day narrative, rather than on a broader sweep of Genesis, is integral to Pico's hermeneutical strategy, because the hexaemeron is precisely that part of the biblical text whose literary form — a numbered, structured sequence from formlessness to complexity, from water and void to the completed creation of humanity — lends itself most readily to the kind of sevenfold allegorical reading the *Heptaplus* enacts. But Genesis for Pico is not merely a convenient literary object. It is the text that Moses, the prophet who had ascended to the forty-nine gates of understanding on Mount Sinai, wrote for two audiences simultaneously: the unlearned, who received the literal narrative of cosmological beginnings, and the initiated, who perceived within it an encoded account of the entire cosmic hierarchy and the proper end of human intellectual life. This dual reception is the fundamental claim of the *Heptaplus*, and understanding it requires placing the text in the multiple exegetical contexts that Pico was simultaneously inhabiting and transforming.

The Latin tradition of Genesis commentary was rich, contentious, and technically demanding by the time Pico engaged with it. Augustine's *De Genesi ad litteram* had established the benchmark for patient, theologically rigorous engagement with the literal sense of the hexaemeron, and had demonstrated that the text resisted simple allegorization in the manner of Origen, who had largely abandoned the literal reading in favor of a spiritual or typological one. The medieval fourfold method — *littera gesta docet, quid credas allegoria, moralis quid agas, quo tendas anagogia*, "the letter teaches what happened, allegory what you are to believe, the moral sense what you are to do, the anagogical where you are headed" — codified a pluralist approach to biblical meaning that assigned different hermeneutical registers to the same text without abolishing the primacy of the literal sense. Nicholas of Lyra's *Postilla super totam Bibliam* (completed 1331), the most widely circulated commentary of the later medieval period and a major presence in the print culture of Pico's own day, exemplified the tradition of sober literal exposition supplemented by selected allegorical glosses. As Crofton Black documents, this fourfold tradition was still the dominant hermeneutical model when Pico was writing, and the *Heptaplus* was composed against the background of its pervasive authority — not to continue it but to replace it with something structurally different (Black 2006, 55–70).

Pico's *Heptaplus* does not use the fourfold method. He acknowledges in the *Apologia* (1487) that the standard Christian approach to biblical interpretation recognized four senses — "apud nos est quadruplex modus exponendi Bibliam, literalis, mysticus sive allegoricus, tropologicus et anagogicus" (*Opera omnia* 178) — but the *Heptaplus* neither applies these four senses to the Genesis hexaemeron nor presents itself as doing so. Instead, it substitutes a sevenfold structure of its own devising, grounded not in the inherited grammatical theology of the schools but in Pico's own philosophical cosmology and Kabbalistic hermeneutics. The seven expositions read the same hexaemeron seven times, each time through a different ontological register: the elemental world, the celestial world, the angelic world, the human world as microcosm, the worlds in their successive order, the affinities and correspondences among all the worlds, and finally *felicitas* — the happiness or blessedness that is the eternal return of created things to their divine source. This substitution is not merely methodological innovation. It reflects a fundamental disagreement with the dominant tradition about the nature of non-literal reading. The mainstream medieval consensus, following Augustine and Aquinas, treated allegorical reading as supplementary to and grounded in the literal sense: allegory could not be established without a firm literal foundation, because the literal sense was the divinely intended primary meaning of the text. Pico's position is that the literal sense of Genesis is a *cortex*, a shell, designed to protect the hidden truth (*medulla abdita*) from those not yet ready to receive it. The hidden meaning is not a supplement to the literal sense but its inner reality, and the relationship between the two is one of esotericism rather than supplementarity.

This esotericist understanding of Genesis is traced through three successive articulations in Pico's career by Black (2006, 97–144). The earliest form appears in the *Commento sopra una canzone d'amore* (c. 1486), where Pico defends poetic theology — the doctrine that the ancient poets concealed philosophical truths in mythological narrative — by appealing to the parallel practice of Moses. A second, more embattled form appears in the *Apologia* of 1487, written under the pressure of the Roman condemnation and designed to defend his theses by arguing that esoteric reading was a legitimate and ancient Christian practice, attested by Origen, Clement, and Dionysius the Areopagite. The *Heptaplus* represents the third and most elaborately argued form, in which the case for esoteric reading of Genesis is grounded in a comprehensive historical and theological argument drawing on Greek, Latin, Jewish, and Neoplatonic sources simultaneously. What is decisive about the *Heptaplus*'s position, as Black emphasizes, is that it does not merely assert that Genesis has hidden meanings but argues that Moses wrote the text *with the specific intention of concealing truths from the unworthy and revealing them to the initiated*. This social and semiotic distinction between exoteric and esoteric audiences is what makes Pico's Genesis hermeneutics structurally different from the mainstream medieval tradition, which never quite arrived at this intentionalist position because it always treated the literal sense as divinely given rather than strategically veiled.

The Jewish exegetical tradition offered Pico a richer and more heterogeneous engagement with Genesis than the Latin tradition provided, and one of the distinctive features of the *Heptaplus* is its use of Hebrew sources — primarily mediated through Flavius Mithridates's Latin translations — alongside the Christian commentators. The Kabbalistic reading of Genesis, which Pico had encountered most fully in Menahem Recanati's commentary on the Pentateuch and in Joseph Gikatilla's *Sha'are Orah* (*Portae Iustitiae*), treated the opening of the book not merely as the account of how the world was made but as a comprehensive encoding of the divine creative process as articulated through the structure of the sefirot. The opening verse — *bereshit bara Elohim et hashamayim ve'et ha'aretz* — was read, in a classic instance of sefirot exegesis preserved in Mithridates's translations and documented by Wirszubski (1989), as a compressed statement of the entire emanatory sequence: *bereshit* ("in the beginning") pointed to Hokhmah, divine Wisdom; *bara* ("created") to Keter, the Ancient of Days; *Elohim* ("God") to Binah, divine Understanding; and the subsequent terms to the remaining sefirot in order. The opening word of Genesis was not merely the introduction to a historical narrative but the encoded announcement of the divine structure that made creation possible. From within this reading, the hexaemeron was a Kabbalistic text, and each day of creation was a figure for a stage in the divine emanatory process rather than a calendar day in the temporal history of the world.

Pico does not deploy this sefirot reading explicitly in the *Heptaplus*. The sefirot, so prominently present in the Kabbalistic conclusions of the *900 Conclusions* (1486), are entirely absent from the vocabulary of the *Heptaplus*, replaced by the more philosophically neutral language of "worlds" and "orders." Black argues that this departure from explicit sefirot mapping represents a deliberate strategy of concealment, consistent with Pico's post-crisis esotericist stance: the Kabbalistic framework operates structurally and implicitly without being named (Black 2006, 140–144). What remains from the sefirot tradition is the principle of correspondence — the idea that the structure of the creative act is encoded within the text that narrates it, so that to read Genesis properly is to read the created order itself — and the conviction that Moses possessed, and encoded in his text, the complete knowledge of that structure. The *Heptaplus*'s claim that its seven expositions reveal what Moses hid is therefore at once a claim about the literary structure of Genesis and a claim about the nature of the divine creative act: both are hierarchically organized, both are simultaneously fully present in their parts, and both reward the kind of patient, systematic, sevenfold reading that the *Heptaplus* enacts.

The Renaissance exegetical context for Pico's Genesis reading extends beyond the specifically philosophical and Kabbalistic dimensions to include the humanist philological tradition with which Pico was in complex relation. The attention to Hebrew philology, associated with such figures as Lorenzo Valla, Ermolao Barbaro, and later Reuchlin, opened up new possibilities for reading the Hebrew text of Genesis rather than relying solely on the Latin Vulgate. Pico was aware of the importance of Hebrew for biblical interpretation — he had studied it, imperfectly but seriously, and he relied on Mithridates and on Johanan Alemanno for access to texts he could not read directly. But the *Heptaplus* is not a philological commentary in the humanist sense. It does not engage in source criticism, text comparison, or the kind of grammatical and lexical precision that humanist scholarship was beginning to demand of biblical commentators. As Black notes, in its neglect of these developing tools the *Heptaplus* was "already outdated before it was composed" as a work of biblical scholarship, and its philological deficiencies were apparent to contemporaries (Black 2006, 284). What Pico offered instead was something no humanist commentator could match: the integration of the Hebrew mystical tradition, the Greek Neoplatonic tradition, and the Latin theological tradition within a single hermeneutical framework capable of reading the same verse seven times without repetition.

The relation of Genesis to the other books of Pico's career is important for understanding how he positioned the *Heptaplus* within his larger project. The *900 Conclusions* of 1486 had stated his syncretic ambitions in the form of bare theses, each compressed and numbered, drawing on 22 source traditions and inviting debate in the scholastic mode. The syncretic system was stated but not demonstrated; the connections between the traditions were asserted but not argued. The *Heptaplus* reverses this approach: instead of proposing theses about all possible sources, Pico takes a single text — a single verse of a single chapter of a single book — and demonstrates that the entire architecture of the syncretic system is encoded within it. Genesis 1:1 becomes, in the *Heptaplus*'s reading, a mandala of the whole philosophical tradition: the Neoplatonic doctrine of the three worlds, the Kabbalistic doctrine of the sefirot (without naming them), the Aristotelian categories of cause and relation (deployed in the sixth exposition), and the Christian eschatology of *felicitas* are all simultaneously present in "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth." The single verse contains all the wisdom that the *900 Conclusions* had assembled from twenty-two traditions; Genesis is the text of which all the other texts are commentaries.

Black's analysis of the *Heptaplus* in relation to its exegetical context establishes clearly that Pico's most important departure from the Latin tradition is not his philosophical system but his theory of allegory. The fourfold method treated allegory as a mode of reading that supplemented the literal sense: the letter still had its value, and allegorical reading was constrained by the literal meaning it supplemented. Pico treats allegory as anagogy in the Pseudo-Dionysian sense: a mode of reading that leads upward along the ontological hierarchy, not merely revealing a hidden doctrinal meaning but actually effecting the intellectual ascent of the reader. To read Genesis through the *Heptaplus*'s seven expositions is not merely to learn what Genesis means; it is to undergo a structured intellectual ascent through the levels of creation, from the elemental through the celestial to the angelic and finally toward the divine. The hermeneutical process is itself transformative, continuous with the spiritual and intellectual ascent that Pico had described in the *Oration* as the defining capacity of the human being. Reading Genesis, properly done, is what human beings are for.

Ficino, the most prominent philosophical presence in the Florentine intellectual world of Pico's period, produced no Genesis commentary. His *Platonic Theology* (1469–74) deals with the soul's immortality and its relationship to the created order, but it does not engage the hexaemeron as a hermeneutical object. Pico's *Heptaplus* is consequently distinctive in the Italian Renaissance for combining a serious, if unorthodox, engagement with Genesis as Scripture with a syncretic philosophical framework; and the Kabbalistic dimension — the claim that the Hebrew mystical tradition provides the deepest key to Genesis — made it more distinctive still. When the first *Opera omnia* of Pico was assembled by his nephew Gianfrancesco in Bologna in 1496, the *Heptaplus* was placed first in the collection, before the *Oration* and the *Apologia* and the philosophical treatises. For early modern readers, Pico therefore appeared first as a Genesis commentator, a reader of Moses — which is, from within the logic of the *Heptaplus*, exactly what Pico would have wanted.
