Astral magic (*magia sideralis*, or what the tradition more broadly calls celestial magic) designates the specific sub-discipline of operative philosophy that works through the powers of the celestial bodies as causes of sublunary effects. It occupies a middle position in the hierarchy of magical practice that Pico della Mirandola constructs in the *900 Conclusions* and defends in the *Oration* and *Apology*: above the natural magic of hidden sympathies in herbs, stones, and characters, and below the Kabbalistic-angelic magic that operates at the level of the divine names and the emanated structure of the godhead. Understanding Pico's complex and ultimately divided relationship to astral magic requires tracing the theoretical foundations of the concept, attending carefully to his engagement with Ficino's *De vita coelitus comparanda*, and taking seriously the tension between his early endorsement and his later demolition of celestial influence as a philosophical concept.

The theoretical basis of astral magic draws on three converging intellectual traditions. Stoic cosmology, particularly the doctrine of *sympatheia* (*συμπάθεια*) — the mutual affection and resonance of all parts of the cosmos — establishes the metaphysical ground for any form of celestial influence: if the cosmos is a unified living organism, then changes in one part must produce corresponding changes in every other part, and the motions of the heavens must continuously affect terrestrial events. Ptolemaic mathematical astronomy provides the empirical framework within which specific celestial influences can be tracked, predicted, and exploited: the positions of planets in zodiacal signs, their aspects to one another, their distances from the horizon, and related computational data define the qualities and quantities of celestial influence at any given moment. Neoplatonic emanationism completes the picture by explaining *why* the celestial bodies radiate power: they are themselves expressions of higher intelligible realities — planetary intelligences, angelic minds, the World Soul — and the light and influence they shed downward into the sublunary world is the material trace of a continuous procession from the One through mind and soul into matter. On this Neoplatonic account, to attract celestial influence is in some sense to attract the intelligible reality that the celestial body mediates, and the practice of astral magic shades imperceptibly into a form of spiritual ascent.

Marsilio Ficino's *De vita coelitus comparanda* (1489), the third book of his medical compilation *De vita libri tres*, represents the most systematic and philosophically elaborated account of astral magic in Pico's immediate intellectual environment. Ficino's text gives a physician-philosopher's account of how the practitioner can draw down (*attrahere*) solar, Jovian, or Venusian influences through appropriate material configurations: specific herbs, incense, stones, colors, musical modes, and talismanic images that share the formal properties of the desired celestial body and thus function as receptive channels through which celestial influence flows. The mediating substance in Ficino's system is the *spiritus* — a subtle, luminous, and mobile pneumatic medium that pervades the cosmos and serves as the vehicle of celestial influence from the stars down to the sublunary world and into the body and soul of the practitioner. Ficino's *spiritus* is explicitly a Neoplatonic revision of medical pneumatology, drawing on Plotinus's *Enneads* (particularly 4.3–5), and Copenhaver has argued in a series of careful papers (Copenhaver 1987; Copenhaver 1988; Copenhaver 2015) that the sources of Ficino's magical pneumatology are more specifically Neoplatonic than Hermetic, contra the Yates thesis that made the *Corpus Hermeticum* the key generative text.

Pico's relationship to Ficino on this question is one of the most carefully studied problems in Pico scholarship, and Farmer's reconstruction of the chronology has significant implications for how it is understood. The *De vita coelitus comparanda* was not composed until approximately two and a half years after Pico introduced his own magical thought in the *900 Conclusions*, *Oration*, and *Apology*, which were all composed in the fall and winter of 1486–1487 (Farmer 1998, 118). During that period, relations between Pico and Ficino were at a historically low point, as evidenced by Pico's pointed criticisms of Ficino in the *Commento* and the *900 Conclusions*. The traditional picture of Pico simply adopting Ficino's natural and astral magic and adding Kabbalistic superstructure to it misrepresents the intellectual relationship and obscures the extent to which Pico's magical thought is independently developed, even where it converges with Ficino's.

In the *Commento alla Canzone d'amore*, Pico's earliest extended philosophical work, written in Italian before the Roman disputation, astral magic appears embedded in a Neoplatonic cosmological framework that is already notably independent of Ficino. Pico attributes to God, to the First Intelligence, and to the World Soul the virtues of Caelus, Saturn, and Jupiter respectively — a mapping of Neoplatonic hypostases onto planetary characteristics that draws on Lactantius's account of Plotinus's divine triad (Akopyan 2021, 12–13). The eight celestial spheres are characterized as animated substances, each with its own character and influence: Venus moderates the destructive influence of Mars; Jupiter moderates the malice of Saturn. The *Commento* presents a cosmos in which celestial influence flows continuously downward from animated planetary intelligences through ten spheres — seven planets, the fixed stars, a ninth rational soul-sphere, and an immovable tenth empyrean — into the sublunary world. This is a fully operative theoretical framework for astral magic, even though the *Commento* does not develop its practical implications in detail.

The *900 Conclusions* give the most technically structured statement of Pico's early position on astral influence. The magic theses, as Farmer has shown (Farmer 1998, 115–32), are organized to distinguish three ascending layers of operative knowledge — natural sympathy, astral influence, and Kabbalistic invocation — and to argue that all three are licit. The theses *secundum opinionem Ficini* — the conclusions Pico presents according to Ficino's opinion — are especially significant for astral magic: they suggest that Pico accepts the broad theoretical framework of celestial influence while holding his own views in some tension with Ficino's specific implementation of that framework. The fact that Pico presents these theses *secundum Ficinum* rather than *secundum opinionem propriam* may reflect a deliberate distancing: Pico endorses the framework but does not fully commit to Ficino's particular celestial medicine.

Among the theses Pico presents in his own voice are several that push toward a specifically Kabbalistic account of celestial influence. The *occultae virtutes* of sublunary things correspond not only to planetary configurations but to the structure of the divine emanations (*sefirot*) as understood in the Kabbalistic tradition. This move is philosophically significant: it subordinates the Ptolemaic-astrological account of celestial influence to a metaphysical account in which the real causes of sublunary effects are the divine names and emanations, of which the visible celestial bodies are merely the material expressions. Astral magic, on this reading, is not ultimately about the planets as physical bodies but about the intelligible realities the planets symbolize and mediate.

The *Heptaplus* (1489) represents an important intermediate position in Pico's evolving stance on astral influence. Written in the same year as Ficino's *De vita coelitus comparanda*, the *Heptaplus* presents a detailed Kabbalistic-Christian reading of the Genesis creation narrative in which the ten-sphere cosmology of the *Commento* reappears but now grounded in Hebrew sources. The highest sphere, the empyrean, is the unique origin of light that diffuses through the ninth sphere into all the lower elements. This vivifying, incorporeal light — continuous with the intelligible light of the Good — is the ultimate source of celestial influence (Akopyan 2021, 23–25). Significantly, the *Heptaplus* explicitly rejects the use of astrological talismans: "Let us not form images of stars in metals" (*Heptaplus* 2.7). This rejection, which appears to target Ficino's practice directly (Ficino alludes to the *Heptaplus* in the *De vita coelitus comparanda*, suggesting he was aware of the criticism), marks a boundary within Pico's own system: astral influence as a theoretical phenomenon is real, but the specific operative practice of drawing it down through images inscribed in metals belongs to the category of superstitious rather than licit natural philosophy.

The *Expositiones in Psalmos* (1491–1492), an unfinished commentary on the Psalms, further develops Pico's account of celestial light and its philosophical problems. In his exposition of Psalm 18 (*Coeli enarrant gloriam Dei*), Pico postulates two Suns — an intellectual Sun or the Good, and a natural Sun that receives its virtues from the intelligible one — and argues that the divine light penetrates everything through the mediation of the natural Sun without losing its incorporeal essence. At the same time, Pico finds himself in a philosophical difficulty that Akopyan has clarified (Akopyan 2021, 27–28): the reconciliation of Neoplatonic light (which remains incorporeal throughout the process of emanation) with Aristotelian-scholastic physics (which requires the transformation of celestial influence as it comes into contact with matter) proves philosophically intractable. The failure to achieve this reconciliation, Akopyan argues, is one of the principal philosophical motivations for the radical revision of Pico's account of celestial influence in the *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem*.

The *Disputationes* (1493–1494, posthumously published 1496) presents the most acute methodological problem for interpreting Pico's relationship to astral magic. In the twelve-book treatise, Pico does not simply critique judicial astrology (the prediction of future events from horoscopes) while accepting the theoretical framework of celestial influence: he undertakes a systematic critique of the physical mechanisms through which celestial influence is supposed to operate. In Book III, the philosophically central section of the work, Pico analyzes light, motion, and heat in Aristotelian terms and concludes that the proposed mechanisms of celestial influence — direct irradiation of sublunary matter by celestial light — are physically incoherent, or at best limited to specific physical phenomena such as tides and the regulation of seasons, which do not require the rich scheme of planetary influences that astrology presupposes (Akopyan 2021, 32–33). This Aristotelian critique of the physical mechanism is philosophically continuous with the impasse Pico reached in the *Expositiones in Psalmos*: having failed to reconcile Neoplatonic emanation with Aristotelian physics, he resolves the tension by abandoning the Neoplatonic framework and working within Aristotle's categories, which provide no support for the kind of specific, qualitatively differentiated celestial influence that astral magic requires.

The question of whether this represents a genuine philosophical evolution or a more radical break has been central to Pico scholarship since Eugenio Garin's foundational Giovanni Pico della Mirandola: vita e dottrina. Farmer argued for substantial discontinuity, and raised the additional question of posthumous tampering: Gianfrancesco Pico della Mirandola, the nephew-editor who published the *Disputationes*, had his own anti-syncretic, Savonarolan agenda, and the possibility that he altered his uncle's text in the direction of greater hostility to astrology cannot be excluded (Farmer 1998, 137–76). Akopyan's systematic analysis of the *Disputationes* reaches a different conclusion: the methodological approach of the *Disputationes* — deconstructing the tradition of authorities to expose its internal contradictions and mistranslations — is continuous with the approach Pico uses in the *De ente et uno*, and the treatise in all likelihood represents Pico's own mature philosophical position (Akopyan 2021, 37–38). Copenhaver has characterized the *Disputationes* as showing that Pico was not a simple defender of magical practice but was working throughout his career to distinguish legitimate from illegitimate forms of celestial influence (Copenhaver 2019, passim).

The implications of this reading for astral magic specifically are significant. What Pico rejects in the *Disputationes* is not celestial influence as such — he allows that celestial bodies affect tides, seasons, temperatures, and other general physical phenomena — but the claim that this influence is specific enough and rich enough to ground the practice of judicial astrology or to sustain the full apparatus of planetary sympathies that astral magic presupposes. The *spiritus* as a vehicle of qualitatively differentiated celestial influence, the planetary intelligences as quasi-personal sources of specific powers, the correspondence between specific celestial configurations and specific sublunary outcomes: these are what Pico attacks, because they cannot be grounded in Aristotelian natural philosophy and therefore fall outside the domain of legitimate natural knowledge. Astral magic, understood as the practical exploitation of these specific correspondences, is thus delegitimized not primarily on theological grounds but on natural-philosophical ones.

This trajectory from enthusiastic appropriation of the theoretical framework of astral magic in the *Commento* and *900 Conclusions*, through the selective restrictions of the *Heptaplus*, to the systematic demolition of its physical foundations in the *Disputationes*, constitutes one of the most intellectually coherent and philosophically ambitious arcs in Pico's thinking. It is a development driven not primarily by theological pressure or Savonarolan influence, though those factors are present, but by an internal philosophical problem — the impossibility of coherently combining Neoplatonic and Aristotelian accounts of how the heavens affect the earth — that Pico recognized and ultimately resolved by choosing Aristotle over Plato.
