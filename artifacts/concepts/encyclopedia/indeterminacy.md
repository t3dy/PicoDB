The doctrine of ontological indeterminacy — the claim that the human being has been constituted without a fixed nature, seat, or form within the cosmic hierarchy — is the most famous and most persistently misunderstood philosophical assertion in Pico della Mirandola's *Oration*. It has served as the foundation for the modern legend of Pico as the herald of Renaissance individualism and the prophet of human dignity; it has also, in the hands of more rigorous scholarship, turned out to be a technical thesis in metaphysical anthropology whose proper context is the Neoplatonic-Kabbalistic cosmology that the *Oration* presupposes rather than explains. To understand the doctrine of indeterminacy correctly is to understand why Pico's text has generated such powerful misreadings, and why the philosophical content of those misreadings differs so profoundly from what Pico actually wrote.

The central passage appears in the earliest section of the *Oration* (I.5–6), where Pico constructs a speech addressed by God to Adam at the moment of creation. The passage deserves careful attention in Pico's Latin. God tells Adam: "We have given you, O Adam, no fixed seat (*sedem certam*), no form of your own peculiar to yourself (*faciem propriam*), no function prescribed (*munus peculiare*), so that according to your longing and your own judgment you may have and possess whatever seat, form, and functions you yourself shall desire. The nature of all other things is determined and constrained within the laws we have prescribed; you, constrained by no limits, shall determine it for yourself, in accordance with your own free will (*tuo arbitrio*), in whose power I have placed you." God continues: "You, like a free and sovereign craftsman (*quasi arbitrarius honorariusque plastes*), may mold and fashion yourself into whatever form you prefer. You can degenerate into the lower forms of life, which are brutish; you can, by your own choice, rise to the higher forms, which are divine" (Oration, in Copenhaver 2019, 459).

The passage has a specific philosophical target: the doctrine of the Platonic Ideas as fixed exemplars of every created kind. As the entry on Ideas in this encyclopedia discusses, in Pico's Neoplatonic cosmos every kind of being has its *idea* — its eternal exemplar in the divine angelic intellect through which it is constituted as what it is. The *idea* of the angel fixes the angel to its angelic nature and station; the *idea* of the animal fixes the animal to its mode of sensitive existence. This is the metaphysical framework within which Pico's claim about human indeterminacy must be understood: the human being alone has not been assigned a fixed *idea* that locks it into a single level of the cosmic hierarchy. Every other kind of being has a nature prescribed by its exemplar in the divine mind (*mentes angelicae*); the human alone has been given a nature that consists precisely in having no such prescription.

It is crucial to be precise about what this indeterminacy means and does not mean. It does not mean that the human being has no nature at all; it does not mean that the human being is free to be anything in a morally or practically unconstrained sense; it does not mean that Pico is anticipating Sartrean existentialism or Kantian autonomy. Copenhaver has documented with exhaustive care the anachronism involved in reading any of these modern frameworks back into Pico's text (Copenhaver 2019, esp. 128–57 and 339–69). The modern concept of human dignity as autonomous self-determination is Kantian in origin, and Kant was writing three centuries after Pico. The existentialist celebration of radical freedom as the human being's defining feature belongs to the twentieth century. Copenhaver's blunt verdict is unambiguous: "Pico never wrote an Oration about human dignity. Instead, he drafted (but never delivered) a speech to promote ascetic mysticism — a way of life incompatible with human dignity, as modern thinkers (including experts on Pico) have used the word 'dignity' in English and other vernaculars" (Copenhaver 2019, 1–2).

The positive content of the indeterminacy claim is more precisely stated as follows: the human being is the one entity in the cosmic hierarchy that is not locked by its constitutive Form into a single level of that hierarchy, and this is a precondition — not a description of, but a *condition of possibility* for — the ascent program that the *Oration* is designed to introduce and promote. The *Oration* is a protreptic text — a speech designed to exhort and invite — addressed to an audience of philosophers who would gather in Rome to debate the *900 Conclusions*. It is not a philosophical treatise but a rhetorical performance whose philosophical claims are compressed and allusive, presupposing rather than arguing for the full metaphysical framework they depend on. The indeterminacy claim is the rhetorical centerpiece of this protreptic, the reason Pico gives for why philosophers should care about pursuing the kind of syncretic philosophical-mystical ascent that the *900 Conclusions* map out in their nine hundred theses.

Howlett's analysis provides the clearest account of why the indeterminacy claim functions as a philosophical thesis about the precondition for ascent rather than a celebration of freedom (Howlett 2021, 173–209). Pico's cosmological scheme, as laid out primarily in the *Heptaplus* and the *Commento*, divides the created universe into three worlds: the angelic or intellectual world, the celestial world, and the elemental or natural world. Each world has its characteristic beings, its characteristic modes of existence, and its characteristic relationship to the divine. Beings fixed to any one of these levels by their constitutive Form can participate in the divine only in the manner appropriate to that level. Angels can know God as their constitutive Idea is constituted by the divine intellect; animals can desire what is good for them instinctively; plants can grow toward the light. But none of these beings can *traverse* the levels — cannot move from the natural to the celestial to the angelic in the way that philosophical ascent requires.

The human being's indeterminacy is precisely the feature that makes such traversal possible. If the human had a fixed *idea* that constituted it as a natural creature, it could not ascend to the celestial mode of existence through mathematical-astronomical contemplation; if it had a fixed *idea* that constituted it as a celestial being, it could not descend to purify itself through the ethical-practical disciplines; if it had a fixed *idea* that constituted it as an angelic being, it could not take on flesh in the natural world at all. The formlessness — the absence of a constitutive exemplary Form — is what enables the soul to take on each of these modes of existence in turn, to become in effect the kind of entity appropriate to each level as it ascends through the hierarchy. The human being can become brute, celestial, or angelic not because it has no nature but because its nature is, uniquely, to be capable of assimilating itself to any nature through the appropriate forms of practice.

Pico maps this program onto a series of figures and authorities in the *Oration*. The figure of Enoch, who "walked with God" and was transformed into the angel Metatron in Kabbalistic tradition (Wirszubski 1989, 125–33), exemplifies the ascent from the human to the angelic. The figure of Jacob's ladder — interpreted by Pico through Kabbalistic and Neoplatonic lenses simultaneously as the structure of the cosmic hierarchy — describes the path the philosopher must traverse. The "cherubic life" of intellectual purification, the "seraphic life" of ecstatic love, and the "priestly life" of the angelic minister describe the modes of existence the ascending soul progressively inhabits (Copenhaver 2019, 360–70). Each of these is a mode of existence that is, for other beings, fixed and permanent; for the human being, each is a stage in a journey whose endpoint is mystical union with the divine. The human's indeterminacy is not a static fact about human nature but a dynamic potential — the potential to become, in practice, the kinds of being that others are by constitution.

The relationship between indeterminacy and free will (*liberum arbitrium*, or in the *Oration*, *arbitrium*) must be handled carefully. Pico does use the language of free will and choice in the passage, and later sections of the *Oration* invoke the Stoic language of moral self-determination. But, as both Copenhaver and Howlett emphasize, this should not be read as a proclamation of secular human autonomy in any recognizably modern sense. Pico's "free will" in the context of the *Oration* is the freedom of the soul to choose which mode of existence to pursue — brute, human, celestial, or angelic — through the appropriate philosophical and ascetic disciplines. It is a freedom that is directed toward a specific end: the mystical ascent toward God that the entire *Oration* is designed to promote. The choice that Pico's Adam is invited to make is not among arbitrary life-options but between degeneration downward (becoming like the brutes through the pursuit of sensory pleasure) and ascent upward (becoming like the angels through philosophical and mystical practice). This is a choice with a correct answer, and the *Oration* is transparently in the business of persuading its audience to choose correctly. Freedom here is freedom from the constraint of a fixed lower nature, not freedom from normative direction.

The historiographical story of how the indeterminacy passage came to be misread as a celebration of Renaissance humanism and modern human dignity is one that Copenhaver traces with remarkable thoroughness across five centuries of Pico's reception (2019, esp. 71–157). The short version is that Jacob Burckhardt's brief but electrifying discussion of Pico in *The Civilization of the Renaissance in Italy* (1860) — drawing on a tradition that had already been building through Tennemann's Kantian reading, Garin's existentialist reading, and others — crystallized the misreading into the cultural commonplace it has become. Burckhardt's Pico is a proto-modern who "discovered man," who first proclaimed the infinite perfectibility of the human subject, who stood at the dawn of the modern age. Copenhaver's verdict on this tradition is categorical: the Burckhardtian reading is not merely an anachronistic simplification but a wholesale misidentification of the *Oration*'s subject, audience, and purpose. "The main problem is that Pico never wrote an Oration about human dignity" (Copenhaver 2019, 2). The speech that Pico actually composed was not delivered (the Roman disputation was suppressed by papal intervention in 1487), was not given its famous title by Pico himself (the title was attached only posthumously), and was concerned from start to finish with the justification of Pico's syncretic magical-Kabbalistic-philosophical program rather than with the celebration of human freedom.

Farmer's analysis of the *900 Conclusions* (1998) connects the indeterminacy doctrine to the concordist project in a philosophically illuminating way. The human being's capacity to traverse all levels of the cosmic hierarchy is the experiential enactment of Pico's theoretical claim that all philosophical traditions contain elements of the same underlying truth. The philosopher who can ascend through the Platonic, Aristotelian, Kabbalistic, and Hermetic traditions — finding in each of them a partial expression of the divine wisdom that underlies all — is the human being whose indeterminacy enables it to inhabit each philosophical mode in turn without being wholly defined by any one of them. Pico's syncretism, in other words, is not merely an intellectual exercise but a form of life, and it is a form of life that only the human being — the being without a fixed Form — can pursue. The concordist thesis and the anthropological thesis of indeterminacy are therefore not independent claims; they are two faces of a single philosophical position.

The Kabbalistic parallel to the doctrine of indeterminacy is profound and underexplored in the scholarship. In Kabbalistic theology, the human being created *be-tzelem Elohim* — in the image (*tzelem*) of God — is a being whose image comprehends all ten *sefirot*, all the divine attributes in their completeness. The *Adam Kadmon*, the cosmic or primordial Adam, is the complete expression of the divine character — not locked to a single attribute but encompassing all of them as the tree encompasses all its branches. Pico's reading of the Kabbalistic sources through Flavius Mithridates's translations, as Wirszubski (1989) and Busi and Ebgi (2014) have established, gave him access to this concept of a human being who is cosmically complete precisely because it is not restricted to a single divine attribute. In the *900 Conclusions*, Pico adapts this Kabbalistic anthropology to his concordist scheme: "I adapt our soul to the ten *sefirot* in such a way that through its unity it is with the first, through its intellect with the second, through its reason with the third" (Conclusions 11 > 201). The soul's comprehensive correspondence to the ten *sefirot* is the Kabbalistic expression of the same indeterminacy that the *Oration* expresses in Platonic-cosmological terms.

The positive philosophical claim that indeterminacy makes is thus this: the human being is the entity that, precisely because it has no fixed exemplary Form assigning it to a single level of the cosmic hierarchy, is capable of *assuming* any Form through the appropriate forms of intellectual and ascetic practice. The potential for degeneration downward to the brutes is real — it is the condition of the unreformed human being, enslaved to the appetites and the senses. But the potential for ascent upward through the philosophical and mystical disciplines is equally real, and it is what the *Oration* is designed to promote. The indeterminacy is not the end of the philosophical story but its beginning: the metaphysical precondition from which the entire edifice of Pico's syncretic ascent program is built.

Finally, it is worth noting the structural role of indeterminacy in the broader economy of the *Oration*. The speech moves from the anthropological claim (the human has no fixed nature) through the protreptic exhortation (therefore pursue philosophy) through the justification of the syncretic program (philosophy in all its traditions points toward the same divine wisdom) to the defense of magic and Kabbalah (these are not demonic arts but the highest forms of philosophical-mystical practice). The indeterminacy claim is the load-bearing foundation on which the rest of the argument rests. Without it — without the claim that the human being is the kind of entity that can traverse all levels of the hierarchy and all traditions of wisdom — the defense of syncretic philosophical magic in the later sections of the *Oration* has no anthropological grounding. The human is the magician-philosopher because the human is the being whose indeterminate nature enables it to operate across all levels of the cosmos. Indeterminacy is not a celebration of human freedom; it is the metaphysical license for the most ambitious intellectual and spiritual program Pico ever conceived.
