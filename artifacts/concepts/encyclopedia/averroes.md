Averroes — Abu al-Walid Muhammad ibn Ahmad ibn Rushd, born in Córdoba in 1126 and died in Marrakesh in 1198 — entered Pico della Mirandola's intellectual world under a name that carried enormous authority and considerable menace: *Il Commentatore*, the Commentator. In the Latin university tradition, this designation was unqualified and unique, reserved for Averroes alone among the commentators on Aristotle in acknowledgment of the comprehensiveness, rigor, and systematic depth of his three-volume commentaries on the Aristotelian corpus. His short, middle, and long commentaries on the *Physics*, *Metaphysics*, *De anima*, and the logical works had been translated into Latin beginning in the early thirteenth century by Michael Scot and others, and had shaped the teaching of Aristotelian philosophy in the western universities for over two centuries before Pico was born. To engage with Aristotle in the late fifteenth century was, in a significant sense, to engage with Averroes.

The aspect of Averroes's thought that most exercised the philosophical and theological tradition was his treatment of the intellect in the Long Commentary on *De anima*. Averroes's argument for the unity of the material intellect — the receptive faculty of cognition — was among the most contested positions in medieval and Renaissance philosophy. His reasoning ran as follows: the material intellect must be in potentiality with respect to all intelligible forms, since it is the faculty whose function is to receive and become those forms. But if the material intellect were the form or perfection of a particular body — as the soul is the form of a living body in Aristotle's general definition — it would already be actualized in some determinate way and could not be the pure receptivity that cognition requires. The material intellect must therefore be something separate from individual bodies, numerically one for all of humanity, connecting to individual human beings only through the individual images (*phantasmata*) that each person's imagination and sensation produce. The agent intellect, on this account, is equally one and separate, the active principle of cognition that operates on the common stock of images to produce actual knowledge. The consequence is that cognition, strictly understood as the actualization of the intellect by intelligible forms, occurs in a substance shared by all human beings: what is individual in each human being is not any intellectual substance but only the particular bundle of sensory images that connects each person to the one material intellect as an occasion for cognition.

This doctrine, which scholars since the Middle Ages have called monopsychism or the unity-of-intellect thesis, had implications that neither medieval nor Renaissance philosophy could easily absorb. If there is only one material intellect for all humanity, and if intellectual cognition occurs in that unified substance rather than in individual souls, then there is no individual intellectual soul capable of surviving bodily death. Personal immortality, the resurrection of individual persons, the doctrine of individual sin and divine judgment — all of these theological commitments appear to be undercut by Averroes's psychology of cognition. This is why Thomas Aquinas wrote his dedicated refutation *De unitate intellectus contra Averroistas* (1270), directed against the Parisian Averroists of his day, and why Marsilio Ficino, more than two centuries later, opened his *Platonic Theology* with the declaration that the whole work was aimed at refuting the two main threats to the soul's immortality: the Alexandrinist position that the intellect is mortal (since Alexander of Aphrodisias had argued the agent intellect was a natural, not divine, disposition of the individual body), and the Averroistic position that the intellect is one for all (and hence not individual). As Ficino put it in a letter that Edelheit cites: "The Peripatetics, that is all the philosophers, are divided in large part into two sects, Alexandrine and Averroicist. The former indeed believe that our intellect is mortal, the latter argue that it is one. Both equally destroy all religion in its foundations" (Opera, in Edelheit 2008, 13146–47).

Latin Averroism as a scholastic current was associated primarily with Padua and Bologna in the fifteenth century. The tradition of defending Averroes's position in natural philosophy while maintaining Christian faith in individual immortality through an *opinio/fides* segregation — a strategy variously described as a doctrine of double truth (usually by its critics) or as the proper compartmentalization of natural philosophy and theology (by its proponents) — had been practiced at Padua since the time of Jacopo of Forli and Pietro d'Abano. By Pico's day the tradition included John of Jandun (fourteenth century, Paris), who had become in some respects more Averroist than Averroes and whose commentaries were still current, as well as contemporary figures associated with the Paduan milieu who had trained or taught there. Pico studied at Padua from 1480 to 1482, during which time he formed his relationship with Elia del Medigo, the Jewish Averroist philosopher who had settled in Padua and had translated Averroistic texts from Hebrew for use by Christian Aristotelians. As S. A. Farmer documents, Pico commissioned Elia to provide fresh Latin translations of previously known and previously unknown Averroistic texts, which in the absence of the Arabic originals had survived in medieval Hebrew translations, and to compile and comment on a wide range of technical conflicts in Arabic philosophy that Pico planned to resolve at Rome (Farmer 1998, 11).

The forty-one theses attributed to Averroes in the *900 Conclusions* (*Secundum Averroem XLI*) constitute one of the largest single dossiers in the historical portion of the text, and their content demonstrates the depth and range of Pico's Averroistic engagement. The theses cover cosmology (the relation of the intelligences to the celestial spheres), natural philosophy (the nature of simple bodies, the continuity of motion, the relationship of matter and form), logic (the structure of demonstration), and — most importantly — psychology and the intellect. In the psychology theses, Pico reproduces the core of Averroistic monopsychism: thesis 7.2 states that "the intellective soul is one in all men" (*Una est anima intellectiva in omnibus hominibus*), while thesis 7.3 defines the highest felicity as the conjunction of the agent intellect with the possible intellect "as its form," and targets John of Jandun's interpretation of this conjunction as "perverse and erroneous" — Jandun had "not only in this, but in almost all questions in philosophy, totally corrupted and twisted the doctrine of Averroes" (*omnino et depravavit*). Thesis 7.4 then makes the syncretic claim that it is "possible, upholding the unity of the intellect, that my soul, so particularly mine that it is not shared by me with all, remains after death."

These theses present interpretive challenges that Pico scholars have debated since the twentieth century. The critical question is whether Pico endorses Averroistic monopsychism, translates it into his own syncretic scheme, or uses it as a foil to be reconciled with Christian orthodoxy. Farmer's analysis suggests that the theses, while formally presented as *Averroes's* positions rather than Pico's own, go beyond anything actually found in Averroes and represent Pico's active reworking of the Averroistic tradition toward a reconciliation with Christianity (Farmer 1998, 113). Pico was, in Farmer's words, "boasting that in his Vatican council he planned to reconcile Averroes's concept of the unity of intellect totally with Christian orthodoxy" — a claim that required not so much a refutation of Averroes as a creative reinterpretation of what Averroes's best doctrine implied when properly understood. The attack on John of Jandun is thus part of this strategy: Pico positions himself as the corrector of the Latin Averroist tradition, recovering Averroes's genuine intent (as Pico construes it) from the distortions introduced by its own most prominent advocate.

Edelheit's analysis in *Ficino, Pico and Savonarola* (2008) provides the theological-methodological framework within which this strategy makes sense. Edelheit argues that the fundamental move in Pico's approach to all philosophical tradition — including the Averroistic — is the distinction between *opinio* and *fides*: between philosophical opinion, which is by its nature disputable, probabilistic, and subject to critical revision, and faith, which rests on divine revelation and cannot be contradicted by natural reason. The *Apologia* (1487), Pico's defense of his condemned theses written under pressure from the papal commission appointed by Innocent VIII, makes this methodological commitment explicit. When Pico acknowledges in the *Apology* that the *900 Conclusions* contain "many impious dogmas of the old philosophers Averroes and Alexander and of so many others, which — although I have always professed, asserted, and proclaimed, both publicly and privately, that they are no less alien from the true and right philosophy than from the faith — still while considering a scholastic exercise I considered worthy of being examined among a few learned persons in a secret assembly in the manner of the Academies," he is invoking the *opinio/fides* distinction to defend the inclusion of Averroistic positions in the disputation (Edelheit 2008, 286–88). The *scholastica exercitatio* — the scholastic exercise — is a genre with its own norms: it admits the examination of positions the examiner does not personally hold, precisely because the point is to understand the full range of philosophical positions and to demonstrate their coherent reconciliation.

The *opinio/fides* distinction has a specific technical reference in the *Apology*'s engagement with the theological commission. Pico's core methodological argument is that the opinions of Doctors like Thomas Aquinas, however authoritative they have become in practice, do not carry the force of divinely revealed dogma. "To err in Thomas's opinion is not to err in faith" (*quia errare in opinione Thomae non est errare in fide*) — a slogan Edelheit identifies as central to Pico's humanist-theological project (Edelheit 2008, 188). If Thomas's position on the intellect, which locates both the possible and agent intellects as individual faculties of each human soul, is a *philosophical opinion* and not a *dogma of faith*, then the space opens for Pico to engage Averroes's contrary philosophical opinion without being guilty of heresy. Averroes's monopsychism becomes a philosophical *opinio*, admissible for academic disputation, whose relationship to *fides* is not one of simple contradiction but of philosophical tension to be resolved by a superior philosophy — Pico's own syncretic system.

The historiographical significance of Averroes's presence in Pico's work extends beyond the narrow question of monopsychism. Averroes represents, within the *900 Conclusions*' organizational logic, the Arabic philosophical tradition as a whole: he is the most prominent of the Arabic authorities listed in the section heading *Conclusiones secundum doctrinam Arabum, qui ut plurimum Peripatheticos se profitentur, Averroem, Avicennam, Alpharabium, Avenpatem, Isaac, Abumaron, Moysen, et Mahumethem* — conclusions "according to the doctrine of the Arabs who profess themselves for the most part Peripatetics: Averroes, Avicenna, al-Farabi, Avempace, Isaac, Abumaron, Moses [Maimonides], and Muhammad." In Farmer's analysis of the *900 Conclusions*' structure, the Arabic section serves as the primary repository of the natural-philosophical tradition that Pico intended to reconcile with Neoplatonism, Kabbalism, and Christian theology. The juxtaposition of Averroes with his predecessor and rival Avicenna within the same section is itself a syncretic strategy: Farmer notes that in the theses' debating structure, "Averroes is opposed to his predecessor and rival Avicenna" on precisely those points where the tradition most diverged, creating a set of antitheses that Pico's synthesis would then be called upon to resolve (Farmer 1998, 24).

Pico's access to Averroes was not simply through the standard Latin versions available in the university curriculum. The translations Elia del Medigo prepared for Pico included texts of Averroes that had survived only in medieval Hebrew renderings and were unknown in the Latin West, drawn from the rich manuscript tradition of Hebrew Averroism that had flourished in Jewish communities in Provence, Italy, and the Iberian peninsula. This access to Arabic philosophy through Hebrew mediation is itself a significant feature of Pico's intellectual situation: the recovery of Averroes was, for Pico, also a recovery of the Jewish philosophical tradition's engagement with the same Arabic sources, an engagement visible in figures like Samuel ibn Tibbon, Zerahyah Gracian, and Elia del Medigo himself. The chain of transmission — Arabic original, Hebrew translation, Pico's commissioned Latin — added layers of interpretation and distortion at each stage, as Farmer notes, but it also provided Pico with access to philosophical positions and debates invisible in the standard Latin versions.

The relationship between Pico and Averroes is complicated further by the circumstance that the two most prominent members of Pico's circle had already staked out positions hostile to Averroism. Ficino's *Platonic Theology* was, as we have noted, explicitly organized against the Averroistic denial of individual immortality. Ficino's strategy was to provide a Neoplatonic account of the soul's self-knowledge and rational self-reflectiveness that made individual immortality philosophically demonstrable without relying on the Thomistic arguments about the individualization of the agent intellect. A further complication, identified by Copenhaver in a study of Ficino's method in *Platonic Theology* Book 15, is that Ficino's anti-Averroist arguments do not derive from direct reading of Averroes's *Long Commentary on the De Anima* but from Aquinas's *Summa Contra Gentiles* 2.59.3–6 — the passage where Aquinas summarizes and polemically refutes Averroes's position in terms already shaped by Thomistic categories. Copenhaver introduces a -P/-F distinction to mark the gap: Averroes's actual position (-P) is that the intellect is not a body or a power inhering in a body; Ficino's charge against Averroes (-F) is that Averroes denies that the intellect is the substantial form of the body. These formulations are related but not identical — the -F charge is Aquinas's paraphrase of Averroes, already a polemic, already a selective redescription. When Ficino argues against "Averroes" in the *Platonic Theology*, he is in large part arguing against a Thomistic construction of Averroes, not against the Arabic philosopher's full position in the *Long Commentary* (Copenhaver, "Ten Arguments in Search of a Philosopher," *Vivarium* 47.4, 2009, 444–479).

Pico's departure from Ficino on this point is characteristic: where Ficino seeks to refute Averroes, Pico seeks to reinterpret him, to show that properly understood Averroes's best insights are compatible with — or even contributions to — the comprehensive philosophical synthesis Pico is constructing.

The Roman condemnation of 1487 and its aftermath forced a partial retreat. Thirteen of Pico's theses were condemned as heretical or suspect by the papal commission, and the *Apology*'s attempt to distinguish the academic context of the disputation from the confessional context of faith-claims was judged insufficient by Innocent VIII, who banned the work and eventually issued a bull excommunicating Pico. Among the questions the commission scrutinized were several that touched on the intellect and on Pico's Averroistic maneuvers. The commission's summary condemnation of one thesis as "scandalous and contrary to the common opinion of the holy Doctors" ("ista conclusio est scandalosa et contra communem opinionem sanctorum doctorum") exemplifies the theological pressure Pico was under: not the pressure of dogmatic contradiction, which Pico could have contested on the *opinio/fides* grounds, but the harder-to-quantify pressure of *scandalo*, the threat to the faith of ordinary Christians posed by publicly disputing positions that seemed to undermine its foundations (Edelheit 2008, 14480–81).

In Pico's later works, written under the influence of Savonarola's reforming piety and the political and spiritual crisis of the early 1490s, the Averroistic materials recede from prominence. The *Heptaplus* (1489) and *De ente et uno* (1491) retain the philosophical architecture that the Arabic tradition helped construct, but do not engage Averroes by name as a living interlocutor. Farmer's evidence of posthumous tampering by Gianfrancesco Pico and his Savonarolan associates — extensive interpolations and suppressions in the later works intended to present a more orthodox Pico — makes it difficult to assess the extent to which Averroes's thought continued to shape Pico's thinking in the final years of his life (Farmer 1998, 151–75). What is certain is that the forty-one theses *secundum Averroem* remain among the most intellectually ambitious sections of the *900 Conclusions* and that Pico's engagement with Averroes represents the most sustained and careful attempt by any fifteenth-century humanist to incorporate the Arabic philosophical tradition — in its most challenging form — into the synthesis of Greek, Hebrew, and Christian thought that defined the Pico project.
