# Gianfrancesco Pico della Mirandola

Gianfrancesco Pico della Mirandola (1469–1533) occupies a peculiar and troubling position in the study of his uncle Giovanni: he is simultaneously the primary source for our knowledge of Giovanni's final years and the figure most responsible for distorting what that knowledge contains. As Giovanni's nephew, editor, and biographer, Gianfrancesco outlived him by nearly four decades, shaping the posthumous publication of his works, controlling the narrative of his intellectual development, and writing the biography that became, through Thomas More's English translation, the dominant portrait of Pico in the English Renaissance. The result is that what most early modern readers, and indeed many modern scholars, took to be Giovanni Pico is a construction — not fraudulent in a simple sense, but filtered through the priorities of a man with very different intellectual commitments, priorities that pointed consistently in the direction of suppressing the philosophical-magical Pico and amplifying a pious, Christian, ultimately anti-intellectual Pico who had renounced his syncretism on his deathbed.

The biographical problem is inseparable from the editorial one. When Giovanni died suddenly in Florence in November 1494 — at the age of thirty-one, during the French invasion, on the very night that Charles VIII entered the city — his papers fell into the hands of Girolamo Savonarola and subsequently into those of Gianfrancesco. The exact circumstances of this transfer remain obscure, and Stephen Farmer, whose edition and analysis of the *900 Conclusions* (*Syncretism in the West*, 1998) remains the most systematic treatment of the posthumous textual problem, argues that the obscurity is itself significant. Savonarola had been Giovanni's spiritual director in his final months, and the *Vita* of Giovanni that Gianfrancesco composed presents a trajectory in which the brilliant young philosopher, having reached the peak of his syncretic ambition, turned away from it: from magic, from astrology, from the proud enterprise of reconciling all traditions, toward the simplicity of Christian Scripture and the devotional life modeled by Savonarola. This narrative has the structure of a conversion story. Like the conversions recounted by Augustine, it requires a before and an after — a proud intellectual past that the converted man repudiates from the vantage of a humbled present. Whether it corresponds to what Giovanni actually underwent in his last months is a question that cannot be settled with the evidence available, and Farmer is appropriately skeptical about taking the narrative at face value.

Gianfrancesco's own intellectual trajectory makes the biographical portrait all the more suspect. Where Giovanni was an eclectic who sought concordance among traditions — who read Kabbalah as a confirmation of Christianity, Pythagoras as an anticipation of Plato, and Aristotle as ultimately compatible with the Neoplatonic emanation hierarchy — Gianfrancesco was a Christian skeptic of a fundamentally different kind. His major work, the *Examen vanitatis doctrinae gentium* (Examination of the Vanity of Pagan Doctrine, 1520), represents perhaps the most thoroughgoing rejection of classical philosophy in early sixteenth-century Italy. Drawing on Sextus Empiricus — whose *Outlines of Pyrrhonism* he was among the first Renaissance thinkers to study closely — Gianfrancesco argued that pagan philosophy was not a tributary stream flowing toward Christian truth but an ocean of contradictions and errors, incapable of establishing any reliable knowledge and therefore not merely insufficient for salvation but positively dangerous. The *prisca theologia* that Giovanni had deployed as an argument for the essential unity of wisdom traditions is, for Gianfrancesco, a form of idolatry: the elevation of pagan wisdom to a status it neither earned nor deserves. Scripture alone — *sola scriptura*, in a formulation that anticipates the Reformers though in a Catholic frame — provides the foundation of knowledge about God. What the pagans called philosophy is vanity, and the Renaissance project of recovering it is intellectual pride.

This is a position radically discontinuous from anything Giovanni consistently held. The question it raises for Pico scholarship is how far Gianfrancesco's editorial work on Giovanni's posthumous texts was infected by these convictions. Farmer's argument in chapter four of *Syncretism in the West* is that the infection was substantial. The central exhibit is the *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem* (Disputations against Divinatory Astrology), Giovanni's unfinished polemical work against astrology, which exists only in Gianfrancesco's posthumous edition. The *Disputationes* is, on its face, an anomaly in Giovanni's corpus: it repudiates astrology with a vehemence that seems incompatible with the theses *secundum Pythagoreos* and the magical framework of the *900 Conclusions*, in which celestial influences play a significant role. Scholars from Eugenio Garin to Paola Zambelli have disagreed sharply about whether the *Disputationes* represents a genuine intellectual evolution — a "palinode" in which Giovanni retracts his earlier enthusiasm for celestial causation — or whether it is in some degree Gianfrancesco's work.

Farmer's answer is uncompromising: he finds "extensive evidence" of posthumous tampering by Gianfrancesco, Savonarola, and their associates. The argument rests on several lines of evidence. First, the *Disputationes* contains positions and formulations that contradict the rest of Giovanni's corpus in ways that are hard to explain by simple evolution. Second, certain passages in the *Disputationes* read more like Gianfrancesco's later skeptical polemic than like Giovanni's characteristic mode, which typically worked by reconciling rather than simply demolishing. Third, the text's hostility to the *prisca theologia* — including the rhetorical reversal of Giovanni's own earlier formulation "me quoque adolescentem olim fallebat" ("these things once deceived me too when I was young"), which appears in the *Disputationes* — suggests an editorial hand working to have Giovanni disown his own earlier commitments. The phrase is, as Akopyan notes, remarkable: the mature Giovanni recanting the younger Giovanni, the philosopher confessing his youthful error. Farmer reads this as suspiciously convenient for Gianfrancesco's purposes.

Ovanes Akopyan's monograph *Debating the Stars in the Italian Renaissance* (Brill, 2021) enters this dispute on the side of substantially defending the *Disputationes*' authenticity, while acknowledging the complexity of Gianfrancesco's role. Akopyan's argument is that Pico's method in the *Disputationes* is consistent with the method he develops in *De ente et uno* — a work whose authenticity is not in doubt — and that the apparent discontinuity with the *900 Conclusions* on celestial influences reflects not forgery but genuine philosophical development. On Akopyan's reading, Giovanni in his mature phase was moving away from the broad syncretic program of 1486 toward more precise Aristotelian natural-philosophical argumentation, and the *Disputationes* belongs to this later phase. Akopyan dedicates a full chapter (chapter 8) to Gianfrancesco's position, showing that Gianfrancesco's own anti-astrological works — particularly the *De rerum praenotione* and the *Quaestio de falsitate astrologiae* — reveal a thinker who used Giovanni's *Disputationes* but consistently pushed it in directions Giovanni had not intended: where Giovanni was arguing against astrology on natural-philosophical grounds, Gianfrancesco was arguing against it as *prisca haeresis* (ancient heresy), part of the repudiation of all pagan learning. These are importantly different arguments. Akopyan's careful distinction between them suggests that while Gianfrancesco certainly appropriated Giovanni's anti-astrological legacy for his own purposes, the appropriation does not necessarily prove that he forged the text he appropriated.

The debate between Farmer and Akopyan is, at bottom, a debate about the relationship between editorial intervention and textual authenticity. Both scholars agree that Gianfrancesco had the opportunity, motive, and ideological incentive to intervene in Giovanni's posthumous texts. They disagree about how extensively he did so. The honest position for the encyclopedia reader is that the *Disputationes* must be read with caution — that its relationship to Giovanni's actual views is mediated by an editorial hand that was interested in a particular story about those views — without concluding that the text is therefore simply Gianfrancesco's work rather than Giovanni's.

The biographical dimension of Gianfrancesco's influence is equally consequential. The *Vita* of Giovanni that Gianfrancesco composed — translated into English by Thomas More circa 1505–1510 and published in 1510 — is a portrait of a holy man rather than a philosopher. More's translation brought this portrait to an English audience that had little or no access to Giovanni's actual texts. The Pico that English readers from Erasmus's circle onward encountered was, by and large, the Pico of Gianfrancesco's biography: brilliant but ultimately humbled by his brilliance, moved to renounce magic and philosophy, drawn by divine grace toward the simple devotion modeled by Savonarola's Dominican community. This "pious Pico" — saintly, penitential, anti-syncretic — became the paradigm for the Pico received by northern European humanists, and it remains influential in popular accounts to this day.

The historiographical damage is not simply that Gianfrancesco created a false portrait. It is that the portrait was so persuasive, distributed so effectively, and reinforced by so many institutional factors — Savonarolan hagiography, the posthumous edition of Giovanni's works, the moral authority of More's translation — that it effectively suppressed the philosophical-magical Pico for generations. The Pico who wrote in the *Oration* about the limitless power of human self-formation, who arranged 900 theses on every known tradition of knowledge and invited the world's scholars to Rome to debate them, who believed that natural magic and Kabbalistic practice could bring the soul into direct contact with the divine — this Pico was available in his texts but screened from view by the biographical tradition Gianfrancesco inaugurated. Modern Pico scholarship, from Ernst Cassirer and Eugenio Garin through Farmer, Wirszubski, Copenhaver, and Akopyan, has been in significant part a project of recovering the suppressed philosophical Pico from beneath the pious Pico of Gianfrancesco's construction.

That recovery requires attending closely to the seams where Gianfrancesco's interests and Giovanni's texts come apart. It requires reading the *Disputationes* against the *900 Conclusions* rather than in isolation from them, noting the disjunctions rather than smoothing them over. It requires treating the *Vita* as a document in the history of reception rather than as a reliable guide to Giovanni's spiritual development. And it requires holding in mind the fundamental asymmetry of the relationship: Gianfrancesco lived, worked, published, and polemicized for nearly four decades after Giovanni's death, with every institutional and archival advantage that longevity confers. Giovanni had no comparable opportunity to defend his own texts or contest his nephew's portrait of them. The Pico that survived was, in large part, the Pico Gianfrancesco chose to transmit.

The irony is that Gianfrancesco himself was not without philosophical interest. His use of Sextus Empiricus was genuinely innovative, and his *Examen vanitatis* is a remarkable document in the history of early modern skepticism. But his intelligence operated in the service of a project diametrically opposed to Giovanni's: where Giovanni sought concordance among all traditions as evidence of a shared divine truth, Gianfrancesco sought the destruction of all non-scriptural authority as evidence of human intellectual vanity. These are not merely different programs but antithetical ones, and their antithesis is the deepest explanation of why Gianfrancesco's editorial legacy poses such persistent challenges for the reader who wants to recover what Giovanni Pico actually thought.
