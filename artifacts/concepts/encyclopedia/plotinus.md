Plotinus (204/5–270 CE) stands at the origin of the philosophical tradition Pico della Mirandola inherited, refashioned, and on at least one crucial question explicitly rejected. As the founder of what we now call Neoplatonism — though no ancient thinker would have recognized or accepted that label — Plotinus organized the Platonic legacy into a systematic metaphysics whose three hypostases (the One, Intellect, and Soul) and whose account of the soul's procession from and return to its source provided the structural grammar of late antique philosophy and, through Ficino's translations and commentaries, of Florentine Renaissance Platonism as well. To read Pico without understanding Plotinus is to encounter his arguments already stripped of their most important theoretical background.

The biographical outline is straightforward. Born in Egypt and educated in Alexandria under Ammonius Saccas, Plotinus later taught in Rome under the patronage of the emperor Gallienus. His disciple and biographer Porphyry arranged his writings into the *Enneads* — six groups of nine treatises — after the master's death, and it was in this form that the work reached Ficino and, through Ficino, Pico. Ficino's Latin translation of the *Enneads* was completed and published in 1492, though manuscript drafts circulated in the Florentine circle considerably earlier; the point matters because Pico's *Commento* (1486), his *900 Conclusions* (1486), and his *Oration* (also 1486) all show detailed engagement with Plotinian ideas before the published translation existed. Michael Allen's careful reconstruction of the Florentine Platonic milieu demonstrates that Ficino's working translations and reading notes functioned as a kind of shared intellectual commons from which younger figures like Pico drew (Allen 2017, 4–5, 518–520). Pico did not wait for the printed edition to encounter Plotinus; he encountered him through Ficino's practice.

The metaphysical architecture of the *Enneads* is built around three principles arranged in a strict hierarchy of priority and derivation. The first and highest principle is the One (*to hen*), which Plotinus insists lies beyond being (*epekeina tēs ousias*), beyond intellect, and beyond all predication. The One is not a being among beings; it is the productive source from which all being emanates, yet it remains undiminished by that emanation, as a sun is undiminished by the light it radiates. Plotinus's most rigorous treatments of the One appear in *Ennead* V.1 and VI.9, and their apophatic insistence — that the One "is not this" — places him squarely in the tradition of negative theology that will later flow through Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite and, in Pico's day, color the reading of the first hypothesis of Plato's *Parmenides* as a revelation of the supreme principle's ineffability. The second hypostasis is Intellect (*Nous*), the realm of pure thinking in which subject and object are identified: Intellect thinks the Platonic Forms, and the Forms exist as thoughts within Intellect. This is a decisive move beyond Plato's own account, in which the Forms exist in a "supercelestial place" (*hyperouranios topos*) that is at least conceptually separate from the thinking mind that apprehends them. For Plotinus, the intelligible world just is the thinking of Intellect, and Intellect just is the intelligible world unfolded into its proper multiplicity. The third hypostasis is Soul (*Psychē*), which proceeds from Intellect and mediates between the intelligible and material realms, animating the cosmos and generating time as a moving image of eternity.

The governing rhythm of this system is the double movement of procession (*proödos*) and return (*epistrophē*). Every derived entity proceeds from what is prior to it and, by a kind of natural desire, strains to return to that prior principle. This is not, in Plotinus, a temporal sequence but a logical structure: at every moment, Intellect is simultaneously proceeding from the One and reverting toward it in the contemplative act that constitutes its being. Soul proceeds from Intellect while tending toward the Good, and individual human souls, embedded in matter, carry within themselves the capacity for return — the ascent through ethical purification, philosophical contemplation, and finally the silent, intuitive union with the One that Plotinus calls *henosis* (literally, "unification"). The closing words of the *Enneads*, "the flight of the alone to the Alone" (*phygē monou pros monon*), epitomize this aspiration (Allen 2017, 1244).

For Pico, these structures are not abstract: they are the operative vocabulary of his two most sustained vernacular and Latin treatments of love, beauty, and the soul's ascent. The *Commento sopra una canzone de amore* (1486), Pico's commentary on Girolamo Benivieni's Neoplatonic canzone, deploys the Plotinian three-hypostasis framework as the organizing principle of its love theory. Where Ficino in his *De amore* had distinguished celestial and vulgar Venus, the *Commento* introduces three Venuses — celestial, intermediate, and earthly — corresponding to a triadic structure Pico derives in part from *Ennead* I.6, Plotinus's great essay on beauty and love. For Plotinus, beauty appears to the soul as a reminder of the intelligible world it once inhabited and now longs to recover; love (*erōs*) is the dynamic of that longing. Pico inherits this analysis and sharpens it against Ficino. In the *Commento* he explicitly criticizes Ficino for misreading Plotinus, charging that Ficino had not fully grasped the passage in *Enneads* V.6.3 or VI.7.37 where God is said to "understand nothing" — intending thereby an apophatic theological move that Ficino, according to Pico, mistook for a simple predication (Allen 2017, 6060–6061). Whether the charge is fully just to Ficino is itself debated; Allen argues that Ficino was well versed in negative theology and the polemical framing of Pico's criticism overstates the case. But the episode reveals something important: Pico's engagement with Plotinus was direct, combative, and independent. He was not simply passing on Ficino's Plotinus.

In the *Oration* (more fully, the *Oratio de hominis dignitate*, though that title is posterior to Pico's own usage), the Plotinian schema of ascent organizes the famous account of Adam's freedom. Adam is invited to choose his form, to move up and down the hierarchy of being, and the highest terminus of that movement — the withdrawal into "the solitary darkness of the Father" — is recognizably Plotinian *henosis* expressed in Christological and scriptural language. Allen notes that the entire concern of the *Oration* with the soul's capacity to inhabit and traverse ontological levels — to be soul, to aspire to angelic intellect, to seek union with God — is in accord with "Neoplatonism's traditional concern with the procession and return of all things to the One" (Allen 2017, 1203). The freedom Pico celebrates is not modern liberal freedom; it is the Plotinian freedom of the soul to choose its level of participation in the hierarchy of being.

In the *900 Conclusions*, the Plotinian matrix is structural in a different way. Stephen Farmer's critical edition and commentary demonstrates that the *Conclusions* organize their theses by source family — there are theses *secundum Plotinum* as well as theses drawing from Porphyry, Iamblichus, Proclus, and others. Farmer shows that the Neoplatonic henadic structure, the notion of derived orders of being mediating between the One and matter, constitutes one of the principal theoretical matrices through which Pico attempts to correlate Greek metaphysical hierarchies with Kabbalistic *sefirot* (Farmer 1998, 1612). The *sefirot* and the Plotinian (and post-Plotinian) hypostases are positioned as variant expressions of the same underlying emanationist logic, the "occult agreement" (*occulta concordia*) that Pico believes the ancients shared.

The most philosophically consequential point of Plotinian tension in Pico's corpus, however, is the one that ultimately led him away from his most obvious Plotinian inheritance. The question is whether the One is identical with Being or whether it transcends Being. Plotinus's answer is unambiguous: the One is *epekeina tēs ousias*, beyond being, and its elevation beyond all predication — including the predication "is" — is essential to what makes it absolute. Ficino, following this Plotinian position (often filtered through Proclus's Platonic Theology), consistently maintained the same doctrine. The One, identified with God, transcends even Being and is the ground from which Being proceeds.

Pico's *De ente et uno* (1491), his last completed philosophical work, is a systematic argument against precisely this position. Taking his stand with Proclus over Plotinus on the formal question of the relation between the One and Being, Pico argues that the two are convertible: whatever is one is a being, and whatever is a being is one. He reads Plato's *Parmenides* not as a revelation of the One's transcendence but as a rhetorical exercise unsuited for drawing metaphysical conclusions, and he argues that Aristotle and Plato agree on the co-extensiveness of Being and Unity. This is Pico's most explicit act of philosophical independence from Ficino, and it directly reverses the Plotinian position on the status of the first principle. As Sophia Howlett summarizes the dispute, Ficino, "holding with Platonic orthodoxy, deriving from Plotinus, often filtered through Proclus," took the standard post-Plotinian view that Plato "argues the One is above Being"; Pico in the *De ente et uno* is arguing the contrary, and Ficino's subsequent commentary on the *Parmenides* (composed 1492–94, published 1496) responds section by section to refute him (Howlett 2020, 3138–3139).

The irony is that Pico uses Proclus — in some respects a yet more rigorous advocate of the One's transcendence than Plotinus himself — against Ficino's Plotinianism. The Proclian position Pico embraces in *De ente et uno* is the convertibility thesis; the Procline henadic structure he mobilizes in the *Conclusions* for syncretic purposes. What Pico selects from Plotinus and what he rejects depends on the argumentative purpose at hand, and this selectivity is characteristic of his philosophical practice throughout. It also underscores what the scholarly record confirms: Pico's Platonism is not a passive inheritance but an active, critical, and deliberately contentious engagement.

For Allen, the organizing truth about Ficino's mediation of Plotinus for Pico is that Ficino's entire project of Renaissance Platonism is "Neoplatonist" in the precise sense of being constituted by the Plotinian interpretation of Plato — the Plato seen through Plotinus's eyes (Allen 2017, 5877). Pico inherits this interpretation, disputes it at specific points, and repeatedly asserts his own capacity to return to the source beyond Ficino's version of it. In the *Commento* he tells his reader that Ficino's *De amore* makes "mistakes on every subject in every part" and that Ficino fails to go back to Plotinus himself rather than reading him through intermediaries (Allen 2017, 5781). The posture is that of a more faithful Plotinian than Ficino — even as, in *De ente et uno*, Pico takes a position that departs from the most fundamental Plotinian axiom. This tension between proximity and distance, inheritance and critique, constitutes the specifically Pican relationship to Plotinus and distinguishes it from mere discipleship.

The historiographical caution appropriate to this entry is that the category "Plotinian" has been applied too broadly in the secondary literature as a synonym for any mystical, emanationist, or apophatic element in Pico's thought. The actual textual genealogy is more specific. Pico's Plotinianism is mediated by Ficino's translations and notes; filtered through Porphyry, Iamblichus, and Proclus; activated in the specific contexts of commentary (the *Commento*), syncretic disputation (the *900 Conclusions*), and scholastic argument (*De ente et uno*); and complicated by Pico's own decisive intervention on the question of the One and Being that defines his philosophical independence. Each of these registers requires separate analysis, and no single characterization of Pico as "Plotinian" or "anti-Plotinian" will survive contact with the full complexity of the evidence.
