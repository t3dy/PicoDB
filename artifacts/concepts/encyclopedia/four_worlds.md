The doctrine of the four worlds (*Arba Olamot*, אַרְבַּע עוֹלָמוֹת) is one of the most characteristic structural features of mature Kabbalistic cosmology, and its partial presence in Pico della Mirandola's thinking raises important questions about the relationship between Kabbalistic and Neoplatonic cosmological hierarchies, the particular form in which Pico encountered this doctrine through Mithridates' translations, and the interpretive challenges facing any reader who tries to coordinate the layered world-scheme of the *Heptaplus* with the Kabbalistic sources from which it partly descended.

In the developed Kabbalistic tradition, the four worlds are: *Atziluth* (עֲצִילוּת, "emanation"), the world of the sefirot proper, closest to *Ein Sof*; *Beriah* (בְּרִיאָה, "creation"), the world of the divine Throne and the highest angels; *Yetzirah* (יְצִירָה, "formation"), the world of the lower angels associated with the divine chariot (*Merkavah*); and *Assiyah* (עֲשִׂיָּה, "action" or "making"), the material world. Each world, in the most systematic presentations, contains all ten sefirot in a diminished or reflected form, so that the same sefirotic structure repeats at each ontological level — a feature that gives the four-worlds doctrine its distinctive monadological quality: each world is a microcosm of the whole divine structure. This fully elaborated four-world scheme belongs primarily to the later stratum of Kabbalistic thought and is most fully articulated in sixteenth-century Kabbalists, particularly in the Lurianic tradition. Earlier texts of the thirteenth and early fourteenth centuries, which are the texts Pico actually encountered through Mithridates, have precursor forms rather than the complete systematic scheme. The question of what exactly Pico encountered is therefore inseparable from the question of which texts Mithridates translated and what doctrinal emphasis those texts had.

Pico's main cosmological statement, the *Heptaplus* (completed and published in 1489), organizes the universe into three worlds — the *mundus angelicus seu intellectualis* (angelic or intellectual world), the *mundus caelestis* (celestial world), and the *mundus sublunaris* (sublunary world) — plus, in the prologue, a fourth world that is the human being itself. The famous opening of the *Heptaplus*' proem declares: "Tres mundos figurat antiquitas" — antiquity figured three worlds — and proceeds to identify the highest with what theologians call the angelic world and philosophers call the intellectual, the middle with the celestial, and the lowest with the sublunary world that human beings inhabit. These three worlds are the frame within which Moses is understood to have concealed the entirety of cosmic knowledge in the Hexameron, the six-day narrative of Genesis, and the seven *expositiones* of the *Heptaplus* interpret those days in terms of this three-world scheme. At the end of the proem, however, Pico introduces a fourth entity: "Est autem, praeter tres quos narravimus, quartus alius mundus in quo et ea omnia inveniantur quae sunt in reliquis. Hie ipse est homo" — there is, besides the three worlds we have narrated, a fourth other world in which all those things found in the others are found. That is the human being himself. This fourth element — the human being as microcosm or fourth world — is explicitly distinguished from the cosmological triadic scheme and serves a different function: it is the theological climax of the entire commentary, the place where the anagogical ascent of the soul is articulated.

Chaim Wirszubski's careful detective work in *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989) established what he called "a noteworthy fact": that the doctrine of the three worlds, angelic, celestial, and elementary as advanced in the *Heptaplus*, is of Kabbalistic origin, even though it evidently also presupposes the Platonic distinction between the intelligible and visible worlds and the Aristotelian distinction between the celestial and sublunar. The Kabbalistic pedigree runs through two texts that Pico likely encountered either directly or indirectly: Bahya ben Asher's Commentary on the Pentateuch and the Kabbalistic anthology *Me'irat 'Eynayim* (Enlightening the Eyes) by Isaac of Acre, both of which develop the analogical identification of the three worlds with the three parts of the Tabernacle and the three parts of the human body (Wirszubski 1989, 244–249). The common source behind Bahya and Isaac was Baruch Togarmi, who was Abraham Abulafia's teacher. Abulafia himself in the *Liber Redemptionis* (a text Mithridates translated and which Pico demonstrably read, his marginal markings appearing in the manuscript) contains the succinct statement: "Et intelliges secretum hoc a tabernaculo cuius edificium est instar edificii corporis hominis et corporis mundi quae sunt tria secuta, seculum scilicet tabernaculum et homo" — understand this secret from the tabernacle, whose structure is like the structure of the human body and the body of the world, which are three succession-worlds: the world, the tabernacle, and the human being.

The elaborate analogical scheme that Pico develops in the *Heptaplus* — identifying the Holy of Holies with the angelic world, the Holy with the celestial, the outer court with the sublunary, and correspondingly the head with the angelic, the chest with the celestial, and the lower body with the sublunary — is descended from this Kabbalistic analogical tradition, even though its immediate literary elaboration is Pico's own. At the same time, Wirszubski identifies a second, monadological strand in Pico's doctrine of the three worlds: the principle that each world contains all the others in its own mode. This is stated in the *Heptaplus* in terms that Wirszubski traces to Proclus: "esse hos tres mundos mundos esse modo suo" — each of these three worlds contains within itself all things that are in all of them, but in its own mode. The fusion of a Kabbalistic analogical motif with a Proclean monadological motif within a single cosmological doctrine is characteristic of Pico's synthetic method, and it also illustrates the complexity of tracing any single element in his thought to a single source.

The problem of "four worlds versus three worlds" is not a simple discrepancy to be explained away. Pico's three-world cosmology of the *Heptaplus* does not straightforwardly match the Kabbalistic four-world scheme. The Kabbalistic scheme in its developed form distributes the ten sefirot across four levels: *Atziluth* contains the sefirot in their purest emanative form; *Beriah* is the world of the divine Throne and Merkavah, associated with the sefirot in their creative dimension; *Yetzirah* is the angelic world of the divine chariot; *Assiyah* is the material world. This is a fundamentally different organization from Pico's three-world scheme, in which the tripartition runs between the purely intelligible (supercaelestis), the celestial, and the sublunary. The Kabbalistic *Atziluth* has no clear parallel in Pico's cosmology, since Pico's highest world is the angelic/intellectual, which would correspond more naturally to *Beriah* or *Yetzirah*. The full Kabbalistic scheme with *Ein Sof* above and *Atziluth* as the first emanated world — the world of pure sefirot — is not replicated in Pico's *Heptaplus* tripartition.

S. A. Farmer, in his edition and commentary on the *900 Conclusions* (*Syncretism in the West*, 1998), emphasizes that the architectural challenge for Pico's concordist program in the *Conclusions* was precisely to coordinate cosmological schemes derived from fundamentally different intellectual traditions — Neoplatonic, Aristotelian, Hermetic, Orphic, and Kabbalistic — into a coherent structure for purposes of public disputation. The *Conclusions* organize their 900 theses by source tradition, and the Kabbalistic theses include several that bear on cosmic levels and worlds. The mapping problem is visible in the gap between what the Kabbalistic texts actually say about cosmic levels and what Pico presents as their convergence with Platonic and Aristotelian cosmology. The four-worlds doctrine in its systematic form was not the primary vehicle for Pico's cosmological syncretism; the three-worlds doctrine, with its cleaner alignment with Platonic triads, served him better. The fourth world — the human being as microcosm — enters the picture not from the Kabbalistic four-world scheme but from the Kabbalistic anthropological tradition, in which the human being recapitulates all the cosmic levels within itself.

Pico's access to Kabbalistic cosmological material through Mithridates was extensive but selective and uneven. The forty or so texts that Mithridates translated — amounting to some 5,500 pages in their entirety, of which roughly 3,500 pages survive — represent several different schools and periods of Kabbalistic thought. Pico encountered Abulafia's ecstatic Kabbalah (which is primarily about the technique of letter-combination and the ascent to prophetic union, not primarily a cosmological system), Azriel's Geronese speculative Kabbalah (which is primarily about the sefirot and their relationship to *Ein Sof*), Recanati's commentary tradition (which draws on theosophical Kabbalah and is organized around scriptural commentary), and the anonymous *De Proportione Divinitatis*, which provides glossaries of sefirotic symbolism. None of these texts presents the four-world scheme in the full systematic form it would take in later Kabbalistic compilations. What they provide instead are various proto-systematic elements: hierarchical distinctions among levels of being, correlations between the sefirot and cosmic regions, analogical identifications of the Tabernacle and the human body with cosmic structure. Pico assembled these elements into his own cosmological synthesis, which in the *Heptaplus* takes the form of a three-world scheme with a fourth anthropological dimension, and in the *900 Conclusions* takes the form of compressed theses scattered across several source-family clusters.

The eighth element of the *Heptaplus* — the eighth proem, which is nominally outside the seven-fold scheme and devoted to the hidden meaning of the first word of Genesis (*Bereishit*, בְּרֵאשִׁית) — is the location where Pico's Kabbalistic letter-combinatory practice (the *ars combinandi* derived primarily from Abulafia) is most directly at work. That eighth element does not correspond to a fourth world in the Kabbalistic *Arba Olamot* sense, but rather to the mystical superstructure of human destiny that the letter-combinatory analysis of *Bereishit* is supposed to reveal: the hidden Trinitarian and Christological content of the very first word of Scripture. This is the dimension of the *Heptaplus* that Ermolao Barbaro, in his celebrated letter of 4 September 1489, found most arresting, praising the work for the way its prose both "teaches and moves" — a quality he associated with the ancient theological mode — and noting how many excellent things from Plotinus Pico had touched on, "swiftly and elegantly."

The *Heptaplus* tripartition remained influential in Christian Kabbalistic writing after Pico, partly because it offered a cosmologically organized reading of Genesis that could be defended within a Christian framework without requiring full commitment to the internal logic of the Kabbalistic four-world scheme. Reuchlin, Egidio da Viterbo, and later Pico's nephew Giovanni Francesco Pico della Mirandola all worked with versions of the three-world scheme that reflected Pico's synthesis rather than a direct engagement with the Kabbalistic *Arba Olamot* as a systematic structure. In this sense, what Pico transmitted was not the four-world doctrine as such but a Platonized and Christianized version of Kabbalistic cosmological hierarchy in which the tripartite structure of intelligible, celestial, and sublunary could function as the shared grammar of several traditions simultaneously.
