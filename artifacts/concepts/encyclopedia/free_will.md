# Free Will (Liberum Arbitrium) in Pico della Mirandola

The concept of *liberum arbitrium* — free will or free choice — is among the most consequential and most misunderstood ideas in Pico della Mirandola's philosophy. Its most famous articulation appears in the speech known as the *Oration on the Dignity of Man* (1486), where God addresses Adam in terms that have since been read as a founding charter of modern individualism: "We have made you neither of heavenly nor of earthly stuff, neither mortal nor immortal, so that with free choice (*arbitrio*) and with honor, as though the maker and molder of yourself, you may fashion yourself in whatever shape you prefer." This passage has borne an interpretive burden out of all proportion to Pico's own intentions. To read it as a Renaissance proclamation of self-invention — of the autonomous subject constructing identity against a blank social background — is to import a set of assumptions entirely foreign to the intellectual world in which Pico wrote. What Pico means by *liberum arbitrium* is something both more specific and more demanding: the capacity of the rational soul to direct itself toward its proper end through a disciplined ascent of the hierarchy of being, an ascent accomplished through philosophy, natural magic, and Kabbalah, and culminating in a union with God that dissolves the very selfhood that made the ascent possible.

## The Oration Passage in Context

The famous *Oration* passage does not stand alone. Pico places it within a carefully constructed cosmological argument. He has just explained that God, having created all the orders of being — the celestial intelligences, the heavenly spheres, the four elements and their creatures — found no room in the plan of nature for a being who could survey and mirror the whole. Adam is therefore made last, not as the crown of creation but as its mirror: placed "at the center of the world" so that "from there you might look around all the more easily at whatever is in the world." The dignity involved is not a dignity of intrinsic worth — Pico is quite clear that the lower animals have their natures fixed and their forms determined — but a dignity of indetermination. Adam is the only creature without a fixed form, without a nature sealed into one place in the hierarchy. From this negative condition — the absence of a determinate nature — Pico draws his positive conclusion: the human being can become any kind of being it chooses to emulate. "You can sink into the lower, brutish forms of life; you can, by your own choice, rise to the higher forms, which are divine."

The point is that free will is not freedom of indifference — the mere scholastic *libertas indifferentiae* of equal power to choose either of two alternatives — but a directional capacity oriented toward ascent. The passage is protreptic in register: it is not a philosophical treatise on the freedom of the will but an exhortation to undertake the philosophical life. The speech as a whole, as Copenhaver has argued in *Magic and the Dignity of Man* (2019), is not primarily an essay on human dignity at all; that title, which was attached to it only posthumously by Gianfrancesco Pico in the 1496 edition, imposes a theme that the speech does not contain. The actual argument concerns two things: the program of philosophy, magic, and Kabbalah that Pico had assembled in his 900 *Conclusiones*, and the concordance of all philosophical traditions as the royal road to wisdom. Free will enters this argument as the precondition for undertaking the program, not as its conclusion.

## Liberum Arbitrium and the Ascent Program

The soul's free self-direction does not begin in emptiness. Pico describes a specific sequence of disciplines through which the philosophical soul must pass. The *Oration* lays this out in terms borrowed from Pseudo-Dionysius and from the Neoplatonic hierarchy: moral philosophy purges the soul of its passions, dialectic prepares the intellect for contemplation, natural philosophy elevates the mind through the study of causes, theology brings the soul into the presence of the divine. But in Pico's version of this sequence, natural magic and Kabbalah are not optional supplements; they are structural components. Natural magic — "the most perfect and most eminent part of natural science," as Pico calls it in his *Apology* — works by discovering and activating the hidden sympathies of the world, drawing down the powers of the heavens into lower matter. Kabbalah — "the science of the names of God," as he defines it — provides the intellectual and linguistic means to address the divine attributes directly, transforming the soul in the process.

It is here that the distinctively Pican account of free will diverges from that of his Florentine contemporary Marsilio Ficino. For Ficino, the soul's ascent is driven primarily by love: *eros* draws the soul upward through the hierarchy of beauty, from sensible beauty to intelligible beauty to the divine, in a movement that is fundamentally affective and erotic. The paradigm is Plato's *Symposium* and the *Phaedrus*, mediated by Plotinus's account of the soul's return to the One through desire. Pico knows this model intimately — he commented on Benivieni's *Canzona d'amore* in the Neoplatonic tradition — but he subordinates it to an intellectual and ascetic discipline that is more demanding and more solitary. Where Ficino imagines a community of philosophical lovers ascending together under the guidance of a *magus* figure (himself, in his more self-aggrandizing moments), Pico describes what Howlett calls an "exceptionalist" journey: a lonely, individuated, almost monastic movement toward a form of *henosis* — union or assimilation with God — that has no prescribed step of return. Pico's soul does not come back to reform the world; it ascends toward an ending that looks more like mystical death than civic virtue.

This means that free will, in Pico, is a noetic as well as a volitional capacity. The soul directs itself not merely by wanting the right things but by knowing them, and the ascent is achieved through intellectual comprehension of the hierarchy of being, not through love's more intuitive upward pull. The intellect and the will are not opposed — Pico is indebted to the Thomist synthesis that holds the will's freedom to depend on the intellect's prior act of judgment — but the intellect has priority in Pico's architecture in a way it does not quite have in Ficino.

## The Disputationes and Astrological Fatalism

The most sustained and systematic engagement with free will in Pico's corpus appears not in the *Oration* but in the *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem*, the enormous polemic against judicial astrology that Pico was still completing at his death in 1494 and that his nephew Gianfrancesco published in 1496. The work runs to twelve books and is one of the most thoroughgoing philosophical critiques of astrology produced in the Latin West. Its argument is complex, but the central thread is clear: astrology, insofar as it claims to predict human actions and choices from the positions of the stars, violates the freedom of the rational soul. The stars, Pico insists, may incline but they do not compel. They act on the body and through the body on the sensitive appetite; they cannot reach the rational soul, which is immaterial and therefore beyond the reach of any corporeal influence.

The argument draws on several philosophical traditions simultaneously. From Aquinas, Pico inherits the distinction between the sensitive appetite, which can be swayed by external causes including the influence of the heavenly bodies, and the rational appetite or will, which operates through a judgment of reason and remains free with respect to any particular good that falls short of the divine essence. The stars, being corporeal, belong to the order of natural causes that determines the behavior of material things; but the human will, being the faculty of an immaterial soul, falls outside this order. The *Disputationes* also deploys Aristotelian arguments about the unpredictability of contingent events and the logical absurdity of determinism about the future, and it makes extensive use of the ancient tradition of medical and philosophical criticism of astrology, citing Plotinus's *Enneads* (II.3) — which argues that the stars signify rather than cause human affairs — as a decisive authority.

What is philosophically interesting about the *Disputationes* from the perspective of free will is that the polemic against astrology is not merely a negative argument for indeterminism. Pico is not simply insisting that the stars do not cause human choices; he is insisting that the rational soul has a positive capacity for self-direction that makes it the first principle of its own actions. This positive account of rational self-determination is what distinguishes human beings from the rest of the natural order and is what grounds the ethical seriousness of the philosophical life. If the stars compelled human choices, there would be no point in undertaking the ascent program described in the *Oration*; the whole apparatus of philosophical discipline would be useless. The *Disputationes* thus provides the negative complement to the positive program announced in the *Oration*.

## Free Will in the Scholastic Debate

Pico's engagement with the scholastic debate about free will is concentrated primarily in the 900 *Conclusiones* of 1486, where several families of theses address the positions of Aquinas, John Duns Scotus, William of Ockham, and their successors. The scholastic debate about *liberum arbitrium* centered on several interlocking questions: Is the will a faculty distinct from the intellect? Does the will follow the intellect's judgment necessarily, or does it retain a capacity to resist? Can the will, in the absence of a clear judgment that one object is better than another, choose freely between them? And can the will be moved by God without losing its freedom?

Aquinas held that the will is the rational appetite, the faculty by which the soul tends toward the good as presented by the intellect. Because the will's object is the good as such — the universal good, which only the divine essence fully realizes — the will cannot be compelled by any finite good, which always falls short of that plenitude. In this sense the will is free: it retains the power to withhold its assent from any particular good. Scotus insisted more emphatically on the will's intrinsic indifference and gave it greater independence from the intellect's direction; for Scotus, the will has a sui generis power of self-determination that cannot be reduced to the intellect's act of judgment. Ockham radicalized the Scotist position and pushed it toward a voluntarism in which the will's freedom is nearly absolute, constrained only by God's own sovereign freedom.

Pico's theses navigate this terrain with characteristic eclecticism. He is sympathetic to the Scotist emphasis on the will's intrinsic freedom, but he does not adopt the full Ockhamist voluntarism; his philosophical instincts are too deeply Neoplatonic and intellectualist to place the will's indifference above the intellect's act of knowing. The *Conclusiones* include several theses drawn from Albert the Great on the soul's rational powers and several from Aquinas on the relationship between intellect and will. In his *Apologia tredecim quaestionum* (1487), Pico also engages Question 8, which concerns the nature of heresy and the role of the will in matters of belief, suggesting that the question of whether a false belief is heretical depends on whether it is willful — whether the believer has the freedom to consider contrary evidence and refuses to do so. This juridical-theological context shows that Pico's account of free will is not confined to metaphysical speculation but has direct implications for the ethics of intellectual responsibility.

## Kabbalistic Sources

Alongside the Aristotelian and Thomistic sources, Pico draws on Kabbalistic ideas about the soul's capacity for union with God. In the Kabbalistic tradition, particularly in the Abulafian strand to which Pico was most attracted, the highest form of human fulfillment involves *devekut* — literally "cleaving" or "adhesion" to God. This is not the merger of two equal terms but the soul's assimilation to the divine intellect through an intense practice of contemplation focused on the divine names and letters. For Abulafia, this practice is both intellectual and volitional: the soul must will the act of concentration, sustain it through disciplined effort, and direct its attention without deflection toward the letters of the Tetragrammaton and the operations performed upon them. The freedom required for *devekut* is therefore not the freedom of indifference but the freedom of directed, sustained effort — precisely the kind of ascetic self-discipline that Pico describes in the *Oration* as the condition for the soul's transformation.

As Howlett has observed, this Kabbalistic dimension reinforces the exceptionalist character of Pico's free will. *Devekut* is not available to everyone; it requires a level of intellectual preparation, philosophical formation, and spiritual discipline that most human beings do not possess and do not pursue. Pico never claims that the ascent is possible for everyone; the *Oration* addresses an elite of philosophical practitioners, not humanity in general. The democratic resonance that modern readers find in the passage about Adam's free self-fashioning is an anachronistic imposition. What Pico offers is not a universal right to self-determination but a vocation — an arduous mystical program available to those capable of undertaking it and willing to make the sacrifices it demands.

## The Exceptionalist Reading

Howlett's *Re-evaluating Pico* (2021) has made the exceptionalist structure of Pico's philosophy one of its central analytical findings. Unlike Ficino, whose vision of the soul's renewal has a collective dimension — he imagines leading a community of philosophical souls upward together, on the model of Socrates guiding his friends — Pico describes a solitary journey. The ascent is individual, the discipline is personal, and there is no programmatic return. In Howlett's reading, this means that Pico's philosophy does not generate a politics; it does not tell us how to organize a just society or how to reform institutions. It tells a philosophical soul what to do with itself, and the answer is to detach from the world, master the disciplines of philosophy and magic and Kabbalah, and ascend toward a union with God that has the character of an ending rather than a beginning.

This exceptionalism has implications for how we understand free will in Pico's system. Freedom, for Pico, is not an attribute of human beings as such — a possession that attaches to the species regardless of cultivation or effort. It is a capacity that must be actualized through discipline, and one that most people never actualize to the degree required for the ascent. The unfree soul, enslaved to the passions and to the inclinations imposed by celestial influence, is precisely the soul that astrology might successfully predict; the free soul, the soul that has worked through the philosophical and Kabbalistic disciplines to achieve rational self-governance, is the one that has escaped the domain of natural causation that the astrologer presumes to read. Free will is thus, in the final analysis, an achievement rather than a given — a prize of philosophical labor rather than a metaphysical birthright.
