# Soul

No concept in Pico della Mirandola's thought is more structurally central, or more densely layered with competing traditions, than the soul. Pico's soul-doctrine is not a coherent synthesis assembled in advance and then applied consistently across his works. It is, rather, a live intersection of at least three major philosophical traditions — the Platonic, the Aristotelian, and the Kabbalistic — which Pico holds in productive tension, drawing on each for particular purposes and mapping their vocabularies onto one another in ways that illuminate his syncretic program even as they create genuine doctrinal instability. Understanding what Pico means by the soul requires tracing these three sources, seeing how they bear on his cosmological account of the soul's intermediate position, and grasping how that position grounds both his ascent program and his engagement with magic.

The most fundamental inheritance is Platonic. Plato's tripartite soul — rational (logistikon), spirited (thumoeidês), and appetitive (epithumêtikon) — is the background structure for Pico's anthropology throughout his writings. The tripartition means that the human soul is internally differentiated, containing within itself a hierarchy that mirrors the cosmic hierarchy: the rational part looks upward toward the intelligible; the appetitive part looks downward toward the material; the spirited or passionate part is the mediating faculty that can be aligned with either. For Pico, as for Ficino and for the Neoplatonic tradition generally, the Platonic tripartition is not merely a psychological taxonomy but a metaphysical statement about the soul's position in the order of being. The rational or intellective part of the soul participates in the angelic intelligences and, through them, in the divine mind. The appetitive part connects the soul to the sensory and material world. The human being is thus internally the whole cosmos in miniature — a microcosm that contains within its own structure the full range of the created order.

The Aristotelian tradition adds a different and in some ways tension-generating vocabulary. Aristotle's hylomorphic account of the soul defines it as the form of the body — the actuality (entelecheia) of a natural body that has the potential for life — and this account sits uneasily with the Platonic view of the soul as a substance capable of independent existence apart from the body. Pico's engagement with Aristotelianism is mediated heavily by his Paduan formation and by his relationship with Elijah del Medigo, the Cretan Jewish Averroist who served as his informal teacher during the late 1470s and early 1480s. The Averroist reading of Aristotle on the intellect — specifically the question of whether the Aristotelian intellect is individual or universal — is one of the most contested issues in Renaissance philosophy, and Pico's position on it is not straightforward. Among his 900 Conclusions are theses attributed to Averroes, including positions that appear to deny individual immortality by identifying the intellect with a single universal Active Intellect in which individual human intellects participate but into which they are ultimately dissolved. Pico presents himself as reconciling these Averroist positions with orthodox Christian doctrine on individual immortality, but the precise terms of that reconciliation are among the most debated questions in Pico scholarship. What is clear is that his engagement with the Averroist tradition is not merely academic: the Aristotelian account of how the individual human intellect is related to the universal Active Intellect shapes his account of mystical union, because union with the Active Intellect is, for the Peripatetic mystical tradition running through al-Farabi, Avicenna, Averroes, and Maimonides, the philosophical equivalent of what Pico elsewhere calls the mors osculi.

The Kabbalistic neshamah/ruah/nefesh tripartition provides Pico with a third vocabulary for the soul that he maps systematically onto the Neoplatonic distinctions. The highest soul — neshamah — is the divine breath breathed into Adam at creation, the intellectual and spiritual dimension of the human being most closely connected to the divine. The ruah — spirit or breath in the sense of animating life-force — corresponds to the intermediate, emotional, and volitional dimension. The nefesh — the vital or vegetative soul — corresponds to the life-force shared with animals. Pico reads this threefold structure as confirmation of the Platonic tripartition: the neshamah maps onto the rational or intellective soul, the ruah onto the spirited or passionate soul, and the nefesh onto the vegetative or appetitive soul. Whether this mapping represents a genuine convergence of traditions or an imposition of one tradition's categories onto another is precisely the kind of question that guides Pico's syncretic method: he presents the convergence as evidence that the three traditions — Greek philosophical, Latin Christian, and Hebrew mystical — are approaching the same truth from different directions. The Kabbalistic vocabulary adds particular richness because the neshamah, in Kabbalistic theology, is not merely a psychological faculty but a divine spark — something of divine origin embedded in the human creature and capable, through proper cultivation, of returning to its source in the divine emanation.

The soul's position in the cosmic hierarchy is for Pico the metaphysical basis of everything else he wants to say about human beings. Following Plotinus and, through Plotinus, the later Neoplatonic tradition as Ficino transmitted it, Pico holds that the human soul is uniquely intermediate — a being that exists at the boundary between the intelligible and material worlds and connects them. This intermediacy is not accidental: the soul is the copula mundi, the bond of the world, whose function in the cosmic order is to provide the connecting link between the higher, purely intelligible realm and the lower, purely material realm. Ficino makes this doctrine central to his Platonic Theology; Pico inherits it but inflects it in his own way. The soul's intermediacy means that it is not purely at home anywhere in the cosmic order. It does not belong definitively to the angelic world, whose inhabitants are purely intellective beings without the downward pull of embodiment, nor to the animal world, whose inhabitants are defined entirely by sensory and appetitive life. The human soul belongs to both worlds because it contains within itself the faculties proper to each — and this double belonging is, as the Oration argues, both the danger and the glory of the human condition.

The danger is degeneration. The soul that allows its lower faculties to govern its higher ones — appetite dominating reason, sensory pleasure displacing intellectual aspiration — descends in the direction of brutality. Pico's imagery for this descent is vivid: the chameleon that takes on the colors of whatever surrounds it, Proteus who shifts through every form without settling into one. Both figures carry negative connotations in Pico's sources, and both are used in the Oration to illustrate the possibility of downward assimilation. The glory is ascent: the soul whose rational faculty governs appetite and whose intellective faculty governs reason is positioned for progressive assimilation to the angelic life and ultimately, through the Kabbalistic techniques of divine-name meditation and letter combination, for union with the divine. The soul is suited by its intermediate nature for operations that neither pure intelligences nor purely material beings can perform, and this suitability is the ground of the ascent program.

The soul's intermediate position also explains what natural magic means for Pico and why it is not, as it might first appear, a descent into material manipulation. The soul's capacity to connect the intelligible and material worlds is precisely what makes it the natural instrument of magic. The magician who understands the sympathies binding different levels of creation together — the correspondences between herbs, stones, celestial bodies, and angelic intelligences — is using the soul's mediating function in a theoretically articulate way. The vehicle through which these operations are accomplished is the spiritus — the subtle body or pneumatic medium, neither fully material nor fully immaterial, that the Stoic and Neoplatonic traditions posited as the connecting medium between the soul and the gross physical body. In Ficino's theory of spiritus-magic, as in the related traditions that Pico inherits, the spiritus is the channel through which celestial influences flow into the human organism and through which the prepared soul can attract and concentrate those influences. Pico's treatment of natural magic in the Oration and in the magical theses of the 900 Conclusions presupposes this spiritus-doctrine: the soul, through its spirital medium, is naturally oriented toward the cosmic sympathies that magic exploits, and the properly trained philosopher-magician is simply exercising the soul's natural mediating function in a disciplined and conscious way.

The Heptaplus, Pico's sevenfold commentary on the first chapter of Genesis composed in 1489, develops the soul-doctrine in the genre of biblical exegesis. The text's structural architecture distributes the analysis of creation across seven expositions corresponding to seven levels of reality: the supracelestial or angelic world, the celestial world, the elemental or material world, a fourth level corresponding to the "human world," and then books five through seven that treat the interconnections among these worlds and their relationships to the divine source. The fourth book, focused on the human world, analyzes the human soul in its own proper nature — its tripartite structure, its intermediacy, its capacity for both ascent and descent. The final, eighth "book" — technically an epilogue added to the seven — addresses the opening words of Genesis, "In the beginning" (Bereshit), anagogically, reading them as pointing to the divine source from which all being flows and to which the perfected soul returns. This anagogical epilogue is the highest level of Pico's Heptaplus commentary, corresponding to the unitive stage of the Dionysian triplex via, and it is here that the soul's relationship to its divine origin is addressed most directly. The soul in its highest dimension — the neshamah, the intellect, the Platonic rational soul — is, in Pico's reading of Genesis, not created from nothing alongside the material world but breathed into Adam from the divine essence itself, giving the soul a dignity and a directedness toward God that distinguishes it from every other creature.

The Commento sopra una canzona d'amore, Pico's commentary on Girolamo Benivieni's Platonic love poem, approaches the soul from yet another angle: through love theory. Drawing on Plato's Symposium, Pico's Commento analyzes the soul's ascent as a movement of desire — eros conceived not as appetite but as the soul's recognition of and attraction toward the Beauty that is its original source and proper end. The mors osculi — the kiss of death that represents mystical union — appears in the Commento in connection with the soul's dissolution of its individual boundaries in the experience of divine love. This treatment makes explicit the affective dimension of the ascent program that in the Oration is carried more by imagery than by systematic argument: the soul does not merely ascend through intellectual effort but is drawn upward by love, by an erotic force that originates in the divine and that the soul, in recognizing Beauty, is experiencing as a reflection of its own deepest nature.

The Averroist question — whether the individual human intellect is genuinely distinct from the universal Active Intellect or is ultimately absorbed into it — haunts these positive accounts of union without being fully resolved in Pico's corpus. Some of the theses attributed to Averroes in the 900 Conclusions appear to entail that individual immortality is philosophically indefensible — that what survives death is not a personal intellect but an impersonal intellective principle returning to the universal Active Intellect from which it was temporarily differentiated. Pico's claim to reconcile this with Christian doctrine of individual immortality is presented in the 900 Conclusions but not elaborated with the philosophical precision that would be required for a convincing demonstration. What Pico does consistently is locate the value and goal of the individual soul's existence in its capacity for union with something higher than itself, and to treat the dissolution of individual boundaries in that union not as a loss but as the soul's highest fulfillment. Whether this constitutes genuine personal immortality in the Christian theological sense remains a productive ambiguity in his thought — one that the Roman commission found troubling and that subsequent scholarship has never fully resolved.
