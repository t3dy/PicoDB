Moses ben Nachman Gerondi (1194–1270 CE), known in the Jewish tradition as *RaMBaN* (Rabbi Moshe ben Nachman) and in European scholarly usage as Nahmanides, was the dominant rabbinic authority of thirteenth-century Catalonia: a halakhic decisor, Talmudic commentator, physician, communal leader, and the most influential biblical exegete of medieval Kabbalistic Judaism. Born in Gerona in northern Spain, he represented the culmination of the Geronese Kabbalistic circle, a group of early thirteenth-century Spanish Jewish mystics who synthesized Neoplatonic theosophy with Talmudic learning and biblical commentary. His *Commentary on the Torah* (*Perush ha-Torah*), completed around 1267 and regarded in subsequent generations as one of the authoritative commentaries on the Pentateuch, weaves together the four levels of scriptural interpretation recognized in the Jewish tradition — *peshat* (plain sense), *remez* (allegory), *derash* (homiletical exposition), and *sod* (mystical secret) — and grounds its deepest interpretations in the system of the ten divine attributes or *sefirot* (*sefiroth*) that defines Kabbalistic theology. This commentary became one of the most widely copied biblical texts in medieval Jewish manuscript culture and, through Recanati's elaboration and Mithridates' Latin translation of Recanati, a foundational source for Pico della Mirandola's encounter with Kabbalistic thought.

Pico's relationship to Nahmanides is one of the most philologically intricate questions in the study of his sources, and Chaim Wirszubski's meticulous investigation in *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (Harvard University Press, 1989) remains the authoritative point of departure. Wirszubski begins from the firm fact that Nahmanides is explicitly named by Pico — unlike Abraham Abulafia, who is never mentioned by name despite being one of Pico's most important Kabbalistic sources. The earliest explicit reference to Nahmanides in Pico's surviving writings appears in the *Heptaplus* (1489), where Pico calls him "Moses Gerundinensis, theologus primae celebritatis apud Hebraeos" — Moses of Gerona, a theologian of the highest reputation among the Hebrews (Garin ed., 350; Opera, 52). The *Heptaplus* also refers to "uterque Moses" — both Moses[es], meaning Maimonides and Nahmanides together — as the two supreme Hebrew theologians whose authority validates Pico's interpretive program. This explicit identification in the *Heptaplus* contrasts with the *900 Conclusions* of 1486, where Nahmanides is not mentioned among the Jewish authorities in the famous *Apology* passage listing the four modes of biblical interpretation, and where no thesis requires the hypothesis of direct acquaintance with his commentary.

Wirszubski's argument is that the apparent resemblances between Pico's Kabbalistic theses in the *Conclusions* and Nahmanidean doctrine are almost certainly mediated by Recanati rather than by direct reading of Nahmanides himself. Menahem Recanati (late thirteenth — early fourteenth century), an Italian Kabbalist who drew systematically and extensively on Nahmanides' commentary, wrote a *Commentary on the Torah* that Wirszubski established as the single most influential Kabbalistic source for Pico's Kabbalistic theses in the *Conclusiones* (Wirszubski 1989, 63, summarized in the foreword). Mithridates translated Recanati's Pentateuch commentary for Pico — the translation is now lost, though a summary of its contents made by Gaffarel in 1651 survives, and a manuscript fragment has been identified in Genoa — and Wirszubski demonstrated that this lost translation was the primary vehicle through which Nahmanidean ideas entered Pico's *Conclusions*. The chain of transmission is thus: Nahmanides' *Commentary on the Torah* (Hebrew, 1267) → Recanati's *Commentary on the Torah* (Hebrew, c. 1300), which quotes and elaborates Nahmanides extensively → Mithridates' Latin translation of Recanati (summer 1486) → Pico's Kabbalistic theses in the *900 Conclusions* (November 1486).

The most vivid illustration of this mediated transmission is the tradition of the fifty gates of understanding (*sha'are binah*), a Talmudic-Kabbalistic concept that Pico encountered in multiple sources and that became structurally central to his *Heptaplus*. The *Babylonian Talmud* (Tractate *Rosh Hashanah* 21b) records the tradition that fifty gates of understanding were created in the world and that forty-nine of them were given to Moses, with the fiftieth withheld — the basis for the Talmudic homily that Moses was made "a little lower than the angels" (Psalm 8:6). This tradition was elaborated in different ways by Kabbalistic writers. In the introduction to his *Commentary on the Torah*, Nahmanides alludes to the mystical significance of the divine names encoded in the Torah and to the tradition that Moses received special revelatory access to the Torah's deeper meanings on Mount Sinai. His commentary on Genesis opens with the assertion that the opening word *Bereshit* "is a hint at the sefirah called *Hokhmah*" — divine Wisdom, the second of the ten sefirot — establishing from the very first word of scripture the Kabbalistic hermeneutic principle that the narrative of creation is simultaneously a disclosure of the inner life of the divine. The *Supplements* volume on Abravanel, Alemanno, and Pico notes that Nahmanides "hypostatized *Hokhmah* in the manner of the Talmud, but also explicitly drew upon the Targum Yerushalmi in equating *Reshit* [beginning] with *Hokhmah*," declaring at the outset of his commentary that *bereshit* "is a hint at the sefirah that is called *Hokhmah*, the foundation of everything" (Ogren 2014, 32–33, citing Nahmanides on Genesis 1:1).

This hermeneutic move — the identification of the Genesis creation narrative with the dynamic of the sefirot — passed through Recanati's elaboration into the Latin materials that Pico read, and it lies at the heart of the *Heptaplus*. Black (2006) has demonstrated, in what remains the fullest analysis of the *Heptaplus* as a work of biblical hermeneutics, that the structure of the *Heptaplus* — seven expositions of the six days of creation plus a final exposition of the opening word *Bereshit* — is specifically keyed to the forty-nine gates of understanding: "I argue that this structure reflects the forty-nine gates of understanding through which, according to texts read by Pico, Moses ascended, and knowledge of which he concealed in the Genesis account" (Black 2006, 14). The *Heptaplus*'s seven-fold structure is not merely a convenient schematism but an encoded reference to the Kabbalistic tradition that the seven days (and their multiples, up to the jubilee of forty-nine) represent the full compass of revealed knowledge. Pico makes this explicit in the second proem of the *Heptaplus*, where he describes Moses' writing as a true image of the world "according to the exemplar which he had seen on the mountain" — the forty-nine gates of understanding that Moses received at Sinai and concealed within the seemingly simple narrative of creation.

Wirszubski identifies a quotation from Nahmanides' preface in the *Heptaplus*: the passage where Pico declares that the entire law consists of divine names is an elaboration of Nahmanides' statement that "the whole Torah consists of His names" — the tradition, recorded in Nahmanides' commentary, that the words of the Torah can be differently divided so as to form a continuous sequence of divine names (*Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism*, 213–14, with the relevant Hebrew text). However, the chronology of Pico's engagement with this tradition matters: Wirszubski argues that the most likely direct source for this idea in the *Conclusions* is Abulafia's *De Secretis Legis*, not Nahmanides directly, since Abulafia quotes Nahmanides verbatim in this passage and Pico demonstrably read Abulafia before the *Conclusions* were completed, while direct access to Nahmanides cannot be established for the same period. Reuchlin's later collation in his *De Arte Cabalistica* (1517) conflates Nahmanides and Pico in a way that confirms both the Nahmanidean background and Recanati as Pico's actual source, since the text Reuchlin italicizes as a quotation from Nahmanides turns out to be a word-for-word quotation of Pico's thirty-third Kabbalistic thesis, which is itself a word-for-word quotation of Recanati (Wirszubski 1989, 212–13).

The picture that emerges from Wirszubski's analysis, confirmed and extended by Black's work on the *Heptaplus* and by Howlett's account of Pico's Jewish sources more broadly, is that Nahmanides functioned for Pico in two distinct registers. In the first register — the *900 Conclusions* of 1486 — Nahmanides' ideas entered Pico's thought through the thick mediation of Recanati and Mithridates, without Pico necessarily having access to Nahmanides' own commentary in Latin translation. In the second register — the *Heptaplus* of 1489 — Pico had by this point read at least some material directly from a translation of Nahmanides' Genesis commentary (the *Liber de Ordine Geneseos* translated by Mithridates and surviving in the Chisiana manuscript, though with a lacuna in the crucial passage about the two mirrors), and invokes Nahmanides explicitly by name as one of the two supreme Hebrew theologians. The *Heptaplus* is thus the work in which Nahmanides becomes a named authority rather than a background presence, and in which the forty-nine gates of understanding — the structuring principle that Nahmanides had established for Kabbalistic reading of Moses' revelation at Sinai — become the explicit architectonic principle of Pico's own hermeneutical enterprise.

The significance of Nahmanides within Pico's thought extends beyond specific textual borrowings to a deeper hermeneutical orientation. Nahmanides represents, within the Jewish tradition, the position that rigorous literal and legal scholarship and mystical Kabbalistic interpretation are not in tension but are complementary dimensions of a single practice of reading. His commentary constantly moves from grammatical and legal precision to Kabbalistic depth, treating the literal sense (*peshat*) as the necessary surface beneath which the mystical senses operate. This hermeneutical pluralism — the view that a single text can sustain simultaneous literal, philosophical, and mystical readings without any one level cancelling the others — is structural to Pico's own project in the *Heptaplus*, where seven complete expositions of the same opening verses of Genesis are offered not as competing interpretations but as complementary illuminations of a text that contains all levels of meaning at once. The Nahmanidean tradition of Kabbalistic commentary on Genesis provided Pico with both a specific body of exegetical content and a broader model of reading: the idea that the Genesis text, properly deciphered, contains encoded within it the full taxonomy of created things — the forty-nine gates of understanding — and that Moses' prophetic ascent at Sinai was simultaneously an intellectual ascent to the knowledge of all created natures and a mystical reception of the divine names that underlie them. Pico translated this Nahmanidean and post-Nahmanidean tradition into Latin, incorporated it within a Neoplatonic framework of ascending worlds, and presented it to his Italian audience as the deepest confirmation that philosophy, Christian theology, and Jewish mystical reading all point toward the same transcendent truth.
