Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn Yahya ibn al-Sa'igh, known in the medieval Latin West as Avempace, was born in Zaragoza around 1085 and died in Fez in 1138 or 1139. He stands as the first major philosopher produced by Andalusian Islam and the founding figure in a distinctively western Islamic philosophical tradition that would culminate with Averroes a generation later. His Latin name, Avempace, is a corruption of the Arabic *Ibn Bajja* (the name in the Italian Apologia edition is rendered *Ibn Bagja*), and it is under this Latinized form that he appears in Pico della Mirandola's *900 Conclusions* of 1486. Though his philosophical output survives largely in fragmentary form, Ibn Bajja's thought on the intellect and on the possibility of philosophical self-sufficiency exerted an influence on the subsequent Arabic peripatetic tradition that Pico encountered, primarily through the mediating commentary of Averroes.

The intellectual context for understanding Ibn Bajja's importance to Pico is the Arabic peripatetic tradition of Aristotelianism as it developed in Islamic Andalusia. Pico assembled in his *900 Conclusions* an extraordinary inventory of philosophical authorities, organizing the Arabic thinkers under a single rubric: "Conclusiones secundum doctrinam Arabum qui ut plurimum peripateticos se profitentur: Averroem, Avicennam, Alpharabium, Avempacem, Isaac, Abumaron, Moysem, et Maumeth" — that is, conclusions according to the doctrine of the Arabs who for the most part profess to be Peripatetics: Averroes, Avicenna, Al-Farabi, Avempace, Isaac [of Narbonne], Avenzoar, Maimonides, and a figure identified as Mohammed of Toledo (Farmer 1998, 250). This formulation is remarkable for what it claims: that the Arab thinkers constitute a recognizable philosophical tradition, defined by Aristotelian commitments, and that Avempace belongs within this family alongside the more widely cited Averroes and Avicenna. Of the eight Arabic authorities named in this section, Pico devoted forty-one conclusions to Averroes, twelve to Avicenna, eleven to Al-Farabi — and only two to Avempace, making this one of the most compressed encounters in the entire collection.

The two theses ascribed to Avempace in the *900 Conclusions* are numerically catalogued 14.1 and 14.2 in Farmer's edition. The first asserts that in the heavens matter exists as a different kind from the matter of inferior, sublunary things (*In coelo est materia alterius rationis a materia inferiorum*). The second proposes that light and color do not differ essentially (*Lux et color essentialiter non differunt*). These are not propositions that Pico invented; they are extracted positions representing Avempace's positions within the Arabic peripatetic debate over celestial matter and the theory of perception. Farmer's commentary identifies Averroes as Pico's immediate source here — Averroes frequently cites and debates Avempace in his commentaries on Aristotle — and notes that shortly before the Roman disputation of 1486, Pico attempted to obtain further information from his associate Elia del Medigo concerning Avempace's *De anima*, the indirect source for the second of these two theses (Farmer 1998, 280–81, citing Kieszkowski 1964, 74). This detail reveals something important about Pico's scholarly methods: he was aware that Avempace held distinctive positions within the Arabic philosophical tradition and was actively attempting to access them more directly, even if his immediate textual access was mediated by Averroes's commentaries and by del Medigo's reports.

Elia del Medigo, the Jewish Averroist philosopher who was Pico's principal guide through the Arabic peripatetic corpus during his Paduan years (c. 1480–82), was himself deeply informed by Averroes's evolving positions on the intellect. Edelheit (Amos Edelheit, *A Philosopher at the Crossroads*, Maynooth University) notes del Medigo's awareness that Averroes changed his views on the unicity of the intellect over the course of his career: "after following Avempace (Abū Bakr ibn Bāğğa), he turned in another direction as a result of much contemplation" (Edelheit, *Crossroads*, 52, citing del Medigo's text). This is a crucial datum. The early Averroes, in his commentaries on the soul and the intellect, was in dialogue with and frequently critical of Avempace's distinctive position; the mature Averroes shifted toward the monopsychist doctrine for which he became infamous in the Latin West. The fact that del Medigo communicated this intellectual history to Pico means that Pico would have understood Avempace not simply as an Arabic authority but as a specific position in an ongoing debate — the position against which Averroes's more radical doctrine of the unitary intellect was partly defined.

Ibn Bajja's contribution to the intellect debate rests chiefly on his surviving treatise *Risalat al-Wada'* (the Epistle of Farewell) and, more importantly for the medieval transmission, his *Tadbir al-Mutawahhid* — which Latin readers knew as the *Regimen del Solitario* or, in the English scholarly convention established for this period, "The Governance of the Solitary" or "The Rule of the Solitary." This work, incomplete in Ibn Bajja's own lifetime, takes as its central question how the individual philosopher can achieve intellectual perfection without the aid of either prophetic revelation or the ideal political community described in Al-Farabi's political philosophy. The answer Ibn Bajja develops is that the philosopher — the *mutawahhid*, literally the "solitary one" — can, through rigorous philosophical discipline and the progressive purification of his intellective acts, achieve contact or conjunction (*ittissal*) with the Active Intellect. This conjunction, conceived as the highest human felicity (*sa'ada*), is accomplished through philosophical self-governance, not through mystical illumination or political participation in a virtuous community. The *mutawahhid* is not a hermit or an ascetic in any religious sense; he is the philosopher who, living in a corrupt or imperfect city, turns inward toward the intelligible and governs himself according to intellectual principles.

This account of philosophical felicity as a form of self-sufficient intellectual ascent has an obvious resonance with certain passages in Pico's *Oration on the Dignity of Man*, though the connection must be drawn carefully. In the *Oration*, Pico describes a program of philosophical ascent from moral philosophy through dialectic to natural philosophy and finally to theology and mystical union, characterizing the different stages through their representative philosophical figures: "Among the Arabs, in Averroes there is an unshaken firmness, in Avempace, as in Al-Farabi, something grave and well meditated, in Avicenna something divine and Platonic" (*Est apud Arabes in Averroe firmum et inconcusum, in Avempace, in Alpharabio grave et meditatum, in Avicenna divinum atque Platonicum* — Borghesi, Papio, and Riva, eds., *Oration*, 187). This passage, which assigns each Arabic philosopher a distinctive philosophical character, places Avempace in the company of Al-Farabi as figures of "grave and well-meditated" philosophical seriousness — a characterization that resonates with the earnest, self-disciplined figure of the *mutawahhid* in the *Tadbir al-Mutawahhid*. The parallel is circumstantial rather than demonstrably textual, since no evidence suggests that Pico had direct access to the *Tadbir al-Mutawahhid* in any form. Yet the intellectual temperament Pico assigns to Avempace is consistent with what the Arabic tradition reports of his central concerns.

The question of Pico's textual access to Avempace is therefore of considerable importance. Farmer's identification of Averroes as the immediate source for the Avempace theses in the *900 Conclusions* points to the character of Pico's engagement: he encountered Avempace as a debated figure within the Arabic peripatetic tradition, not as a thinker whose works he had read independently. Recent scholarship by Simone Fellina, cited by Edelheit, argues that del Medigo's texts may have served as sources for Pico's theses on Averroes, Avicenna, Al-Farabi, and Avempace — suggesting that the compact group of Arabic theses in the *900 Conclusions* reflects, in part, the philosophical program that del Medigo transmitted during the Padua years (Edelheit, *Crossroads*, 52, n. 79, citing Fellina 2017). If this is correct, then Pico's two Avempace theses should be read as positions within the debates that del Medigo's Averroism made available to him, rather than as evidence of independent access to Avempace's texts.

The two theses themselves — on celestial matter and on the identity of light and color — are not immediately the most philosophically striking in the entire Arabic section, but they participate in the broader metaphysical and psychological debates of the Arabic tradition. The thesis about celestial matter (14.1) engages the question, which runs from Al-Farabi and Avicenna through Averroes, of whether the heavenly spheres are composed of the same prime matter as sublunary bodies or of a distinct kind of matter. Farmer notes that this thesis is directly opposed to Avicenna's position (8.3) established earlier in the collection, indicating that Pico understood the two theses as staking out competing positions in a genuine philosophical disagreement. The second thesis (14.2), on light and color, points toward the theory of perception and the psychology of sensation — issues that bear directly on the theory of the intellect and the question of how the passive intellect receives intelligible forms, one of the defining questions of the whole Arabic peripatetic tradition.

For readers of Pico's work as a whole, the significance of Avempace lies less in these two compressed theses than in what they represent: Pico's commitment to including every major figure of the Arabic peripatetic tradition within his syncretic encyclopedia of philosophical opinions. The *900 Conclusions* are not a collection of positions Pico endorses; they are, as Farmer established in his definitive 1998 edition and study, a map of the entire landscape of philosophical and theological opinion as Pico understood it. Within that map, Avempace occupies a modest but clearly defined place: the first and, in the Latin tradition, the foundational Andalusian philosopher, whose positions on matter and perception were contested by Averroes and whose account of philosophical felicity through self-governing intellectual discipline represented one possible model for the philosophical life. The fact that Pico felt the need to represent him, however briefly, alongside the much more extensively cited Averroes and Avicenna testifies to his ambition to create a genuinely comprehensive inventory of Arabic philosophical opinion. The *mutawahhid* — the solitary philosopher governing himself through intellectual discipline — haunts the margins of Pico's own program, even if the textual connections remain indirect and mediated through commentary tradition rather than through the *Tadbir al-Mutawahhid* itself.
