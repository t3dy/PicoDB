The term *anagoge* (ἀναγωγή, Latin *anagoge* or *anagogia*), meaning literally "a leading upward," designates in ancient and medieval usage both an interpretive movement — the movement of reading that carries the mind from the letter of a text toward its highest spiritual significance — and an ontological movement — the soul's actual ascent through successive levels of reality toward union with the divine. For Giovanni Pico della Mirandola, these two meanings are not merely analogous but structurally identical: to read Scripture anagogically is to perform, in and through the act of reading, the very ascent toward God that anagogical interpretation points toward. This identification of hermeneutical and metaphysical ascent is the governing insight of the *Heptaplus* (1489), the sevenfold commentary on the first words of Genesis that represents Pico's most sustained experiment in biblical hermeneutics and arguably his most complete statement of what it means to read a sacred text philosophically.

In the standard medieval formulation, the anagogical sense is the fourth of the four senses of Scripture — literal, allegorical, moral (or tropological), and anagogical — that together constitute the "fourfold method" (*quadruplex modus exponendi*) codified in the prologues to the *Glossa ordinaria* and Nicholas of Lyra's *Postilla super totam Bibliam*. As Pico himself acknowledges in the *Apology*, this is the normal Christian framework for biblical interpretation: the literal sense treats of events as they happened; allegory reads Old Testament figures as types of the New; the tropological or moral sense draws instruction for conduct; and the anagogical sense orients the reader toward the eschatological fulfillment to come. The pedagogical mnemonic that condensed this scheme for generations of readers ran: *littera gesta docet, quid credas allegoria, moralis quid agas, quo tendas anagogia* — "the letter teaches what happened; allegory what you should believe; the moral sense what you should do; anagogy where you are tending." In this formulation, "where you are tending" — *quo tendas* — refers to the eschatological horizon, the heavenly Jerusalem as destination, the life of God "which the heavenly inhabitants behold with face unveiled." The Jerusalem example is itself formulaic: according to the Glossa, Jerusalem read anagogically is "the life of all heavenly things, which behold God with his face revealed."

Crofton Black's study of the *Heptaplus* has shown with particular clarity that Pico's relationship to this mainstream fourfold tradition is one of selective transformation rather than simple adoption (Black 2006). The *Heptaplus* does not apply the four senses in any schematic way; the work proceeds through seven sequential expositions of Genesis 1 — one each for the elemental world, the celestial world, the angelic world, the human nature, the interrelation of all worlds, the happiness of all worlds, and the condition of eternal life — before arriving at an eighth section that is formally separate and structurally exceptional. This eighth section, the *Expositio primae dictionis* or exposition of the single opening word *bereshit* ("in the beginning"), is where the anagogical dimension of the *Heptaplus* most explicitly surfaces, and where Pico departs most dramatically from the medieval commentarial tradition he claims to surpass.

By the seventh of the seven regular expositions, the *Heptaplus* has moved through its analysis of each of the three worlds and their interrelations to arrive at the theme of *felicitas* — the beatitude that is the proper end of human life. This seventh exposition describes the soul's return to God as the ultimate goal of all creation, a return figured in the progressive movement of sacred history from the Fall through the giving of the Law, the coming of Christ, and the continuing journey toward the heavenly Jerusalem. But it is the eighth section that enacts, rather than describes, the anagogical movement. Having completed seven expositions corresponding to the seven days of creation, Pico turns in the eighth section to "another method of interpretation" (*aliam interpretandi rationem*) in which he reads the single word *bereshit* through a technique of letter combination derived from his engagement with Kabbalistic exegesis. By combining and rearranging the letters of *bereshit*, Pico extracts from the single opening word of Scripture a sentence that encapsulates the entire meaning of the *Heptaplus* as a whole. The eight is the number beyond the seven — beyond the week, beyond creation — and the eighth section of the *Heptaplus* points toward a mode of reading that cannot be captured by any of the seven expositions, a mode that is in principle inexhaustible, just as divine wisdom itself exceeds the finite categories of the created world.

The structure of the *Heptaplus* as a whole is itself an anagogical encoding. Black argues that the work's 7×7 arrangement — seven expositions each of seven chapters — generates forty-nine interpretive units corresponding to the forty-nine gates of understanding through which, according to Kabbalistic texts known to Pico through the Latin translations of Flavius Mithridates, Moses ascended on Sinai (Black 2006). The fiftieth gate, *Ein Sof* in the Kabbalistic tradition — the Infinite, the gate beyond all gates — is what Moses alone approached, attaining forty-nine gates but not the fiftieth, which remains beyond human grasp in this life. This numerological structure is not ornamental but architecturally significant: the reader who moves through the forty-nine chapters of the *Heptaplus* is being invited to perform the same anagogical movement that Moses performed in ascending the mountain. The reading itself, conducted rightly, is the ascent.

The model for this identification of exegetical ascent with spiritual ascent is Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite. In the *Mystical Theology*, Moses serves as the paradigmatic figure of the soul's movement toward divine union. Moses first purifies himself, then separates himself from the multitude, then ascends the mountain in the company of the elect priests, and finally breaks free even from them to penetrate alone into the divine darkness where God dwells — a darkness that is the excess of light, not its absence. This movement is described by Pseudo-Dionysius as an ascent that leaves behind all human knowledge and all positive affirmations about the divine until the soul stands before what is "beyond all assertion" and "beyond all negation." Black identifies Pseudo-Dionysian thought as a crucial source for Pico's theory of allegory in the *Heptaplus*, particularly for the idea that proper reading (*anagoge*) is not merely a cognitive operation but an epistemological transformation in which the intellect is itself carried upward and changed by its encounter with what exceeds it (Black 2006). The parallels between Pseudo-Dionysius's language of ascent and Pico's account of interpretive method in the second proem of the *Heptaplus* are among the strongest structural connections in the work's intellectual genealogy.

In the *Oration* (written as an introduction to the *900 Conclusions*), the ascent program that Pico sketches out for the disputant — moving through moral philosophy to purify the soul, natural philosophy to investigate the cosmos, theology to contemplate the divine — is anagogical in structure even if the word does not appear. The three ascending registers of philosophy correspond to the three stages of spiritual purification familiar from Pseudo-Dionysian hierarchiology: purgation (*katharsis*), illumination (*photismos*), and union (*henosis*). Pico's famous image of the philosopher as one who first becomes "like to the people" in moral self-formation, then "like to Ezekiel" in the investigation of nature, and finally "like to Paul" or "like to Dionysius" in the mystical ascent beyond all created categories, describes a movement that is at once philosophical, moral, and anagogical. The culmination of this ascent — the *mors osculi*, the "death by kiss" through which the soul is united to God in the manner of Moses — represents the point at which the anagogical orientation of the entire philosophical program reaches its term and can go no further.

The Kabbalistic dimension of anagogy in Pico is mediated primarily through Abraham Abulafia's prophetic or ecstatic Kabbalah, known to Pico through Mithridates' Latin translations of Abulafia's commentaries on Maimonides' *Guide of the Perplexed*. As Chaim Wirszubski has demonstrated, these texts present a programme of mystical ascent through the combination of letters of the divine names — techniques that are functionally anagogical in the sense that they are designed to lift consciousness from ordinary discursive thought toward union with the Active Intellect and beyond it toward the divine source (Wirszubski 1989). The letter-combination practices (*hokmat ha-tseruf*) that Abulafia proposes as the "path of prophecy" are exercises in concentrated mental ascent, stripping away ordinary cognitive content in favor of the pure combinatorial activity that mirrors, at the level of human practice, the divine creative speech through which the world was made. When Pico in the eighth section of the *Heptaplus* performs his reading of *bereshit* through letter combination, he is not merely being eclectic or displaying his Hebraic learning: he is translating an Abulafian practice of anagogical technique into the register of Christian biblical hermeneutics.

The forty-nine gates of understanding provide the most concrete expression of the numerological dimension of anagogy in Pico's thought. According to texts translated for him by Mithridates, Moses received at Sinai forty-nine of the fifty gates of understanding (*sha'arey binah*) — forty-nine levels of divine wisdom, all but the fiftieth and final gate, which is identified with *Ein Sof* and lies beyond any created intellect's capacity to receive. Black argues that the *Heptaplus*'s structure of 7×7=49 chapters is designed precisely to encode this teaching: the reader who completes the forty-nine chapters of the work has traversed the same forty-nine gates that Moses traversed, and stands, like Moses at the summit of Sinai, at the threshold of what cannot be said, analyzed, or articulated. The fiftieth gate — the Infinite, the divine abyss — is the anagogical horizon of the entire enterprise, the point toward which every interpretation is oriented but which no interpretation can reach.

This understanding of anagogy as a structural and not merely a rhetorical or pedagogical category has consequences for how we read the relationship between the *Heptaplus* and Pico's other works. The *Commento* frames the soul's ascent through the three Venuses — earthly, intermediate, and celestial — as a movement through levels of beauty toward the divine intellect, a movement that is erotic in character and Platonizing in idiom. The *Oration* frames the same ascent in terms of the philosophical disciplines and the mystical traditions. The *Heptaplus* frames it as a hermeneutical event: the reader's movement through the seven expositions, guided by the second proem's account of the three worlds and their interrelations, enacts in the temporality of reading the same upward movement that the soul must perform in the temporality of contemplative life. In each case, anagogy names the movement from what is available at the level of sense and discursive reason toward what exceeds both — and identifies the practice of that movement as the proper goal of philosophical and theological existence.

The medieval fourfold method, with its systematic allocation of anagogy to the fourth and highest sense of Scripture, provided Pico with the terminological framework within which to articulate this programme. But Pico's actual practice of anagogical reading — enriched by the Pseudo-Dionysian tradition of ascent through divine darkness, the Abulafian tradition of letter-combination as ecstatic technique, and the Kabbalistic numerology of the forty-nine gates — goes well beyond anything that Nicholas of Lyra or the *Glossa ordinaria* had in mind when they defined the anagogical sense as the sense that orients the reader toward future beatitude. For Pico, anagogy is not simply a hermeneutical category alongside others but the very telos of all interpretation and the structure of all authentic human existence.
