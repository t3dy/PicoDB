# Poros

Poros (Πόρος), whose name is variously translated as Resource, Resourcefulness, or Plenty, is a figure in the mythological genealogy of Eros recounted by Diotima in Plato's *Symposium* (203b–e). He is also, through the philosophical allegory that Marsilio Ficino and Giovanni Pico della Mirandola derive from that genealogy, one of the fundamental structural principles of Renaissance Neoplatonic love theory. The figure occupies a small but pivotal position in Pico's *Commento sopra una canzona d'amore* (c. 1486), where the birth-myth of Eros is read as an account of love's metaphysical constitution — the division of love's nature between an ascending, resourceful, striving component and a deficient, needy, earth-bound one. Understanding Poros means understanding why love, for Pico, is not a unified appetite but a permanently tensioned structure whose philosophical energy derives from the conflict between its two parental inheritances.

Diotima's account of the birth of Eros, which Socrates reports in the *Symposium* as the most authoritative account he knows, runs as follows. On the day that Aphrodite was born, the gods held a feast. Poros, the son of Metis (Μῆτις, Wisdom or Cunning Intelligence), became drunk on nectar — wine had not yet been invented — and went out into the garden of Zeus to sleep. Penia (Πενία, Poverty or Need), who had come to the feast as a suppliant and been barred from its pleasures, saw her opportunity and lay down beside the sleeping Poros, conceiving a child. The child born from this union was Eros, and he inherits the natures of both parents. From Penia he takes his poverty, his roughness, his shoelessness, his homelessness, his habit of sleeping in doorways and in the open air; from Poros he takes his courage, his ingenuity, his tenacity in pursuing the good and the beautiful, his capacity for scheming and resourcefulness (*poros* in Greek also means a way through, a passage, a contrivance). Eros is therefore always poor and never self-sufficient, yet always resourceful and always pressing forward toward his object; he is not the beautiful beloved but the striving lover, not the possession of beauty but the desire for it.

The philosophical exploitation of this myth in Platonic and Neoplatonic literature is substantial, and Pico inherits it primarily through Ficino's *De amore* (Commentary on Plato's *Symposium*, completed c. 1469, printed 1484), the work that established the conceptual vocabulary for all subsequent Florentine discussions of love and beauty. Ficino's reading of the birth of Eros centers on the identification of Poros as a figure for the divine mind or intellectual substance, and Penia as a figure for the deficiency or lack that characterizes the material, desiring soul. Poros as son of Metis (Wisdom) is the offspring of divine intelligence; he belongs to the intellectual realm, and the resources he possesses — his boldness, his scheming energy, his capacity to find a way through — are the properties of intellect when it engages the world. That he falls asleep in the garden of Zeus drunk on nectar is itself allegorically meaningful in Ficino's reading: the garden of Zeus is the world of Ideas, and the nectar is the superabundant goodness that flows from the divine; Poros is, as it were, saturated with intelligible being, overflowing with the richness of the intellectual world. The child conceived from his union with the needy Penia is therefore a being constituted by the tension between abundance and lack — between the richness of the intelligible world, which Poros embodies, and the emptiness of the desiring soul, which Penia embodies. Love is this tension animated and directed upward.

Pico's allegory in the *Commento* is both continuous with Ficino's and markedly more precise in its metaphysical assignment of roles. As Michael Allen has documented in his study of Pico's Neoplatonism and his relation to Ficino — particularly in the essays collected in *Studies in the Platonism of Marsilio Ficino and Giovanni Pico* (Allen 2017) — Pico's *Commento* is in substantial part a philosophical quarrel with the *De amore*, and the handling of the Symposium myths is among the sites of deepest disagreement. Where Ficino tends to read the birth of Eros as an allegory of love's mediation between divine abundance and creaturely need, Pico sharpens the ontological stakes considerably. For Pico, Poros represents not merely "resourcefulness" in a vague sense but the intellectual substance itself — what Neoplatonists following Plotinus call *Nous*, the first and highest mind, the locus of the intelligible forms or Ideas. Poros as son of Metis is the son of divine wisdom, and he therefore carries within him the wealth of the intelligible world: the forms of all things, the archetypes of beauty, goodness, and being that constitute the intellectual realm's inexhaustible content. His drunkenness in the garden is his saturation with ideal content — a temporary receptivity that makes him available to union with Penia.

The allegorical precision matters because it determines what love inherits from Poros and what it inherits from Penia, and therefore what love fundamentally is. Love's Poros-component is the upward, striving, formally oriented aspect of desire — the impulse that seeks the intelligible, that presses through obstacles toward the good, that is capable of resourcefulness and invention in the service of its ascent. This is the component that makes love philosophical: without the Poros-inheritance, love would be merely the passive, needy receptivity of Penia, stuck at the level of material lack without the energy to press upward toward its object. The Poros-component is what enables love to be, as Diotima insists throughout her discourse, a philosopher — an intermediate being between wisdom and ignorance, between being and non-being, between the mortal and the immortal. Only because love inherits from Poros, from the son of divine wisdom, does it have the capacity to ascend from the sensible world toward the intelligible beauty it lacks.

This account has significant consequences for how Pico understands the relationship between love and beauty, which is among the central disputes between his *Commento* and Ficino's *De amore*. In Ficino's handling, beauty is distributed across all levels of being — divine beauty, intellectual beauty, psychic beauty, sensible beauty — and each level of beauty can generate a corresponding love. The soul that loves a beautiful body is already, however imperfectly, responding to the divine beauty that shines through sensible form. The Poros-principle is therefore operative even at the lowest level of erotic experience, because the resourceful, striving character of love is present whenever a soul is genuinely drawn toward something beautiful, at whatever level. Pico's account is stricter. As Sears Jayne's translation and commentary on the *Commento* (Jayne 1984) makes clear, and as Allen confirms, Pico locates genuine beauty — the formal object of genuine love — exclusively in the intelligible realm. Sensible beauty is an image of real beauty, not beauty itself; and the love of sensible beauty, while it may contain the shadow of a Poros-impulse, does not qualify as *amor* in Pico's precise sense. Genuine love — the love that constitutes philosophical ascent — is the love whose Poros-component operates in the proper orientation: not toward the beauty of a body or even of a soul, but toward the beauty of the intelligible Ideas as such.

The Poros figure is connected in Pico's allegory to the framework of three Venuses that organizes the metaphysical architecture of the *Commento*. The celestial or Uranian Venus corresponds to the intellectual world, the world of the angelic intellect and the Ideas. The lower or generative Venus governs the world soul and the production of forms in the sensible world. Love in its highest form — the love of the celestial Venus — is love in which the Poros-component fully predominates: the upward-striving, idea-seeking, intellectual force of desire, which takes as its object the beauty of the angelic intellect. Love in its middling form — psychic or celestial but not purely intellectual — is love in which Poros and Penia are more equally mixed, the aspiring intellect still partly compromised by its material situation. Love at its lowest — the attachment to sensible beauty that Pico does not quite dignify with the name of love — is love in which the Penia-component overwhelms the Poros-component, desire remaining stuck at the level of material lack and sensory satisfaction. The Poros-Penia dialectic maps directly onto the three-Venus hierarchy: Poros ascends, Penia descends, and love is constituted by the direction in which their tension resolves.

There is a further dimension to Poros in the *Commento* that concerns love's connection to the philosophical life as a whole. Diotima in the *Symposium* describes Eros as always in need but always resourceful, always devising schemes, always striving after wisdom, a lover of wisdom and therefore a philosopher — *philosophos*, not sophos. Pico's allegorization of this description maps it onto the condition of the philosophical soul in embodiment: the soul that finds itself in the material world (Penia-condition) but carries within it an inheritance from the intellectual world (Poros-condition) and therefore cannot rest in material satisfaction but is always pressing upward. The philosophical life is precisely the life in which the Poros-inheritance is cultivated and the Penia-condition progressively overcome: the soul awakens to the resources it carries from its intellectual origin and orients those resources toward the recovery of the intelligible beauty it lacks. In this sense, Poros functions not only as one term in a genealogical allegory but as a figure for the soul's intellectual vocation — the innate orientation toward the divine that makes philosophy possible.

Pico's account of the Poros-Penia allegory had a complicated reception history. As Jayne documents and Allen confirms, the *Commento* was not published among Pico's own works but appeared in the 1519 Giunti edition of Benivieni's poems in a version that had been substantially revised after Pico's death, with the most combative anti-Ficinian passages removed (Jayne 1984, introduction; Allen 1986, 189–190). The allegory of Poros and Penia was among the passages that survived the expurgation, and it made its way into the French and English Renaissance through Gabriel Chappuys's partial French translation (1578), which was known to Edmund Spenser and other poets. Art historians have drawn on the *Commento*'s Poros-Penia allegory in reading Botticelli's representations of Venus and Eros, and the figure of Poros as the resourceful, intellectually endowed, male principle has been connected with the figure of the young man in the *Primavera*. These art-historical applications are suggestive but speculative; what the *Commento* itself offers is a strictly philosophical reading in which Poros names the intellectual substance that gives love its aspirational, upward-tending character, and whose presence in love is what makes the erotic experience of beauty a potential path toward the divine.

For readers of PicoDB, the Poros entry is inseparable from the Penia entry: neither figure is intelligible without the other, and the whole force of Pico's allegory lies in the tension between them. Together they constitute the metaphysical parentage of love, and the account of their union — the needy Penia seizing her moment beside the drunk and sleeping Poros — is Pico's mythopoetic account of why love is neither purely intellectual nor purely material but always both at once, always constituted by the gap between its aspirational and its deficient dimensions, always pressing upward through the resources of its Poros-inheritance toward a beauty it can never quite fully possess while still in the condition of Penia.
