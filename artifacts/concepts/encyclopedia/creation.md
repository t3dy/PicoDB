# Creation

The doctrine of creation (*creatio*) stands at the intersection of Pico della Mirandola's philosophical ambitions and his most sustained work of biblical scholarship. To speak of "creation" in Pico's intellectual world is to invoke a cluster of irreducibly distinct problems: the metaphysical question of how the universe proceeds from God, the theological question of whether that procession is voluntary or necessary, the hermeneutical question of how the opening of Genesis encodes the answer, and the mystical question of what it means for a created intellect to ascend back toward the principle from which it came. These problems converge most powerfully in the *Heptaplus* of 1489, Pico's sevenfold commentary on Genesis 1:1–27, which represents his single most elaborated and architecturally coherent statement of the structure of the created world and the means by which the human mind can comprehend it. Yet the *Heptaplus* is not a treatise on creation in the technical metaphysical sense. It does not engage the standard scholastic debate about *creatio ex nihilo* — the question, pressing since the condemnations of Etienne Tempier in 1277, of whether Aristotelian prime matter could be understood as having always existed, or whether Christian orthodoxy required that even matter was produced from nothing by a voluntary divine act. Pico, as Crofton Black observes, simply sets that problem aside: the *Heptaplus* "projects" the creation events out of their literal temporal frame and treats the six days of Genesis not as a cosmogony — a narrative of how things came to be — but as a cosmology, an encoded revelation of how the universe is (Black 2006, 266). The act of creation is, for Pico's purposes in that work, less interesting as a temporal event than as a structural fact, and what interests him structurally is the hierarchy of worlds that creation produced and the principle that binds those worlds together.

That hierarchy had been worked out in outline already in the *Commento sopra una canzone d'amore* (c. 1486) and restated in the *Oration on the Dignity of Man* (1486), where Pico describes God as having "decorated the super-celestial region with minds, animated the heavenly spheres with eternal souls, and filled the degraded and filthy parts of the lowest world with a crowd of all sorts of animals" (*Garin* 1942, 104). The passage is a precise capsule statement of the tripartite cosmic scheme that organizes the entire *Heptaplus*: three worlds arranged hierarchically — the angelic or intellectual world (*mundus angelicus vel intellectualis*), the celestial world (*mundus caelestis*), and the elemental or sublunary world (*mundus elementaris*) — with man constituting a fourth, which synthesizes the first three within himself and thereby occupies a unique position as the bond and link of the whole. This tripartite scheme, described in the *Heptaplus*'s second proem as drawn from "antiquity" (*Tres mundos figurat antiquitas*: H. P2; *Garin* 184), is more Neoplatonic in origin than its biblical framing suggests. It derives from the Plotinian hypostases — One, Mind, Soul — rearticulated and given a cosmological register through engagement with Proclus, Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, and the Hermetic tradition. But Pico's three-world scheme is not simply a repetition of Plotinian tripartition. The crucial difference lies in his account of their mutual relationship and in the relationship of the whole structure to God.

The principle that governs the *Heptaplus*'s approach to cosmic structure — and that makes Pico's account of creation distinctively his own — is the maxim articulated in the second proem: *omnia in omnibus, sed in unoquoque secundum propriam naturam*, "all things are in all things, but in each according to its own nature." This principle, drawn in part from Proclus and mediated through Pseudo-Dionysian theology, means that each of the three worlds contains, in its own mode, everything that the other worlds contain. The elemental world contains an echo of the angelic; the angelic world contains its own analogue of the material. Creation is not a series of successive, distinct ontological registers but a single reality refracted through different conditions. This has immediate consequences for Pico's hermeneutics — it is what licenses the seven consecutive expositions of the same Genesis verse through seven different ontological registers — but it also reflects a substantive metaphysical commitment. The created order is not a collection of separate beings standing alongside one another but a web of mutual implications and correspondences, each element reflecting the whole in its own degree. The unity of the creation is not the unity of a sum of its parts but the unity of a mirror: each part reflects all the others according to its capacity.

This account of creation is situated between the Neoplatonic doctrine of emanation and the Christian doctrine of *creatio ex nihilo*, and it inhabits that middle ground deliberately. In strictly Plotinian emanation, the One overflows from its own superabundance by necessity, without will or decision: the Good cannot but radiate goodness, as the sun cannot but shine. The procession of Mind from the One, and Soul from Mind, is not a voluntary act but an ontological consequence of the fullness of each prior principle. Medieval Christian theology had systematically resisted this account because it threatens divine freedom: a God who creates by necessity, rather than by a sovereign act of will, is not the God of Scripture. *Creatio ex nihilo* asserts, against emanationism, that God could have chosen not to create, that matter itself is produced by the divine will rather than being an eternal substrate, and that the relationship between Creator and creature is therefore one of absolute ontological dependence rather than ontological continuity. Pico does not directly address this tension in the *Heptaplus*, but the way he positions his three-world cosmology reflects a mediating strategy. He adopts the Neoplatonic framework — the emanatory structure of the three worlds, the principle of correspondence, the movement from unity through multiplicity and back to unity — but gives it a distinctly voluntarist register by insisting that the creation expressed in Genesis is Moses's revelation of a structure that God the "almighty craftsman" (*omnipotens Deus opifex*) deliberately arranged (H. P2; *Garin* 192–194). Creation, for Pico, is a voluntary divine act, but what that act produced is a hierarchical structure that Neoplatonic philosophy was uniquely equipped to describe.

The first three expositions of the *Heptaplus* read the six days of Genesis through the first three of the four worlds: the first through the register of the elemental world (*De mundo elementari*), the second through the register of the celestial world (*De mundo caelesti*), the third through the register of the angelic and invisible world (*De mundo angelico et invisibili*). The fourth exposition reads the same verse through the register of man as microcosm (*De mundo humano idest de hominis natura*). By this point, Pico has established not merely that Genesis is an allegory for each world separately but that Moses wrote a single verse that simultaneously encodes the structure of all four worlds and their mutual relationship. The fifth and sixth expositions then move from description of each world to the perception of their connections (*De omnibus mundis divisim ordine consequenti*; *De mundorum inter se rerum omnium cognatione*), culminating in the seventh, which turns to *felicitas* — the happiness or blessedness that is the return of created things to God. This arc from multiplicity through correspondence to eschatological unity is itself the interpretive image of the creative act reversed: creation is a procession outward into the plurality of worlds; the human intellectual ascent, traced by the hermeneutic movement of the *Heptaplus*, is the return of that plurality back to its source.

What Pico is careful not to make explicit, but what Black's analysis demonstrates in detail, is the Kabbalistic substrate underlying the *Heptaplus*'s architecture. The sefirot — the ten divine attributes or emanations that, in Kabbalistic theology, constitute the structure through which Ein Sof, the infinite and unknowable God, is present to and in the created world — do not appear by name in the *Heptaplus*. Pico had deployed them extensively in the Kabbalistic conclusions of the *900 Conclusions* (1486), mapping the three highest sefirot (Keter, Hokhmah, Binah) onto the Christian Trinity, and using the emanatory structure of the sefirot to provide a Hebrew confirmation of the Neoplatonic hierarchy. After the Roman crisis, he does not repeat this explicit mapping. The sefirot vanish from the *Heptaplus*'s vocabulary, replaced by the more philosophically neutral language of "worlds" and "orders." But, as Black argues, the overall structural logic — the simultaneous presence of all within each, the principle of downward emanation and upward reversion, the identification of creation with a procession that begins in divine wisdom and reaches down to material substance — remains deeply indebted to the Kabbalistic sources Pico had read in Mithridates's translations, above all Menahem Recanati's commentary on the Pentateuch and Joseph Gikatilla's *Portae Iustitiae* (Black 2006, 140–144). The Kabbalistic dimension of Pico's creation doctrine in the *Heptaplus* is encoded rather than explicit, structural rather than terminological, and this encoding is itself part of the esoteric strategy that governs the whole work.

One of the *Heptaplus*'s recurring preoccupations — one that connects its creation doctrine to the specifically hermeneutical ambitions of the text — is the relationship between the production of the cosmos and the production of the biblical text. Moses, in Pico's account, did not merely narrate the creation; he encoded within his narrative the same structure that God the craftsman had arranged within the creation itself. The *Heptaplus* second proem states that Moses, "when intending to speak sufficiently of the world, discussed all of them [the four worlds]; and given that a writer is portraying nature, if he is knowledgeable about nature (which I believe our writer, if anyone, was), it is probable that his doctrine about these things has been arranged in the same way that God the almighty craftsman arranged them in themselves" (H. P2; *Garin* 192–194). This means that the literary structure of the Genesis account is itself a microcosm of the structure of creation: Scripture mirrors the cosmos, just as the cosmos mirrors God. To read the *Heptaplus*'s seven expositions of Genesis 1:1–27 is therefore to follow the pattern of creation itself, layer by layer, down through the hierarchy of worlds and back up through their mutual correspondences toward the divine source.

The 49-unit structure of the *Heptaplus* — seven expositions each of seven chapters — encodes a further Kabbalistic frame, analyzed in detail by Black's seventh chapter. The doctrine of the forty-nine gates of understanding, found in the Babylonian Talmud (*Rosh Hashanah* 21b) and elaborated in the Kabbalistic texts available to Pico through Mithridates, holds that Moses ascended through forty-nine of the fifty gates of understanding on Mount Sinai, acquiring thereby knowledge of the entire taxonomy of created things — from minerals through plants and animals to the heavenly spheres — while the fiftieth gate, corresponding to knowledge of the Creator himself, remained inaccessible even to Moses. The forty-nine gates correspond to knowledge of the structure and essence of every created category; the fiftieth is the knowledge of God in himself, which is Ein Sof, beyond all gates. Pico explicitly invokes this framework in the *Conclusiones*: "He who knows in Kabbalah the mystery of the gates of understanding will know the mystery of the great jubilee" (*Conclusions* 96). Black argues that the *Heptaplus*'s structure — forty-nine units of commentary before the final, unnumbered eighth section on the word *bereshit* — is not coincidental. The work in its seven-by-seven division reflects the forty-nine gates that Moses received; the eighth section, the exposition of *bereshit* alone, points toward what lies beyond the gates, the divine unity that is the ground of the entire creative act.

The structure of Pico's creation doctrine is therefore at once cosmological, hermeneutical, and mystical. Cosmologically, creation produces a hierarchically ordered triad of worlds, each containing and reflecting the others according to the principle of universal correspondence. Hermeneutically, Moses encoded this triad within the opening verse of Genesis in a manner accessible only to initiated allegorical readers. Mystically, the proper reading of that verse — which the *Heptaplus* enacts — is itself a form of intellectual ascent through the same structure that the creative act originally produced, culminating in the possibility of union with the divine source. The recovery of creation's meaning is not separable from the recovery of the human intellect's own origin and end.

Pico's position differs markedly from what his most immediate Florentine context might suggest. Marsilio Ficino, who dominated the Platonic Academy and whose influence Pico acknowledged while also resisting, was deeply concerned with the soul's relationship to the created order but did not produce a Genesis commentary. Ficino's *Platonic Theology* treats the soul's immortality and its capacity to traverse the orders of being, but the biblical framing of creation — the hexaemeron as a hermeneutical object — is not his primary vehicle. Pico's turn to Genesis as the master text for his philosophy of creation is distinctive in the period, and its distinctiveness is partly explained by the Kabbalistic dimension: no other contemporary Florentine thinker had access to the Hebrew exegetical tradition that Mithridates's translations had opened up, or saw in that tradition the resources to demonstrate that Moses had encoded the entire Neoplatonic-Kabbalistic account of cosmic hierarchy within the opening words of his Torah.

The *Heptaplus*'s creation doctrine was not received without controversy. The papal secretary Lanfredini reported to Rome in 1489 that Roman theologians had examined the work on Genesis and condemned it on the grounds that Pico was "in many places repeating the same heresies from the *Conclusiones*, merely introducing them indirectly" (*Berti*, quoted in Black 2006, 11). The charge points to what is genuinely continuous between the *Heptaplus* and the condemned theses: the underlying framework of a hierarchically structured creation readable through Kabbalistic and Neoplatonic keys is present in both. The *Heptaplus* does not abandon the doctrinal commitments of the *Conclusiones*; it reorganizes them, embedding them more deeply within the texture of scriptural exposition, so that the syncretic cosmology is no longer stated as a bare thesis but enacted through a sustained reading of Moses. Whether this reorganization was primarily a defensive strategy or a genuine intellectual development remains a matter of scholarly discussion. Black's analysis suggests it was both: the post-crisis circumstances shaped the form, but the *Heptaplus*'s creation doctrine represents a genuine deepening and clarification of positions that the *Conclusiones* had only sketched.
