# Neoplatonism

Neoplatonism is the name modern scholars give to the philosophical tradition that descends from Plotinus of Lycopolis (205–270 CE) through Porphyry, Iamblichus, Proclus, and Damascius, and that entered the Latin West primarily through Boethius, Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, and, in the fifteenth century, through the translations and philosophical syntheses of Marsilio Ficino. Its characteristic doctrines include an emanationist metaphysics in which all reality flows, without division or diminishment in the source, from the absolutely transcendent One through successive levels or hypostases — Intelligence (Nous), Soul (Psyche), and finally Matter — and a complementary account of return in which the soul reascends through these levels toward its source in the One. This double movement of procession (*proodos*) and return (*epistrophē*) underlies not only the Neoplatonists' cosmology but their ethics, their psychology, their theology, and their account of philosophical activity itself. To know, for the Neoplatonist, is always already to ascend; the intellect that apprehends truth is participating, however partially, in the divine Intelligence from which it proceeds.

Giovanni Pico della Mirandola encountered Neoplatonism through multiple channels, and any account that treats his Neoplatonism as simply derived from Ficino's will misrepresent the intellectual situation. Ficino's influence was real and formative: the Florentine circle in which Pico moved from the early 1480s onward was saturated with Ficino's translations of Plato, Plotinus, Proclus, Iamblichus, and Porphyry, and with Ficino's own synthetic masterwork, the *Platonica Theologia* (1482), which attempted to show that the Platonic tradition provided the most coherent philosophical account of the immortality of the soul and its compatibility with Christian theology. Pico read these translations and knew this synthesis. But he also read Proclus directly — or as directly as the available Latin versions permitted — and his engagement with Proclus is in several respects more technically exacting and more philosophically consequential than Ficino's. Where Ficino's Neoplatonism tends toward what Michael Allen has described as an erotic and thematic Platonism, drawn to the ascent of the soul through beauty and love, Pico's engagement with the Neoplatonic tradition is more systematic, more concerned with structural correspondence and metaphysical architecture, and ultimately more interested in Proclus than in Plotinus.

The *Oration on the Dignity of Man*, composed as the preface to the 900 *Conclusiones*, displays Neoplatonic structure at every level, though it deploys that structure in a rhetorical register rather than a technical philosophical one. The famous opening meditation on the creation of Adam presents God as fashioning man after all the other orders of being — Angels, Intelligences, Celestial Spheres, and elemental creatures — have already been assigned their places in the cosmic hierarchy. Man alone is given no fixed place: "We have given you no fixed seat, no form of your own, no gift peculiarly yours, O Adam, so that whatever seat, whatever form, whatever gifts you may, with premeditation, select, these same you may have and possess through your own judgment and decision." The Neoplatonic resonance of this passage is unmistakable. Man is not simply undefined; he is positioned at the intersection of all levels of the hierarchy, capable of descending toward the beasts and matter or ascending toward the Angels and the divine Intelligence. He is, in the vocabulary Pico uses elsewhere, a *copula* or bond of the cosmic hierarchy — the being that unifies the levels because it can inhabit any of them.

The ascent program that structures the second half of the *Oration* is explicitly organized through the triplex via that Pico derives from Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite — the three ways of purgation, illumination, and union. The soul first purifies itself of sensory attachment through moral philosophy; it then illuminates its intellect through natural philosophy and the study of nature's harmonies; and it finally rises to mystical union with the divine through theology. These three stages correspond, loosely but recognizably, to the three Neoplatonic hypostases through which the soul must pass in its return: it moves from the domain of Soul (moral and natural philosophy) through Intelligence (dialectical theology) toward the threshold of the One. Pico frames this ascent in terms drawn from both Dionysius and the Neoplatonic tradition, collapsing the Christian mystical theology of the *Celestial Hierarchy* with the philosophical psychology of Proclus and Plotinus. The synthesis is characteristic: rather than choosing between the Christian Dionysian and the pagan Neoplatonic accounts, Pico treats them as parallel expressions of the same structural truth, to be collated through the correlative method of *omnia sunt in omnibus modo suo*.

Pico's engagement with Proclus, as Farmer's analysis of the *Conclusiones* makes clear, is more technically precise and more philosophically ambitious than anything in Ficino's comparable treatments of the same material. Proclus had developed, in the *Elements of Theology* and the *Platonic Theology*, a refined account of the henads — divine unities that mediate between the absolutely transcendent One and the level of Intellect. The henads are not the One itself, which remains absolutely beyond all predication, including numerical predication; nor are they the intellectual entities of the Nous. They are individual divine units, each participating in the One in a distinct way, each presiding over and unifying a domain of Intellectual reality. For Proclus, the henads are also identifiable with the traditional Greek gods, whose multiplicity and distinctness they explain without compromising the ultimate unity of the One.

Pico draws on the Procline henad theory for his Kabbalistic synthesis in a move that Farmer identifies as one of the most technically ambitious maneuvers in the *Conclusiones*. The ten Kabbalistic sefirot — the divine emanations through which Ein Sof, the infinite divine being, manifests itself in creation — function in Pico's system as a parallel symbolic series to the Procline henads. Both series describe intermediate divine unities that mediate between the absolutely transcendent divine source and the levels of reality accessible to creaturely participation. The sefirot are not identical to the henads — they differ in number, in their internal relationships, and in the theological context that gives them meaning — but they are functionally parallel: both series occupy the same structural position in their respective cosmological hierarchies, and both serve, in Pico's synthesis, as different vocabularies for the same metaphysical reality. Farmer's edition is the first to make this collation explicit in a way that allows the reader to see how the henad theses in the historical section of the *Conclusiones* and the Kabbalistic theses in the final section are designed to be read together as parallel articulations of the same divine structure.

Where Pico most sharply diverges from Ficinian Neoplatonism is in his integration of Aristotelian causality into what might otherwise appear to be a straightforwardly emanationist framework. Amos Howlett's work on Pico's scholastic formation has been most rigorous in tracing this divergence. The Aristotelian tradition, particularly in its scholastic development through Aquinas and Albertus Magnus, distinguished four causes — formal, material, efficient, and final — and organized its ontology around these causal categories rather than around the processive and emanative language of Plotinian Neoplatonism. Pico, unlike Ficino, refuses to set these frameworks in opposition. His 900 *Conclusiones* include a substantial body of scholastic theses, including careful treatments of the Thomistic distinction between essence and existence and the Albertian account of intellectual abstraction, and Pico's *philosophia nova* is in part an attempt to show how Aristotelian causality and Platonic participation can be mapped onto each other as different vocabularies for the same ontological structure. Howlett argues that this integration is not superficial eclecticism but reflects Pico's genuine conviction, grounded in his reading of both the scholastic and the Platonic traditions, that the distinction between formal causality and participation is a distinction of level, not of kind.

The treatment of the Active Intellect in Pico's thought illustrates this complexity particularly well. The doctrine of the Active Intellect — the divine or semi-divine intellective principle that illuminates the human intellect and makes conceptual cognition possible — appears in Aristotle's *De anima* in a famously cryptic passage that generated centuries of interpretive controversy. Averroes, whose account of the *Conclusiones*' Arabic philosophical sections is comprehensive, identified the Active Intellect with the separate Intellect that moves the outermost celestial sphere, thus making human intellectual activity dependent on a single cosmic principle shared by all knowers. Plotinus and the Neoplatonic tradition located the equivalent function at the level of universal Nous, the divine Intelligence from which individual intellects proceed and to which they return in contemplation. Pico's treatment in the *Conclusiones* does not simply choose between these accounts: it assigns them to different levels of the same hierarchical structure, with the Averroist Active Intellect operating at the level of cosmic Soul and the Neoplatonic Nous operating at the superior level of pure Intelligence. The apparent contradiction between Arabic Aristotelianism and Neoplatonism is, in Pico's architecture, a difference of level, not a philosophical disagreement.

In the *Heptaplus* (1489), Pico's sevenfold commentary on the opening of Genesis, the Neoplatonic framework operates as the organizing principle of the entire exegetical project. The three worlds that structure the *Heptaplus* — the angelical world, the celestial world, and the sublunary or elemental world — correspond to the three principal levels of Neoplatonic reality below the One: Nous (here reinterpreted as the angelic intellects), Soul or the celestial spheres, and Matter articulated into the four elements. The sevenfold commentary proceeds by showing how each of the seven days of creation can be read as describing reality at each of these levels simultaneously, with man — the microcosm — embodying all three worlds in himself and thus serving as the bond of the cosmic hierarchy. The argument is structurally Neoplatonic even when its vocabulary is biblical, and Wirszubski's analysis of the Kabbalistic elements in the *Heptaplus* shows that the doctrine of three worlds Pico deploys there derives from Kabbalistic sources translated by Mithridates as much as from Neoplatonic precedent, though the two traditions are themselves mapped onto each other through the correlative method.

In *On Being and Unity* (1491), Pico's final published work, the Neoplatonic framework is subjected to direct philosophical scrutiny in a way that illuminates its role in the earlier writings. The treatise argues that being and unity are not, as the Thomistic tradition maintained, convertible transcendentals — that everything which is, is one, and everything that is one, is — but are in fact distinct, with unity the superior predicate and being its subordinate. This thesis has direct Neoplatonic ancestry in Proclus, for whom the One absolutely transcends being, and in Plotinus, for whom the One is beyond even the predication of intellect. Pico's argument in *On Being and Unity* thus represents a return to a more technically rigorous Neoplatonism than the loose concordances of the *Conclusiones*, and Michael Allen's work on the treatise has traced its precise debts to the Procline rather than the Plotinian lineage of the tradition. The work also represents, as Allen notes, a deliberate challenge to Ficino, who maintained the Thomistic convertibility of being and unity and who responded to Pico's treatise with a letter defending the traditional position. In *On Being and Unity*, Pico's divergence from Ficinian Neoplatonism crystallizes into explicit philosophical disagreement — a disagreement that, characteristically, is conducted through the close reading of Platonic texts and their scholastic commentators, not through a simple preference for one tradition over another.
