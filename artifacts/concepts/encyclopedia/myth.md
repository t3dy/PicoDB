# Myth

The Greek word *μῦθος* (mythos) designates in its most general sense a narrative, a story told about events involving supernatural actors — gods, heroes, cosmogonic forces, the origins of the world and its institutions. In classical philosophical usage, and above all in Plato, the term carries additional weight: *mythos* is set against *logos* (rational argument or discourse) as its narrative and non-demonstrative counterpart, the mode of speaking that proceeds through images, characters, and events rather than through premises, definitions, and conclusions. This classical distinction is the starting point for understanding what myth means in Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's intellectual practice — and understanding why the distinction, once established, is immediately and systematically complicated by everything Pico and the Neoplatonic tradition do with it.

Plato's own ambivalence about myth is philosophically instructive. In the *Republic* (376E–392C), Socrates proposes to expel the mythological narratives of Homer and Hesiod from the ideal city because they present the gods as wicked, deceptive, or immoral — as warring, conspiring, seducing mortals, changing shape, weeping, and displaying all the passions that philosophy associates with the lower parts of the soul. The critique is not that myths are merely false or fictional: Socrates treats the stories with the seriousness reserved for claims that might actually corrupt a guardian's understanding of the divine. But the *Republic* simultaneously employs myths: the Allegory of the Cave (*Republic* 514A–521B), which is not called a myth but functions as one, narrates a philosophical truth about education and vision through an extended image rather than through argument. The Er myth that closes the *Republic* (614B–621D) describes the soul's journey after death in the mode of a traveler's tale. The *Phaedrus* (246A–249D) depicts the soul's pre-incarnate vision of the Forms through the image of a winged chariot ascending to the rim of the intelligible heaven. The *Timaeus* presents the creation of the world as a narrative account by a wise Pythagorean — a story (*eikos mythos*, a "likely myth," 29D) about what the demiurge did in the beginning. The Symposium, which is the primary Platonic source for Pico's *Commento*, is structured as a series of speeches about Eros, several of which — Aristophanes' story of the original round humans, Diotima's account of Love's birth from Poros and Penia — are explicitly mythological in form.

Plato's practice thus repeatedly undermines the theoretical distinction his dialogues establish between myth and logos. What emerges from the Neoplatonic tradition's meditation on this paradox — the philosopher who condemns myth while producing myths — is the claim that myth is not the opposite of philosophical discourse but its natural complement for certain kinds of subject matter. Proclus makes this argument most explicitly in the *Theologia Platonica* (I.3–4): the things that the philosopher wishes to say about the highest realities — the procession of the gods, the generation of intellect, the soul's descent and ascent — cannot be adequately expressed in the mode of logical demonstration, because logical demonstration operates on the level of discursive reason (*dianoia*), which is always mediated and sequential, while the realities in question are immediate, simultaneous, and superrational. Myth can gesture at these realities more adequately than argument because myth operates through images and analogies that can suggest what transcends literal statement. A narrative in which Zeus swallows Metis, or Kronos swallows his children, or Dionysus is dismembered by the Titans, captures something about the structure of the intelligible hypostases — the way that the second principle absorbs and contains the third, or the way that soul fragments itself into many lives — that a propositional argument cannot capture with the same economy and force.

It is this Neoplatonic rehabilitation of myth that Pico inherits, and his practice of mythological allegoresis in the *Commento* and the *900 Conclusiones* is built on this foundation. But Pico also has access to a more immediately available Renaissance precedent: the tradition of mythographical allegoresis exemplified by Boccaccio's *Genealogia Deorum Gentilium*. Boccaccio had argued, in the fourteenth and early fifteenth centuries, that the apparent moral indecency of mythological narrative — the stories of Jupiter's adulteries, Mars and Venus caught in the net of Vulcan, Saturn castrating his father Ouranos — was the deliberate protective covering of philosophical wisdom: the ancient poets (*poeti*, *theologi*) encoded their deepest insights in the attractive and memorable vehicle of sensational narrative precisely so that the vulgar would be drawn to the vehicle while only the philosophically prepared could penetrate to the content. This principle of *integumentum* or *velamen* — the covering that conceals and simultaneously preserves — makes myth a fundamentally esoteric genre: it is structured for differentiated reception, offering different things to different readers, and its proper reading requires an initiation that the myth itself cannot supply.

Pico's most extended and philosophically consequential practice of mythological allegoresis occurs in the *Commento sopra una canzona d'amore* (c. 1486), where he works through several mythological episodes from Plato's *Symposium* and the wider Orphic tradition. The central myths of the *Commento* are four: the birth of Venus (derived from Hesiod's *Theogony* and alluded to in Plato's *Symposium* 180D, the distinction between Ouranian and Pandemic Aphrodite); the birth of Eros from Poros and Penia in the gardens of Jupiter (Plato's *Symposium* 203B–C, Diotima's narrative); the descent of Orpheus to recover Eurydice from Hades (alluded to in *Symposium* 179D); and the figure of the *mors osculi*, the death by kiss, which Pico draws from Kabbalistic sources and connects to the Song of Songs. In each case, the interpretation proceeds by identifying the mythological figures with levels or processes in the Neoplatonic metaphysical hierarchy. Venus is the principle of beauty as it exists at different levels of the cosmic order. Poros and Penia are the plenitude and deficiency of the angelic intellect before and after its turn toward God. Orpheus's failure to recover Eurydice is the philosopher's failure to achieve genuine intellectual vision because he has not undergone the philosophical death required to disengage from the faculties of imagination and passive reason.

What is philosophically distinctive about Pico's allegorical practice in the *Commento*, as against Ficino's in the *De amore*, is its greater metaphysical precision and its greater willingness to use the allegorical reading as a vehicle for philosophical argument rather than philosophical illustration. Ficino's *De amore* is, among other things, a work of sustained Platonic allegoresis, but its interpretations tend to confirm and enrich doctrines that Ficino has established on independent grounds. Pico's *Commento* uses the allegorical readings more aggressively: the interpretation of the Orpheus myth is simultaneously an argument against Ficino's account of the conditions of philosophical ascent. Orpheus failed because he went into Hades still alive, still in possession of his imaginative and passive rational faculties — and this is not merely an allegorical observation but a substantive philosophical thesis about the cognitive requirements of genuine intellectual vision. The myth, correctly decoded, does not merely illustrate a philosophical position; it is the medium through which the philosophical position is articulated and defended.

Allen's essay "Eurydice in Hades: Florentine Platonism and an Orphic Mystery" (reprinted in Allen 2017) is the most thorough analysis of what Pico does with the Orphic materials in the *Commento*, and it demonstrates how intricately the mythological and philosophical registers are interwoven. Allen shows that Pico's treatment of Eurydice — identified with the intelligible beauty that the philosophical soul seeks — is not a casual allegorization but a worked-out account of the soul's cognitive relation to the intelligible world: Eurydice, as beauty in its intelligible form, can only be encountered by a soul that has fully disengaged from sensible cognition, and the myth's narrative of Orpheus's backward look is therefore the myth's philosophical thesis about what happens to any soul that attempts to grasp the intelligible while still employing the faculties of imagination and passive reason. The myth is philosophically reliable — it teaches what it appears to teach — but only for the reader capable of conducting the allegorical decoding.

The *900 Conclusiones* (1486) apply the same principle of mythological allegoresis to a much wider range of material, and Stephen Farmer's analysis of the *Conclusiones* is essential for understanding how myth functions within Pico's broader syncretic program. The theses "according to the method of the Orphic Hymns" (section 10) claim to extract philosophical content from the Orphic poems that no previous reader had discovered — including, implicitly, Ficino, who had worked with the same texts in his *De vita coelitus comparanda* (1489). Thesis 10.7 states the hermeneutical principle directly: to understand the Orphic Hymns requires knowing "how to intellectualize perceptible properties by the way of secret analogy." Thesis 10.15 identifies Night — the primordial deity from whom all things spring in Orphic cosmogony — with *Ein Sof*, the infinite divine ground in Kabbalistic mysticism. Thesis 10.13 identifies Typhon in Orpheus with Samael in Kabbalah. These identifications are the *Conclusiones*' mode of poetic-theological decoding: each mythological figure is decoded as naming a metaphysical reality, and the identification of Orphic with Kabbalistic names demonstrates — or is meant to demonstrate — that both traditions encode the same cosmic structure.

Farmer's reading of the *Conclusiones* identifies a structural feature with important implications for the concept of myth in Pico's intellectual program. The historical section's reverse-chronological arrangement of twenty-eight traditions encodes a theory of intellectual history in which the earliest traditions are the most authoritative because they stand closest to a primordial divine revelation, while the later traditions represent increasingly fragmented and mediated versions of the same truth (Farmer 1998, 54–80). Myth, on this scheme, is not a deficient mode of cognitive expression that precedes philosophy but the original form in which primordial wisdom was preserved — the mode chosen by the earliest theologians (Orpheus, Zoroaster, Hermes, Moses) precisely because its symbolically encrypted form would protect the wisdom it contained from profanation and dilution. The philosophers who came later (Plato, Pythagoras, Aristotle) did not surpass the ancient theologians by moving from myth to logos; they translated into discursive form what the mythological tradition had already encoded. Philosophy, on this account, is the exoteric version of what myth preserves in esoteric form.

The hermeneutical challenge that this claim raises — and that Farmer and Copenhaver have both, in different ways, insisted on — is the problem of method. If every mythological tradition can be decoded as encoding the same Neoplatonic and Kabbalistic truths, then the decoding procedure is not discovering anything about what the traditions independently teach but projecting onto them a set of commitments that the decoder brings to the reading. The concordances that Pico finds between Orphic Night and Kabbalistic *Ein Sof*, between Typhon and Samael, between the birth of Venus and the constitution of the angelic intellect, depend on the interpreter already knowing what the referents are supposed to be: the identification is possible because the Neoplatonic framework determines in advance what the myth could be encoding. Farmer (1998, 124–135) raises this concern explicitly as a methodological limit of the syncretic program, and Copenhaver, in his work on Pico's *Oration* and the Hermetic tradition, extends the concern to the legitimation strategy: Pico's opening catalog of ancient wisdom-traditions is not a neutral survey but a rhetorically constructed genealogy whose purpose is to position Pico's own synthesis as the culmination of a chain of transmission in which he is the most recent and most adequate decoder (Copenhaver 2019, passim).

The *Heptaplus* (1489) presents the most methodologically radical extension of mythological allegoresis in Pico's corpus, because it applies the same procedures to the Hebrew text of Genesis that Pico and the Neoplatonic tradition apply to the Orphic poems. The opening verses of the Torah — *Bereshit bara Elohim et ha-shamayim ve-et ha-aretz* (In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth) — are read as a philosophically encrypted account of the three worlds: the angelic or intelligible world, the celestial or astronomical world, and the elemental or terrestrial world, along with the human being who comprehends all three as the microcosm. Moses is presented as a poetic theologian in the full sense: he encoded in the narrative of the six days of creation the entire Neoplatonic cosmology, deliberately veiled in the form of a simple historical narrative accessible to all readers but concealing metaphysical content accessible only to those equipped to read it allegorically. Crofton Black's analysis of the *Heptaplus* documents the sophisticated hermeneutical tradition on which Pico draws — including the medieval Christian fourfold sense (*littera*, *allegoria*, *tropologia*, *anagogia*) and the Kabbalistic *pardes* (*peshat*, *remez*, *derash*, *sod*) — and shows that Pico's treatment of Genesis as a poetic theology participates in interpretive traditions that are themselves already ancient and theologically serious (Black 2006, passim). The implication is that the gap between Greek mythological allegoresis and Jewish biblical hermeneutics is, for Pico, essentially a gap in vocabulary rather than in method: both are practices of reading encoded divine wisdom from a sacred text whose surface is not its content.

The epistemological status of myth in Pico's *Oration* is somewhat different from its status in the *Commento* and the *Heptaplus*, and the difference is instructive. The *Oration* opens with what Copenhaver (2019) has analyzed as a deliberate construction of philosophical genealogy through selective citation of ancient authorities: Hermes Trismegistus, Pythagoras, Heraclitus, Zoroaster, and the Kabbalists are invoked as witnesses to the truth of the philosophical program that Pico is about to announce, and their invocation proceeds through a series of testimonial citations that are themselves mythologically inflected — Pythagoras's injunction not to eat beans, Heraclitus's paradoxes, the Zoroastrian account of the soul as a chariot ascending toward light. What Pico is doing in the *Oration*, on this reading, is itself a performance of mythological legitimation: he is constructing a philosophical genealogy through selective citation of ancient myth-encoded wisdom, positioning his own synthesis as the heir and completion of a tradition that runs from the most ancient theological sources through Plato to the present. The *Oration* is therefore not merely a philosophical argument but a piece of poetic theology in performance: it enacts the method it describes, presenting philosophical doctrine through the mythological medium of genealogical narrative.

None of this means that myth, for Pico, is simply identical with philosophy or that the distinction between *mythos* and *logos* is without significance. The distinction remains operative: the proper task of the philosopher is always to move from mythological surface to philosophical content, and the myths are valuable precisely because they point beyond themselves to truths that philosophy articulates more precisely. What Pico refuses is the conclusion that follows from a too-sharp reading of the Platonic distinction: the conclusion that myth is therefore deficient, that the properly philosophical treatment of a subject leaves myth behind. For Pico, myth and philosophy are related as the encrypted and the decoded, the esoteric and the exoteric, the beautiful covering and the truth it contains. The philosopher who can move between them — who can read the Orphic hymn and recognize the Neoplatonic metaphysics it encodes, and who can translate the scholastic demonstration back into the mythological narrative that was its original form — is practicing the hermeneutical art that Pico regards as the highest expression of philosophical *pietas* toward the ancient tradition. Myth, on this account, is not a primitive form of thought that philosophy supersedes; it is the original and permanent medium through which the deepest truths are preserved, and the philosopher who dismisses it forfeits access to the wisdom it contains.
