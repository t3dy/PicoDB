Iamblichus of Chalcis (c. 245–325 CE) is the most philosophically provocative of Pico's Neoplatonic sources and, in certain respects, the most dangerous. Dangerous not because Iamblichus was condemned or marginal — on the contrary, he was revered as "divine" (*theios*) by later Neoplatonists — but because his central theoretical innovation, the priority of ritual theurgical practice (*theourgía*) over discursive philosophical contemplation as the means of the soul's union with the divine, provided Pico with the most explicit ancient precedent for a philosophical defense of ritual magic. Where Plotinus had ascended to the One through intellectual purification and where Proclus systematized the hierarchy of being in axiomatic form, Iamblichus insisted that neither philosophical argument nor intellectual contemplation could by itself accomplish the soul's return. Only theurgy — the performance of divinely established rites using material objects, names, sounds, and symbols invested with divine power — could bridge the gap between human limitation and divine transcendence. This insistence, worked out most fully in the *De mysteriis Aegyptiorum* (On the Mysteries of the Egyptians), was both philosophically radical and religiously productive, and it shaped the theoretical framework within which Pico placed his magic theses in the *900 Conclusions*.

The biographical outline of Iamblichus is less well documented than that of Plotinus or Proclus. He was a Syrian Greek from Chalcis who studied under Porphyry, Plotinus's most important disciple and editor, and eventually founded his own school in Syria. His surviving works include *De vita Pythagorica* (On the Pythagorean Life), the *Protrepticus* (an exhortation to philosophy), and several books of what was projected as a ten-volume encyclopedia of Pythagorean doctrine. The *De mysteriis* is preserved under the name of the Egyptian priest Abammon and purports to be a reply to a letter from Porphyry to Iamblichus's disciple Anebo — a letter in which Porphyry posed skeptical questions about Egyptian ritual and theurgy. Iamblichus (as Abammon) defends theurgical practice not merely empirically but on philosophical grounds: the rites work, he argues, not because of human intention or intellectual comprehension but because of the divine power intrinsic to the symbols and materials themselves. As Brian Copenhaver summarizes the traditional characterization of Iamblichus in the context of Renaissance magic, he is to be found "explained by Porphyry, Iamblichus, Proclus, and" related Neoplatonists in the context of Egyptian temple rites and their philosophical justification (Copenhaver 2019, 13662).

The specific philosophical dispute at the origin of Iamblichus's theurgical theory is the quarrel with Porphyry about the soul's relation to the divine. Porphyry, following Plotinus's interiorizing emphasis, held that the soul could return to its source through philosophical contemplation alone; theurgy — if legitimate at all — was at best a useful preparatory practice for those not yet capable of pure intellectual ascent. Iamblichus, by contrast, argued that Porphyry's view rested on a misunderstanding of the soul's condition. The human soul is not naturally divine; its "summit" (*akron*) does not remain permanently in the intelligible realm while only its lower parts descend into matter, as Plotinus had implied. The whole soul descends, and it cannot raise itself back to the divine by its own efforts. The gods must act on the soul, and they do so through the material objects and ritual acts that they themselves have ordained as channels of divine power. The superior principle can only be activated by what already participates in it, which is precisely what sacred material objects do: a stone, a plant, an animal, a letter, or a sound that has been established by the gods as a divine symbol (*synthēma* or *symbolon*) carries divine power not because it "resembles" the divine intellectually but because the divine has chosen it as a vehicle. This is the key claim that distinguishes Iamblichan theurgy from mere philosophical analogy or sympathetic magic.

For Pico's purposes, Iamblichus offers something no other Neoplatonic source can provide: a philosophically sophisticated ancient precedent for the claim that ritual operations — including operations with words, letters, and material objects — can effect real contact with the divine without being demonic. This is the theoretical crux of Pico's magic theses in the *900 Conclusions*. Pico's first magic thesis asserts that "nothing is more powerful in natural magic than the hymns of Orpheus, if there be added to them the appropriate music, the disposition of the soul, and the other circumstances known to the wise" — a claim that invokes the theurgical tradition's emphasis on the combination of appropriate material and vocal elements with the correct inner disposition. More directly, his thesis that magic consists not in demonic manipulation but in the natural and divinely sanctioned operation of cosmic sympathy and celestial virtue implicitly draws on the Iamblichan distinction between demonic magic (which operates through inferior spirits) and divine theurgy (which operates through divinely ordained channels). S. A. Farmer notes that a thesis loosely drawn from Iamblichus's *De mysteriis* appears on the very folio of the *editio princeps* bearing the heading *Conclusiones secundum Proclum* (Farmer 1998, 10470), confirming that the Iamblichan and Procline material was arranged in close proximity within the syncretic architecture of the Roman debate.

The *Conclusions* include a section *secundum Iamblichum* (theses attributed to Iamblichus), and Wirszubski cites one representative example from this cluster: "Qui finalem causam diluviorum incendiorumque cognoverit, licet potius purgationes quam corruptiones vocabit" — "He who knows the final cause of floods and conflagrations may fittingly call them purifications rather than destructions" (Wirszubski 1989, 10689). The example is characteristic of Iamblichan thinking in its insistence that natural events, understood through the hierarchy of divine causation, are not random catastrophes but ordered operations of higher principles on lower ones. This is a theurgical reading of natural theology: floods and fires, like ritual cleansings, operate as purifications within the cosmic hierarchy.

The channel through which Iamblichus reached Pico most directly is Marsilio Ficino. Ficino began translating Iamblichus in 1488, and his paraphrase of the *De mysteriis* was ready by the beginning of 1489, though it was not published until later; by this time it had already been folded into the intellectual context that produced *De vita coelitus comparanda* (the third book of Ficino's *De vita libri tres*, 1489), the most influential statement of philosophical magic in the Renaissance. Ovanes Akopyan's detailed study of the relationship between Ficino's Iamblichan sources and his magical theory notes that Ficino started his translation and commentary on Iamblichus in 1488 and that this work provided the philosophical underpinning for his theory of astral magic: drawing light from the celestial hierarchy, working through the material correspondences between planets and terrestrial objects, using music, number, and sympathetic objects to attract and channel celestial influence (Akopyan 2021, 343–344, 6013–6046). Ficino's theory draws on Iamblichus's account of light (*phōs*) as a vehicle of divine power, adapting the theurgical account of material divine symbols into a naturalized theory of celestial influence.

Pico's relationship to Ficino's Iamblichan magic is not one of simple inheritance but of critical appropriation and revision. The *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem* — Pico's unfinished polemic against divinatory astrology, written in the last years of his life — directly attacks the astrological component of Ficino's magic theory while leaving intact (or even endorsing) the theurgical-magical claims that do not depend on astrology. As Akopyan shows, "the name of Iamblichus appears in the *Disputationes* only once," in Book III, and Pico there "adopts Iamblichus' arguments" against "Porphyry's fascination with astrology" (Akopyan 2021, 2437–2438). The polemic is specifically against astrological determinism and natal-chart prediction; Pico uses Iamblichus — who had also criticized Porphyry's susceptibility to astrological thinking — as a weapon against Ficino's astral magic. The historical irony is considerable: Pico deploys the theurgist par excellence against the astrological elaboration of theurgical magic.

The distinction Pico draws in his magic theses between "natural magic" and "demonic magic" depends substantially on the Iamblichan framework. Demonic magic, in the *Conclusions*, is the manipulation of inferior spirits through invocations or operations that give those spirits improper power over human affairs. It is condemned. Natural magic, by contrast, operates through the intrinsic powers of natural things, the correspondences and sympathies embedded in the structure of creation by the divine creator. This is not demonic; it is a learned use of the cosmic hierarchy. And Pico adds a third category — cabalistic operation — which he treats as operating through the divine names and letters in a way that exceeds even natural magic in efficacy and dignity, since it works not through created intermediaries but through the divine language itself. The Abulafian Kabbalah of letter-combination that Pico encountered through the materials provided by Flavius Mithridates functions, as Wirszubski demonstrates, as a kind of Jewish theurgy: the manipulation of the divine Hebrew letters (*otiyot*) according to the combinatorial techniques of Abraham Abulafia's *ars combinandi* achieves effects analogous to what Iamblichus attributes to the divinely established ritual symbols (Wirszubski 1989, passim). The parallel is not explicit in Pico — he does not say "Abulafia is like Iamblichus" — but the structural homology is real: both traditions posit ritual operations with divinely established formal symbols that exceed human comprehension and work through the divine power inherent in the symbols themselves.

The condemned thesis that caused Pico the most difficulty with the papal commission of 1487 is instructive in this connection. His magic thesis 9 in the *Conclusions* (in Farmer's numbering) asserts that "nothing is more efficacious in natural magic than the hymns of Orpheus" combined with "the appropriate music, the disposition of the soul, and the other circumstances known to the wise" — and he adds, in what became theologically explosive, that these hymns work "in the way that the magnetic stone attracts iron." The comparison to the magnet is deliberately Iamblichan: Iamblichus uses the magnet analogy in the *De mysteriis* to illustrate how material objects can attract divine power through their intrinsic sympathetic constitution rather than through any rational resemblance or intellectual comprehension. To say that Orphic hymns work like magnets is to say that they work theurgically, not intellectually — which is precisely what Iamblichus argues about theurgical rites in general. In his *Apology*, Pico defended this thesis by insisting that he meant only "natural magic" and not demonic invocation; the defense is philosophically coherent given the Iamblichan framework but did not satisfy his inquisitors, who recognized the theological problems in any claim that natural-material operations could effect the soul's contact with the divine without the mediation of the Church's sacramental system.

Ficino's commentary on Iamblichus's *De mysteriis*, composed during the same period as *De vita coelitus comparanda*, provides important context for understanding how Pico's magic theses related to the broader Florentine intellectual environment. Akopyan's analysis shows that Ficino's commentary follows Iamblichus's hierarchy of different kinds of light (*phōs*), making the lowest of these — material solar light — the vehicle through which celestial and divine influence descends into the sublunary world (Akopyan 2021, 6154–6163). This Iamblichan light-theory is what allows Ficino to treat astral magic as a form of natural philosophy rather than demonic manipulation: the physician who draws solar virtue through a gold talisman is operating within the cosmic hierarchy, not invoking demons. Pico accepts the general Iamblichan framework of cosmic hierarchy and sympathetic operation but rejects the specifically astrological mediation that Ficino builds on it. The stars, for Pico in the *Disputationes*, are not the proximate causes of human events; the divine hierarchy operates more directly, through the angelic intelligences and the divine names, than through planetary influence.

The question of what Pico actually read in Iamblichus in the original Greek deserves some attention. The *De vita Pythagorica* and *Protrepticus* are cited directly in the *Oration* (Borghesi and Riva's edition identifies multiple references), and the *De mysteriis* is referenced both in the *Oration* and in the magic theses. Pico's library, documented by Pearl Kibre, included Greek manuscripts; Ficino's translations and paraphrase would have been available from 1488 onward, possibly in manuscript form earlier. The *Oration* cites Iamblichus alongside Plotinus, Porphyry, and Proclus as philosophers "in whom you will be awed by an occult philosophy" (della Mirandola / Borghesi-Riva 2012, 4086), placing him in the lineage of ancient sages whose hidden wisdom the syncretic program is designed to recover. The characterization is consistent: Iamblichus represents the theurgical-ritualistic dimension of the ancient wisdom tradition, the side that cannot be reduced to intellectual argument.

The historiographical challenge Iamblichus poses for PicoDB is that his influence on Pico has sometimes been either overstated in the direction of making Pico a ritual magician or understated in the direction of treating the magic theses as peripheral to his "real" philosophical interests. The truth is more nuanced. Iamblichus provides Pico with a philosophically legitimate ancient precedent for the claim that ritual operations — including Kabbalistic letter-combination, Orphic hymns, and the application of natural sympathies — are not demonic and are not merely poetic metaphors but genuine operations within the cosmic hierarchy. Whether Pico "believed in" theurgy in any strong existential sense is a question the evidence does not clearly resolve; what is clear is that he constructed a theoretical framework in which such operations are philosophically defensible, and that this framework draws directly and self-consciously on Iamblichus. The fourteen condemned theses that provoked the papal ban included his magic theses, and Pico's defense of them in the *Apology* is the most sustained piece of philosophical argument about ritual magic in the early Renaissance. Iamblichus is the ancient authority that makes the defense coherent.
