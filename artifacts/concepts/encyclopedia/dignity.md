# Dignity (dignitas hominis)

The concept of "dignity" as it attaches to Pico della Mirandola is one of the most consequential misattributions in the intellectual history of the Renaissance. For more than a century, scholars, anthologists, and teachers have read Pico's undelivered prefatory oration as a proto-modern declaration of human self-determination and universal worth — a founding text of Renaissance humanism and, by extension, of the modern liberal subject. This reading, though deeply entrenched, rests on a cluster of historiographical distortions that recent scholarship, above all Brian P. Copenhaver's *Magic and the Dignity of Man* (2019) and Sophia Howlett's *Re-evaluating Pico* (2021), has systematically dismantled. What Pico actually wrote is a more demanding, stranger, and philosophically richer document than the canonical digest would suggest.

**The Text and Its Title.** The speech Pico composed in 1486 was intended as a preface to the public disputation he had announced for January of 1487 in Rome, in which he proposed to defend his *Conclusiones nongentae* — nine hundred philosophical and theological theses drawn from Platonism, Aristotelianism, Scholasticism, Hermetism, and Kabbalah, assembled at enormous expense and advertised with a standing invitation to any scholar in Europe willing to debate them, with all travel costs covered by the author. The Oration was, in other words, not an independent work but a prolusione, an inaugural address whose purpose was to introduce that debate, explain its intellectual program, and make an argument for the unity of all philosophical traditions. The debate was never held. Pope Innocent VIII forbade it within weeks of the theses being published, appointed a commission to examine them, condemned thirteen outright and declared the remainder suspect, and ultimately prohibited the entire book. The Oration, never delivered, circulated in manuscript and was first printed by Pico's nephew Gianfrancesco in 1496 as part of a posthumous edition of the elder Pico's works. The title by which it is now universally known — *Oratio de hominis dignitate*, "Oration on the Dignity of Man" — was not given by Pico himself. The earliest printed text to attach this phrase to the speech as a title dates from 1504, a decade after Pico's death (Copenhaver 2019, note 44). The speech has no title in its earliest witnesses.

More consequentially, Copenhaver demonstrates that the word *dignitas* appears only twice in the Oration, and in neither instance does Pico apply it to humanity as a general or universal property. Gianfrancesco Pico, who knew the text better than any reader since, used the word five times in his biography of his uncle, but each time in the narrow Roman sense of "rank" or "title." He said almost nothing about the Oration in his life of Giovanni, neither summarizing its argument nor endorsing its opening claims; the early biographer found the speech clever but awkward, connected as it was to the condemned Conclusions. Thomas More, who translated Gianfrancesco's Life into English for a pious nun in the early sixteenth century, likewise imposed no reading of the speech as a text about human dignity; he dropped the phrase *propria dignitas* from one letter, and elsewhere gave the word only in its old-fashioned sense of heavenly rank or station (Copenhaver 2019, 177–78). Neither of the two writers closest to the text in time thought "dignity" was what the Oration was about.

**What the Oration Is About.** The opening movement of the Oration presents an extended creation fable whose logic is protreptic, not celebratory. After the Demiurge has furnished all other creatures with fixed natures and assigned stations, he finds that no specific form remains for Adam. God therefore addresses the first man in words that have become the most quoted passage in all of Pico's writing: "No fixed seat, no special look, nor any particular gift of your own have we given you, Adam, so that what seat, what look, what gifts you choose for yourself, those you may have and hold as you wish, according to your purpose." Man alone is undetermined; he can sink downward into the vegetable or the brutish, or rise to the angelic and the divine. Pico names this creature a "chameleon" — and the valence of the chameleon figure in classical tradition was, as Copenhaver carefully shows, primarily negative: the chameleon was a symbol of flattery, servility, and inconstancy (Copenhaver 2019, 362–63). The astonishment invited at the end of section eight — "Who would not be astonished at this chameleon of ours?" — is not straightforward praise.

The indeterminacy of human nature is presented not as an achievement to celebrate but as a charge to act upon. From section eleven onward, the register of the Oration shifts from protreptic fable to ascetic program. Pico's argument is that this undetermined creature must, precisely because it has no fixed nature, subject itself to a rigorous program of philosophical and spiritual discipline. "Let us scorn things of earth, let us despise those of heaven, and then, leaving behind whatever belongs to the world, let us fly up to the hypercosmic court nearest the most exalted divinity." The Oration describes a graduated ascent through moral philosophy (which purges the passions), dialectic (which clarifies the reason), natural philosophy (which surveys the structure of the world), and finally theology, which alone confers the "peace" Pico calls the end of all philosophy. This peace is not a social or political achievement but a mystical henosis, a union of the individual soul with the divine intellect. As Howlett argues, Pico's philosophical vision is fundamentally exceptionalist and solitary: unlike Ficino, who imagined a community ascending together and a philosopher returning to reform the world, Pico describes a lonely, austere journey whose telos appears to be an almost permanent absorption in the divine — "a more majestic divinity cloaked in human flesh" (Howlett 2021, 565–75). The program is ascetic mysticism, not a humanist manifesto.

The angelic hierarchy of Pseudo-Dionysius provides the structural spine of the Oration's middle section. Seraphim burn with love; Cherubim blaze with intellectual light; Thrones stand firm in moral judgment. The soul seeking ascent must first purify itself through moral philosophy in the manner of the Thrones, then illuminate itself through philosophy in the manner of the Cherubim, and finally be consumed in theological love in the manner of the Seraphim. The model for this threefold discipline is explicitly Dionysian and Pauline, drawing on the pseudo-Areopagite's celestial hierarchy and on Paul's account of his rapture to the third heaven. Pico then moves into the sections on natural magic and Kabbalah — the portions of the Oration most directly tied to the 900 Conclusions — where he defends both disciplines as legitimate paths of philosophical and theological inquiry. Natural magic is described as the practical completion of natural philosophy, the art of drawing down the powers latent in the world's fabric; Kabbalah, the secret tradition of Hebrew interpretation, is presented as confirming the truth of Christianity through methods unknown to the Latin philosophical tradition. These are not peripheral ornaments to a central argument about dignity; they are the argument's destination. The Oration exists to justify the Conclusions, and the Conclusions are dominated by magical and Kabbalistic theses.

**The Word and Its Absence.** Copenhaver's philological finding is as significant as any interpretive claim in his book: the word *dignitas* — meaning not "dignity" in the Kantian sense of inherent worth but "rank," "status," or "honor" in the Roman and medieval senses — does not function as a governing term in the Oration. Even Giannozzo Manetti, whose *De dignitate et excellentia hominis* (c. 1450) is the genuine predecessor-text for the tradition of Renaissance writings on human excellence, uses *dignitas* without ever defining it or constructing a philosophical argument about what it is; the word appears in Manetti's book roughly once every seven pages, always as a term of comparison (humans have *dignitas* because they surpass other creatures) but never as a concept given independent content (Copenhaver 2019, 47). Pico's case is more extreme. The speech that modern culture has named after *dignitas* almost never uses the word, and when it does, the context is traditional rank-language, not a philosophical claim about universal human worth. The modern concept of dignity — the inherent, incomparable, unconditional worth of every rational being regardless of rank or achievement — was theorized by Immanuel Kant in the late eighteenth century and reaches its fullest post-Kantian expression in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights of 1948. Copenhaver is emphatic that to read Pico as a prophet of this concept is to misread the Latin word *dignitas*, to misread the Oration, and to misread the historical relationship between Pico's moment and the Enlightenment (Copenhaver 2019, 24–30, 55–69).

**The Making of the Pico Myth.** The process by which Pico was transformed into the herald of Renaissance humanism and the prophet of human dignity unfolded in stages spanning four centuries, and understanding that process is inseparable from understanding the concept itself. The foundational moment in the English-speaking tradition came with Jacob Burckhardt's *The Civilization of the Renaissance in Italy* (1860), which did not focus on Pico but gave the Oration its decisive modern framing. In a brief passage of fewer than two hundred words at the end of his fourth chapter, Burckhardt cited the opening of the speech as the "most exalted premonitions" on a topic he had been developing — the social mobility and aesthetic freedom distinctive of Italian culture. Burckhardt paraphrased God's speech to Adam as demonstrating that man was "bound to no fixed place, to no specific activity, to no necessary obligations, but gave him mobility and free will" (Copenhaver 2019, 85–86). The prince's audience in this passage was not the philosopher's study but the Italian peasant going freely to town, the artist shifting perspective at will — Burckhardt's own cultural preoccupations read back into a late-fifteenth-century Latin speech. The passage made Pico an icon of individualism and freedom in a narrative that was Hegelian in its philosophical scaffolding and nationalist in its cultural stakes.

The Italian rehabilitation of Pico gathered momentum through the early twentieth century, culminating in Eugenio Garin's Giovanni Pico della Mirandola: Vita e dottrina (1937), the book that, more than any other, fixed the "dignity of man" as the center of Pico's philosophical achievement and established him as the presiding genius of Renaissance humanism. Garin's Pico was a philosopher of human freedom whose synthesis of ancient and medieval traditions pointed toward a modern, progressive concept of autonomous selfhood. The political valences of this portrait were complex — Garin worked in Fascist Italy, under the patronage of Giovanni Gentile — but the scholarly prestige of the book was enormous and durable (Copenhaver 2019, 108–21). When Ernst Cassirer, Paul Oskar Kristeller, and John Herman Randall published *The Renaissance Philosophy of Man* in 1948 — Phoenix Books Number 1 of the University of Chicago Press, which would reach tens of thousands of American students over the following decades — the Oration was presented as Pico's masterpiece, its topics as "the dignity of man founded on man's freedom" and the "glorification of man," and its significance as the defining statement of Renaissance humanism (Copenhaver 2019, 318–20). Magic and Kabbalah were not problems to be explained but embarrassments to be minimized. The "dignity of man" became the caption for a textbook Pico, a proto-modern subject stripped of his Dionysian angelology, his Kabbalistic theses, his ascetic mysticism, and his condemnation by Rome.

Howlett identifies the same distortion from a different angle. Pico's fame, she argues, has consistently outrun his actual output — a fact that reflects partly his early death (at thirty-one, with most major projects unfinished), partly his extraordinary self-fashioning as "the prince of concord," and partly the desires of later readers to find in him a figure who prefigures what they already value (Howlett 2021, 3–4, 27). The Oration is a short text, provocative and unfinished, written for a specific occasion that never materialized. To read it as the magnum opus of Renaissance thought is to let the history of reception substitute for the history of the text.

**What Pico Celebrated.** What the Oration actually celebrates is more specific and more demanding than the humanist portrait allows. The text praises not the dignity of the human species as such but the exceptional capacity of the philosophical individual to undertake a rigorous ascent that most people will never attempt and that the speech itself describes as austere, costly, and potentially lifelong. The indeterminate Adam of the opening fable is not everyman; he is the philosophical soul confronting its own responsibility. The "seeds of every kind" that God has planted in humans are not automatically cultivated; they require the philosophy, dialectic, natural science, and mystical theology that the rest of the Oration prescribes. The text is protreptic — an invitation to philosophy — directed at an audience of learned disputers, not a declaration addressed to humanity as a whole.

The "dignity" that Pico's text actually contains is closer to a dynamic, performative excellence than to a static universal property. A person who "has surrendered to the belly" has become a vegetable; one "blinded by false enchantments of fantasy" has become a beast; but a "philosopher distinguishing everything by right reason" is "a heavenly animal, not of the earth," and "a pure contemplator, unaware of the body, withdrawn to the mind's sanctuary" is "a more majestic divinity cloaked in human flesh." This is not the dignity that belongs incomparably to every human being regardless of achievement; it is an achievement-dignity, a dignity won through discipline and self-transformation, available only to those who pursue the full program of ascetic philosophy. It is closer to the Roman *dignitas* of earned status — or to the Aristotelian concept of *eudaimonia* as the excellence of the fully realized soul — than to the Kantian dignity Pico's admirers have wished to find in him.

The Kabbalistic and magical dimensions of the Oration are not separable from this picture. Natural magic, for Pico, is the discipline that reads the hidden sympathies and powers of the natural world as evidence of its divine structuring; it is not theurgy or superstition but the practical summit of natural philosophy. Kabbalah, which Pico had learned from the converso scholar Flavius Mithridates (who translated Hebrew and Aramaic texts for him at enormous cost), offered what Pico took to be confirmation of Christian doctrines of Trinity and Incarnation from within the Jewish esoteric tradition itself. Both practices belong to the ascent: they are ways of moving from the visible world upward toward the divine intellect. The Oration defends them as legitimate precisely because the 900 Conclusions will propose specific theses about both, and those theses require a theoretical justification. The speech that was later named for "dignity" is, in its actual texture and argument, a defense of natural magic and Christian Kabbalah as paths of philosophical ascent.

**Scholarly Correction.** The correction offered by Copenhaver and Howlett is not simply negative. It does not reduce Pico to a minor figure or dismiss the Oration as unimportant. It restores both figure and text to a more accurate and philosophically richer profile. Pico's contribution, as Howlett emphasizes, lies in his distinctive synthesis of Aristotelianism, Platonism, and Jewish Kabbalah — three pillars whose combination, even when it generates tensions he cannot fully resolve, produces a form of theological philosophy focused on the mystical ascent of the individual soul (Howlett 2021, 399–424). The Oration is a genuine philosophical document; it simply is not the document that a century of textbook pedagogy has made it out to be. Its opening fable is not a triumphalist account of human freedom but a challenge: given that you have no fixed nature, what will you make of yourself? The answer the Oration offers is unambiguous — philosophy, asceticism, mystical union — and that answer is closer to Neoplatonic contemplation and Christian ascetic theology than to modern individualism or Renaissance optimism.

Copenhaver situates the Oration within the history of how the Latin word *dignitas* actually functioned before Kant transformed its semantic range. In the Roman tradition Pico inherited, *dignitas* was above all a term of social rank and civic honor, not an inherent property of persons as such; in the Christian tradition, it attached to the soul's created resemblance to God, and to offices within the ecclesiastical hierarchy. Neither the Roman nor the Christian sense of *dignitas* is the concept that modern readers of the Oration have found there (Copenhaver 2019, 9–69). The gap between those historical senses and the modern concept is not bridged by Pico. It is bridged by Burckhardt's prose, by Garin's Italian idealism, by Cassirer's neo-Kantianism, and by the pedagogical needs of postwar American universities — all of which found in the Oration's opening pages a text that could be excerpted, anthologized, and taught as the founding moment of a modernity whose philosophical vocabulary was not Pico's own.
