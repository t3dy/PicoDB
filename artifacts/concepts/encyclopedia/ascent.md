# Ascent

The concept of ascent is not one theme among others in Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's Oration on the Dignity of Man — it is the organizing principle of the entire text. Read in its original context as a prolusion to the proposed Roman disputation of the 900 Conclusions, the Oration is a protreptic: a formal exhortation to a particular form of life, addressed to the assembled philosophical and theological intelligentsia of Rome, designed to persuade them that the journey from moral purification to mystical union with God is the defining vocation of the philosophically prepared human being. Brian Copenhaver's formulation — that the Oration is "a mystic's manifesto for ascent, by way of magic and Kabbalah, to self-annihilating union with God" — captures what Pico actually argues. The Adam passage at the text's opening is not a celebration of free self-creation but the metaphysical premise on which the ascent program rests: Adam alone among created beings was assigned no fixed nature, precisely so that the direction of his development — downward into brute materiality or upward through the angelic intelligences toward union with the divine — would be determined by the quality of his philosophical and spiritual formation.

The indeterminacy of Adam's nature is thus not a general statement about the human condition in the abstract. It is a specific claim about ontological liminality. Every other creature occupies a fixed position in the cosmic hierarchy, with determinate properties and a determinate mode of being. Adam is placed "at the centre of the world" — in mundi positum centro — not as a privileged location but as a liminal one, neither wholly heavenly nor earthly, neither mortal nor immortal, capable of assimilation to any level of the hierarchy precisely because he is committed to none by nature. This extraordinary metaphysical plasticity is, for Pico, the anthropological basis of ascent: it explains why the human soul can undertake a journey that no other created being can attempt. Angels are already at the summit; animals are fixed below; the human soul is uniquely positioned to traverse the entire hierarchy in the direction of its choosing.

Pico articulates the stages of this ascent with programmatic precision in the Oration's second, longer section, which is often neglected by readers fixated on the Adam passage. The ascent proceeds through seven distinguishable stages, though Pico presents them not as a numbered list but as a narrative of philosophical formation. The first stage is moral philosophy, understood as the purgation of the passions — the cleansing of the soul from the disorder of appetite, anger, and sensory distraction that prevents it from exercising its rational faculties properly. This is the cathartic work without which no higher progress is possible. The second stage is dialectics and the exercise of reason in argumentation, by which the soul learns precision, self-consistency, and the proper relations among concepts. The third is natural philosophy, the study of the physical world in its hidden as well as apparent dimensions. The fourth is theology, approached through what Pico calls the "Platonic frenzies" — the divine madnesses of love, poetry, prophecy, and Socratic questioning through which the soul is lifted beyond its ordinary rational operations. The fifth stage is natural magic, the understanding and manipulation of cosmic correspondences and sympathies that binds the different levels of creation together. The sixth is Kabbalah, the Hebrew mystical tradition that provides, through the doctrine of divine names and the art of letter combination, specific techniques for ascending through the highest reaches of the divine emanation. The seventh and final stage is mystical union — the mors osculi, the "kiss of death," in which the soul is absorbed into the divine intellect.

The influence of Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite on this program is explicit and structuring. Pico names Dionysius in the Oration as a model and draws on the Dionysian triplex via — the three ways of purgation, illumination, and union — to organize his own account. The Dionysian scheme, developed in the Mystical Theology and the Divine Names and mediated to Pico through scholastic commentary traditions as well as through direct reading, maps onto Pico's program at the highest level of generality: moral philosophy and dialectics accomplish the purgative work; natural philosophy and theology accomplish the illuminative work; natural magic, Kabbalah, and mystical union accomplish the unitive work. Pico's innovation is to insert magic and Kabbalah into the Dionysian framework, so that they become not departures from the ascent but its technical instruments. The three ways of Dionysius become, in Pico's hands, a seven-stage program in which the specifically Renaissance disciplines of natural magic and Christian Kabbalah occupy the critical middle position between philosophical preparation and mystical terminus.

Natural magic's role in this program deserves particular attention, because it is easy to misread magic as a descent into material manipulation rather than a step in spiritual ascent. For Pico, natural magic is emphatically not demonic or goetic practice, which he condemns as "a thing to be abhorred." It is, rather, "the deepest contemplation of the most secret things and ultimately the knowledge of all nature" — a mode of wisdom that reads the universe as a system of hidden correspondences binding together the celestial, aerial, and elemental worlds. The magus who has mastered natural philosophy perceives the sympathy between particular stones, herbs, celestial bodies, and angelic intelligences, and can act as a servant of nature, activating the latent powers bound into creation by the divine craftsman. This activity is for Pico a form of contemplation: the magician does not impose alien forces on nature but discovers and cooperates with the rational order already present in it. More importantly, the understanding of cosmic correspondences that natural magic requires is precisely the preparation needed for the higher Kabbalistic work. The magician who knows how the created world hangs together in its hidden sympathetic connections is positioned to understand the divine names that underlie those connections and to work with them in the Kabbalistic mode.

Kabbalah occupies the sixth stage of ascent — the position immediately before mystical union — because it provides what neither natural magic nor natural philosophy can: specific techniques for moving through the highest levels of the divine emanatory structure. Pico's Kabbalistic sources, mediated almost entirely through the Latin translations of Flavius Mithridates, included materials from both the major currents of the Kabbalistic tradition. The Kabbalah of the sefirot — the ten divine emanations through which the infinite God (Ein Sof) is present in creation — gave Pico a structured account of the divine hierarchy through which the soul must travel in its ascent. The prophetic or speculative Kabbalah associated with Abraham Abulafia gave him the specific techniques: letter combination (hokmat ha-tseruf), the meditation on and permutation of the letters of divine names, and the gematria that establishes numerical correspondences between Hebrew words of the same numerical value. In Abulafia's tradition, systematic practice of these techniques produces a state of consciousness in which the ordinary rational ego is dissolved and the intellect is united with the Agent Intellect — a state of mystical union that Abulafia describes in terms Pico recognized as consonant with both Aristotelian accounts of intellectual union and Neoplatonic accounts of henosis. What Wirszubski demonstrated in his reconstruction of Pico's Kabbalistic sources is that Pico read Abulafia as providing the technical bridge between philosophical intellection and mystical union — the specific practice that carries the philosophically prepared soul across the threshold from illumination to union.

The goal of this entire program is the mors osculi — the "kiss of death," mitat neshikah in Hebrew — a concept drawn from Maimonides' discussion of the highest mode of death in the Guide for the Perplexed and from Talmudic passages on the death of Moses and Aaron, who are said to have died by divine kiss rather than by natural causes. The mors osculi in Pico's framework designates the supreme stage of ascent in which the soul achieves henosis, union, with the divine intellect — a union that is not a merging with impersonal Absolute but an absorption into the life of God that constitutes the soul's highest and most complete actualization. This union is the positive content of what the Oration calls Adam's "dignity": dignity consists not in autonomy or self-sufficiency but in the capacity to be drawn into God. Pico describes it through the figure of the Seraph: "He who is a Seraph, that is, a lover, is in God, and God is in him; indeed, he and God are one." The language of self-annihilating union, borrowed from Christian mystical theology, from Abulafia, and from the scholastic interpretation of the Aristotelian Active Intellect, converges in this single image of the soul consumed in the fire of divine love.

Sophia Howlett's revisionary reading of the Oration presses an important corrective that the ascent program requires. The modern reception of the Oration has frequently treated Adam's indeterminate nature as an affirmation of universal human potential — every human being endowed by nature with unlimited capacity for self-development. Pico does not say this. His ascent program presupposes not merely philosophical interest but extraordinary philosophical formation: years of work in moral philosophy, dialectics, natural philosophy, and theology; mastery of Latin, Greek, Hebrew, and Aramaic sufficient for the Kabbalistic work; and a degree of intellectual discipline that only a very small number of individuals can achieve. The Adam passage establishes a metaphysical possibility — not a democratic dispensation of dignity across the species, but a description of what the most developed human beings can, in principle, attain. The program of the Oration's second section is addressed to the intellectual elite gathered in Rome, not to humanity as a whole. Howlett's point is that Pico's ascent is solitary and exceptional, in the tradition of Plotinian mystical ascent rather than of any egalitarian humanism. The "flight of the alone to the Alone" that Plotinus describes in the Enneads is the model, and Pico's seven-stage program is the Renaissance specification of what that flight requires and how it is to be accomplished.

This has consequences for the proper interpretation of the dignity claim. If ascent is the content of dignity — if what makes the human soul unique and admirable is its capacity for this particular journey — then dignity in Pico's sense is not a property distributed equally across all human beings simply in virtue of their humanity. It is a potentiality that is actualized only through the disciplined philosophical and spiritual work that the Oration describes. Adam's indeterminacy is a metaphysical endowment; its actualization in the direction of the divine is a demanding spiritual achievement. The Oration ends not with a celebration of what humans are but with an exhortation to what a properly formed philosopher can become: "Let us be led away, fathers, let us be led away by the Socratic frenzies that will so lift us beyond our minds as to put our mind and ourselves in God." This is the language of rare, transformative experience, not of a general human right.

Read in this way, the ascent program of the Oration connects directly to the 900 Conclusions, which are its proper context. The Conclusions are structured as a hierarchical survey of philosophical traditions — from medieval scholastics backward through Platonic, Aristotelian, Orphic, Zoroastrian, Hermetic, and finally Kabbalistic materials — designed to demonstrate that all these traditions, when properly interpreted, point toward the same summit. The Kabbalistic theses crown the collection precisely because Kabbalah, in Pico's account, provides the most specific and most powerful techniques for the final stages of the ascent the Oration describes. The Heptaplus, written three years later, returns to the same program in the genre of biblical commentary, reading the creation narrative of Genesis as an anagogical account of the soul's ascent from the material world through the celestial intelligences toward the divine source. In all three texts, the movement is the same: upward, through progressive purification and illumination, toward the annihilating union with God that Pico calls dignity and that the Kabbalists call the kiss.
