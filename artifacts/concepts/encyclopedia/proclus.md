Proclus (412–485 CE) is the single most important Neoplatonic source in Pico's *900 Conclusions*, and his *Elements of Theology* (*Stoicheiōsis theologikē*) provides the axiomatic-deductive skeleton for the syncretic program Pico proposed at Rome in December 1486. More than any other late antique philosopher, it is Proclus who gives Pico's system its characteristic combination of logical precision and baroque metaphysical elaboration — the intricate orders of henads, intellects, and souls through which Pico attempts to demonstrate that Kabbalistic, Platonic, Orphic, Zoroastrian, and Christian traditions converge on the same structural truths. That Pico should also use Proclus to take a position directly against Ficino's Plotinian henology in *De ente et uno* (1491) — and that Ficino should respond by composing a section-by-section rebuttal — makes Proclus the philosopher at the center of the most technically significant philosophical dispute of the Florentine Platonic circle.

Proclus succeeded Syrianus as head of the Platonic Academy in Athens around 437 CE and led it until his death. He was, as Sophia Howlett observes, not merely a philosopher with theological interests but something closer to a religious figure: "vatic in his inclinations," he "enhanced a growing ceremonial (theurgical) strand within Neoplatonism" and "took Neoplatonism to its final stage within the Academy form: a theological philosophy assimilated with Gnosticism, among other traditions, baroque in its complexity with layers and layers of beings and substructures to the Platonic emanations, and with a 'practicing' arm" (Howlett 2020, 3313–3330). The two major surviving works that mattered most for Pico are the *Elements of Theology* and the *Platonic Theology* (*Theologia Platōnikē*), though Proclus's commentaries on the *Timaeus*, *Parmenides*, *Alcibiades*, and *Republic* are also present in the *Conclusions* and in background to *De ente et uno*.

The *Elements of Theology* is an axiomatic work in 211 propositions, modeled formally on Euclid's *Elements* but devoted not to geometry but to the structure of reality: beginning from the simplest propositions about wholes and parts, the text proceeds by strict logical demonstration through the necessity of the One, the generation of henads, the structure of intellect, soul, and body. The henads (*henades*) — distinct unities that participate directly in the One and mediate between the One's absolute simplicity and the multiplicity of the intelligible realm — are Proclus's most characteristic contribution beyond Plotinus. Where Plotinus had moved directly from the One to Intellect to Soul, Proclus interposes a rich intermediate order: the henads are, as it were, the "many gods" of Greek polytheism rationalized into the Neoplatonic system, each one a unity that participates in "the One" without losing its distinction. The *Platonic Theology* is the comprehensive synthetic reading of all of Plato through this framework, arguing that the sequence of Platonic dialogues, culminating in the *Parmenides*, reveals the hierarchical structure of reality as a whole.

A crucial condition of Pico's access to Proclus distinguishes his relation to this philosopher from his relation to Plotinus. The *Enneads* had no complete Latin translation before Ficino's 1492 edition, and Pico's pre-1492 knowledge of Plotinus depended heavily on manuscript drafts and shared reading in Ficino's circle. Proclus was differently situated. William of Moerbeke had produced a Latin translation of Proclus's *Commentary on the Parmenides* in the thirteenth century, and the *Elements of Theology* had circulated in a Latin version (and its derivative, the *Liber de causis*, long attributed to Aristotle) throughout the medieval period. Allen notes that Moerbeke's translation of the *Parmenides* commentary was "selected for translation" and "made available to a few medieval readers," and that Ficino — the "first since antiquity who clearly achieved a mastery of Proclus's complex works" — built his understanding partly on Moerbeke's pioneering but limited rendering (Allen 2017, 474, 483). Pico, as Farmer documents, was likely the first Latin philosopher since antiquity to approach the *Platonic Theology* "in anything approximating its original exegetical spirit" (Farmer 1998, 1527). The implication is that Pico's Proclus was, in an important sense, more direct and more philologically adequate than the Proclus available to most of his contemporaries — including, at the relevant period, Ficino himself. Farmer notes pointedly that all of Ficino's translations from Proclus and other post-Plotinian Neoplatonists, as well as the most clearly Proclean of Ficino's own works (the commentaries on the *Parmenides* and *Phaedrus*), appeared *after* rather than before the *900 Conclusions* in which Pico first "strongly underscored the views of those writers" and that Pico "not only claimed precedence in the *Oration* and *Apology* in debating those views — a claim whose polemical intent was not lost on Ficino — but drew more than a hundred of his theses from Proclus alone, making the latter writer by far the single most important source in the nine hundred theses" (Farmer 1998, 1595–1599). The figure Farmer and Howlett give varies slightly in the counting, but both confirm the priority: Proclus with 55–100 or more theses depending on how one counts source clusters, constituting more citations than any other thinker.

The theses *secundum Proclum* in the *900 Conclusions* (section 24, with 55 theses) are the primary site of Pico's technical engagement with Procline philosophy within the disputational framework of the Roman project. These theses address the structure of henads, the ordering of divine and intellectual principles, the relationships between limit and infinity (*peras* and *apeiron*) at different levels of the hierarchy, and the metaphysical basis of the soul's return. Pico reads these alongside the Kabbalistic *sefirot* — the ten divine emanations or powers of God as elaborated in the Kabbalistic tradition — and finds in them, as Farmer puts it, a genuine historical connection as well as a syncretic occasion: "the *sefirot*, or emanated states of God's being, originated in ancient speculation on God's secret names and properties... To form the *sefirot*, these were syncretically fused by medieval Hebrew exegetes with the gnostic aeons, Neo-Platonic henads, and similar concepts" (Farmer 1998, 4139–4145). Pico's identification of the Procline henads with the Kabbalistic *sefirot* is the architecturally central demonstration of his syncretic program. It is not merely rhetorical or decorative; it claims a real structural parallel between two independent traditions of hierarchical divine emanation. Farmer observes that "Pico's identification of the *sefirot* with the henads was not thus simply the wild fantasy of a Renaissance syncretist" — the parallel has genuine historical and structural grounds even if Pico's interpretation of those grounds differs from modern scholarship's (Farmer 1998, 4169). Wirszubski confirms the import of this move and traces its specific textual genealogy through the Kabbalistic materials that Pico received from his tutor Flavius Mithridates (Wirszubski 1989, passim).

Allen's reconstruction of the Florentine Platonic context helps locate Proclus within the disputational texture of the *Commento* as well. In *Commento* 1.3, Pico explicitly cites Proclus, Hermias, and Syrianus (Proclus's teacher) alongside the Pseudo-Dionysius in support of his account of the angelic hypostases mediating between the One and Soul (Allen 2017, 6084). The identification of Procline henadic distinctions with the Christian theological category of "angels" — and the claim that these distinctions postdate Plato but can be read back into the Platonic text "as did the later Neoplatonists as a matter of principle" — is characteristic of Pico's exegetical method. He is not merely reporting Proclus; he is using Proclus's framework to argue that the ancient theological traditions, including the Christian one, converge on a common account of the intermediate orders between God and the material world.

Within the *Commento*, however, Pico's relationship to Proclus is already complex. He credits Proclus while simultaneously using a Plotinian framework for his own account of the three Venuses and criticizing Ficino for not following Plotinus closely enough. As Howlett notes, "both Pico and Ficino use Proclus," but the purposes differ: Ficino uses Proclus's *Platonic Theology* when discussing the attributes of God in his own *Theologia Platonica*, while Pico "favors Proclus and a Plotinian 'mysticism' above all other forms of Platonism" in a way that is sometimes difficult to disentangle (Howlett 2020, 3290–3332). The tension between using Proclus for structural and syncretic purposes and using Plotinus as the touchstone of mystical depth characterizes much of Pico's philosophical writing before *De ente et uno*.

It is in *De ente et uno* that the Proclus-Ficino-Pico relationship crystallizes most sharply. Pico's argument in this treatise is that Being (*ens*) and the One (*unum*) are convertible — that whatever is, is one, and whatever is one, is. This is the scholastic doctrine of the *transcendentalia*, which Pico presents as consistent with the true reading of Plato and Aristotle and as demonstrably correct against the Plotinian-Ficinian position that the One transcends Being. The irony has been noted by all the commentators: Proclus himself is in many respects the most rigorous advocate of the One's transcendence of Being, yet Pico appeals to a specifically Procline theorem — the convertibility of the good and the one in the *Elements of Theology*, propositions 12–13 — in support of his convertibility thesis, while rejecting the Plotinian position as a misreading of Plato. Allen summarizes the situation: in Ficino's reading, the Plotinian position "that the One is above Being" is confirmed by Proclus and constitutes Platonic orthodoxy; Pico in *De ente et uno* "argues he is going back to the real Plato rather than seeing him through the faulty lens of Proclus" — despite, as Howlett immediately adds, his "copious use of Proclus" (Howlett 2020, 3375–3377). Pico deploys a selective Proclus against a Ficinian Proclus. The selectivity is not dishonest but rather diagnostic of Pico's characteristic philosophical method: the same source is used in different ways depending on whether the goal is syncretic demonstration, mystical commentary, or scholastic argument.

Ficino's response to *De ente et uno* was the commentary on the *Parmenides*, composed between 1492 and 1494 and published in 1496, two years after Pico's death. According to Howlett, the commentary "follows a sequence that specifically takes on Pico's arguments section by section to refute him" (Howlett 2020, 3136–3137). This makes Ficino's *Parmenides* commentary a primary document for understanding the dispute and for reconstructing what was at stake philosophically. Ficino holds that the *Parmenides* second hypothesis — where Plato argues through Parmenides that the One, if it exists, must have all properties — does not reduce to the claim that the One and Being are identical but rather that the derivation of Being from the One must be understood hierarchically, with the One's own mode of "being" being something entirely different from the being of derived things. Pico, he implies, has not attended carefully enough to these distinctions and has, by reducing the question to a scholastic formula about convertibility, obscured rather than clarified the original Platonic vision.

The significance of this dispute for PicoDB is not limited to its intrinsic philosophical interest, substantial as that is. It also provides the most precise dating evidence for a shift in Pico's philosophical priorities. The *900 Conclusions* (1486) use Proclus for syncretic demonstration and correlate his henadic orders with Kabbalistic *sefirot*. The *Commento* (1486) uses Proclus within a Plotinian framework for mythographic commentary. *De ente et uno* (1491) uses Proclus selectively to argue a scholastic thesis against Plotinian henology. The trajectory moves from the synthetic-encyclopedic toward the analytically precise, from the universal correlation of all traditions toward the focused defense of a specific philosophical position. Proclus is present throughout, but the mode of his presence changes substantially as Pico's intellectual priorities evolve.

The question of what Pico could actually read in Proclus requires a note. The *Elements of Theology*, available in medieval Latin as the *Liber de causis* derivative and in other partial versions, was more accessible than the *Platonic Theology*, which required Greek. Farmer's testimony that Pico was the first Latin philosopher since antiquity to read the *Platonic Theology* "in anything approximating its original exegetical spirit" implies a level of Greek philological competence that is well attested in Pico's biography (Farmer 1998, 1527). Pearl Kibre's work on Pico's library and Kristeller's census of manuscripts confirm that Pico possessed or had access to Greek manuscripts of Proclus (Kristeller 1976). The Florentine context provided, via Ficino's circle, a network of Hellenists who could assist with difficult passages. What Pico reads, he reads closely; the theses *secundum Proclum* are not summaries but technical propositions drawn from the *Platonic Theology*'s dense exegetical apparatus, sometimes citing specific passages in ways that reward comparison with the Greek text.

The historiographical caution appropriate here is that the sheer volume of Pico's citations of Proclus can obscure the question of what he actually understands and appropriates versus what he deploys strategically. Farmer is careful to note that some of Pico's Procline theses "are special" in the sense that they are drawn from Proclus's most technical exegetical discussions (Farmer 1998, 10260) and resist easy paraphrase. Not every thesis *secundum Proclum* indicates deep engagement with its source; some function as syncretic markers, positioning Proclus within a correlational schema whose primary interest is the demonstration of inter-traditional agreement. But many of them — particularly those bearing on the henads and *sefirot*, on the structure of the divine orders, and on the metaphysical grammar of procession and return — do reflect serious philological and philosophical work, and it is a disservice to Pico's intelligence to assimilate them to mere name-dropping.
