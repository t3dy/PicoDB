The One (τὸ ἕν, *to Hen*; Latin *unum*) is the supreme metaphysical principle of Neoplatonism — the absolutely first, simple, and ineffable source from which all things proceed. No concept in Pico's intellectual world carries more philosophical weight or generates more controversy, because the question of what the One is, and how it relates to Being, sits at the center of the *De ente et uno* (1492), which is in turn the most precisely argued philosophical text Pico ever wrote and the site of his sharpest disagreement with Ficino. To read *De ente et uno* is to read a systematic challenge to one of the foundational commitments of Renaissance Neoplatonism; to understand that challenge, one must first understand the Plotinian doctrine it targets.

In Plotinus's *Enneads*, the One (treated especially in *Enneads* I.7, V.1–3, and VI.9) is the absolutely first principle, prior to Being, prior to Mind (*Nous*), prior to Soul. It is the source from which everything proceeds but which itself comes from nothing and is nothing that can be named or predicated. The One is beyond Being (*epekeina tēs ousias*), beyond Mind (*epekeina nou*), and even beyond the predicate "one" in any determinate sense: to call it "the One" is already to say too much, since any name would imply a determination that the One, as absolute simplicity, cannot have. The classic Plotinian argument runs as follows: if we say that the One is, we would be ascribing being to it, making it an instance of a category (being) that would then be prior to it; but nothing can be prior to the first principle; therefore the One is not being but is the source of being. Similarly, if we say the One thinks, it would require a knower and an object of knowledge, introducing a duality incompatible with absolute unity. The One therefore does not think and is not a mind; it simply is what it is, in a mode of superlative simplicity that overflows into Mind as its first emanation without any act of will or deliberation.

This Plotinian doctrine was canonized for the Renaissance principally through Ficino's monumental *Theologia Platonica* (1474), his commentaries on the *Parmenides* and *Enneads*, and his prefaces to the Platonic dialogues. Ficino reads the first hypothesis of Plato's *Parmenides* — where Parmenides argues that if the One exists it would be without parts, without location, without being — as a systematic theology of the supreme principle: the One is God in his absolute transcendence, beyond all that can be said or thought, approachable only by a negative theology that strips away every predication. For Ficino, the Neoplatonic One corresponds to the Christian God in his utter ineffability, and the Platonic ascent through the levels of being — from sensory particulars up through soul to Mind and finally toward the One — is the philosopher's mystical itinerary toward union with a God who exceeds every concept. Ficino thus preserves Plotinus's radical disjunction between the One and Being as a central pillar of his entire philosophical theology.

Pico's *De ente et uno* confronts this interpretation directly and without equivocation. Written in 1492 as a letter to Poliziano and addressed ultimately to the debate over the relation of Plato and Aristotle, the treatise argues that Being and the One are convertible transcendentals — that they are the same reality described in different registers, each implying the other at every ontological level. The argument draws on Aristotle's *Metaphysics*, where *Metaphysics* Gamma (1003b23) establishes that "being" and "one" are said in as many senses as each other, are coextensive in their range, and are reciprocally convertible: whatever is, is one; whatever is one, is. Aristotle had demonstrated, against Parmenides, that the concept of being is not univocal but analogical, applying in different modes to substances and accidents, to act and potency; and that within each mode, being and unity are inseparable. The first and most perfectly actual being — the Unmoved Mover — is therefore also the most perfectly one, and its unity and its being are not two distinct things but a single reality grasped under two aspects.

As the English translation of *De ente et uno* (in Hamm's 1957 Marquette edition) shows, Pico begins his own treatment by surveying the Neoplatonic position that "the followers of the Academy" — by which he means chiefly Plotinus and Proclus — held the One to be anterior to Being, understanding it as "a concept more simple and universal" that could define even God as the One rather than as Being (*De ente et uno*, Ch. I). Against this, Pico argues that Plato himself never clearly states the superiority of the One over Being: the *Parmenides*, read carefully, is a dialectical exercise rather than a dogmatic treatise, and its first hypothesis — "if the One is, it would be nothing" — is a reductio of an improperly conceived unity, not an affirmation that the divine principle transcends Being (*De ente et uno*, Ch. II). As for the *Sophist*, Plato there rather indicates the equivalence of Being and Unity than their separation (*De ente et uno*, Ch. II).

Pico then marshals a sophisticated philosophical argument through the concrete/abstract distinction. "Being" (*ens*) is a concrete term: it names a thing as a participant in being (*esse*). Just as "the luminous" participates in light without being light itself, so every being participates in Being without being Being itself. Now, God alone is not a being in this participatory sense but is Being itself (*esse ipsum*), the source in which all participated beings have their existence. In this second sense, we may indeed say that God surpasses being — not because he is below being or other than being, but because he *is* Being in its pure abstract form, just as whiteness surpasses "white" things not by lacking whiteness but by being whiteness itself (*De ente et uno*, Ch. IV). The apparent tension between calling God "above being" and calling God "Being itself" dissolves once we recognize that the concrete/abstract distinction is doing the philosophical work: the One surpasses particular, participated being while being identical with Being-in-itself. Under this analysis, the One and Being are convertible: God is the One because he is uniquely, eminently, the source of the unity of all things; and God is Being because he is the source of the being of all things.

This argument carries a deeply Thomistic signature, as the introduction to Hamm's translation and subsequent scholarship both acknowledge. Aquinas, in the *Commentary on the Sentences* (I, dist. xix, q. 4), had already argued that the transcendentals — being, one, true, good — are convertible, and that God as *esse ipsum subsistens* exceeds created being not by lying outside the range of being but by being Being eminently, in the mode that a form exceeds its participants. Pico is in effect bringing the Thomistic doctrine of the transcendentals to bear against the Plotinian-Ficinian thesis of the One-beyond-Being. What had looked to Ficino like a recovery of the deepest mystery of Platonic theology looked to Pico like a philosophical confusion imported by Proclus and the later Neoplatonists — one that Proclus himself had obscured by basing the priority of the One on the "primacy of simplicity and indivisibility," thereby making the One "ineffable and unknowable" and turning theology "into a kind of magic" (*De ente et uno*, Ch. I, in Hamm's paraphrase). Proclus, not Plato, is the real target; and Aristotle, correctly understood, turns out to be an ally.

Michael J. B. Allen, whose detailed analysis of the Ficino-Pico exchange on *De ente et uno* in *Studies in the Platonism of Marsilio Ficino and Giovanni Pico* (2017) remains the most illuminating scholarly treatment in English, traces the tension back to Ficino's *Theologia Platonica* and forward into the objections and responses that followed *De ente et uno*'s circulation. Allen shows that Ficino's response was sharp — he saw Pico as Aristotelianizing Plato and as sacrificing the transcendence of the divine in the interest of a scholastic concord that Ficino found philosophically and spiritually impoverishing (Allen 2017, 84, drawing on the broader arc of the volume's essays). For Ficino, preserving the ineffability of the One above Being was not a technical point but a commitment to the irreducibly mystical character of the ascent: the One-beyond-Being is what makes the summit of philosophical theology an experience of darkness and silence rather than of illuminated comprehension.

Pico's *De ente et uno* responds to this mystical claim by retaining — but repositioning — the apophatic moment. At the end of the treatise (Ch. V), Pico offers his famous fourfold ascent toward "the cloud which God inhabits," drawn from Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite's *Mystical Theology*. In the first two steps, one strips away imperfection and particularity from every predication of God, recognizing that the divine life and wisdom surpass their creaturely analogues. In the third step, one recognizes that even the transcendentals — being, one, true, good — predicate God only in a concrete, participated mode, not in the abstract mode of what he really is; so one can say God is above being, above the one, above the good. In the fourth step, one enters what Dionysius calls the "light of ignorance," acknowledging that even this purified language falls infinitely short of the divine reality. Pico thus retains apophatic theology as the final word on the divine nature, but he denies that it requires the Plotinian thesis that the One is hypostatically distinct from Being and superior to it as a principle. The apophatic moment comes at the end of a conceptual ascent through Being; it does not bypass Being at the outset.

The Kabbalistic dimension of the question is one of the most genuinely unresolved tensions in Pico's syncretic project. The Kabbalistic tradition posited above the ten sefirot an utterly infinite and undifferentiated divine ground called *Ein Sof* (אין סוף, "the Infinite," or literally "without end") — an absolute that has no attributes, no name, no relation to the created order, and that exceeds even the first sefirah *Keter* (Crown) as a higher silence exceeds its first utterance. This *Ein Sof* structure maps structurally onto the Plotinian One-beyond-Being: it is the abyss of divinity that underlies but transcends the entire emanative hierarchy of the sefirot. Chaim Wirszubski's *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989) demonstrates that Pico's access to these doctrines came filtered through the Latin translations of Flavius Mithridates, translations that often imposed Neoplatonic conceptual frameworks onto Kabbalistic materials and thereby strengthened the apparent homology between *Ein Sof* and the Plotinian One (Wirszubski 1989, 105–6, 299–300). The emanation of the second sefirah *Chokhmah* (Wisdom) from the first — or even from the *Ein Sof* above *Keter* — could be read as analogous to the procession of Nous from the One.

The philosophical difficulty for Pico is that his *De ente et uno* pulls against this structural parallel: if Being and the One are convertible, then God as One is not the *Ein Sof* above all determination but is identical with Being and knowable under the aspect of Being. This would make the Kabbalistic *Ein Sof* — if it is genuinely a principle beyond the sefirot and beyond Being — more Plotinian than Pico's theology can accommodate. Pico does not resolve this tension in any surviving text. The *900 Conclusions* place Kabbalistic and Platonic theses in proximity without always fully harmonizing them, and the *De ente et uno* was written without systematic attention to the Kabbalistic materials. Amos Edelheit, in *Ficino, Pico and Savonarola: The Evolution of Humanist Theology* (2008), notes the broader philosophical difficulty of Pico's syncretism at precisely this juncture: the attempt to hold together Plotinian transcendence, Thomistic convertibility of transcendentals, and Kabbalistic *Ein Sof* generates a productive but unresolved tension that would have required a much longer life to work through systematically.

What is clear is that the *De ente et uno* represents Pico's most mature and technically precise metaphysical statement, and that it defines a genuinely distinctive position in Renaissance philosophy. Pico is neither simply Ficino's student nor simply an Aristotelian in Platonist dress: he reads Plato through Proclus and Aquinas simultaneously, finding in the scholastic doctrine of the transcendentals a key that unlocks what he takes to be the genuine Platonic-Aristotelian concord beneath the Neoplatonic overlay. The consequence for the One is that it loses its absolute ineffability as a metaphysical principle separate from Being, while God's transcendence is preserved — and even intensified — through the apophatic ascent that concludes the treatise. The One is Being; God is both; and the darkness of divine incomprehensibility lies not below Being but above it, at the summit of the highest predication.
