# Syncretism

The word "syncretism" did not originate as a description of philosophical method. Its earliest documented use is Plutarch's remark in the *Moralia* that the Cretans, for all their quarreling among themselves, would unite against a common enemy — a political practice of temporary alliance that Plutarch called *synkrētismos*, from *Krēs*, a Cretan. The term passed into Renaissance Latin, where Erasmus deployed it in his correspondence to commend the virtue of scholars setting aside their differences to stand together against barbarism. In neither usage does syncretism mean the philosophical blending of doctrines; it means the tactical cohesion of parties that normally disagree. It is a mark of how thoroughly the term has since changed its meaning that Stephen Farmer can title his critical edition of Pico's 900 theses *Syncretism in the West* and be immediately understood as describing something entirely different: the systematic effort to show that all philosophical and theological traditions converge on the same truths at their deepest level.

Farmer's title announces his central interpretive claim. Pico's project in the *Conclusiones nongentae* of 1486 is not merely pluralistic or tolerant; it is not the cheerful eclecticism of a scholar who has read widely and found good things everywhere. It is a coordinated attempt to demonstrate structural identity among traditions — Platonic and Aristotelian metaphysics, scholastic theology, Neoplatonic emanationism, Arabic philosophy, Orphic poetry, Chaldean theosophy, and Hebrew Kabbalah — by showing that their apparently contradictory formulations all express the same hierarchically organized truth when properly decoded. Syncretism in this sense is not latitude but system. To understand what Pico means by it, one must understand both the machinery he deploys and the costs that machinery exacts.

The distinction between syncretism and eclecticism is fundamental, and Farmer is precise about it. Eclecticism selects the best from multiple sources: the eclectic reader judges Plato superior to Aristotle on the soul, finds Aristotle's logic more rigorous, borrows the Stoic concept of natural law, and assembles a working philosophy from what survives the comparison. Selection requires rejection; the eclectic philosopher picks winners. Syncretism, as Farmer describes Pico's version, operates on an entirely different premise. The syncretic thinker does not choose among traditions because the traditions do not ultimately disagree. Apparent contradictions between, say, the Platonic doctrine of participation and the Aristotelian doctrine of formal causality, or between the Kabbalistic sefirot and the Neoplatonic henads, are not conflicts to be adjudicated but differences of idiom to be resolved by hierarchical distinction. Each tradition speaks the truth, but speaks it at a particular level of a cosmological hierarchy and in a vocabulary appropriate to that level. The contradictions dissolve once the analyst assigns each thesis to its proper place in the system. This is what Farmer means when he identifies the governing principle of the *Conclusiones* as *omnia sunt in omnibus modo suo* — all things are in all things, but each in its own mode. The phrase, which has Neoplatonic ancestry in Anaxagoras and Proclus, is the correlative key to Pico's entire undertaking.

The practical architecture of the *Conclusiones* makes the syncretic method visible in its structural form. The first or historical section of the nine hundred theses distributes 402 propositions across twenty-eight source traditions, which Pico organizes in deliberate reverse chronological order — beginning with the Latin scholastics of the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries and working backward through the Arabic philosophers, the Greek Neoplatonists, the ancient Chaldeans, the Egyptians, and finally the Hebrew Kabbalist sages, whom he places last because they stand nearest in time to the primordial revelation from which all wisdom flows. The movement of the text thus mirrors the movement of wisdom itself, flowing forward through time from its divine source into progressive corruption and fragmentation, while the *Conclusiones* move backward toward that source. Each of the twenty-eight sections presents theses *secundum* the relevant authority — according to Thomas Aquinas, according to Averroes, according to Proclus, according to Zoroaster — as if Pico were faithfully reporting what each tradition teaches. The second or topical section then presents, *secundum opinionem propriam*, Pico's own reconciliative conclusions, which will show at the projected Roman debate how the positions of the first section cohere when arranged hierarchically.

The collation logic of the text — the *occulta concatenatio*, the hidden chain of connections, that Pico mentions in the *Apology* — is what Farmer's edition is designed to recover. Theses from disparate source clusters are implicitly cross-referenced so that the attentive reader can begin to see how Proclus's henads, understood at one level of the hierarchy, correspond to the Kabbalistic sefirot, understood at another; how Platonic participation and Aristotelian formal causality name the same structural relation at different levels of analysis; how Orphic mythological symbols encode Neoplatonic truths that encode Kabbalistic truths that encode, ultimately, the Christian doctrines of the Trinity and Incarnation. Farmer's indispensable methodological contribution is to demonstrate that this collation cannot be performed thesis by thesis, in isolation, but requires simultaneous attention to the organizational logic of the whole — the numerological symbolism of section lengths, the pairing of adversarial sources, the placement of pivotal theses at cosmologically significant positions in the numbered sequence.

The risk in this kind of syncretism is one that Farmer identifies candidly and that any honest engagement with Pico's project must confront. When a thinker resolves apparent contradictions by assigning conflicting authorities to different levels of a prior hierarchical schema, the sources are not being allowed to speak on their own terms. They are being absorbed into a framework that was constructed independently of them and that determines in advance what they can mean. Pico's "harmonization" of Plato and Aristotle, for instance, is not the product of a careful reading of both philosophers that discovers unsuspected agreement; it is the product of a metaphysical commitment — announced as the first thesis of the topical section — that there must be such agreement, followed by a reinterpretation of whatever Plato and Aristotle say to fit that commitment. The same is true of the Kabbalah, which Wirszubski's patient philological reconstruction shows was available to Pico primarily through translations by Flavius Mithridates that had been systematically Christianized before Pico saw them. Pico's "discovery" that Kabbalah confirms the Trinity was in significant part a discovery of what Mithridates had already put there. Farmer's broader point is that Pico's syncretic method systematically risks distortion of this kind because it treats each source tradition not as a set of positions to be evaluated but as raw material to be recoded within a correlative schema that precedes and governs the reading.

It is at this point that the concept of syncretism connects indissolubly with the related concept of the prisca theologia, the ancient theology. Pico's harmonization of diverse traditions is not simply a methodological preference for agreement over conflict; it rests on a positive historical claim about why the traditions agree. If Orpheus, Zoroaster, Pythagoras, Plato, and Moses all teach the same truths — truths that a trained reader can also find in the Kabbalistic sages, the Chaldean Oracles, and the Christian scholastics — the explanation is genealogical: all these traditions derive from a primordial revelation, a wisdom given by God to humanity at the beginning that has been transmitted through various channels, imperfectly preserved, encoded in different symbolic vocabularies, but never entirely lost. The traditions agree because they are, at their source, one tradition. Syncretism as a method depends on this genealogical assumption because, without it, the convergences Pico claims to find among traditions would be philosophically puzzling and would demand independent justification thesis by thesis. The prisca theologia provides the explanation in advance: apparent coincidences among traditions are not coincidences but traces of a common origin.

Modern scholars have pressed the question of what kind of intellectual achievement Pico's syncretism represents. One line of interpretation, represented by scholars in the tradition of Eugenio Garin, treats Pico's project as a genuine philosophical pluralism — an early and admirable attempt to honor the plurality of intellectual traditions and to find common ground across religious and cultural difference. On this reading, syncretism is a virtue: it reflects the humanist conviction that truth is too large for any one tradition to contain and that wisdom requires breadth of learning and generosity toward alternative formulations. Amos Edelheit's work on Pico's scholastic context urges that the philosophical ambitions of the *Conclusiones* be taken seriously on their own terms, reading the reconciliation of Plato and Aristotle as a genuine contribution to metaphysical debate rather than merely a rhetorical gesture.

Farmer's own position is considerably more skeptical, and it has shaped the dominant methodological orientation of recent scholarship. For Farmer, Pico's syncretism is best understood not as philosophical pluralism but as the systematic application of a prior correlative schema to a diverse collection of sources, producing apparent harmony through the suppression of genuine difference. The harmonization is real in the sense that Pico sincerely believed in it, but it is achieved by methods that cannot survive rigorous scrutiny: the sources are selectively quoted, imperfectly understood (often through doctored translations), and absorbed into a framework they did not generate. The intellectual ambition of the project is not in question; what is in question is whether the project delivers what it promises, namely a demonstration that the traditions genuinely agree rather than a demonstration that they can be made to appear to agree by an ingenious enough reader.

This debate about the nature and value of Pico's syncretism is unlikely to be resolved because it touches on a genuine tension within the project itself. Pico is simultaneously a serious philosopher who knows his sources carefully — his mastery of scholastic distinctions, Kabbalistic symbolism, and Neoplatonic metaphysics is evident in the theses themselves — and a systematizer whose commitment to harmony sometimes outpaces the evidence his sources provide. Whether one reads the *Conclusiones* primarily as philosophical argument or primarily as rhetorical performance determines how one evaluates the syncretism they enact. What Farmer's edition has made permanently clear is that neither reading is adequate without the other: the rhetoric is built on a genuine philosophical scaffold, and the philosophical scaffold is organized by a rhetorical commitment to concordance that shapes every structural decision in the text.
