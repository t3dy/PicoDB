Textual transmission is at once the most elementary and the most treacherous problem in Pico scholarship. Elementary, because no text can be read without first knowing what text one actually has; treacherous, because the gap between what Pico wrote and what readers have received is, in several crucial cases, large enough to distort the picture of his philosophy entirely. The question of transmission divides naturally into two dimensions: the history of Pico's own writings as they passed from manuscript to print, from autograph to editor, from death to posthumous publication; and the history of the sources Pico himself received, which came to him already damaged, interpolated, or misattributed before he wrote a word.

The most striking case in the first dimension is the *Oration*, the text most frequently read as the "manifesto of the Renaissance" and quoted as evidence for Pico's philosophy of human dignity and freedom. The *Oration* was never published during Pico's lifetime. The disputation it was written to introduce — the great Roman debate of January 1487, before the college of cardinals with the pope as supreme judge — was prohibited by Innocent VIII in February 1487 after a papal commission found thirteen of the nine hundred theses heretical and eleven suspect. The text Pico composed as an opening address for that debate therefore remained in manuscript. It was first printed in 1496, two years after Pico's death, in an edition that appeared under the auspices of his nephew Gianfrancesco Pico della Mirandola. Farmer (1998, 2) has established the striking fact that the title *Oratio de hominis dignitate* — the *Oration on the Dignity of Man* — by which the text is universally known, was not Pico's own. It first appeared in a corrupt German reprint of the Opera about ten years after Pico's death. Pico's own title, to the extent he had one, was something closer to *Oratio ad laudes philosophiae* — an oration in praise of philosophy, not a manifesto of human dignity. The difference is philosophically consequential: an oration designed to open a scholastic disputation on every knowable thing is a very different document from a humanist proclamation of man's special cosmic status.

Farmer has further drawn attention to a textual discrepancy between an early draft of the *Oration* and the version published by Gianfrancesco. In the earlier version, Pico explicitly names Flavius Mithridates as "an expert teacher of these languages" — Hebrew, Chaldean, and Arabic — in the context of explaining his own polyglot program of philosophical synthesis. This reference to Mithridates, a convicted criminal who had fled the papal court, was suppressed in the posthumously published version (Farmer 1998, 34). This is not an isolated case of discreet editing but, Farmer argues, a pattern of systematic intervention by Gianfrancesco and his Savonarolan associates. The nephew obtained control of Pico's papers after the latter's sudden death in November 1494, and the textual evidence — drawn from the large body of posthumous publications — suggests what Farmer calls "extensive evidence of massive literary fraud": passages tampered with, texts distorted, and what he believes may be materials from Pico's lost *Concord of Plato and Aristotle* absorbed and plagiarized into the nephew's own writings (Farmer 1998, 151–67). These claims remain debated, but they issue in a general methodological caution: the posthumously published works of Pico cannot simply be treated as transparent records of Pico's own views without attention to the editorial layer through which they were transmitted.

The *900 Conclusions* occupy an unusual position in this context, for they are the one major text Pico himself published in his lifetime. They were printed by Eucharius Silber in Rome on 7 December 1486, in preparation for the disputation Pico had invited all scholars of Europe to attend, promising to pay their travel expenses. The *editio princeps* is therefore as close to Pico's own intentions as any text in his corpus, though even here Farmer notes that the erratic punctuation of the original creates its own interpretive barriers, and that "massive corruption in all accessible versions" of the text — meaning the sixteenth-century Opera editions on which scholars have long relied — has made "meaningful study of the theses next to impossible" until his own edition offered a reliable Latin text for the first time (Farmer 1998, xi–xii). The *900 Conclusions* also lack a formal title in the original: Pico referred to them as his "theses" or "conclusions" (*conclusiones*), and the title by which the collection is conventionally known is again a later imposition.

The *Commento sopra una canzone d'amore* presents a different case, and one that is linked to the related problem of editorial transmission through collaboration. Pico wrote the *Commento* in 1486 as an Italian vernacular philosophical commentary on a love poem by his friend Girolamo Benivieni, but it was not published in his lifetime. The first printed edition appeared in 1519, twenty-five years after Pico's death, as part of the Venetian *Opera omnia* edited by Gianfrancesco. The edition of the *Commento* is complicated by the fact that it included Benivieni's canzone, and that Benivieni himself survived Pico by more than four decades. Wirszubski's philological work and the complementary studies of Michael Allen have established that the published text of the *Commento* contains interventions — it is not always easy to separate Pico's original prose from editorial changes introduced by Benivieni and Gianfrancesco — that bear on questions of doctrinal emphasis and the relationship between the *Commento*'s Neoplatonic love theory and Ficino's rival positions. Farmer notes that Pico's rude remarks about a "great Platonist" who misunderstood Plotinus — widely understood as aimed at Ficino — appear at the beginning of the *Commento*, in a passage whose textual status matters greatly for understanding the nature of the Ficino–Pico relationship in 1486 (Farmer 1998, 35 n. 35).

The *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem*, Pico's massive late polemic against judicial astrology, poses the most acute problem of posthumous editorial tampering. Pico died in November 1494 with the work unfinished; Gianfrancesco edited and published it in 1496. Farmer's argument is that the *Disputationes* as published represents a substantially distorted version of what Pico was writing, shaped by the Savonarolan theological agenda of the nephew and his associates. The case of "Adeland the Arab" is instructive here: Pico had attributed a series of theses in the *900 Conclusions* to an "Adeland" who he believed was Plotinus's fellow student in Egypt. Farmer establishes that this figure was in fact Adelard of Bath, the twelfth-century English philosopher, whose works had reached Pico in a loose Hebrew adaptation that gave them an exotic Near Eastern character — and that the *Disputationes*, written years later, contains a passage on the tides attributed to "Adeland out of the opinions of the Saracens" that is clearly drawn this time from the Latin text of Adelard's *Quaestiones naturales*. By the time the *Disputationes* was written, Pico had apparently discovered his earlier error — but whether the published version of that correction accurately reflects Pico's own rethinking, or has been further modified by Gianfrancesco, is precisely the kind of question the transmission history opens and cannot close.

The second dimension of textual transmission concerns the sources Pico himself received. The most heavily studied case is the Kabbalistic library assembled for Pico by Flavius Mithridates, a Sicilian Jewish convert to Christianity who served as Pico's principal teacher of Hebrew, Aramaic, Arabic, and the Kabbalistic tradition. Between roughly May and November 1486 — the six months before the *900 Conclusions* went to press — Mithridates produced what Wirszubski describes as "a remarkable feat of translation": a small Kabbalistic library in Latin, comprising roughly forty books of varying size and character from the Hebrew and Aramaic traditions (Wirszubski 1989, 11). The extant autograph manuscripts of these translations are preserved in the Vatican Library (Codices Vaticani Ebraici 189–191 and Codex Chigi A.VI.190), and in their present state of preservation they amount to nearly 3,500 folio pages. Wirszubski estimates that even this represents only a portion of the original corpus, which may have run to some 5,500 folio pages — roughly fifteen times the length of the *Aeneid* (Wirszubski 1989, 11).

This massive body of Latin translation was Pico's principal access to Kabbalah. As Wirszubski documents in granular detail across the entirety of his 1989 monograph, the translations are not neutral conveyances of the Hebrew and Aramaic originals. Mithridates consistently introduced interpolations — sometimes personal remarks addressed directly to Pico, sometimes doctrinal glosses — that systematically Christianized the Kabbalistic material he was rendering. He introduced christological readings of passages that bore no such meaning in the originals, added commentary that identified Kabbalistic terms with Christian theological concepts, and in some cases appears to have invented or fabricated textual content to match Pico's expectations and his own self-interest. Wirszubski demonstrates that Mithridates was "something of a con man" whose predilection for "twisting texts to match Pico's fancies" was recognized even in his own lifetime (Farmer 1998, 14). The philological implication is fundamental: when Pico argues in the *Apology* that "seventy-two" Kabbalistic theses confirm the divinity of Christ, or when the *Heptaplus* deploys Kabbalistic material to confirm a specifically Christian reading of Genesis, those arguments rest on a textual foundation already reshaped by Mithridates. The "discoveries" Pico claimed to have made in the Kabbalistic texts — the apparent confirmation of Christian doctrine by the most ancient Hebrew wisdom — were in significant measure artifacts of the transmission process itself.

A related problem governs Pico's encounter with the texts he treated as the most ancient records of pagan wisdom: the *Chaldean Oracles*, the Orphic Hymns, and the *Corpus Hermeticum*. These texts reached the Latin West through Byzantine manuscript traditions and through Ficino's translations. The *Corpus Hermeticum* was translated by Ficino in 1463 at Cosimo de' Medici's urgent request; the Platonic corpus was completed in Latin by 1484; the *Enneads* of Plotinus appeared in Latin translation in 1492, the same year as Pico's *De ente et uno*. As Allen's work on Ficino as translator establishes, Ficino's renderings were monuments of energy and philological commitment that shaped how the entire Neoplatonic tradition was understood in Renaissance Europe (Allen 2017, 327–54). But they also carried forward the ancient misattributions: the *Corpus Hermeticum* was believed to be the work of Hermes Trismegistus, an Egyptian sage contemporary with Moses, when in fact it was a product of the second and third centuries CE; the Orphic Hymns were taken as the genuine compositions of the mythical Thracian bard; the *Chaldean Oracles* were attributed to Zoroaster. Isaac Casaubon's philological demolition of the Hermetic writings would not come until 1614. In the meantime, Pico read these late antique texts as ancient wisdom and built his *prisca theologia* — his account of a universal philosophical tradition running from Zoroaster through Pythagoras to Plato — on a transmission history that was itself fictitious.

In the case of the *Chaldean Oracles*, the problem was compounded by Mithridates. Pico wrote to Ficino boasting that he possessed the original "Chaldean" version of the Oracles, which supplied "complete and absolute" (*integra et absoluta*) what was "full of faults and mutilated" (*mendosa et mutila*) in the Greek version known to scholars. Farmer's commentary on this passage raises the possibility that the "Chaldean" texts Pico claimed to possess were forgeries by Mithridates (Farmer 1998, 13–14 n. 38). If so, then what Pico believed were the purest and most ancient testimonies to his universal wisdom were in fact the most recent, most manipulated, and most fraudulent texts in his library.

This double transmission problem — the unreliable posthumous editing of Pico's own texts and the distorted transmission of his sources — does not make Pico unreadable, but it fundamentally shapes what responsible reading requires. Scholarship after Wirszubski (1989) and Farmer (1998) can no longer treat the *Opera omnia* of 1557 as a reliable record of what Pico thought, nor treat the Kabbalistic arguments in Pico's works as straightforward engagements with the Hebrew mystical tradition. The text one reads, and the texts Pico read, are both already interpretations — already transformed by the passage through translators, editors, and scribes whose purposes were not always the same as Pico's own.
