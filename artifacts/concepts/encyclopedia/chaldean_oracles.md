The *Chaldean Oracles* (χαλδαϊκὰ λόγια, *Logia Chaldaica*) occupy a peculiar and revealing position in Pico della Mirandola's intellectual universe. For Pico and his contemporaries, they were among the oldest and most authoritative texts in human possession — an oracular revelation from the Chaldean tradition that predated Greek philosophy and expressed the primordial wisdom that philosophy had subsequently attempted to recover through reason. In the framework of the *prisca theologia* (ancient theology) that Pico shared with Ficino and inherited ultimately from Gemistus Plethon, the *Oracles* represented a stratum of wisdom continuous with the revelations of Zoroaster, the Egyptian theology of Hermes Trismegistus, the Orphic hymns, and the Pythagorean numbers — a sequence of sages and traditions all testifying, in different idioms, to the same divine truth. The historical actuality is different: the *Oracles* are a late antique composition, probably of the second century CE, and their claim to Chaldean antiquity is a pious fiction that the Renaissance inherited from its late antique Neoplatonic sources. But this gap between belief and reality is itself historically significant, because it shaped the entire structure of Renaissance Platonic theology — and because its eventual exposure in the seventeenth century had consequences for the whole edifice of *prisca theologia* on which Pico's syncretic program rested.

What the *Oracles* actually are is a collection of hexameter verses attributed in late antiquity to the divine Julian (*ho theourges*, "the theurgist") or to two Juliani, father and son, who reportedly lived under the emperor Marcus Aurelius in the second century CE. The father was said to be a Chaldean magus who received oracular revelations; the son, Julian the Theurgist, was credited with writing them down and with developing from them a system of ritual practice. The verses themselves — about two hundred survive in quotation, from an original collection that may have been much larger — describe a theological cosmos organized around a supreme Father (*Pater*), an intermediate principle often identified with Hecate or the World Soul (*Hekate*, functioning as the *psyche kosmou*), and a Demiurge or second intellect who creates and governs the visible world. The metaphysical medium pervading this cosmos is fire (*pyr*, *pyros*): not ordinary material fire but a divine, intellectual fire that the *Oracles* call the "paternal fire" or "empyrean fire," which emanates from the Father and sustains all levels of reality in its luminous heat. The soul is a "fiery spark" (*spinthêr pyrinos*) that has descended from the empyrean realm into matter and must ascend back to its source through a combination of intellectual purification and theurgical ritual.

The theurgical dimension of the *Oracles* is what most distinguishes them from purely philosophical texts and what gave them their particular appeal in late antique Neoplatonism. The *Oracles* not only describe the structure of the cosmos but prescribe a set of ritual practices — the use of material objects, sounds, gestures, divine names, and fire-based rituals — through which the soul can attract the attention of the higher powers and prepare itself for the ascent. This combination of philosophical theology and ritual prescription is what Iamblichus called *theurgia* (divine action) — the practice of performing rituals that align the practitioner with the divine order in a way that surpasses purely intellectual contemplation. In Iamblichus's *De Mysteriis* ("On the Mysteries"), theurgy is superior to philosophy because it works not just on the intellect but on the whole soul, including those lower parts that philosophy cannot easily reach; the ritual actions of the theurgist, properly performed, are themselves divine actions, participated in by the higher powers who draw the soul upward. The *Chaldean Oracles* provided the textual warrant for this theurgical theology: Iamblichus, Proclus, and the Neoplatonist tradition generally treated them as authoritative scripture on a par with Plato.

The Renaissance received the *Oracles* primarily through the mediation of Michael Psellus (1017/1018–1078/1096), the Byzantine scholar who had preserved a significant collection of the verses along with his own commentary. Psellus treated the *Oracles* as genuine Chaldean theology — pre-Platonic wisdom from the ancient Near East — and his commentary, available in manuscripts that circulated in fifteenth-century Italy, connected the *Oracles* to Neoplatonic philosophy and made them legible within the framework of late antique Platonism. Ficino, who was translating the Platonic corpus and the *Hermetica* in the early 1460s under Cosimo de' Medici's patronage, also engaged with the *Oracles*. He believed them to be the genuine ancient theology of Zoroaster (*Zoroastres*) and read them as the earliest stratum of the *prisca theologia*: the original divine wisdom that had flowed from Zoroaster to Hermes Trismegistus, from Hermes to Orpheus, from Orpheus to Pythagoras, and from Pythagoras to Plato. On this reading, the *Oracles* were not late antique compositions but the primordial revelation from which all subsequent philosophy had derived. This conviction about their antiquity was shared by Pico and is fundamental to the use he made of them.

Pico's engagement with the *Chaldean Oracles* is most concentrated in the *900 Conclusions*, where section 8 presents theses *secundum Chaldeam disciplinam* — "according to Chaldean teaching." These twenty-six theses draw on the *Oracles* as filtered through Proclus and Psellus, and they articulate a theology of fire, soul, and divine union that closely follows the *Oracles*' own idiom. The Chaldean Hecate (*Hekate*) appears in Pico's theses as the mediating cosmic principle — the World Soul that the *Oracles* call the "Lynx" or the "Synoches" (*synocheis*, "holders-together"), an intermediate power between the paternal Intellect above and the material world below. The soul's ascent through the Chaldean cosmos proceeds through a series of fire-mediated encounters with these intermediate powers, culminating in union with the paternal fire itself. Pico's theses systematically identify these Chaldean figures with their equivalents in Proclus's Platonic theology: the Chaldean Father corresponds to the Platonic One, the Chaldean Hecate to the Neoplatonic World Soul, the Chaldean fire to the Plotinian *noetic* light. This identif­ication is an instance of the same syncretic method that operates throughout the *900 Conclusions*: Pico is not simply reporting what the *Oracles* say but demonstrating that they say the same thing, under Chaldean symbols, that Proclus says in Greek philosophical terminology — thereby confirming the *concordantia* of traditions that was his master thesis.

The philosophical significance of the *Oracles* for Pico's project went beyond their cosmological content. The *Oracles*' prescription of theurgical practice — ritual action as a path to divine union — provided Pico with a framework that could be coordinated with two other traditions he valued: Iamblichus's account of Greek theurgy and Abraham Abulafia's technique of Kabbalistic letter-combination (*tzeruf otiyot*). All three traditions taught that the soul's return to the divine required not only intellectual effort but a form of practice — a disciplined engagement with divine symbols, names, or ritual forms that would loosen the soul's attachment to matter and strengthen its connection to the higher powers. For Abulafia, the permutation and combination of Hebrew letters and divine names could induce altered states of consciousness and prepare the practitioner for prophetic or mystical experience. For Iamblichus, the performance of theurgical rites activated the "divine marks" (*symbola*, *synthêmata*) implanted in the soul by the Demiurge and opened it to the action of the gods from above. For the *Oracles*, the use of fire, incantation, and specific material objects in ritual contexts drew down the paternal fire and prepared the soul for its ascent through the spheres. Pico's genius — or his audacity — was to present these three traditions as convergent testimonies to a single truth about the soul's relationship to the divine and the conditions of its ascent. The *Oration*'s famous account of the human being as capable of ascending through all the levels of the cosmos, assimilating itself to each level in turn, draws on precisely this convergence: the practices that enable that ascent are, in their different idioms, the practices prescribed by Chaldean theurgy, Greek theurgy, and Kabbalistic letter-combination.

The historical problem with this entire framework is that the *Chaldean Oracles* are not what the Renaissance believed them to be. They are not the ancient wisdom of the Chaldeans, not the writings of Zoroaster, not a pre-Platonic revelation from the ancient Near East. They are late antique Greek compositions — probably written in the second century CE in the context of the syncretic religious ferment of the high imperial period, drawing on Middle Platonic and early Neoplatonic philosophy as well as on various magical and oracular traditions. Their Chaldean coloring is a literary fiction: the attribution to a Chaldean Julian was part of the texts' own self-presentation, designed to claim for them the antiquity and authority that the ancient Near East, and Chaldean astral theology in particular, possessed in the imagination of late antique readers. When Pico used the *Oracles* as evidence that the *prisca theologia* reached back beyond Plato to the ancient Chaldeans, he was accepting at face value a claim that the texts themselves had been constructed to make.

The same problem extended to the other texts in Pico's syncretic constellation. The Hermetic *Corpus Hermeticum* that Ficino translated was not the ancient Egyptian theology of Hermes Trismegistus but a collection of Greek philosophical texts probably composed in the second and third centuries CE. The *Orphic Hymns* were not the works of the mythical Orpheus but late antique compositions. Isaac Casaubon's epoch-making demonstration in *De rebus sacris et ecclesiasticis exercitationes* (1614) that the *Corpus Hermeticum* was post-Christian in date — based on its dependence on Platonic and even New Testament vocabulary — was a decisive blow to the *prisca theologia* framework. Casaubon showed that the supposed ancient Egyptian wisdom was in fact contemporary with the New Testament or later, and this demonstration had devastating implications for any argument that Pico-style syncretism rested on genuinely ancient convergent testimonies. If the Hermetic texts were not ancient, the *Oracles* were not ancient, and the Orphic hymns were not ancient, then the whole argument from the convergence of independent ancient traditions collapsed: what appeared to be independent confirmations of a single truth were in fact dependent on the same late antique Platonic tradition. The ancient wisdom chain from Zoroaster through Hermes, Orpheus, and Pythagoras to Plato was not a chain of transmission from a common primordial source but a Renaissance and late antique mythological construction.

The collapse of the *prisca theologia* framework did not immediately displace the *Chaldean Oracles* from scholarly attention. In the seventeenth century, Ralph Cudworth and other Cambridge Platonists continued to engage with them within a modified theological framework. The first modern critical edition of the surviving fragments, by Wilhelm Kroll (*De Oraculis Chaldaicis*, 1894), placed them within the history of Greek philosophy and late antique religion rather than the history of ancient oriental wisdom. The twentieth-century study of the *Oracles* by Hans Lewy (*Chaldaean Oracles and Theurgy*, 1956) and subsequently by John Dillon, Ruth Majercik, and Luc Brisson situated them definitively within the development of Middle and Neoplatonism and the late antique theurgical tradition, analyzing their relation to Numenius, the Platonists of the second century, and Iamblichus's later elaboration. From this perspective, the *Oracles* are genuinely significant philosophical and religious documents — but significant as evidence for the religious imagination of the high imperial period, not as representatives of primordial Chaldean wisdom.

For reading Pico, what matters is not the actuality of the *Oracles* but their function in his system. They provided him with a set of theurgical and cosmological symbols — the paternal fire, the Hecate-figure as cosmic mediator, the fiery ascent of the soul through the spheres — that could be coordinated with Proclus's Platonic theology and with Kabbalistic mysticism to produce a unified account of the soul's relationship to the divine and the means of its return. The *Conclusions secundum Chaldeam disciplinam* are not, on the evidence of the theses themselves, a straightforward report on what the *Oracles* teach but a Pican reworking in which the Chaldean figures are assigned their places in the syncretic architecture of the nine hundred conclusions. Whether Pico had read the *Oracles* directly in the Psellus manuscript tradition, or absorbed them through the mediation of Ficino and the Florentine Platonic circle, remains a matter of scholarly discussion. What is not in doubt is that the *Oracles* played a structural role in his conception of the *prisca theologia* and in his argument that the various traditions of ancient wisdom — Chaldean, Hermetic, Orphic, Platonic, Pythagorean, and Kabbalistic — were converging testimonies to a single divine truth that Christian theology could acknowledge and appropriate. The exposure of that framework as historically unfounded did not dissolve the philosophical interest of the convergences Pico identified; it simply changed the question from "do these traditions share an ancient common source?" to "why did these traditions develop such striking structural similarities in the late antique period?" — a question that scholarship has not yet fully answered.
