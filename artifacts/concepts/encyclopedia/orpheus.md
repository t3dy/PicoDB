# Orpheus

In the intellectual world of Giovanni Pico della Mirandola, Orpheus appears under two quite different descriptions that are related yet distinct, and conflating them produces a picture that falsifies both. Orpheus the mythological singer and descent-figure is the protagonist of an allegorical narrative in the *Commento sopra una canzona d'amore*, where his descent into Hades to retrieve the dead Eurydice becomes the occasion for a sustained philosophical meditation on the soul's relation to beauty, matter, and contemplative attention. Orpheus the ancient theologian — the *theologus* who stands as a privileged link in the chain of primordial wisdom that descends from Zoroaster through Pythagoras to Plato — is an entirely different figure, operative in the *900 Conclusions* and the *Oration on the Dignity of Man*, functioning there not as the protagonist of a story but as the authority behind a body of encrypted theological doctrine. What unites these two registers is Pico's conviction that ancient poetry encodes genuine metaphysical truths, and that the task of the philosopher is to unlock those truths through allegorical exegesis. Understanding what Pico actually does with Orpheus in each context, however, requires keeping the two registers carefully apart.

The Orpheus who appears in the *Oration* and provides the organizational rationale for section 10 of the *900 Conclusions* belongs to the tradition of *prisca theologia* — ancient theology, the doctrine that a chain of inspired sages received and transmitted a primordial divine wisdom whose core truths anticipate and confirm Christian theology. Marsilio Ficino had elaborated this tradition with particular energy, constructing a genealogical sequence that ran from Zoroaster and Hermes Trismegistus through Orpheus and Pythagoras to Plato. In Ficino's account, Orpheus occupies a structurally crucial position: he is the figure through whom the primordial wisdom of the East — wisdom encoded in the *Chaldean Oracles* and the Hermetic texts — enters Greek culture. Orpheus is the Thracian bard who crossed from the oriental world into the Hellenic, carrying the ancient theological insights in the symbolically encrypted form of hexameter verse. His Hymns, together with the cosmogonic and theogonic poems attributed to him, constitute the Orphic corpus that the Neoplatonists — above all Proclus — had treated as the highest poetic expression of Platonic metaphysics.

Pico inherited this framework but modified it in ways that reveal his distinctive priorities. In the *Oration*, he explicitly invokes the genealogical chain: Pythagoras is said to have derived from Orpheus, as Orpheus derived from more ancient sources, and Plato's philosophy culminates and clarifies what this chain of transmission had encoded. But where Ficino had made Hermes Trismegistus the figure whose authority most closely rivaled Plato's — and had placed the translation of the *Corpus Hermeticum* before the translation of Plato in his own scholarly agenda — Pico effectively demotes Hermes in favor of the Kabbalah. Stephen Farmer's analysis of the *900 Conclusions* shows that Hermes is conspicuously absent from Pico's list of ancient magical authorities, which names Pythagoras, Plato, Zoroaster, Plotinus, and Roger Bacon but not the Egyptian sage (Farmer 1998, 367n). Orpheus remains in Pico's genealogy, but the final authority behind the genealogy shifts: the Kabbalists, not the Hermeticists, possess the most direct access to primordial wisdom, because the Kabbalistic tradition claims descent from Moses on Sinai. In Pico's modified *prisca theologia*, Orpheus is an important transmitter but not the apex; what he transmits ultimately corroborates not merely Platonic metaphysics but the revealed theology of Hebrew scripture as interpreted by the Kabbalists.

Section 10 of the *900 Conclusions* — thirty-one theses "according to the method of the Orphic Hymns, concerning the very different things that are concealed in them" — is the document in which Pico most directly claims to have found something in the Orphic corpus that no previous reader had discovered. His heading boasts that the magical content of the Hymns has been "first discovered in them by me" — a pointed gesture toward Ficino, who had composed a commentary on the Orphic Hymns before 1486 without, Pico implies, recognizing their proper significance (Farmer 1998, 118). The theses that follow demonstrate Pico's characteristic operation: identifying correspondences between Orphic divine names and figures in other traditions. Thesis 10.13 identifies Typhon in Orpheus with Samael in Kabbalah; thesis 10.15 identifies Night — the primordial deity from whom all things spring in Orphic cosmogony — with *Ein Sof*, the infinite divine ground in Kabbalistic mysticism; thesis 10.9 brings together "Guardians" in Orpheus, "Powers" in Dionysius the Areopagite, and the *sefirah* Gevurah in a single cross-traditional identification. The Orphic Hymns, in this procedure, are not interpreted for their religious content as such but decoded as a symbolic vocabulary whose referents are the structures of Neoplatonic and Kabbalistic metaphysics.

The theoretical model underlying this decoding is one Pico draws in part from Proclus, who had elaborated a method of Orphic allegoresis in the *Theologia Platonica* that treated the narrative genealogies of the gods as symbolic encodings of metaphysical hypostases. Proclus reads the myth of Dionysus's dismemberment as an account of the Soul's descent into multiplicity; the story of Persephone's abduction as an allegory of the soul's entry into the body; Night as the transcendent undifferentiated ground underlying the determinate divine processions. Pico was a close reader of Proclus, and this allegorical method — translating mythological narrative into philosophical structure, treating divine names as symbols of ontological levels — is essential to understanding what the Orphic theses in the *900 Conclusions* are doing. When Pico says in thesis 10.7 that "whoever does not know how to intellectualize perceptible properties by the way of secret analogy will understand nothing sound from the Orphic Hymns," he is describing a reading practice that requires philosophical initiation as its precondition. The Hymns encode truths that only the philosophically trained reader can extract.

Ficino's engagement with the Orphic Hymns before Pico had been oriented primarily toward natural magic rather than philosophical allegory. In the *De vita coelitus comparanda* (1489), the third book of his *De vita libri tres*, Ficino developed the theory that the Orphic Hymns, recited with appropriate music and incense, could function as instruments for drawing down celestial influences into the operator's *spiritus*, the subtle pneumatic medium through which cosmic sympathies operated. Each deity named in the Hymns corresponded to a celestial power — a planet, a constellation — and addressing that deity by its correct names in the appropriate ritual context activated the sympathetic connection between the operator and the corresponding cosmic force. This is an explicitly operational account of the Hymns' efficacy: they work not by encoding truths to be decoded philosophically but by producing real effects in the natural world through the medium of celestial sympathy. Pico's claim that the magical content of the Hymns has been discovered by him for the first time suggests that he regards Ficino's approach as fundamentally misguided — that the celestial sympathy framework misses what the Hymns are actually about, which is something closer to a philosophy of divine names that, decoded correctly, maps the structure of reality from the Orphic to the Kabbalistic vocabulary.

The second, entirely different appearance of Orpheus in Pico's writings occurs in the *Commento*, where the mythological narrative of his descent to Hades and attempted rescue of Eurydice becomes the vehicle for a philosophical allegory about the soul's relation to beauty, matter, and contemplative attention. This allegory is developed in the commentary on the fourth stanza of Girolamo Benivieni's *Canzona d'amore*, and it represents Pico's most extended and philosophically pointed departure from Ficino in the *Commento*. Ficino had explicitly declined, in *De amore* 1.4, to interpret the Orpheus story at the point in the *Symposium* (179A–D) where it appears, writing that he did not intend to inquire into the allegory of Alcestis and Orpheus. Pico seizes on this silence and offers what he regards as the correct interpretation — one that, characteristically, is more philosophically severe than anything Ficino would have been inclined to say.

In Pico's allegorical reading, Orpheus represents the philosopher-soul that descends into the realm of matter and lower cognition in pursuit of beauty. Eurydice — who in the myth is Orpheus's wife, bitten by a serpent and drawn down to the realm of the dead — represents beauty itself in its intelligible form, the proper object of philosophical love: what Allen's essay "Eurydice in Hades: Florentine Platonism and an Orphic Mystery" (Allen 2012, 19–40) analyzes as the figure who embodies the intelligible beauty that the philosophical soul seeks. Orpheus's descent is therefore the soul's willing entry into the embodied world in pursuit of the beautiful; his goal of reclaiming Eurydice is the goal of recovering intelligible beauty while still alive, while still in contact with the lower faculties. The myth's tragedy is philosophically instructive: Orpheus fails not because he cannot move the gods of the underworld — his music does move them — but because he cannot fulfill the condition of not looking back. He looks back, Eurydice dissolves, and he returns to the upper world empty-handed.

The philosophical weight of Pico's interpretation falls on why Orpheus looks back, and what this means for the soul. Orpheus failed, says Pico, because he "was not dead" in the philosophically required sense: he went down into Hades still alive, still possessing the functions of imagination and passive reason, still capable of what the Platonists called *phantasia* and the lower operations of the cognitive soul. This means that what he saw in the underworld was not the true Eurydice — not genuine intelligible beauty — but a phantom, a *simulacrum* of beauty generated by his own imagination operating on the traces of sensible perception. The act of looking back is not merely a failure of will but the index of this deeper failure of preparation: Orpheus looks back because he has never fully detached himself from the sensible, never undergone the philosophical death that would have made him capable of genuine intellectual vision. His ascent with the seeming Eurydice is the soul's attempted ascent still contaminated by lower cognitive faculties; his look back is the moment at which the soul's lingering attachment to the sensible undoes whatever progress it appeared to have made.

This interpretation connects directly to the broader argument of the *Commento* about the conditions of genuine philosophical love. For Pico, genuine love of beauty requires that the soul have first undergone what he calls, drawing on Kabbalistic sources, the *mors osculi* or death by kiss — the withdrawal of all cognitive functions from the body into a state of pure intellectual rapture. Only in this state, he argues, can the soul encounter the true Eurydice: only when it has ceased to operate through imagination and passive reason can it apprehend intelligible beauty directly, without the mediation of sensible forms. Orpheus's mistake is the mistake of any philosopher who believes that beauty can be grasped while the lower faculties remain active — who thinks that the ascent to intelligible beauty is compatible with continuing to use the imagination and the senses as cognitive tools. For Pico, it is not. Genuine philosophical vision requires a total disengagement from the sensible that amounts to a kind of death, and only the soul that has undergone this death can recover its true Eurydice without the risk of turning back.

The contrast with Ficino here is philosophically significant in a way that reflects their broader divergence. In Ficino's treatment of the ascent of the soul, beauty is distributed through all levels of being — divine, angelic, celestial, corporeal — and the soul's response to sensible beauty is the legitimate beginning of a genuine philosophical movement toward the divine. The soul that loves a beautiful body is, on Ficino's account, already responding to the divine beauty that shines through that body, and its task is to be led by this response upward through the hierarchy of beauties rather than away from sensible beauty altogether. Pico's reading of the Orpheus myth implies a more severe diagnosis: the soul that goes down into Hades carrying its imagination with it — that enters the realm of lower cognition while still using the faculties proper to that realm — is not on the ladder of ascent but in danger of remaining trapped in the underworld. The philosopher must be genuinely dead to the sensible before the ascent is possible, and Orpheus, who descended alive, could never have succeeded.

The *prisca theologia* and the *Commento* allegory, different as they are in form and function, are united by one presupposition: that the figure of Orpheus encodes genuine philosophical wisdom about the soul's situation and the conditions of its ascent. Whether Orpheus is read as the author of the Hymns — the ancient theologian whose verse encodes the same truths that Plotinus, Proclus, and the Kabbalists state discursively — or as the protagonist of the descent myth — the failed philosopher whose mistake illuminates the conditions of philosophical success — his authority depends on the assumption that ancient poetry is philosophically serious in the way that the *prisca theologia* tradition claimed. The Orpheus whom Pico inherits from Ficino and the Neoplatonic tradition is, in both registers, a teacher — someone from whom, read correctly, genuine philosophical insight can be extracted. The difference is that Pico extracts a harsher lesson than Ficino does: where Ficino's Orpheus is one of the ancient witnesses to a wisdom that is essentially generous in its distribution across traditions, Pico's Orpheus — in both the theological and the allegorical register — teaches that the path to intelligible beauty is more demanding than easy syncretism suggests, and that only the soul willing to die to the sensible will find what it seeks.
