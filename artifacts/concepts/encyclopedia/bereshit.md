# Bereshit

The single Hebrew word *bereshit* (בְּרֵאשִׁית), conventionally translated "in the beginning," is the opening word of the Torah, the first term in the divine act of creation as narrated in the first verse of Genesis: *bereshit bara Elohim et hashamayim ve'et ha'aretz*, "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth." For the Kabbalistic tradition available to Pico della Mirandola through the translations of Flavius Mithridates, this single word was not merely the temporal marker that introduces the creation narrative. It was, in the reading preserved in Recanati's commentary on the Pentateuch and the Zohar passages that Recanati cited, a word that "contains all the rest of the utterances" (*verbum id quod collegit omnia alia verba*), a compressed totality from which the entire creative program could be unfolded. Pico's *Heptaplus* (1489) devotes its final, unnumbered section — placed after the seven main expositions have been completed — to an extended meditation on this single word, and it is in this section that the deepest hermeneutical ambitions of the whole work are revealed. The exposition of *bereshit* alone is, as Crofton Black argues, the apex of the *Heptaplus*'s hermeneutical design: the point at which the convergence of biblical, Neoplatonic, and Kabbalistic reading is most concentrated, and where the claim that Moses encoded the complete philosophy of creation in a single word is most dramatically enacted (Black 2006, 214–232).

The word itself contains six Hebrew letters: *bet* (ב), *resh* (ר), *alef* (א), *shin* (ש), *yod* (י), *tav* (ת). Its morphological structure is a combination of the preposition *be-* ("in" or "by" or "with") and the noun *reshit* (רֵאשִׁית, "beginning" or "first-fruit" or "chief thing"), itself derived from the root *rosh*, "head." This derivational complexity had been seized upon by Kabbalistic interpreters long before Pico. The Aramaic Targum Yerushalmi had already rendered *bereshit* as *behokmeta*, "in wisdom" — reading the word as announcing that God created the world by means of divine Wisdom (*Hokhmah*), the second sefirah in the standard Kabbalistic enumeration. This reading, preserved in Recanati's commentary, identifies *reshit* with *Hokhmah* through a series of scriptural cross-references, particularly Proverbs 8:22 — *Adonai qanani reshit darko*, "The Lord possessed me as the beginning of his way" — where Wisdom herself speaks and identifies herself as *reshit*, as the first and originating principle. By this reading, *bereshit* does not merely announce a temporal beginning but declares that all creation proceeds *from and through Wisdom*, that the second sefirah is the generative matrix of the entire creative act. Pico's Kabbalistic conclusion XXV in the *900 Conclusions* (1486) states this principle with lapidary economy: "Idem est *bresith*, id est *in principio creavit*, ac si dixisset *in sapientia creavit*" — "It is the same thing whether one says 'in the beginning he created' or 'in wisdom he created'" (Wirszubski 1989, 41). The equation of *bereshit* with *Hokhmah* is not a learned flourish; it is the foundational claim of Kabbalistic reading of Genesis, and Pico's appropriation of it connects his hermeneutics to the deepest roots of Jewish mystical exegesis.

The letter *bet* (ב) that opens the Torah and therefore opens creation attracted its own layer of interpretation. Rabbinic tradition had asked why the Torah begins with *bet* rather than *alef*, the first letter of the alphabet, which might seem the more fitting opening for the divine utterance. The Bahir, one of the earliest Kabbalistic texts and one that Mithridates translated for Pico (it survives in Vat. Ebr. 191), and the Zohar both provide answers to this question in ways that connect the shape and position of the *bet* to the structure of the sefirot. The *bet* is open on one side and closed on three: it faces forward (to the right, in the direction of reading) and indicates that one should ask about what is above and what is before, while what is behind and below is concealed. Nahmanides, whose commentary on the Pentateuch was also available to Pico in Hebrew, wrote that "the word is crowned with a crown of *bet* because the *bet* with its tail is an indication of it [i.e., of Keter, the highest sefirah]," suggesting that even the visual form of the letter encodes a reference to the transcendent ground of all sefirot (Recanati, quoted in Black 2006, 217, from the passage translated by Mithridates). The *bet* thus points simultaneously backward and downward — to Keter, which precedes all articulation — and forward into the word *bereshit*, which begins the divine utterance. The letter is simultaneously the origin and the boundary of what can be said.

The word *bereshit* was also susceptible to a combinatory permutation that had already attracted Kabbalistic attention: the letters *shin*, *bet*, and *tav* (שבת) — the last three letters of *bereshit* after the letters *alef*, *yod*, and *resh* have been considered — spell *shabbat* (שַׁבָּת), the Sabbath. Recanati, in the passage quoted at length and analyzed by Black, draws on the Zohar's identification of the Sabbath as "the foundation of the world" (*yesod olam*) and reads this permutation as pointing to the fact that creation, which begins with *bereshit*, is already oriented toward the Sabbath — toward the rest and completion that the seventh day represents and that is, in Pico's reading of the *Heptaplus*'s seventh exposition, identical with *felicitas*, the blessedness that is the proper end of human life (Black 2006, 220–221). The word that opens creation thus contains within its own letter-fabric an announcement of creation's end: *bereshit* includes *shabbat*, and the beginning includes the consummation. This is the kind of totalizing reading that makes *bereshit* such a dense hermeneutical object for the Kabbalistic tradition, and it is this tradition — specifically Recanati's encoded transmission of Zoharic exegesis — that underlies Pico's own exposition.

Pico's eighth section of the *Heptaplus* — the "Expositio primae dictionis, idest *in principio*" — announces itself as something new, departing from the allegorical method of the previous seven expositions to adopt "another method of interpretation" (*per aliam interpretandi rationem*: H. 50; *Garin* 374). Where allegory operates at the level of words, finding correspondences between the words of the Genesis text and the entities and concepts of the philosophical and theological tradition, this new method investigates "the letters themselves, from which the words of the law are made up" (H. 50; *Garin* 374). The letters of *bereshit*, treated as independent combinatory elements rather than as components of a fixed word, can be rearranged and recombined to yield a series of new words, each carrying its own meaning. Pico derives twelve such words: *ab* (father), *bebar* (in/through the son), *resith* (beginning), *sciabat* (rest/end), *bara* (he created), *rosc* (head), *es* (fire), *seth* (foundation), *rab* (great), *hisc* (of man), *berith* (with a pact), *thob* (good). Reading these in sequence produces a single sentence: "The father, in/through the son, who is the beginning and the rest/end, created the head, the fire and the foundation of the great man with a good pact" (H. 50; *Garin* 378–382). This sentence summarizes the *Heptaplus* as a whole: it refers to the Trinity (father, son), to the three worlds encoded in their cosmological symbols (head for the angelic world, fire for the celestial, foundation for the elemental), to man as the macrocosm (*magnus homo*, "great man"), and to the covenant (*berith*, foedus) that binds all the worlds together in a unity that is "good" because it returns them to their divine source.

Black's analysis traces the source of Pico's letter-combination technique to the *Liber combinationum*, a text translated for him by Mithridates (Vat. Ebr. 190, ff. 60v–62v), in which seven of the twelve words Pico derives from *bereshit* — father, fire, son, he-created, covenant, great, foundation — are already present in the context of letter-combination exercises (Black 2006, 216, citing Wirszubski 1989, 220–221). The ultimate inspiration for the method is the tradition of *hokmat ha-tseruf* (the science of letter combination) associated above all with Abraham Abulafia, one of whose works, the Sitrei Torah, was translated for Pico by Mithridates and contains extended examples of exactly the kind of letter-combinatory exegesis that Pico applies to *bereshit* in the eighth section of the *Heptaplus*. In the *Apologia* of 1487, Pico had explicitly described the *ars combinandi* as a quintessentially Kabbalistic procedure — "a way of proceeding in sciences similar to the Art of Raymond Lull, which is called *ars combinandi*" (*Opera omnia* 180–181) — and had associated it with what he called in the *900 Conclusions* "alphabetary revolution" (*alphabetaria revolutio*). When he uses it in the *Heptaplus*, however, he carefully avoids naming it as Kabbalistic, describing it only as "another method of interpretation." The strategic concealment of the Kabbalistic character of the technique is consistent with the general pattern of the *Heptaplus*, which encodes Kabbalistic frameworks throughout while naming them only rarely.

The deeper significance of the *bereshit* exposition, however, lies not simply in the combinatory technique but in the idea that technique enacts: that a single word contains everything. This claim — that *bereshit* is "an utterance which contains all the rest of the utterances" — is explicitly drawn from Recanati's compilation of Zoharic material and is the theological center of the entire eighth section. The opening of the Recanati passage that underlies Pico's exposition states, in Mithridates's Latin rendering: "Sefer ha-zohar: Rabbi Yizhak said this word bereshit is an utterance which contains all the rest of the utterances, and it points to Hokhmah, which contains everything" (Black 2006, 217–218). Pico absorbs from this tradition not the sefirot symbolism — which, as Black shows, is entirely absent from the *Heptaplus* itself — but the totalizing claim about the word's compressive power. If *bereshit* contains everything, then to unfold *bereshit* letter by letter is to unfold the entire structure of creation. The twelve words Pico derives are not arbitrary combinations but are the *latent content* of the word itself, present there from the moment Moses wrote it, waiting to be recovered by the initiated reader who knows the rules. The sentence those twelve words form, read in order, is the *summary of the Heptaplus*: it does not merely paraphrase the seven expositions but compresses within a single grammatical structure the same content that the seven expositions took forty-nine chapters to unfold. This recursive structure — the *Heptaplus* as an unfolding of the word that its final section refolds — gives the work its extraordinary formal coherence.

The structure of the *Heptaplus* as a whole — seven expositions each of seven chapters, generating forty-nine units before the eighth, final section — is itself a Kabbalistic datum, and it connects the *bereshit* exposition to the doctrine of the forty-nine gates of understanding analyzed in detail by Black in his seventh chapter. The doctrine, attested from the Babylonian Talmud (*Rosh Hashanah* 21b) through Nahmanides's introduction to his Pentateuch commentary to the Kabbalistic texts of Gikatilla — all available to Pico — holds that fifty gates of understanding were created with the world, of which forty-nine were revealed to Moses on Mount Sinai. Each gate corresponds to knowledge of a different category of created being: from minerals through plants and animals to the celestial spheres. The fiftieth gate — corresponding to knowledge of the Creator himself, to Ein Sof, to what lies beyond all possible articulation — was not opened even to Moses. Pico makes this framework explicit in the *Conclusiones*: "He who knows in Kabbalah the mystery of the gates of understanding will know the mystery of the great jubilee" (*Conclusions* 96). The *Heptaplus*'s forty-nine units of commentary before the *bereshit* section therefore correspond structurally to Moses's forty-nine gates: the seven-by-seven structure is the numerical image of the complete knowledge of creation that Moses encoded in his text. And the eighth section — the exposition of *bereshit* alone, placed after the forty-nine chapters as something that lies beyond them, "something which it seemed should have been expounded in the first place" (H. 50; *Garin* 374) — points toward what lies beyond the forty-nine gates: not the fiftieth, which is Ein Sof and is by definition inaccessible to any created mind, but the word that precedes the entire hexaemeron narrative, the word that is the utterance prior to all utterances, the beginning that precedes the account of beginning.

The relationship between *bereshit* and the Sabbath, mediated through Recanati's quotation of the Zohar, is therefore also a relationship between the beginning of creation and its end. The letters of *bereshit* contain *shabbat*; the word that opens the Torah points to the divine rest that completes it; the creative act that begins in *bereshit bara Elohim* culminates in the sabbatical rest of the seventh day, which is the figure for *felicitas* — the supernatural happiness that is the reversion of all created things back to the source from which they proceeded. The *Heptaplus*'s seven-expositions-plus-eighth-section structure mirrors this: the forty-nine chapters of seven expositions are the six days of creation (the creative act of producing, describing, and relating the worlds); the seventh exposition itself is the sabbath within the work (turning toward Christ, toward felicitas, toward the end that is also a return); and the eighth section on *bereshit* is the discovery that the entire structure was already contained in the first word — that the end was present in the beginning, that the Sabbath was folded into *bereshit* before creation had taken even its first step.

Wirszubski's *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989) remains the essential guide to the specific Kabbalistic sources behind Pico's *bereshit* exegesis. Wirszubski's reconstruction of the Mithridates corpus — five manuscripts held in the Vatican library containing more than 3,500 pages of Hebrew texts translated into Latin specifically for Pico — establishes the textual basis on which any reading of the *bereshit* section must rest. His analysis of Pico's Kabbalistic conclusion XXV, which identifies *bereshit* with Wisdom, traces the conclusion through three possible textual antecedents in the Mithridates translations and concludes that Recanati's Pentateuch commentary, which cites both the Bahir and the Targum Yerushalmi on this point, is the most probable direct source (Wirszubski 1989, 41). His further analysis of the letter-combination technique documents the presence of the method in the Abulafian texts of the corpus and connects Pico's otherwise cryptic references to "the rules which they [the Kabbalists] themselves hand down" (*iuxta regulas, quas ipsi tradunt*: H. 50; *Garin* 374) to the specific tradition of *hokmat ha-tseruf* as it appears in the Mithridates translations. Together, Black and Wirszubski establish the *bereshit* exposition as a textually dense and precisely sourced engagement with Kabbalistic tradition, not a vague invocation of Hebrew mysticism.

For the reader encountering the *Heptaplus* for the first time, the *bereshit* section presents a challenge because it requires holding simultaneously in mind the philosophical content of the seven main expositions and the formal content of the eighth — the claim that the sentence derived from *bereshit*'s letters says the same thing as the seven expositions together. The section does not supersede the expositions; it reveals that they were always already contained in what Pico calls the word's "concealed kernel" (*medulla abdita*). The *Heptaplus* is a work that reads Genesis forward through seven registers and then folds it back into its opening word; and what *bereshit* unfolds, when the letters are properly combined, is nothing less than the syncretic philosophy of creation that the entire work has been enacting — the Trinity, the three worlds, man as microcosm, the covenant of goodness that binds them all. Moses hid it in the first word, Pico claims, because the first word was enough: *bereshit* alone contains everything that the initiated reader needs to know.
