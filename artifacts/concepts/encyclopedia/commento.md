# Commento

Among all of Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's writings, the Commento sopra una canzona d'amore occupies an unusual and somewhat melancholy position: it is the work in which he is most personal, most lyrical, most willing to write about love in a register that blends metaphysics with something approaching feeling, and yet it is also the work he never finished, never published, and whose text arrived at posterity in a form altered by other hands. To read the Commento is to encounter Pico at his most brilliant and his most exposed, in a vernacular Italian that carries both the elegance of the Florentine literary tradition and the technical weight of Neoplatonic philosophy—and to encounter him, simultaneously, through the mediation of editors who had reasons to soften what he wrote.

The immediate occasion of the Commento is a canzone, or philosophical love poem in Italian, by Pico's close friend Girolamo Benivieni (1453–1542). The poem, known from its opening words as "Amor dalle cui man" and sometimes titled the Canzone dello amor celeste e divino, was itself a vernacular adaptation of ideas drawn from Marsilio Ficino's Commentary on Plato's Symposium, the De amore. Benivieni was a poet of genuine gifts and a committed member of the circle gathered around Ficino and the Medici, and his canzone translated the abstractions of Florentine Neoplatonism into the inherited formal structures of Italian poetry. Pico's decision to write a commentary on it was both an act of friendship and a philosophical provocation. He was commenting on a poem that was already commenting on Ficino, which meant he was inevitably placing himself in a triangulated relationship with Benivieni's verse and with Ficino's prose—a relationship that quickly became a dispute.

The Commento was probably begun in 1486, the same year in which Pico was preparing his nine hundred theses for the Roman disputation and composing the Oration that was to introduce it. This dating matters. It situates the Commento before the papal commission's condemnation of thirteen of his theses and before the crisis of 1487 that forced him first to flee to France and then to submit to a period of quasi-house arrest in Florence under Medici protection. The Commento is therefore a pre-crisis document, the work of a young man of twenty-three who still believed he was about to transform the intellectual life of Rome, and who wrote with the urgency and confidence—and occasional impatience—of someone who feels that time is short and the stakes are high. What became of the Commento during the crisis, why Pico never published it, and what he thought of it in the last years of his life under Savonarola's influence, we cannot be certain. We know only that he continued to revise and redistribute its material, that he quarried it for his later Heptaplus, and that it remained unfinished at his death in 1494.

The architecture of the Commento as we have it is elaborate. It unfolds in three books, followed by a section of detailed glosses on individual stanzas of Benivieni's poem. The first book deals with the metaphysics of love and beauty in a relatively general way; the second turns to the cosmological and ontological hierarchy that structures Pico's account; the third descends into more particular mythological and allegorical interpretation. The tripartite structure reflects Pico's sustained effort to make the commentary something grander than its occasion: not merely an explication of one poem by one friend, but, as Michael Allen has observed, a work that Pico may have anticipated developing into a commentary on Plato's Symposium, or a full treatise on love, or even what Allen calls a "poetic theology." In this sense the Commento belongs to a tradition of love treatises that runs from Plato's Symposium through Plotinus and the Arabic commentators, through Ficino's De amore, and into the Italian vernacular discussions of love that flourished in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries—but Pico intended it to be the definitive contribution to that tradition, the one that got the metaphysics right.

The metaphysical center of the Commento is a hierarchical account of love and beauty organized around the figure of the three Venuses. Pico distinguishes among a celestial or Uranian Venus (identified with the divine mind or angelic intellect), a lower or Vulgar Venus (identified with the generative power of the world soul), and ultimately a cosmic principle of Beauty that both transcends and illuminates them. This tripartite scheme is not simply borrowed from Ficino; it is in part a correction of Ficino. Where Ficino had presented the two Venuses of the Symposium as both celestial—one associated with contemplative, the other with generative love—Pico insists on sharper metaphysical distinctions and is not content to let the Platonic tradition rest in Ficino's characteristically affective synthesis. Allen's review of Sears Jayne's translation notes that Pico attacks Marsilio by name, faults his logic and acuity, complains of his ignorance of particular authorities, and specifically takes issue with Ficino's views on the metaphysical status of beauty and love, the meaning of Plato's two Venuses, and the correct interpretation of the Symposium's presentation of the Orpheus and Eurydice story. These are not marginal complaints but go to the heart of Ficino's project.

The deeper disagreement concerns the relationship between Beauty and the Good. For Ficino, the soul's ascent is driven by Eros—love as a divine madness, a fury that sweeps the soul upward through beauty toward God—and the experience of beauty is, in an important sense, the experience of God's own outpouring into the world. The soul loves beauty because beauty is the splendor of the divine face; and love, guided by beauty, returns the soul to its source. This is an emotionally rich and phenomenologically attentive account; it takes seriously the experience of falling in love with beautiful things as the first movement of a journey that ends in contemplation of the divine. Pico does not deny the structure of ascent, but he insists on a more rigorous metaphysical ordering. For Pico, the Good stands above Beauty in the ontological hierarchy; Beauty is the manifestation of the intelligible order for souls at a certain level of vision, but it is not the ultimate term. Love that is properly philosophical moves through Beauty toward the Good that is beyond Beauty—a Platonic distinction derived from the Republic and the Philebus, mediated through Plotinus and Proclus, but one that Pico applies with a scholastic precision that Ficino's more rhapsodic treatment tends to blur.

The practical consequence is a more severe account of love. Pico distinguishes among three modes or degrees of love—sensual or vulgar love, celestial love, and divine love—and insists that the differences among them are not merely affective gradations but metaphysical distinctions. Sensual love attaches to the beauty of particular bodies; celestial love attaches to the beauty of the intelligible order as reflected in souls and minds; divine love moves beyond all particular beauty toward the absolute Good. Only the third is genuine philosophy in Pico's sense. This hierarchy is not alien to Ficino, but Pico's version of it is less sympathetic to the lower stages and less willing to describe the soul's erotic journey in Ficino's luxuriant mythological terms. Pico is, in this respect, a harsher teacher.

The relationship between the Commento and the Oration is not accidental. The two works are complementary documents of the same intellectual moment, and together they sketch the architecture of Pico's philosophical program for 1486. The Oration sets out the anthropological and cosmological argument for human ascent: man is the being of no fixed nature, placed at the center of the world and endowed with the freedom to ascend or descend; the philosophical life is the life of ascent through the disciplines, the Platonic purifications, the angelic intelligences, and beyond. The Commento provides, in a different register, the account of what drives that ascent: love, understood not as an emotion but as a metaphysical appetite, the desire of the lower for the higher, of the imperfect for the perfect, of multiplicity for unity, of the image for the original. In the Oration Pico tells us why we can ascend; in the Commento he tells us what draws us upward. The two works are the twin faces of a single philosophical ambition, and a reader who knows only one of them has only half of Pico's account of the philosophical life.

The publication history of the Commento is entangled with the circumstances of Pico's life and death in ways that profoundly affect our access to his thought. Pico died in 1494 at the age of thirty-one, and the Commento was not among the works included in the Opera omnia published by his nephew Gianfrancesco in 1496. This omission is significant. The 1496 edition omitted the Commento and the nine hundred Conclusiones—the two works most obviously implicated in Pico's conflict with papal authority and with Ficino—and established instead an image of Pico as a harmonizing, orthodox, mystical Neoplatonist. The Commento did not reach print until 1519, when it appeared in a Giunti edition of Benivieni's own poems. But it appeared there in a form that had been worked over, as Allen's review makes clear, by Pico's friends after his death: the offensive references to Ficino had been excised, some of the more combative and heretical remarks removed, and the material reordered. This expurgated text then reappeared in the Basel editions of Pico's Opera omnia in 1557, 1572, and 1601. The result is that the Commento as posterity knew it for centuries was not the Commento Pico wrote but a diplomatically altered version produced by people who had reasons—personal, institutional, and polemical—to present Pico in a favorable light and Ficino in an undamaged one.

The recovery of the earlier, unexpurgated stage of the text is largely the achievement of twentieth-century Italian scholarship, above all the work of Eugenio Garin, whose critical edition of 1942 remains the authoritative basis for modern study. Garin drew on manuscripts that preserve earlier stages of the text before the expurgation, including manuscripts in which Pico's direct attacks on Ficino appear without softening. The story of how the text was cannibalized—by Pico himself for his Heptaplus, by Benivieni for marginal glosses in the 1500 edition of his poems, and by Benivieni's grand-nephew for a later editorial proposal—makes the Commento one of the most textually complex documents in Pico's corpus.

Modern scholarship in English received the Commento primarily through Sears Jayne's translation, published in 1944 by the University of New Mexico Press and reissued in expanded form by Peter Lang in 1984. Jayne's translation, based on Garin's edition and supplemented by two manuscripts that came to light after 1942, is the foundation for English-language discussion of the text. Allen's 1986 review in Italica offers a careful assessment: the translation is described as fluent and accurate, the introduction as useful, and the stemmatic reconstruction proposed by Jayne as controversial (it elevates the status of Benivieni's personal adaptation of the text and postulates four lost drafts for the unexpurgated stage). Allen praises the end notes for suggesting interpretive possibilities but notes the bibliography's inaccuracies and the absence of an index. There was also a 1967 Johns Hopkins dissertation translation by Stephen J. Salchenberger—the first complete English version—but Jayne's remains the standard. Italian scholarship includes work by Olga Pugliese on the transmission and literary character of the text.

The influence of the Commento extends beyond the confines of technical philosophy. Despite its tortured publication history, the text made a genuine impact on the English Renaissance through a partial French translation by Gabriel Chappuys (1578), which was known to Edmund Spenser, George Chapman, and Ben Jonson. Some of Pico's allegorical interpretations—particularly those involving the birth of Venus and the figures of Porus and Penia—have been mined by art historians working on Botticelli and Michelangelo, and the Commento is now understood as a key document in the interpretation of Florentine Neoplatonic art. But these influences came filtered through the expurgated version. The philosophically interesting Commento—the one that argues with Ficino, insists on metaphysical precision, and subordinates beauty to the Good—remained substantially invisible to early modern readers.

For the reader of PicoDB, the Commento is indispensable on several grounds. It is Pico's only extended vernacular work and therefore a crucial document for understanding his engagement with Italian literary and humanist culture. It is his most direct contribution to the theory of love and beauty, and the work in which the Platonic tradition is most extensively discussed and contested. It preserves evidence, however imperfect, of Pico's early and sustained disagreement with Ficino—a disagreement that would continue, in different registers, in the De ente et uno and the Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem. And it provides the emotional and philosophical content that makes the Oration's program of ascent intelligible: what is at stake when a soul rises toward the One, what it loves, what it sees, what lies beyond sight. To read the Commento alongside the Oration is to understand Pico's 1486 project in its full ambition and complexity—the philosopher as lover, the lover as metaphysician, ascending through beauty toward a Good that is beyond beauty, toward a One that is beyond being.
