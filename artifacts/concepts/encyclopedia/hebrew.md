Hebrew held a distinctive position in Pico della Mirandola's intellectual universe — not merely as the language of Scripture, not merely as the philological key to Old Testament scholarship, but as a theological and cosmological object in its own right. For Pico, Hebrew was the *lingua sacra*, the sacred language in which God spoke creation into being, in which the divine names participate directly in the divine creative power they designate, and through which the Kabbalistic tradition claimed to have preserved the most ancient and complete form of divine wisdom. This claim was inseparable from Pico's broader program of the *prisca theologia* — the ancient theology — but Hebrew occupied a special position within that program because of its status as the language of revelation, as the medium of Kabbalistic letter-combination, and as the privileged site where Pico believed philosophical truths and theological truths could be shown to converge.

The theological claim about Hebrew was not original to Pico; it had deep roots in patristic and medieval thought. Jerome's view that Hebrew was the original sacred tongue, the language in which God conversed with the patriarchs and prophets, was a commonplace of medieval biblical scholarship. What was distinctive about Pico's appropriation of this claim was the Kabbalistic framework within which he embedded it. For the Kabbalists, Hebrew was not simply the historically earliest language or the providentially chosen language of revelation: it was a cosmologically generative language whose letters and combinations were the actual structural principles through which creation had been accomplished. The foundational text for this claim is the *Sefer Yetzirah* (ספר יצירה, Book of Formation), one of the earliest and most cryptic texts of Jewish mystical tradition, which teaches that the twenty-two Hebrew letters together with the ten *sefirot* — thirty-two paths of wisdom in total — are the instruments through which God created the world. The letters are not conventional signs whose relationship to their referents is arbitrary; they are concentrated forms of divine creative energy.

Pico encountered this claim both through the Kabbalistic texts that Mithridates translated and through his own early study of the *Sefer Yetzirah* tradition. In his letter to an unknown friend from Fratta, dated 10 November 1486 — one of the most important documentary letters for understanding Pico's early Kabbalistic studies — Pico refers explicitly to a commentary on "the book that the ancient father Abraham published on the Creation" (*Sefer Yetzirah*), in which the commentators interpret the thirty-two gates as composed of ten *numerations* [sefirot] and twenty-two letters. Pico uses this passage as evidence in a long discussion of the question whether the Hebrew letters used by Moses were the same as those currently in use, against Jerome's claim that Ezra had invented a new alphabet after the Babylonian exile. The Kabbalistic authors, Pico tells his correspondent, "maintain and demonstrate that Moses concealed divine mysteries under the very letter-forms and figures by which we now have the Law written; which would be entirely impossible if Moses had used other characters than those we now use." The argument is revealing: it anchors the theological claim about Hebrew letters in the continuity of the specific letter-forms, making the physical shape of the aleph-beit (אלף-בית) itself the medium of divine encoding. The five final letter-forms (*sofit* forms of mem, tsade, pe, kaph, and nun), which differ from the regular medial forms, Pico attributes to "the bishops or perfects" — perhaps the scribes of the rabbinic period — following a commentarial tradition. This careful distinction between the twenty-two primary letters and the five supplementary final forms shows Pico engaging with the internal technical distinctions of the *Sefer Yetzirah* tradition, not merely with its headline claim.

As Chaim Wirszubski established with philological precision in *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989), the question of Pico's actual Hebrew competence is crucial and often misunderstood. Pico began studying Hebrew in the summer of 1486, at precisely the time when Mithridates' translations were flowing in great quantity — the approximately 3,000-page body of Kabbalistic texts in Latin that constituted Pico's actual working library. By October 1486, some weeks before he completed the *900 Conclusions*, Pico could, by his own account, compose a moderate Hebrew prose exercise. He had studied the language intensively for a month of days and nights. But to read a Kabbalistic text in the original Hebrew without a translation would have required years of preparation, not weeks. Wirszubski's conclusion is firm: "it is quite out of the question that Pico could at that time have read an untranslated Kabbalistic book unaided" (1989, 4). Pico's access to Hebrew Kabbalistic thought was mediated almost entirely through Mithridates' Latin translations. And Mithridates himself introduced a crucial further complication: his translations were shot through with Hebrew words and phrases left untranslated, with marginal notes in Hebrew, and — as Mithridates himself acknowledged in a note to Pico preserved in the manuscript — with material that could not be understood without knowing the Hebrew original. "O Pice," Mithridates wrote in the margin of the *Liber Redemptionis* manuscript, "magna sunt hec sed impossibile est ut intelligantur a te nisi in propriis literis et verbis hebraicis" — these things are great, but it is impossible for you to understand them except in the proper Hebrew letters and words.

This epistemic situation — in which Pico was deeply invested in the theological significance of the Hebrew language while working from Latin translations that frequently reminded him of their own inadequacy — is not a simple irony but a structural feature of what Hebrew meant for Pico. His interest in Hebrew was not primarily a philologist's interest in a source language; it was a mystic's and a philosopher's interest in a language whose very materiality — the shapes of the letters, their numerical values, their combinational possibilities — carried theological content that could not be fully reproduced in translation. As Gershom Scholem had formulated it, and as Wirszubski quotes: the process that the Kabbalists described as "the emanation of divine energy and divine light was also characterized as the unfolding of the divine language... The secret world of the godhead is a World of language, a world of divine names that unfold in accordance with a law of their own. The elements of the divine language appear as the letters of the Holy Scriptures. Letters and names are not only conventional means of communication. They are far more. Each one of them represents a concentration of energy and expresses a wealth of meaning which cannot be translated, or not fully at least, into human language" (Wirszubski 1989, 6, citing Scholem). This was precisely what attracted Pico to Hebrew: it was a language in which the sign and the signified were not merely conventionally related, in which names were not arbitrary labels but participations in the reality they named.

Abraham Abulafia's ecstatic Kabbalah, which Pico encountered through Mithridates' translation of the *Liber Redemptionis* and *De Secretis Legis*, centers on the manipulation of Hebrew letters as the primary technique for achieving the prophetic union (*devekut*, דְּבֵקוּת) in which the mystic's intellect unites with the Active Intellect (*sekel ha-po'el*, שֵׂכֶל הַפּוֹעֵל). Abulafia's *ars combinandi* — the methodical combination, permutation, and transposition of Hebrew letters, particularly of the letters of the divine name — was for him the royal road to prophetic experience. Pico was deeply attracted to this tradition and built on it in two significant ways. First, in his *900 Conclusions*, he articulated a series of theses about the art of Kabbalistic letter-combination, distinguishing it from but relating it to magical *characteres*, and claiming that "as characters are proper to magical work, so numbers are proper to Kabbalistic work" (*Sicut caracteres sunt proprii operi magico, ita numeri sunt proprii operi cabalae*). Second, in the *Heptaplus*, the eighth and final proem uses a letter-combinatory analysis of the first word of Genesis, *Bereishit* (בְּרֵאשִׁית, "in the beginning"), to extract a Trinitarian and Christological formula hidden within the Hebrew text: "Pater in Filio et per Filium principium et finem sive quietem creavit caput ignem et fundamentum magni hominis foedere bono" — the Father in the Son and through the Son, as beginning and end or rest, created the head, fire, and the foundation of the great human being by a good covenant. This spectacular piece of letter-combination, in which the Hebrew consonants of *Bereishit* are rearranged and their syllabic components assigned Latin translations, demonstrates Pico's conviction that Hebrew was not merely the language in which Scripture happened to be written but the code in which Scripture's deepest doctrinal content was embedded.

The *Conclusiones Cabalisticae* in the *900 Conclusions* include several theses that bear directly on the Hebrew language as a theological object. Conclusio lxx states: "Per modum legendi sine punctis in lege, et modus scribendi res divinas, et unialis continentia per indeterminatum ambitum rerum divinarum nobis ostenditur" — through the mode of reading the Law without vowel points, both the manner of writing divine things and the unitive containment through the indeterminate scope of divine things is shown to us. The practice of reading the Torah *sine punctis*, without the vowel-pointing added by the Masoretes, is not merely a liturgical convention but a theological revelation: the consonantal text without vocalization admits of multiple readings, and that multivalence is itself a figure for the inexhaustible meaning of divine revelation. Wirszubski's interpretation of this thesis emphasizes that what Pico means by "unialis continentia per indeterminatum ambitum rerum divinarum" is precisely this: the Hebrew consonantal text, read without points, is like a reservoir of potential meaning that cannot be reduced to any single reading, because the divine reality it encodes exceeds any finite determination (Wirszubski 1989, 181). Like *Ein Sof* in the cosmological dimension, the Hebrew text as *sine punctis* is without limit and without final fixation — the two concepts belong together in Pico's Kabbalistic theology of language.

Gematria (*gematriah*, גְּמַטְרִיָּה), the practice of assigning numerical values to Hebrew letters and finding correspondences between words or phrases with the same numerical total, was another dimension of the Hebrew language's theological significance for Pico. The *Liber Redemptionis* passage that Mithridates translated and annotated for Pico provides an example: the combination of the three divine names YHWH, Adonai, and Elohim yields a total of 177, which equals the numerical value of "the Cause of that which exists" (*hivvah amishah*) and "the Cause of the World" (*hivvah ha-olam*) — demonstrating, through the arithmetic of the sacred language, that God is the cause of all that exists. Mithridates appended a note: "Amore Pici volo declarare hunc passum" — out of love for Pico, I want to explain this passage. The intimacy of this pedagogical relationship between Pico and his translator, working through the Hebrew letters and their numerical correlates, is itself part of the history of what Hebrew meant for Pico: not an object of detached philological study but a living medium of doctrinal discovery.

The significance of Pico's program for the subsequent history of Christian Hebraism was substantial. His *Kabbalistic Conclusions* helped legitimate Hebrew as a Christian scholarly language and provided a framework within which Hebrew study could be defended not merely as an aid to biblical philology — which was the humanism of Erasmus and Lorenzo Valla — but as access to a tradition of wisdom confirmed by Jewish mystical theology itself. The *Apology* (1487), written in defense of the condemned theses, contains Pico's famous claim that he believes himself to be the first to have made explicit mention of Kabbalah among Latin writers: "Haec est prima et vera Cabala, de qua credo me primum apud Latinos explicatam fecisse mentionem." Johann Reuchlin's *De Verbo Mirifico* (1494, the year of Pico's death) and *De Arte Cabalistica* (1517) build directly on Pico's program, with the Hebrew divine name as the key to both Kabbalistic wisdom and Christological disclosure. Reuchlin makes explicit what is implicit throughout Pico's *Conclusions*: the Hebrew *Shem ha-Meforash* (שֵׁם הַמְּפֹרָשׁ, the Explicit Name) — particularly the Tetragrammaton and its elaborations — is the site where the divine creative and redemptive power is most directly concentrated in language, and the Hebrew letters through which that name is written carry more than phonemic or graphic information.

One important historiographical caution concerns the distinction between what Pico himself claimed about Hebrew and what his actual linguistic competence permitted him to verify independently. Pico's claims about Hebrew — about the theological significance of its letters, about the continuity of the letter-forms from Moses to the present, about the doctrinal content encoded in Kabbalistic letter-combination — rested on Mithridates' translations and on oral instruction from Mithridates and from other Jewish interlocutors whose identities and doctrinal positions are not always recoverable. The November 1486 letter to an unknown friend makes clear that Pico was still asking basic questions about the Hebrew alphabet and the Josephus question, which suggests that his knowledge, however intense and rapidly accumulated, was not yet systematically grounded. Mithridates himself, as Wirszubski shows, was a learned but idiosyncratic mediator who imported non-Kabbalistic material into his translations and whose own theological commitments as a converted Jew shaped what he chose to foreground and what to suppress. The Hebrew that Pico encountered was in this sense always a Hebrew already mediated by Latin — a sacred language whose sacredness was disclosed through the translations of a translator who had his own reasons for the way he rendered it.
