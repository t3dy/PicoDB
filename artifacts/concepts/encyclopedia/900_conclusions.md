# The 900 Conclusions (*Conclusiones nongentae*, 1486)

## What the Text Is

The *Conclusiones nongentae* are the nine hundred theses that Giovanni Pico della Mirandola, count of Concord, published in Rome on 7 December 1486, at the age of twenty-three, as the material for a grand public debate he proposed to hold at the Vatican beginning no sooner than the Feast of the Epiphany, January 6 of the following year. The printed text carried no formal title—the theses were intended to be debated, not simply read—and the various names attached to it by tradition lack strong textual support; Farmer consistently refers to "the theses" or "the conclusions" without imposing a conventional title. Pico himself, in different contexts, called the propositions his *conclusiones*, *theses*, and *propositiones*. The text was printed by Eucharius Silber in Rome, following several intense months of composition, and was disseminated widely: copies were sent to all Italian universities, and Pico announced at the colophon that he would personally pay the travel expenses of any philosopher or theologian, "even from the ends of Italy," willing to come to Rome to debate him.

The scope of the work was immediately recognized as exceptional. Pico himself described it as covering "all the most ambiguous and controversial questions on which the principal schools battle," and at more than one point in the text he promises a discussion "of everything knowable" (*de omni re scibili*). The nine hundred theses include propositions in moral philosophy, logic, metaphysics, physics, epistemology, theology, astrology, numerology, natural magic, and Kabbalah. They range from technical questions about the figures of the syllogism to speculation about the date of the end of the world, from debates about the order of Aristotle's writings to the magical powers hidden in individual letters of the Hebrew alphabet. No other known fifteenth-century work covers so wide a range of traditions. It was no accident that this was also the first printed book to be banned universally by the church: on 4 August 1487, Pope Innocent VIII issued a bull condemning the text in its entirety, ordering all copies burned within three days and threatening excommunication for anyone who read, copied, printed, or heard the text read aloud. The condemnation effectively suppressed the work, and the *Conclusiones* survives today in very few copies of the 1486 *editio princeps*.

## Organization: Source Clusters and Structural Logic

The nine hundred theses are divided into two major sections whose organization is neither random nor merely encyclopedic but encodes a systematic philosophical and historical argument.

The first or **historical section** contains 402 theses (originally 400, with minor last-minute additions) distributed across twenty-eight subsections representing the opinions of what Pico called the *gentes* (nations of thinkers) and their *heresiarchae* (sect leaders). The term *heresiarch* carries deliberate religious connotations: the debate was explicitly modeled as a council (*concilium*) at which the pope and college of cardinals would preside as supreme judges, and the "nations" of thinkers would be summoned like the gentiles in the persons of the Magi, whose submission to Christ is commemorated on the Feast of the Epiphany—the date originally chosen to open the debate. The six nations of thinkers grouped in twenty-eight sections (both "perfect numbers" in the Pythagorean sense, composed of the sum of their factors) are arranged in reverse historical order as Pico understood it: the Latin scholastics appear first, followed by the Arabs, the Greeks, the Chaldeans, the Egyptians, and finally the Hebrew Kabbalist sages. This descending sequence from corrupt modernity toward primal wisdom is not a historical accident but a structural statement: it mirrors, as Farmer argues, the emanationist cosmology of Pico's "new philosophy," in which reality proceeds from divine unity through progressive multiplicity toward matter. The flow of wisdom through history runs in the opposite direction, from the unitary source of divine revelation through progressive corruption. The *Conclusiones* thus enact a reverse genealogy of thought.

Within the historical section, sources are paired and grouped for systematic purposes. Thomas Aquinas is juxtaposed to his master Albert the Great (whom Pico regarded as Thomas's superior); Giles of Rome is matched with his adversary Henry of Ghent; Averroes is opposed to Avicenna. The Latin scholastics arrive at the beginning in their most contentious and contradictory form; by the end of the historical section, in the theses on the Chaldean Oracles, on Mercury Trismegistus, on Orpheus, and on the Hebrew Kabbalists, the symbolic language of the text shifts from technically precise Latin scholasticism toward increasingly esoteric and numerologically charged formulations that only Pico himself, at the debate, would have been positioned to explain.

The second or **topical section** contains 498 theses (originally 500) presented *secundum opinionem propriam*—according to Pico's own opinion. There is no doubt that he endorsed all the conclusions in this section. This part of the text is itself divided into ten subsections (originally, apparently, to emphasize the Pythagorean "number of numbers"), covering Pico's reconciliative conclusions in theology, his "eighty philosophical conclusions dissenting from the common philosophy," his treatment of specific doctrines in natural philosophy and metaphysics, his mathematical or numerological conclusions, his magical theses, and finally two sets of Cabalistic conclusions. The first thesis of the topical section—literally and figuratively the central principle of Pico's new philosophy—proclaims the harmony of Plato and Aristotle; the final section contains seventy-two theses, corresponding to the seventy-two letters of one of the most secret names of God in the Cabala, outlining Pico's plan to convert the Jews by demonstrating, through Christian readings of the Kabbalah, that Moses had encoded Christological truths in the Torah from the beginning.

Numerological symbolism pervades both sections. The total of nine hundred theses was itself chosen deliberately. In a letter to Girolamo Benivieni written less than a month before publication, Pico explained that the theses had grown from seven hundred and threatened to reach a thousand, but it was proper to stop at nine hundred: "For if my doctrine of numbers is correct, this is the symbol of the excited soul turning back into itself through the frenzy of the muses." The five hundredth thesis in the text—which happens to be the first thesis of Pico's "paradoxical dogmatizing conclusions," the core of his *philosophia nova*—was positioned, Farmer argues, to embody the numerological symbol of eschatological return.

## The Disputation Format and Its Stakes

To understand what Pico proposed to do with these theses, it is essential to recognize that the *Conclusiones* were composed to be debated, not merely read. The public disputation was the primary intellectual institution through which scholastic philosophy was conducted, tested, and transmitted in the medieval and early modern university. In the most demanding form of disputation, the *quodlibetal* debate, a respondent (*respondens*) would defend a list of theses against attacking objectors (*arguentes* or *opponentes*) who could challenge their logical coherence, theological orthodoxy, or philosophical foundations. Pico's proposed debate was unprecedented in its scale—no earlier example of a debate approaching nine hundred theses is known—but it was continuous with, not discontinuous from, the scholastic tradition of public disputation.

As *respondens*, Pico would have been required to defend all nine hundred theses against whatever objections were raised. But his own declarations about what this would have meant are complex and sometimes contradictory. In the *Apology* for his aborted debate, composed in twenty hectic nights in 1487, Pico claimed that the first or historical section contained not only true opinions that he planned to defend but also "many impious doctrines of the old philosophers" that he undertook to dispute "in the manner of the academies," as a scholastic exercise and not as expressions of his personal views. This disclaimer, written as his relations with the church were becoming dangerous, allowed Innocent VIII to argue that the entire debate had been misconceived—that theses he never personally endorsed had been used as a pretext against him. Farmer regards the disclaimer as tactical and notes its inconsistency with the *Oration*, where the debate is presented as a fully public event rather than a "secret congress."

The debate's planned venue was itself a statement. The "apostolic senate"—before the college of cardinals, with the pope as supreme judge—was a spiritually charged setting. Pico referred to the gathering as a *concilium* (council) in texts written before his troubles with the church, invoking the symbolism of an ecumenical council at which the divisions of Christendom would be resolved. The date of the Epiphany, the feast celebrating the submission of the pagan *gentes* to Christ in the persons of the Magi, reinforced this eschatological framing. Farmer argues that Pico's Vatican debate was planned not merely as a philosophical exhibition but as a quasi-apocalyptic event intended to trigger the intellectual and spiritual reunification of mankind before or coincident with the Second Coming—a reading supported by scattered numerological hints in the theses themselves, including a hesitant Cabalistic calculation that places the end of the world in the year 2000.

## The Role of Magic Theses

Pico's magic theses appear primarily in the second or topical section of the *Conclusiones*, in the section titled "Seventy-six theses according to my own opinion on magic and Kabbalah" (the number varied slightly through last-minute revisions). These theses distinguished emphatically between natural magic (*magia naturalis*) and demonic magic or necromancy, a distinction Pico maintained consistently throughout his early works. Natural magic, as Pico defined it in the *Apology*, was "the practical part of natural science," the art of exploiting the sympathies and antipathies latent in the cosmic hierarchy—the correlative correspondences between celestial and terrestrial levels of reality—to produce effects that seemed miraculous but were in fact natural. The magus who operated through natural magic was not invoking demons but drawing on the same principle of cosmic correspondence that underlay Pico's entire metaphysical system: *omnia sunt in omnibus modo suo*.

The most important sources Pico cited as ancient magicians were Pythagoras, Plato, Zoroaster, Plotinus, and Roger Bacon—noticeably not Hermes Trismegistus, whose inclusion one would expect if Yates's account of a Ficinian-Hermetic magical synthesis were correct. Farmer observes that this omission is significant: Pico was explicitly distancing his magic from the astrological-talismanic tradition that Ficino endorsed in the *De vita coelitus comparanda*, a work written two and a half years after Pico published the *Conclusiones*. Indeed, Farmer reverses the traditional direction of influence, arguing that Ficino's magical synthesis was in part a response to Pico's prior formulation rather than its source.

Pico's magical theses are also inseparable from his Cabalistic theses, which form the final and climactic section of the *Conclusiones* as a whole. In the *Apology*, Pico claimed that he was "the first among the Latins" to mention Cabala, and he explicitly linked Cabalistic methods with natural magic, arguing that the techniques of gematria (numerical transformation of letters), anagram, and alphabetical combination that he drew from kabbalistic sources—particularly from the thirteenth-century Spanish kabbalist Abraham Abulafia—provided a means of uncovering the hidden correspondences between traditions that his syncretic method required. Pico's claim to have discovered magic in the Orphic Hymns was similarly a claim about the structural consonance of Orphic symbolism with his philosophical system, not about independent Hermetic magic.

## The Condemned Theses

In the event, the Vatican debate never took place. Following the publication of the *Conclusiones*, Innocent VIII appointed a high-level papal commission to investigate the text's orthodoxy. The commission identified thirteen theses as objectionable with varying degrees of harshness. Pico's spirited *Apology* defended these thirteen theses in detail and was itself promptly condemned. On 4 August 1487, Innocent's bull came down not on the thirteen theses alone but on the project as a whole, condemning the entire text for theses that "savored of heresy," for propositions "renovating the errors of pagan philosophers," for those "cherishing the deceits of the Jews," and above all for conclusions "promoting certain arts, disguising themselves as natural philosophy, harmful to the Catholic faith and humankind."

The thirteen condemned theses touched on several sensitive areas. Four of the five theological theses condemned by the commission bore on the Eucharist, where Pico's Neo-Platonized treatment of the substance-accident distinction threatened the orthodox doctrine of transubstantiation. The standard Thomistic account held that in the Eucharist the accidents of bread and wine persist without their substance; Pico's denial that accidents could exist independently of substance—grounded in his proportional ontology, which treated accidents as inferior images of substance—seemed to undercut this explanation. Pico proposed alternatives but his wording was tentative enough to attract condemnation. Two theses were condemned for their treatment of the Incarnation and the assumption of natures. Several more were condemned for magical and Cabalistic claims: a thesis asserting that no science demonstrates the divinity of Christ more effectively than magic and Kabbalah drew particular hostility. A thesis about the soul's union with God in contemplation was condemned for what the commission read as a denial of the distinction between the soul and the divine intellect.

The papal commission was not, however, the primary target of Innocent's fury. The bull condemned the whole *Conclusiones* as a systematic danger: propositions in the historical section that Pico had not personally endorsed, esoteric doctrines whose intelligibility was limited to specialists, and the entire framework of a syncretic debate that proposed to place Zoroaster and Averroes alongside Scripture as sources of theological truth. It was the breadth and ambition of the project, more than the specific contents of any thirteen theses, that provoked an unprecedented response. This was the first broad inquisitional action in the history of printing—as the bibliographer Hirsch observed, it heralded the later *indices librorum prohibitorum*—and it was issued in the same year that Innocent ordered the first universal prepublication review of printed books.

## Relationship to the Oration

The *Oration*—traditionally but misleadingly titled *De hominis dignitate*—was composed by Pico as a preface to the Roman debate. Its famous opening meditation on the indeterminacy of human nature, in which God places Adam at the center of the world with the freedom to shape himself as he chooses, is only the first movement of a text whose primary goal was to defend the value of philosophy in general and Pico's philosophical project in particular. The section that has received the most scholarly attention—on human dignity and the freedom of the will—is in fact subsidiary to the *Oration*'s central concern, which is to announce the structure and goals of the debate itself.

In the *Oration* Pico formally introduces the second or topical section of the *Conclusiones*, promising to bring forward in "seventy-two new physical and metaphysical doctrines" a philosophy capable of "resolving every proposed question on natural and divine things by a far different method than we are taught by that philosophy read in the schools." He then surveys the content of the historical section, tracing the history of wisdom from the Hebrews and Chaldeans through the Greeks to the Latins—a reverse genealogy corresponding to the organization of the *Conclusiones* themselves—and concludes with a lengthy meditation on the soul's ascent through philosophy toward mystical union with God that provides the religious and eschatological frame for the entire project.

The title *De hominis dignitate* first appeared not in Pico's lifetime but in a corrupt 1504 Strasbourg reprint of his collected works, in an edition that—like all early collected editions—omitted the *Conclusiones* entirely, decontextualizing the *Oration* and allowing it to be read as a general humanist manifesto rather than as the introduction to a specific and systematic philosophical program. Farmer observes that the tradition of reading the *Oration* in isolation from the *Conclusiones* has produced a romantic image of Pico as the prophet of human autonomy that is almost the inverse of what the text actually argues. The *Oration*'s meditation on human freedom is explicitly subordinated to the doctrine of divine grace and the need for philosophical preparation: freedom matters because it opens the possibility of ascending the ladder of knowledge toward mystical union, not because it is an end in itself.

## Farmer's Source-Ecology Methodology

The most sustained methodological contribution of Farmer's *Syncretism in the West* is his demonstration that the *Conclusiones* must be read structurally—in terms of the organizational logic that connects sections and source clusters—rather than atomistically, thesis by thesis. Farmer uses several interlocking analytic tools.

The first is attention to the **collation logic** of the text: the way theses from different source clusters are designed to be brought together at Rome and harmonized or opposed. The *occulta concatenatio* that Pico mentioned in the *Apology* refers to the network of implicit cross-references and structural correspondences that connect theses across the text. Without understanding this logic, it is impossible to determine what Pico personally endorsed in the historical section, where contradictory theses from conflicting authorities appear side by side. Farmer's edition provides the first edition designed to facilitate the reconstruction of these cross-references.

The second is attention to the **numerological architecture** of the text—the deliberate choice of totals, the placement of significant theses at numerologically charged positions, the grouping of source sections in quantities that carry symbolic meaning—as evidence for the seriousness with which Pico regarded the text as a structured whole rather than a miscellaneous collection.

The third is what Farmer calls the **correlative key**: the recognition that Pico's reconciliative method is not eclectic tolerance but the systematic application of the principle *omnia sunt in omnibus modo suo* to all his sources. Apparent conflicts between traditions are resolved by assigning them to different levels of a cosmological hierarchy; the traditions do not speak for themselves but are translated into the vocabulary of a prior correlative metaphysics. This means that studying what Pico "took from" any given source tradition (kabbalism, Orphism, Neoplatonism, Arabic philosophy) in isolation yields a distorted picture, because it mistakes the raw material of Pico's synthesis for its product.

The fourth is historical contextualization of the **source clusters themselves** within the structure of the debate. Farmer demonstrates, for example, that the kabbalistic *sefirot* and the Neo-Platonic henads function as parallel symbolic series in the *Conclusiones*—both are used by Pico as analogical vocabularies for levels of cosmic emanation—and that understanding Pico's Cabalistic theses requires simultaneous attention to the henad theses, which have been almost entirely neglected in the scholarship.

## The *Conclusiones* in the History of Scholarship

Five centuries of scholarship on the *Conclusiones* have been hampered by three interlocking problems: the rarity and corruption of the text, the decontextualization produced by its exclusion from all early collected editions, and the tendency to read isolated sections (particularly the magical and Cabalistic theses) without reference to the structural whole. The romantic tradition initiated by Burckhardt, which claimed the *Oration* as the "manifesto of the Renaissance," extended this decontextualization by severing Pico's preface from the theses it introduced. Scholars like Cassirer, Garin, and Kristeller produced influential readings of Pico's thought from the *Oration* and selected other works while leaving the *Conclusiones* largely unstudied.

Frances Yates's *Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition* (1964) restored the *Conclusiones* to prominence by arguing for their centrality to a Renaissance Hermetic-magical revival, but did so by treating the magical and Cabalistic theses in isolation from the scholastic and philosophical theses that compose the bulk of the text. Kieszkowski's 1973 edition provided the first modern critical text but was hampered by textual corruption and methodological limitations. Farmer's 1998 edition marked the first serious attempt to read the *Conclusiones* as a whole, attending both to the structure of the text and to its relationship to Pico's other works, his library, and the intellectual culture of his circle.

Subsequent scholarship has continued to explore individual aspects of the *Conclusiones*—the Cabalistic theses (Wirszubski, Busi), the magical theses (Copenhaver), the relationship to Ficino and the Platonic tradition (Allen)—while Farmer's structural reading remains the unavoidable methodological starting point for any attempt to engage with the text as a whole.
