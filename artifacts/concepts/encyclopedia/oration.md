# Oration on the Dignity of Man

## What the Oration Actually Is

The Oration on the Dignity of Man is not a freestanding philosophical treatise and was not conceived as one. It is a formal introductory speech — technically a prolusion, in the humanist genre of academic prefaces — composed by Giovanni Pico della Mirandola in the autumn of 1486 to introduce his proposed public disputation of the 900 Conclusions (Conclusiones nongentae) in Rome. Pico wrote it in four stages between September 1486 and his arrival in Rome in late November of that year, timing that places it in the midst of his frantic production of the Conclusions themselves. The disputation was intended to take place before the pope and assembled cardinals on the feast of Epiphany (January 6, 1487), a date Pico chose with obvious theological symbolism: the day when the Magi submitted to the newborn Christ, the moment when the pagan world recognized its Christian redeemer. The Roman disputation was suppressed by papal commission before it could occur. Pico never delivered the speech.

The title "Oration on the Dignity of Man" was not given by Pico. He gave it no title at all. The subtitle de hominis dignitate was attached retrospectively in the Strasbourg edition of 1504, referring to the apparent topic of the opening section of the speech. Pier Cesare Bori, Michael Papio, and Brian Copenhaver have all noted that the phrase "dignity of man" does not organize the work as a whole: as Copenhaver observes, the Oration is in crucial respects "silent about human dignity" in any sense that the modern reader would recognize (Copenhaver, Magic and the Dignity of Man, 2019, p. 158). The work was not included in the first 1496 edition of Pico's collected works assembled by his nephew Gianfrancesco — who, according to Copenhaver, regarded it as "an embarrassing piece of juvenilia" (2011, p. 186) — and appeared only in the third posthumous volume of the second and later editions, treated as ancillary material rather than a centrepiece.

The Oration has a structural complexity that the "dignity of man" title obscures. It falls into at least two major sections. The first introduces Pico's framework for the place of the human being in the created order, centered on the figure of Adam as an indeterminate image. The second, considerably longer section is an encomium on philosophy broadly conceived — on the variety of philosophical traditions, on the method of disputation (explicitly following the Parisian style of academic debate), and on the value of Kabbalah and natural magic as paths toward wisdom. In May 1487, when Pico needed to produce a rapid defence of his condemned theses, he cannibalized a substantial portion of the Oration for the proemium of his Apology (Apologia tredecim quaestionum), demonstrating the provisional, instrumental status of the text within his larger project.

## Adam at the Centre of the World: The Famous Passage

The opening of the Oration's first section contains the passage that has made the work famous, and that has been most consistently misread. Pico begins not with Adam but with a series of ancient authorities who have lauded the dignity or excellence of humankind: the Saracen Abdala, Hermes Trismegistus, the prophet David. He then introduces his own distinctive contribution: the reason why humans, in Pico's view, deserve to be regarded as "the most fortunate of living things and, consequently, the most worthy of all admiration" (Oration, 2012, 109) is not one that earlier thinkers have articulated. The answer appears in the form of a divine address to Adam at the moment of creation.

God speaks: "We have made you neither of heavenly nor of earthly stuff, neither mortal nor immortal, so that with free choice and dignity, as though the maker and molder of yourself, you could fashion yourself in whatever form you prefer. You can degenerate into the lower, brutish forms of life. You can, by your own choice, rise to the higher forms, which are divine" (Oration, 2012, 116-17, sections 22-23; cf. Pico, Heptaplus 1965, pp. 224-25 for a near-identical version). The Latin reads: "tui ipsius quasi arbitrarius honorariusque plastes et fctor, in quam malueris tute formam effingas." Adam is described as "quasi" — as if — the free and honorary moulder and sculptor (plastes et fctor) of himself. The conditional quality of the simile matters.

Two features of this speech deserve emphasis. First, the indeterminacy of Adam's position in the created order is for Pico not a general statement about human nature in the abstract but a specific metaphysical claim about Adam's unusual ontological status. Every other creature was assigned a fixed seat and specific properties within the hierarchy of creation. Adam alone was given no fixed nature, placed "at the centre of the world" (in mundi positum centro) not as an affirmation of centrality but as a statement of ontological liminality — neither wholly heavenly nor earthly, neither mortal nor immortal. As Sophia Howlett describes it, Adam is "an indeterminate image" (Howlett 2021, p. 175), a being "called to become God, Who is beyond all representation." The language of the "indiscretae opus imaginis" — the work of an undifferentiated image — appears in the Oration at section 18, and its Kabbalistic resonance with Adam Kadmon, the primordial human who maps onto the Tree of Life, is not accidental.

Second, and more critically, the mutability Pico attributes to Adam is not a capacity for self-invention in any modern liberal sense. It is not the freedom to construct one's identity in defiance of fixed essences. Adam's freedom operates within a hierarchical metaphysical framework — a universe of angelic intelligences, celestial spheres, and elemental nature — and the full weight of the Oration's second section is devoted to explaining how that freedom, properly directed through philosophy, Kabbalah, and prayer, must be used to ascend through that hierarchy rather than to descend into it. The downward possibility — degeneration into brutish forms — is real and present in Pico's account, figured through the chameleon and Proteus, both of which Pico knew carried negative connotations (the chameleon was associated with inconstancy in the Nicomachean Ethics; Proteus with shapeshifting deception). The upward possibility, not unlimited freedom to become anything, is what he celebrates.

## What the Oration Is Not: Dissolving the Modern Myth

The identification of the Oration with the announcement of Renaissance individualism, proto-Enlightenment humanism, and modern selfhood has a specific and traceable genealogy. It begins with Jacob Burckhardt's anecdotal treatment of Pico in The Civilization of the Renaissance in Italy (1860), which helped establish Pico as an exemplary figure of Renaissance "all-sidedness" and connected him to a general cultural claim about the emergence of the individual in Italian Renaissance culture. The philosophical apparatus was supplied primarily by Ernst Cassirer, whose essay on Pico in The Individual and the Cosmos in Renaissance Philosophy (1927) read the Oration as a celebration of the infinite plasticity of human nature and the boundless freedom of human self-creation — a reading that found natural allies in mid-century existentialism and in the broader post-war interest in human dignity.

The decisive consolidation of this reading in the English-speaking scholarly tradition came through Eugenio Garin, whose influential Italian studies of Pico from the 1930s onward, translated and disseminated widely from the 1960s, placed the Oration at the centre of Pico's achievement and read it as the decisive philosophical statement of Renaissance humanism. Garin's approach — which Brian Copenhaver characterizes as framing Pico's perceptions of "man as the self-fashioner" in terms that "reflect the rising interest of the times in existentialism" (Copenhaver 2019, p. 143) — also led Garin to downplay Kabbalah and to subordinate the Conclusions to the Oration. The effect was to produce a Pico palatable to secular modernity: a figure who had shed his theological context and emerged as a forerunner of humanist autonomy.

Several decades of revisionary scholarship have worked to dismantle this construction. The crucial recent intervention is Brian Copenhaver's Magic and the Dignity of Man (Harvard University Press, 2019), which argues with comprehensive philological and philosophical documentation that the identification of Pico with modern individualism or proto-liberal dignity is a historiographical artifact without textual foundation. Copenhaver demonstrates that the concept of "dignity" operative in the Oration is not the modern Kantian concept, and that Pico "could say nothing about it" in the modern sense "because Kant invented the modern concept" (Copenhaver 2019, p. 155). The Oration is instead, as Copenhaver argues, "a mystic's manifesto for ascent, by way of magic and Kabbalah, to self-annihilating union with God" — a formulation quoted with approval by Howlett (2021, p. 192). Copenhaver further attributes the Burckhardtian reading specifically to Burckhardt's anecdotes about Pico having "resulted in scholars associating Pico's work with the dignity of man as a humanist ideal and with the struggle against superstition as a step toward modernity" (Copenhaver 2019, p. 90).

The argument for restoring the Conclusions to primacy — at the expense of the Oration — is not merely academic. Avner Dulles, writing as early as 1941, called the Conclusions "Pico's most inclusive philosophic work" and the Oration "one of the least important in content" (Dulles 1941, pp. 13, 15). Francesco Borghesi, following Bausi's genre analysis, has argued that the Oration "should be classified as belonging to the literary genre of the humanistic prolusiones" (Borghesi 2012, p. 63) — a formal introductory speech, not a philosophical summa. The result of these converging arguments is that the text we have been calling the founding document of Renaissance humanism is better understood as a brilliant but provisional performance piece, composed in weeks, never delivered, unentitled by its author, and designed to introduce a philosophical project of which it represents only the threshold.

## The Actual Content of the Oration: Philosophy, Kabbalah, and the Path to Wisdom

Having established Adam's unique ontological position, Pico moves in the second and longer section of the Oration to a defense of the philosophical method and a survey of the traditions he intends to bring into concordance at the Rome disputation. This section constitutes the bulk of the text and contains its real intellectual substance.

Pico's defense of philosophy is simultaneously personal and structural. He defends the contemplative life against the charge that philosophy offers no worldly advantage — "no market for philosophers, no remuneration given to them" (Oration 2012, 186-87) — distinguishing the true philosopher, a gentleman who pursues wisdom for its own sake, from the mercenary sophist. This is partly self-fashioning (Pico's letter to Andrea Corneo the same year makes the same argument in an explicitly autobiographical key), but it is also a claim about the proper relationship between knowledge and its pursuit. Philosophy for Pico is not instrumentally useful in the ordinary sense; it is a preparation for ascent.

The ascent Pico outlines in the Oration follows a tripartite Pseudo-Dionysian schema: purgation, illumination, and perfection (purgatio, illuminatio, perfectio). The starting point is moral philosophy, understood as the cleansing of the soul from the disorder of the passions. Without this catharsis, the intellect cannot ascend. The second stage is dialectics and natural philosophy, through which the soul learns to exercise its rational faculties in progressively less material domains. The third stage is theology, approached through "the Platonic frenzies" — those modes of divine madness Pico enumerates as Socratic, prophetic, poetic, and erotic — through which the soul, purified and illuminated, may be drawn upward by God's grace: "Let us be led away, fathers, let us be led away by the Socratic frenzies that will so lift us beyond our minds as to put our mind and ourselves in God!" (Oration 2012, section 164 in Howlett's numbering).

Within this framework, the Oration assigns critical roles to both Kabbalah and natural magic — roles that are inseparable from Pico's core philosophical program and that distinguish his ascent sharply from the more purely Platonic or Hermetic programs of his contemporaries. Pico offers an extensive defense of natural magic (magia naturalis or white magic), carefully distinguished from demonic or "goetic" magic, which he condemns as "a thing to be abhorred" (Oration section 72). Natural magic, properly understood, is not the manipulation of supernatural powers but "the deepest contemplation of the most secret things and ultimately the knowledge of all nature" (Oration section 73). The magus is a reader of the universe, perceiving the harmonies and sympathies that bind different levels of creation together and drawing on those sympathies as a servant of nature rather than its master. Pico draws on Porphyry's etymology of magus as "interpreter and worshipper of the divine" to establish the credentials of this practice.

More distinctively, Pico argues in the Oration that Kabbalah is the highest and most powerful form of this ascent. He presents Kabbalah as the oral tradition given by God to Moses on Sinai alongside the written Torah — a secret mystical teaching preserved across generations and now available, through translation, to Christian philosophers. He argues that Kabbalah provides confirmation of Christian theological truths through the Hebrew sages' own principles, and that in the hierarchy of wisdom, the Kabbalistic path to God surpasses even the highest natural magic. In the Conclusions, Pico formalizes this claim: "Just as true astrology teaches us to read in the book of God, so Kabbalah teaches us to read in the book of the Law" (Conclusions 11 > 72, cited Wirszubski 1989). The Oration's survey of philosophical traditions ends not with Plato or Aristotle but with this Kabbalistic summit.

## The Free Will of the Oration: Metaphysical Potentiality, Not Liberal Self-Creation

The philosophical content of the Oration's account of free will is remote from modern liberal conceptions of autonomy. Pico's Adam possesses liberum arbitrium — free choice — but this freedom is not autonomous self-determination. It is a metaphysical gift, a form of ontological openness that no other creature in the created hierarchy possesses, granted by God through an act of divine volition and requiring God's grace to actualize in the direction of ascent. Pico is explicit in Heptaplus that "our nature is such that we cannot go in a circle and come back upon ourselves, but we can be moved in a circle and brought back to God by the motive power of grace" (Heptaplus, 1965, p. 152). The soul is constitutively dependent on divine illumination: "we cannot go, but can be drawn" (Heptaplus, p. 151). Freedom of will in Pico is the latitude to choose one's direction within a hierarchical metaphysical order, not the power to transcend or construct that order.

Howlett's formulation is precise: "our freedom of will" is "connected with God's grace" and operates through "the motive power of grace," meaning that human dignity in the Oration is not a capacity for self-sufficiency but for receptivity (Howlett 2021, pp. 174-75). This is the dignity not of the Enlightenment subject who legislates moral law for itself but of the mystic who, by the proper ordering of the soul's faculties — senses subordinated to reason, reason to intelligence, intelligence open to divine illumination — becomes capable of being lifted toward God. The "indeterminate image" of the Oration is indeterminate not because humans are free to become whatever they wish but because humans, uniquely among created beings, can participate in any level of the cosmic hierarchy, ascending or descending according to whether the life of the soul is directed by sense, reason, or intelligence.

The Oration's account of the human soul maps onto Pico's broader metaphysics. He describes a tripartite soul (intelligence, reason, senses) corresponding to the three worlds of his Platonic-Kabbalistic cosmology: the intelligible or angelic world, the celestial world, and the natural world. Our intelligence participates in the angelic mind. Our reason is peculiar to humans. Our senses align us with the animal world. The ascent proceeds from the governance of senses by reason, and from reason by intelligence, at which point the soul may be illuminated by the divine Active Intellect — understood by Pico through a convergence of Aristotelian, Averroist, and Kabbalistic traditions — and drawn toward union with the divine presence in the Kabbalistic hierarchy of the sefirot, beginning with Binah (understanding) and ascending potentially to Keter (the Crown).

The "seraph" and "cherub" figures that appear in the famous passage on ascending to the angelic life are not rhetorical ornament. The Seraph burns with caritas; the Cherub shines with intelligence; the Throne stands in steadfast judgment (Oration section 54). Each represents a mode of ascent and a type of proximity to God. To become seraphic is to be consumed by the fire of love to the point of self-annihilation: "He who is a Seraph, that is, a lover, is in God, and God is in him; indeed, he and God are one" (Oration section 61). This union is not the modern self finding its authentic essence but the dissolution of the individual self into union with the divine — the mors osculi, the "kiss of death" (mitat neshikah in Hebrew; binsica in Pico's Italian) drawn from Maimonides and the Talmudic tradition, which Pico discusses at length in the Commentary on Benivieni and invokes in the Conclusions as the supreme goal of the Kabbalist's journey.

## The Oration in Pico's Broader Works

The ideas of the Oration are not unique to it. Pico's description of Adam's indeterminate nature appears in almost identical form in Heptaplus (1489), the commentary on the creation narrative of Genesis, in which God addresses Adam: "Neither a fixed abode nor a form that is thine alone nor any function peculiar to thyself have we given thee, Adam, to the end that according to thy longing and according to thy judgment thou mayest have and possess what abode, what form, and what functions thou thyself shalt desire" (Heptaplus, 1965, pp. 224-25). The repetition — nearly verbatim — between the Oration and Heptaplus demonstrates that this is not a passing rhetorical gesture but a considered metaphysical position developed across Pico's major works. The same tripartite structure of the human soul, the same Pseudo-Dionysian schema of purgation and ascent, and the same emphasis on grace as the enabling condition of henosis appear consistently in the Conclusions, the Commentary on Benivieni, and Heptaplus.

The Oration is inseparable from the Conclusions as its structural context. The Conclusions are organized historically, moving backward in time through the strata of philosophical tradition — from medieval scholastics through Platonic and Aristotelian sources, through Orphic, Zoroastrian, and Hermetic materials, and finally through the 71 Kabbalistic conclusions that crown the project — to reveal the deep structure of truth on which Pico's concordance rests. The Oration provides the phenomenological and rhetorical introduction to this journey: it explains why the human being can and should attempt it, what the goal is, and through what traditions the ascent proceeds. Without the Conclusions, the Oration is a preamble to nothing. The modern reading that treats the Oration as complete in itself, and the Conclusions as secondary scaffolding, exactly reverses Pico's own priorities.

Howlett's Re-evaluating Pico is particularly useful for understanding the Aristotelian dimensions of the ascent the Oration describes. Where Ficino's account of henosis is primarily Platonic and affective, Pico's is also noetic: the convergence of the human intellect with the Aristotelian divine Active Intellect. Pico's Paduan formation, especially his study with Elijah del Medigo (a Cretan Jewish Averroist who gave Pico access to Maimonides and to Jewish-Aristotelian traditions in which the Active Intellect was already central to the theory of mystical ascent), shaped an account of union with God that is simultaneously philosophical and experiential, rational and ecstatic. The Oration's defense of philosophy as the necessary preparation for Kabbalistic and theological ascent is not a humanist gesture toward secular learning but the Aristotelian component of a rigorous three-stage program in which reason must be fully exercised before it can be transcended.

## The Oration and the Historiography of Renaissance Studies

The modern myth of the Oration as the founding document of Renaissance humanism has consequences beyond Pico scholarship. Jacob Burckhardt's periodization, which made the Italian Renaissance the cradle of the individual, needed exemplary figures; Pico, distilled to the Adam passage of the Oration, served this function admirably. Cassirer's neo-Kantian reading of the Oration as an affirmation of human freedom as infinite self-determination reinforced this narrative with philosophical prestige. Garin's nationalist-inflected promotion of Pico as the embodiment of Italian Renaissance philosophy — shaped partly by the cultural politics of mid-century Italy and by what Copenhaver describes as the influence of Giovanni Gentile's earlier "ideology of dignity and humanism" (Copenhaver 2019, p. 109) — gave the reading Italian scholarly authority. The result was a century-long consensus that survived multiple empirical objections because it served cultural and philosophical functions transcending any particular reading of the text.

Copenhaver's Magic and the Dignity of Man represents the most systematic demolition of this myth, arguing from philology, intellectual history, and close reading that "Dignity of Man" is an anachronistic imposition on a text occupied with something else entirely: the magical, Kabbalistic, and philosophical ascent of an exceptionally prepared individual toward union with the divine. The argument has been elaborated by Howlett, who gives particular weight to the Aristotelian dimensions that other revisionary accounts have sometimes underemphasized, and who situates the reception history of the Oration within the broader construction and subsequent deconstruction of the "Platonic Academy" myth that had for so long governed Pico scholarship.

The Oration remains, despite all this, one of the most intellectually rich and rhetorically brilliant texts of the Italian Renaissance. Its opening vision of Adam as the freely self-forming image of divinity is genuinely arresting, and its survey of the entire history of philosophy as a path toward religious experience is ambitious in a way that few Renaissance texts match. What it is not, and has never been, is an announcement of modern individualism or a declaration of human autonomy in defiance of metaphysical hierarchy. It is a preface to a disputed list of 900 propositions, composed in the autumn of 1486 by a twenty-three-year-old nobleman who believed he had found the keys to the universe in the convergence of Aristotle, Plato, and the Kabbalah of Moses.
