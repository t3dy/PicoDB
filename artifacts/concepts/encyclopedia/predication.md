Predication (*praedicatio*, κατηγόρησις) designates the logical operation of saying something of something: the act by which a subject receives a term in a judgment, and that term is affirmed to belong to it in some determinate way. As a technical concept it has roots in Aristotle's *Categories* and *Topics*, passes through the medieval Latin scholastic tradition in the works of Aquinas, Scotus, and Henry of Ghent, and arrives at the centre of Pico's *De ente et uno* as the key problem in his attempt to reconcile Plato and Aristotle on the question of whether Being (*ens*) or Unity (*unum*) is the highest divine name. To understand what Pico is doing in that short, dense treatise — and why it matters for his broader program of concordance — requires following the logical and theological problems of predication through several layers of inherited tradition.

Aristotle established in the *Categories* that being (*ens*) is not itself a genus. The ten categories — substance, quantity, quality, relation, place, time, position, state, action, passion — are the ten fundamental ways in which things can be. But being is not an eleventh category above them, because if it were, there would have to be a single, univocal sense in which everything that exists shares in being, and Aristotle argues in the *Metaphysics* that this is precisely what does not hold. Being is said in many ways (*pollachōs legetai*, as Aristotle puts it, in the formulation that opens the fourth book of the *Metaphysics*): a substance "is" in a different and primary sense from an accident; a quality "is" in a dependent way that presupposes the being of the substance it qualifies. This doctrine of the multivocity or analogy of being is the cornerstone of Aristotle's metaphysics and the source of his persistent disagreement with the Platonists, who were prepared to posit Being (or the One) as a genuine universal above the categories. Aristotle, by contrast, holds that unity (*unum*) converts with being (*ens*): each thing is one insofar as it is, and being insofar as it is one. Being and Unity are coextensive — *convertibilia* — because both simply follow the thing as such, without adding any further determination. This is the Aristotelian basis for what the medievals would call the *transcendentals*: being, unity, truth, and goodness are not genera but properties that accompany every being precisely as a being, convertible with each other though not synonymous.

The theological problem of predication inherits this framework and intensifies it. If being (*ens*) is said in many ways, how can it be said of God? The *via negativa* or apophatic tradition — associated with Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, whose *Mystical Theology* was a major source for Proclus and through him for the Latin tradition — holds that God transcends all affirmative predication: no name, no concept, no category can truly apply to God, because God exceeds every limitation that any predicate implies. God is not-being, not-one, not-good in the sense that these terms, as human minds employ them, fail to reach the divine nature. Thomas Aquinas's response to this challenge — the doctrine of analogical predication — became the canonical scholastic solution. Aquinas argues that terms like "good," "being," and "wise" can be predicated of God and creatures neither *univocally* (in exactly the same sense) nor *equivocally* (in entirely different senses) but *analogically*: the term applies to God and to creatures in a proportional relation, where God has the perfection named by the term in an eminent and incomprehensibly higher degree than any creature. God is *good* in the analogical sense: not the same goodness we find in creatures, but the source and measure of all creaturely goodness, which is a participated likeness of the divine original. Aquinas also distinguishes between terms predicated by way of causality (*via causalitatis*), by way of negation (*via negationis*), and by way of eminence (*via eminentiae*), understanding all three as jointly constituting responsible theological language about God.

The Neoplatonic tradition Pico encountered through Ficino's translations complicated this picture substantially. For Proclus, followed in different ways by Pseudo-Dionysius and Plotinus, the One is strictly prior to Being: the One is the cause and source of all being, but it is not itself a being. Being (*ousia*) is already a kind of multiplicity — to be is to be something determinate, and determination involves a kind of limitation that the absolutely simple One transcends. The Neoplatonic henads (the divine unities just below the One in Proclus's elaborate metaphysical hierarchy) participate in the One before they participate in Being. This priority of the One over Being was developed by the Florentine Platonists, and particularly by Ficino in his *Platonic Theology*, as a way of thinking about God's transcendence: God, identified with the One, surpasses even Being and even Unity in their finite creaturely forms. For Ficino, this Platonic theology was not in conflict with Christian theology but its philosophical articulation; the God who transcends Being in Neoplatonic metaphysics is the same God who transcends all human understanding in the apophatic tradition of Pseudo-Dionysius.

Pico's *De ente et uno* was written in 1492 at the request of his friend Angelo Poliziano, to settle a dispute that had arisen between Poliziano and Lorenzo de' Medici about how to understand the Aristotelian and Platonic positions on being and unity. The treatise is described in Pico's preface as a preliminary instalment of the larger *Concord of Plato and Aristotle* that he never lived to complete. The opening claim of the *De ente et uno* is that Aristotle explicitly teaches the convertibility of being and unity — "unity and being are convertible and reciprocal," as the Aristotelian tradition held — and that Plato, rightly read, is in agreement with Aristotle on this point. The apparent priority of the One over Being in Platonism, Pico argues, is a distortion introduced by the Neoplatonists (chiefly Plotinus and Proclus), not a genuine Platonic doctrine. Plato's *Parmenides*, which the Neoplatonists treated as the supreme theological text establishing the absolute simplicity and ineffability of the One, is actually, Pico insists, a dialectical exercise, "nothing else than a sort of exercise in dialectic," without strict affirmative content that could establish any doctrine about the One's priority to Being.

The predication problem enters the *De ente et uno* at its most technically demanding point: the question of how the terms "being" and "unity" can be truly predicated of God without either reducing God to the level of finite beings or rendering the terms entirely meaningless when applied to the divine. Pico's solution turns on a distinction between *nomina concreta* (concrete names) and *nomina abstracta* (abstract names). A concrete name like "being" (*ens*) or "one" (*unum*) names a subject as possessing a certain property without abstracting that property entirely from the subject. An abstract name like "being-itself" (*ipsum esse*) or "unity-itself" (*ipsa unitas*) names the property in its pure abstraction. When we predicate concrete names of God — when we say that God *is* (*est*) or that God *is one* (*unus est*) — we are not predicating these terms in the same sense that they apply to finite beings. God *is* in the eminent mode that is proper to the divine nature, which exceeds any finite mode of being. Pico holds, following the Thomistic tradition but sharpening its application, that the convertibility of being and unity is not undermined by God's transcendence but rather supremely confirmed by it: in God, Being and Unity are not merely coextensive but identical, since in the divine simplicity there is no distinction between the subject that is and the being that it has, between the substance that is one and the unity that it exemplifies. God is not *a* being among beings but *Being Itself*, and precisely this identity of Being and Unity in God grounds the convertibility that the Aristotelian tradition articulated at the creaturely level.

Edelheit's reconstruction of Pico's scholastic formation is essential here. His work (*A Philosopher at the Crossroads*, 2022) documents in granular detail how Pico's philosophical vocabulary and argumentative strategies in the *De ente et uno* were shaped by his study of Thomas Aquinas, Duns Scotus, Henry of Ghent, and Giles of Rome during his years at Padua, Pavia, and Paris. The scholastic debate about whether being is univocal (the position associated with Scotus, who argued that being must mean the same thing when applied to God and creatures if theological language is to be intelligible) or analogical (the position associated with Aquinas, who argued that univocity would collapse the distinction between Creator and creature) runs through Pico's *De ente et uno* without ever being explicitly foregrounded, because Pico presupposes familiarity with these debates among his interlocutors. Edelheit argues that Pico's engagement with the scholastic tradition was not a superficial ornament or a quarry for quotations but a genuine philosophical formation that gave him the conceptual tools without which the *De ente et uno*'s arguments cannot be properly reconstructed (Edelheit 2022, 214–73).

The *900 Conclusions* contain a set of theses under the heading "according to his own opinion" (*secundum opinionem propriam*) that touches on the predication question from a different angle. Pico's own theses propose a "new philosophy" (*philosophia nova*) built on a correlative or "fractal" model of reality, in which the structures of the microcosm mirror those of the macrocosm at every level. The predication question is implicit here: if Being and Unity are truly convertible throughout the hierarchy of reality, from God down to prime matter, then the terms by which we speak of God's being and unity and goodness must be related — analogically or participatively — to the terms by which we speak of these properties in creatures. The *occulta concatenatio* or "hidden connection" that Pico said linked all his theses and that the papal commission failed to notice is partly, on this reading, a systematic account of how predication functions across the levels of a hierarchically ordered reality.

The theological stakes of the predication dispute become clearest in Pico's engagement with the Neoplatonic *via negativa*. The Pseudo-Dionysian tradition, transmitted through Eriugena and the medieval mystical tradition, argued that God surpasses all affirmative predication because all affirmative predicates imply limitation: to say that God is *good* in the positive sense is to imply that God's goodness is a kind of goodness, a specification within the genus of goodness, which is precisely what divine transcendence excludes. On this view, the most adequate divine names are negative ones: God is the In-finite, the Un-created, the Im-mutable, the In-comprehensible. Pico does not simply reject this tradition — he invokes it as a counterweight to the anthropomorphism that threatens affirmative theology — but he resists the conclusion that God is simply beyond all predication. His *De ente et uno* argues instead that both Being and Unity, when predicated of God through the concrete names *ens* and *unum*, refer not to finite modes of being and unity but to the unlimited fullness from which all finite being and unity are derived. The analogy is not an analogy of proportion only (God has more being than creatures) but an analogy of attribution (creatures have being and unity only insofar as they participate in and are referred to the divine Being and Unity as their source).

Among the *900 Conclusions*, the theses drawn from Thomas Aquinas and the theses drawn from Proclus stand in productive tension on this question. The Thomistic theses affirm the convertibility of the transcendentals and the analogical character of their predication across the levels of being. The Procline theses affirm the radical simplicity of the One and its priority to any determinate being. Pico's claim — which he begins to defend in the *De ente et uno* and was developing toward a complete account in the *Concord of Plato and Aristotle* — is that these are not contradictory positions when each is read in its proper philosophical context. The Procline priority of the One is a claim about the absolute simplicity of the divine source; the Thomistic convertibility of being and unity is a claim about the structure of created reality as it participates in that source. Both claims, on Pico's reading, presuppose the same fundamental insight: that the naming of God requires a form of predication that is neither simply affirmative nor simply negative but operates through the analogical or participative relation between the finite and the infinite.

What Pico was attempting in the *De ente et uno*, and what he projected across the larger *Concord of Plato and Aristotle*, was a resolution of the deepest predication problem in Christian Neoplatonism: how to speak truly of God without either domesticating God within the categories of finite thought or reducing theological language to silence. The treatise was interrupted by his early death at thirty-one, but the *De ente et uno* as it survives offers a precise and technically sophisticated contribution to a debate that runs from Aristotle's *Categories* through Aquinas's *Summa Theologiae* to the Florentine Neoplatonic controversies of the 1480s and 1490s. Its predication doctrine — centered on the distinction between concrete and abstract names, the convertibility of the transcendentals, and analogical reference to the divine — represents Pico's most technically accomplished philosophical achievement, and the one most deeply embedded in the scholastic tradition that Edelheit's work has done most to illuminate.
