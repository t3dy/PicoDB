# The Apology (Apologia tredecim quaestionum) of Pico della Mirandola

The *Apologia tredecim quaestionum* — the Apology of Thirteen Questions — is the second book that Giovanni Pico della Mirandola published in his lifetime, printed in Rome in 1487, and it is, as Brian Copenhaver has argued in *Pico della Mirandola on Trial: Heresy, Freedom, and Philosophy* (2022), the most important and most neglected of his works. Where the speech known as the *Oration on the Dignity of Man* has carried his posthumous reputation and given rise to the myth of Pico as a herald of modernity and liberal individualism, the *Apology* shows him as he actually was: a scholastic theologian and philosopher, trained in the disputational methods of Paris and Padua, fighting for his life before a papal tribunal that found his published theses offensive, scandalous, and potentially heretical. The *Apology* is not a philosophical meditation or a humanist essay; it is a juridical-theological defense of thirteen condemned propositions, written in haste, under threat, and in the combative Latin of the medieval schools. As Copenhaver observes, "the defendant in the Apology made his case openly in a voice that was academic and belligerent, not oracular or poetic." No work of Pico's reveals more about what he actually believed and how he was prepared to defend it.

## The Crisis of 1487

The events that produced the *Apology* unfolded with extraordinary and frightening speed. In December 1486, Pico published his 900 *Conclusiones philosophicae, cabalisticae et theologicae* — a collection of theses drawn from across the entire history of philosophy, intended to serve as the agenda for a proposed public disputation in Rome to which he had invited all comers at his own expense. Pope Innocent VIII, under pressure from theologians at the curia who found a number of the theses disturbing, appointed a papal commission in February 1487 to examine the book. The commission, chaired by Bishop Jean Monissart of Tournai and including sixteen members — among them several bishops, a canon lawyer, and the Dominican theologian Pedro Garcia — moved with remarkable speed. By the third week of March, within a matter of weeks from its first meeting, it had condemned thirteen of the 900 propositions.

Copenhaver's account of the commission is damning. The proceedings were not, he argues, a neutral judicial inquiry. Innocent's charge to his commissioners was "angry," characterizing Pico's theses as "confused, deviant, intolerable, irregular, obscure, scandalous, and reeking of heresy." The commission met, heard Pico's explanations of his positions, and in each case — the trial record shows all votes as unanimous and unqualified — found him guilty as charged, ignoring or dismissing the defenses he offered. Pico attended some of the hearings and was combative; the *Apology* itself contains a famous digression in which he describes his altercations with individual commissioners in a Latin that crackles with wit, insult, and schoolboy punning on the word *testis* (witness/testicle). The combination of aristocratic arrogance and genuine intellectual contempt for his judges is on display throughout.

In the wake of the condemnation, Pico wrote the *Apology* — he claims to have composed it in forty days — and had it printed before fleeing to France, apparently hoping to preempt worse action by publishing his defense directly before the pope. The gambit failed. Innocent VIII issued a papal bull in August 1487 condemning the *Conclusiones* in their entirety, and Pico, arrested in France near Lyons at the request of the papal nuncios, was briefly imprisoned at Vincennes before the intervention of sympathetic French nobles and his own connections — including Lorenzo de' Medici — secured his release. He remained in France and then in northern Italy until a partial reconciliation with the papacy allowed him to return to Florence, where he settled under Medici protection and spent the remaining years of his life. A complete absolution was granted only by Alexander VI in 1493, the year before Pico's death.

## The Thirteen Questions

The structure of the *Apology* follows the commission's order of condemnation almost exactly. Pico divides his defense into thirteen *Quaestiones* — numbered Q1 through Q13 in modern scholarship — each corresponding to one of the condemned theses. The topics, as Copenhaver has laid them out, are:

Q1 on Christ's descent to Hell; Q2 on sin; Q3 on the worship of images or demons; Q4 on Christ's incarnation; Q5 on Kabbalah and the proof of Christ's divinity; Q6, Q9, and Q10 on the eucharist; Q7 on Origen; Q8 on the nature of heresy and willful belief; Q11 on a miracle; Q12 on God; Q13 on the nature of the soul.

What ties most of these questions together — Copenhaver's central analytical finding — is the problem of divine embodiment. The eucharistic theses (Q6, Q9, Q10) concern how Christ's body can be present in the sacrament; the incarnation thesis (Q4) concerns how God can take on human flesh without ceasing to be God; the descent thesis (Q1) concerns how the bodiless soul of Christ can be in a place — Hell — after the death of his body. Kabbalah (Q5) and Origen (Q7) are outliers in terms of their philosophical content, but the eucharistic and incarnation questions are deeply interconnected by the scholastic metaphysics of divine embodiment, and they dominate Copenhaver's analysis.

## The Eucharistic Theses

The three condemned eucharistic theses are the most technically demanding part of the *Apology* and the ones that most clearly show Pico's mastery of the scholastic tradition. Q6, the most elaborate, begins with the concept of *paneitas* — "breadness" — a neologism of scholastic coinage designating the formal principle that makes something bread. Pico's condemned thesis had proposed that, without the substance of bread being changed into Christ's body (the standard doctrine of transubstantiation) and without *paneitas* being eliminated, it would be possible for Christ's body to be present on the altar "in keeping with sacramental reality" — provided the statement is understood *de possibili*, about what is possible, rather than *de sic esse*, about what is actually the case. Pico is not denying transubstantiation; he is exploring whether an alternative eucharistic arrangement would be logically possible.

To support this exploration, Pico deploys the scholastic theory of *suppositio* — the technical vocabulary of how terms in a proposition stand for things — and the related concept of *suppositatio*, the act by which a substance is taken on or sustained by another substance. His argument draws extensively on Jean Cabrol's *Defenses of the Theology of Thomas Aquinas*, Pierre de la Palud (*Paludanus*), John Duns Scotus, and other scholastic authorities. Half the text of Q6, Copenhaver establishes, came directly from Cabrol's compilation, though Pico never names him. The Q10 thesis made the additional claim that the words *hoc est enim corpus meum* spoken at the consecration are to be taken "materially, not as signifying" — that is, in the technical sense of *suppositio materialis*, where a term in a proposition refers not to a thing but to itself as a linguistic expression.

The papal commission found Q6's thesis erroneous and Q10's scandalous. Pico argued in his defense that his thesis was *de possibili*, not *de facto*, and that similar formulations could be found in approved scholastic authorities. The commission rejected this defense without engaging its substance. Copenhaver's analysis suggests that the commission's approach was not philosophical but political: the outcome was determined before the arguments were heard.

## Q4: The Incarnation

The thesis on incarnation, Q4, is the most metaphysically ambitious of the condemned propositions and the one that most clearly required Pico's command of scholastic *suppo-* vocabulary. The condemned thesis proposed a particular formulation of the doctrine of Christ's hypostatic union — the union of divine and human nature in the single person of Christ — that the commission found offensive. Pico used the verb *suppositare*, absent from standard classical dictionaries, to describe the act by which the divine Word takes on human nature: the Word "supposits" (sustains, takes on, holds as a *suppositum*) the human nature of Christ. The terminology is Scotist in origin and was used to analyze the Trinity's internal relationships as well as the incarnation, but Pico's specific formulation departed from the standard Thomist account in ways that the Dominican members of the commission — Pedro Garcia in particular — found intolerable.

In Q4's defense, Pico argued that his formulation was consistent with the teaching of Henry of Ghent, a thirteenth-century scholastic theologian whose *Quodlibeta* provided Pico with the philosophical resources he needed. Copenhaver's analysis of Q4 is one of the most detailed pieces of philosophical reconstruction in his book: he shows that Pico had read Henry carefully and had found in him a metaphysical framework in which the human nature of Christ could be understood as taken on by the divine person without being *supposited* in the standard sense — a distinction that allowed Pico to maintain orthodoxy about the incarnation while using technically unusual language to describe it. The commission, dominated by Thomists with little sympathy for the Scotist tradition and apparently without close knowledge of Henry, condemned Pico's formulation without engaging its sources.

## Q5: Kabbalah and the Divinity of Christ

The Kabbalah thesis, Q5, occupies a special position in the *Apology* and in subsequent scholarship. The condemned thesis had stated that "no science gives more certainty of Christ's divinity than magic and Kabbalah." The commission found this outrageous; Pico's judges, as Copenhaver notes, were simply unintelligible to them. In the *Apology*, Pico defends Q5 by rehearsing his understanding of the Kabbalistic tradition — the divine names, the sefirot, the mystical reading of the Hebrew scriptures — and arguing that when one reads these correctly, they point unmistakably toward the Christian doctrine of the Trinity and the divinity of the Messiah. The thesis is not, he insists, a claim about magic in the vulgar sense of superstition and illicit manipulation of spirits; it is a claim about the natural and Kabbalistic knowledge of hidden causes and divine structures that converges, when correctly interpreted, on Christian revelation.

Whether Pico's judges could have followed this argument even if they had been willing to try is doubtful. They had no access to the Hebrew sources, no knowledge of Kabbalistic terminology, and no reason to trust the man defending the proposition. Q5 was not part of the scholastic tradition in any recognizable sense, and it did not fit the *quaestio* format that structured the rest of the Apology. It remained, as Copenhaver puts it, "utterly unintelligible to his judges in Rome and completely detached from the scholastic philosophizing that informed all the other condemned theses."

## The Apology's Method

Pico's defensive strategy throughout the *Apology* is threefold. First, he argues that the censors have misread his technical terminology: that words like *paneitas*, *suppositatio*, and *de possibili* are terms of art with precise scholastic meanings that the commission has failed to understand. Second, he argues that similar propositions are found in approved scholastic theologians — Aquinas, Scotus, Albert the Great, Henry of Ghent, and a further three dozen medieval authorities — and that condemning his formulations therefore implicitly condemns those authorities as well. Third, he argues that his statements were intended in a specifically delimited sense — *secundum aliquem modum dicendi* (according to a particular way of speaking), or under a particular scholastic qualification — that the commission has stripped away in order to produce a simpler, more obviously heretical proposition to condemn.

This method is inherently defensive and inherently limited. It works by showing that the condemned theses are not heretical under the most favorable reading, not by showing that they are correct or even that Pico personally endorsed them without qualification. The *Apology* is an *apologia* in the ancient sense: a speech of self-defense, shaped by the constraints of the legal situation rather than by the demands of philosophical exposition. Copenhaver's key methodological point is that this circumstance makes the *Apology* more revealing than the *Oration*, not less: precisely because Pico was writing in response to specific charges about specific propositions in a specific institutional context, the *Apology* shows his actual philosophical commitments — what he was willing to defend in public under pressure — rather than the polished and deliberately enigmatic performance of the *Oration*.

## The Oration and the Apology

The relationship between the two texts is illuminating in another way as well. When Pico composed the *Apology* and needed an introduction to send to the pope, he did not write a new preface. He cannibalized a portion of the *Oration* — the proemium that he had composed in 1486 to introduce the planned Roman disputation — and adapted it for his juridical-theological purpose. As Copenhaver notes, the prince apparently thought he could "hypnotize the Pope's high-ranking functionaries with recycled oratory." The attempt backfired; the papal commissioners had no taste for Pico's elaborate rhetorical art, and the *Apology* itself quickly drops the elevated humanist register of the *Oration* and shifts into the dense, compressed Latin of scholastic theology.

This recycling of the proemium is significant for understanding the *Oration* itself. It suggests that the famous speech was a provisional piece of rhetoric, assembled for a specific occasion and not yet a finished philosophical statement. Pico stripped it for parts when the occasion required, which implies that its elaborate doctrinal architecture was less fixed in his own mind than it appears to the modern reader who takes it as a philosophical summa. The *Oration* was the advertisement; the *Apology* is the thing itself — the actual philosophical position that Pico was prepared to defend when his life and honor were at stake.

## Copenhaver's Rehabilitation

Copenhaver's *Pico della Mirandola on Trial* represents the first extended monograph on the *Apology* since Gianfrancesco Garcia published his rebuttal of it in 1489. Copenhaver's central argument is that the rehabilitation of the *Apology* as a central Pico text requires a corresponding revision of the standard picture of Pico as a proto-modern, a humanist, and a prophet of individual freedom. The author of the *Apology* was, Copenhaver insists, "in no way progressive." He was a late medieval scholastic philosopher who had provoked the church's wrath with formulations drawn from the most technical and obscure reaches of medieval theology, and who fought back using the same weapons. The familiar Pico of the textbooks — the Pico who proclaimed human dignity and infinite self-fashioning — "did not write the Apology." The grim text that Pico actually published under pressure is a record of "a battle for life and honor" waged in "the proximate medieval past where human history was despised as the annals of sin."

This is a deliberately provocative framing, and it has prompted objections from scholars who find Copenhaver's reading too hostile to the intellectual ambitions that the *Oration* at least gestures toward. But as a corrective to the hagiographic tradition — in which Pico becomes a beacon of Enlightenment progress shining backward into the medieval gloom — Copenhaver's insistence on the *Apology* as the central Pico text performs an essential service. The condemned theses are not eccentric marginalia; they show what Pico actually thought when he was pushing his philosophical program as far as it would go. The eucharistic speculations of Q6, Q9, and Q10; the incarnational metaphysics of Q4; the Kabbalistic Christology of Q5; the theology of willful belief in Q8 — these are not adventurous footnotes to the philosophical program announced in the *Oration* but its deepest and most dangerous expression.

## Sources and Cross-References

The primary source is Pico, *Apologia tredecim quaestionum* (Rome, 1487); the critical edition by Paolo Fornaciari (Florence: SISMEL-Edizioni del Galluzzo, 2010) is the standard reference. Farmer's critical edition of the *Conclusiones* (Tempe: Medieval and Renaissance Texts and Studies, 1998) is essential for the condemned theses in context. Garcia's contemporary rebuttal: Pedro Garcia, *Determinationes magistrales contra conclusiones apologales Ioannis Pici* (Rome, 1489). Secondary scholarship: Copenhaver, *Pico della Mirandola on Trial* (Oxford, 2022); Dulles, *Princeps Concordiae: Pico della Mirandola and the Scholastic Tradition* (Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1941); Edelheit, *Ficino, Pico and Savonarola: The Evolution of Humanist Theology* (Leiden: Brill, 2008); Valcke, *Pic de la Mirandole: Un itinéraire philosophique* (Paris: Les Belles Lettres, 2005).
