# Magic

Magic occupies a peculiar and structurally central position in Pico della Mirandola's intellectual project. It is at once a philosophical category, a cosmological claim, a polemical weapon, and the primary reason that thirteen of his nine hundred theses were condemned by a papal commission in 1487. To read Pico on magic is to enter a field in which ancient authorities are mobilized against one another, the boundary between natural philosophy and revealed religion is made deliberately unstable, and a young aristocrat of twenty-three challenges the theological establishment of Rome with the proposition that no science gives us more certainty about the divinity of Christ than magic and Kabbalah.

## The Fundamental Distinction: Magia Naturalis and Goetia

Every treatment of magic in Pico's corpus begins with and depends upon a foundational distinction between two kinds of practice that the Latin word *magia* notoriously conflated. In the *Oration*, Pico draws directly on the Greek differentiation between *mageia* and *goêteia*, insisting that the former is natural and admirable while the latter is demonic and hateful (Oration §56). This distinction, he argues, was universal in law, philosophy, and religion: eminent authorities praised natural magic as holy, reliable, and reputable, but cursed the demonic kind as false, useless, and disgraceful (§57). Plotinus taught, according to Pico, that the true magus shuns demons and serves Nature by revealing what has been concealed — hidden but natural forces that bind the universe together (§§59–60). Demonic magic destroys human beings by enslaving them to God's enemies; natural magic leads to virtue and piety through astonishment at God's works (§61).

The distinction was not merely academic. Pico's projected debate at Rome in January 1487 — which Innocent VIII suspended before it could take place — would have been held within earshot of the Malleus Maleficarum and the papal bull *Summis desiderantes* of 1484, which licensed inquisitors to prosecute witchcraft. His magical theses, despite all their philosophical pedigree, were always open, as Farmer notes, "to suspicions of demonology and witchcraft" (Farmer 1998: 15). The Apology, written in haste after the condemnations to defend the thirteen contested theses, returns repeatedly to this distinction. Magic is not necromancy, not the trafficking with "matter-bound and unclean spirits" that constituted *goêteia* in patristic and scholastic sources (Copenhaver 2019: 421). It is, rather, the expert knowledge of natural agents, their powers, activities, mutual applications, and proportions — a knowledge whose highest practitioners in Pico's ancestry include Pythagoras, Empedocles, Democritus, Plato, Plotinus, and Apollonius of Tyana.

## The Magic Theses in the 900 Conclusions

The twenty-six Conclusiones magicae form the ninth section of the theses Pico proposed according to his own opinion (*secundum opinionem propriam*). They are therefore not a summary of received tradition but Pico's most personal and self-confident pronouncements. The first four theses distinguish good magic from bad in the same terms as the Oration; the next ten discuss magical forces and argue that understanding them supports Christian doctrine. The fifteenth thesis introduces the indispensability of Kabbalah to magical practice: "No magical action can have any effect unless it has an act of Kabbalah, explicit or implicit, linked with it" (T2M15). The final thesis of the set declares that "if the Kabbalah is pure and unmediated, something happens that no magic touches" (T2M26), suggesting that Kabbalah operates at a level entirely beyond natural magic's reach.

Other theses in the set concern language as the medium for magical effects. Sounds that do not signify by human convention — nonsense words like 'abracadabra,' or a melody without lyrics — carry more power than semantically loaded sounds, because their force derives from intrinsic natural virtue rather than arbitrary assignation. No singular names that are conventionally meaningful can produce magical effects unless they are Hebrew (divine names, names of angels, names of sefirot) or derived from Hebrew roots. Numbers of a special kind — the threefold and the tenfold, mirroring the structure of the ten sefirot with their Trinitarian summit — are magical "numbers of numbers" (T2M23). The most architecturally important thesis for the hierarchical structure of Pico's system is T2M18: nature's magical powers rise only to the horizon between time and eternity. Natural magic cannot cross that boundary unaided; only Kabbalah can carry what magic has prepared into the supratemporal realm.

Alongside the Conclusiones magicae proper, Pico's thirty-one theses on the Orphic Hymns (*secundum propriam opinionem de intelligentia hymnorum Orphei secundum magiam*) present a further dimension. These theses are introduced by the remarkable boast that Pico is the first to have discovered their magical content — an implicit polemic against Marsilio Ficino, who had composed an earlier, non-magical commentary on the same hymns. The thesis T2Or4 declares that "just as David's hymns are wonderfully useful for an act of Kabbalah, so are the hymns of Orpheus useful for an act of true, licit, and natural magic." The Orphic Hymns, read through a Pythagorean-Kabbalistic numerological hermeneutic, become instruments of a magical ascent that parallels the Hebrew Psalmist's ascent to God. Orpheus and David are the twin musicologists of the universe's hidden resonances.

## Magic as the Perfection of Natural Philosophy

The most celebrated formulation of Pico's position comes in the *Oration*: "magic is nothing other than the utter perfection of natural philosophy" (*nihil aliud est magia quam naturalis philosophiae absoluta consumatio*). This claim is philosophically specific. Natural philosophy, in the scholastic curriculum Pico inherited, was the study of physical change in the sublunary world: it analyzed the four elements, the motions of the heavens, the properties of plants and animals and minerals. For Pico, magic is the practical consummation of this theoretical discipline — the knowledge that knows not only what natural agents are but how to make them act. A magus does not work miracles; he reads the text of nature with greater precision than an ordinary natural philosopher and then, by applying agents to patients in the right proportions, produces effects that appear miraculous to the uninitiated. "As a farmer marries elm to vine," Pico writes in the Oration, "so a magus joins earth to heaven." The thesis T2M13 compresses this into an aphorism: "Doing magic is nothing but marrying the world."

The cosmological principle underlying this activity is what Pico states most precisely in the opening of his Heptaplus: *quae sunt in omnis mundis contineri in singulis* — whatever exists in all worlds is contained in each. This principle of universal implication, whereby the higher orders are reflected in the lower and the lower are able to communicate with the higher through their hidden sympathies, makes magic possible as an intellectual discipline. The magus does not violate the order of nature; he exploits it with superior knowledge of its internal grammar. The Plotinian formulation Pico favors is that "the true magic is internal to the All" — it is not something imposed from outside but something discovered by careful attention to what Nature already contains (Copenhaver 2019: 424). Copenhaver's reading of the Oration confirms that for Pico, magical visioning — this epoptic, wonder-struck contemplation of divine works dispersed throughout nature — is not the highest stage of the ascent but its fifth step, above natural philosophy and theology but still preparatory to Kabbalah and ultimately to mystical union (Copenhaver 2019: 427–28).

## The Condemned Theses and the Papal Commission

In early 1487 a papal commission appointed by Innocent VIII examined the nine hundred theses and identified thirteen propositions that were, in varying degrees, "false, erroneous, superstitious, or heretical." Of these thirteen, several touched directly on magic or on the Kabbalah-magic nexus. The most notorious was Thesis 9 of the Conclusiones magicae (T2M9): "There is no science that gives us more certainty of Christ's divinity than magic and Kabbalah." The commission judged this "false, erroneous, superstitious, and heretical." Thesis T2M7, that "the works of Christ could not have been performed either by the way of magic or the way of Kabbalah," was admitted to be "true and tolerable" but reluctantly, since the commission complained it could "easily be taken to a bad sense, since it is connected with magical things" (Farmer 1998: 127; Dorez and Thuasne 1897: 136).

Pico's defense of T2M9 in the Apology is a methodical exercise in philosophical clarification. The commission, he argued, had misread the scope of his claim. He was not comparing magic to revealed theology or to the certitude of faith. He was comparing it to other branches of human, experiential, natural knowledge. Among such branches, natural magic and that part of Kabbalah concerned with the powers of celestial bodies give us the best evidence that Christ's miracles exceeded natural possibility — because they are precisely the sciences that show us the upper limit of what natural agents can accomplish. Christ did not perform his works through magic or through Kabbalah; that is confirmed by T2M7. But magic and Kabbalah, by delineating with maximum precision what nature can and cannot do, provide the best philosophical demonstration that what Christ did was supernatural: "Through these it is known that those works that Christ performed could not be done by means of natural powers" (Farmer 1998: 127, citing Apology in Opera, 171–72). The argument is scholastic and precise; what scandalized the commission was its confidence that any non-theological discipline could contribute to Christology.

The papal condemnation, when it finally came in August 1487, swept far beyond the thirteen theses. Innocent VIII complained of propositions "renovating the errors of pagan philosophers," of those "cherishing the deceits of the Jews," and above all of arts "disguising themselves as natural philosophy, harmful to the Catholic faith and human kind, sharply damned by the canons and doctrines of Catholic doctors" (Farmer 1998: 15–16, citing Garin, Scritti vari, 64–65). All copies of the nine hundred theses were ordered burned within three days. The condemnation effectively made Pico's magic the first victim of what Hirsch has called "the first broad inquisitional action in the history of printing."

## Against Yates: Farmer's Revision of the Hermetic Magic Narrative

Frances Yates's celebrated account of the Hermetic origins of Renaissance magic, advanced in Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition (1964), assigned Pico a starring role as the thinker who married the Hermetic tradition to Kabbalah to create the new Renaissance magus who acts upon the world through science. Farmer's Syncretism in the West (1998) subjects this thesis to systematic correction on five points, two of which are especially important for understanding Pico's magic.

The first is chronological. Yates assumed that Pico's magic was derivative of Ficino's. But Ficino's only magical treatise, the De vita coelitus comparanda — our sole evidence for his magia naturalis — was not written until approximately two and a half years after Pico published his Conclusiones magicae, the Oration, and the Apology in 1486–1487 (Farmer 1998: 118). During the period when Pico composed these texts, he had not been in close contact with Ficino for over a year; their relations were at their lowest point. Pico's theses in fact criticize Ficino covertly at several points, and Pico's claim that he was "the first among the Latins" to discover magic in the Orphic Hymns reads as a pointed repudiation of Ficino's earlier, non-magical commentary on those hymns. If there was a Pico-Ficino magical synthesis in this period, Farmer argues, it was Pico who initiated it, not Ficino (Farmer 1998: 120, 122).

The second correction is even more fundamental to the Yates thesis. Hermes Trismegistus, whom Yates placed at the center of the Renaissance Hermetic magical revival, is conspicuously absent from Pico's lists of authoritative magicians. In the Oration and Apology, Pico provides a long catalogue of ancient magi — Homer, Pythagoras, Empedocles, Democritus, Plato, Zalmoxis, Zoroaster, Eudoxus, Hermippus, Apollonius of Tyana, Plotinus, and several minor Pythagoreans — but Hermes does not appear. "Virtually the only prominent prisca theologus who is not listed as a magician," Farmer observes with some relish, "is Hermes Trismegistus!" (Farmer 1998: 121). The one clear reference to Hermetic magic in Pico's early works is negative, a citation from William of Auvergne's De universo condemning the Egyptian practice of enticing demons into idols — a reference to the same passage in the Hermetic Asclepius that Yates had treated as a central catalyst of the Renaissance magical revival. Farmer's reading of this absence is pointed: Pico closely associated Hermes Trismegistus with Ficino, and he was not prepared to acknowledge him as a real magician (Farmer 1998: 121, n. 63).

This revision has significant consequences. Copenhaver confirms it from within his own reading of the Oration: "Hermes Trismegistus did not make the prince's list" of approved magicians (Copenhaver 2019: 423). The mechanisms of Pico's natural magic also differ from Ficino's. Where Ficino's De vita coelitus comparanda invokes the spiritus mundi as the medium of celestial influence, the spiritus mundi appears nowhere in Pico's nine hundred theses, Oration, or Apology. Pico's magical mechanisms are diverse and syncretic — the Neo-Platonic vehicle or body of the soul, cosmic influxes, the sentience in nature theorized by al-Kindi and Roger Bacon, Pythagorean number theory, and musical resonance — but they do not converge on the single celestial-spiritual medium that Walker and Yates placed at the center of Renaissance magia naturalis (Farmer 1998: 124–25).

## Kabbalah and the Limits of Natural Magic

The structural relationship between natural magic and Kabbalah in Pico's system is hierarchical but not simply successive. Kabbalah is not merely a more powerful version of magic; it operates in a different domain. Natural magic's powers rise only to the horizon between time and eternity (T2M18); the sefirotic space of Kabbalah is above the magus. But the relationship is also necessary: no magical action can achieve its full effect without Kabbalah, implicit or explicit (T2M15). And the Orphic Hymns, which constitute Pico's most original contribution to the magical corpus, only complete their magical "work" when joined to an act of Kabbalah, "whose property it is to practice every formal quantity, continuous and discrete" (T2Or21, discussed by Copenhaver 2019: 431–32).

The magical phase of the Cherubic life — Pico's image for the soul's ascent through philosophy, theology, and contemplation — starts in nature and rises as high as the horizon between time and eternity. If a magus can speak the language of Kabbalah, angelic and sefirotic rites can reach beyond that limit. This is why the naming of angels in the Oration — Gabriel, Metatron, Michael, Raphael — is not mere decoration but part of a magical hermeneutic: these names designate the intermediary agencies through which the ascent above nature becomes possible (Copenhaver 2019: 428). The thesis T2K13, one of the Kabbalistic conclusions, issues a stern warning: "One who makes a mistake in the work or comes to it unpurified will be devoured by Azazel." Magic in Pico's system is serious business, and its dangers are proportional to the heights it aspires to reach.

## The Condemned Thesis 9 and Christological Apologetics

Pico's claim in T2M9 that "there is no science that gives us more certainty of Christ's divinity than magic and Kabbalah" has been the most widely misunderstood proposition in his corpus. Yates admitted she could not fully explain it, speculating about a possible connection with a Eucharistic concept of magic. Farmer and Copenhaver both provide the same corrective: the claim is about the comparative certainty afforded by human, experiential, natural knowledge, not about revealed certainty or sacramental theology. The logic runs through T2M6–8 as a sustained argument. All astonishing effects, whether magical or Kabbalistic, must be referred ultimately to God as First Cause (T2M6). Christ's miracles cannot have been accomplished by any method of magic or Kabbalah (T2M7) — he had no need of secondary causes since he was himself the First Cause. But his miracles are the most certain evidence of his divinity not because of what was done but because of how it was done (T2M8, non ratione rei facta sed ratione modi faciendi). To understand how it was done requires knowing what the natural and celestial order can accomplish on its own — and the sciences that best illuminate this are natural magic and the appropriate portion of Kabbalah. T2M9 is, in Pico's own framing, a contribution to natural theology, not a mystical claim about sacramental power.

## Conclusion

Magic in Pico's thought is not an occult curiosity imported from dubious Hermetic sources. It is a philosophical position about the nature of natural knowledge, its legitimate aspirations, and its precise limits. Natural magic is the consummation of natural philosophy precisely because it presses that philosophy to its outer boundary, where the forces that bind the world together can be exploited by the knowledgeable practitioner and where the gap between human skill and divine power becomes most legible. The demonic magic that Pico repudiates is not simply an inferior technique; it is an ontological error — a confusion between the natural order and the domain of unclean spirits. The natural magic he defends is contemplative as often as it is operative: much of what he calls magical "works" in the context of the Orphic Hymns involves prophetic exegesis and numerical hermeneutics rather than any manipulation of material objects (Farmer 1998: 128–29). And the Kabbalistic magic that crowns the entire structure is not natural at all, but reaches into the angelic and divine orders that lie above the limits nature can attain.

The consequence is that Pico's magic is inseparable from his epistemology, his cosmology, his Kabbalism, and his Christology. To condemn it, as Innocent VIII's commission did, was not to reject one set of esoteric practices; it was to reject a whole way of arguing about the relationship between natural knowledge, divine action, and the human ascent to God. Pico understood this perfectly well. His Apology does not backtrack but simply makes the philosophical architecture explicit: natural magic and Kabbalah are, together, the sharpest instruments available for pressing natural inquiry to the point at which the supernatural becomes visible — the horizon where what nature cannot do becomes the best evidence for what God has done.
