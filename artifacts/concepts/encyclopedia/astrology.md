Astrology stands at the intersection of two of the most consequential intellectual decisions in Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's brief career. The first is his early, syncretic embrace of celestial influence as a component of a comprehensive philosophical cosmology in which Neoplatonic, Kabbalistic, Hermetic, and Aristotelian traditions are harmonized under the principle that all things exist in all things in their own mode. The second is his late, systematic demolition of judicial astrology — the practice of predicting future events and individual fates from the horoscope — as an epistemologically fraudulent, physically incoherent, and theologically dangerous discipline. The distance between these two positions, both the arc of development that separates them and the degree of genuine continuity that underlies their apparent contradiction, has been one of the central problems in Pico scholarship for the better part of a century.

The study of astrology in the ancient and medieval world was not a unified discipline but a cluster of related practices distinguished by their objects and methods. Natural astrology (sometimes called astronomical astrology) studied the effects of celestial motions on sublunary physical processes — the regulation of seasons and tides, the influence of the moon on biological cycles, the medical significance of planetary configurations in relation to the humors and critical days in illness. This form of astrology was largely uncontroversial and was institutionally integrated into university curricula throughout the medieval period as a practical application of Aristotelian natural philosophy and Ptolemaic mathematical astronomy. Judicial astrology, by contrast, claimed to predict specific future events and individual human destinies from the positions of celestial bodies at the moment of birth (the nativity or horoscope), election (choosing auspicious times for actions), interrogation (answering specific questions by casting a chart), or mundane events (the fates of kingdoms, cities, and religions derived from planetary conjunctions). It is judicial astrology that Pico targets in the *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem* (1493–1494, published posthumously 1496), while leaving at least some room for the naturalistic version of celestial influence.

Pico's early engagement with astrological ideas begins in the *Commento alla Canzone d'amore* (1486), his Italian philosophical commentary on a love poem by his friend Girolamo Benivieni. There, working in a predominantly Neoplatonic idiom, Pico constructs a cosmological framework in which the eight celestial spheres are animated substances, each with its own character and influence, and in which the planetary intelligences mediate between the divine intellect and the sublunary world. Jupiter, associated with the World Soul, governs politicians and active people; Saturn, associated with the First Intelligence or *nous*, inclines toward meditative contemplation. Venus, positioned near Mars, moderates her husband's destructive influence; Jupiter performs the same moderating function in relation to Saturn (Akopyan 2021, 12–13). This account follows medieval astrological tradition closely and assumes without argument the basic framework of judicial astrology — the qualitative differentiation of planetary influences, the significance of planetary positions relative to one another, and the causal efficacy of these relationships for human character and activity.

The *900 Conclusions* (1486) and the *Oration* that introduces them develop the early astrological framework in a more complex and philosophically ambitious direction. In the *Oration*, Pico distinguishes natural magic — the "absolute consummation of natural philosophy" — from demonic magic, and places knowledge of celestial influences within the domain of the former. His praise of ancient philosophers who endorsed the study of astrology and magic includes Pythagoras, Empedocles, Democritus, Plato, al-Kindi, and Roger Bacon (*De hominis dignitate*, pp. 150–52). The *Conclusions* themselves include theses *secundum opinionem Ficini* (according to Ficino's opinion) that situate celestial influence within the framework of the *spiritus*, the subtle pneumatic medium through which planetary powers reach the sublunary world. Alongside these theses, the section of Kabbalistic conclusions articulates an ascending hierarchy of operative knowledge in which the specific influences of individual planets are ultimately grounded in the structure of the divine emanations (*sefirot*). From this perspective, astrology is not simply a descriptive science of celestial motions and their effects but a hermeneutic that decodes the divine structure of reality in its material manifestation.

The *Heptaplus* (1489), Pico's Kabbalistic-Christian commentary on the Genesis creation narrative, maintains the ten-sphere cosmology of the *Commento* while grounding it more explicitly in Hebrew sources. The empyrean tenth sphere is the unique origin of light that diffuses through the ninth sphere into all the lower elements. Celestial influence flows continuously from this highest source through the planets and stars into the sublunary world, and specific planets — particularly Saturn and the Sun — receive detailed treatment. Yet the *Heptaplus* also introduces the first clear note of anti-astrological polemic in Pico's corpus: in Book V, he attacks divination by the stars and the claim to predict upcoming events, characterizing this practice as condemned by Basil, Apollinarius, Cyrillus, and Diodorus, as well as by the best Peripatetics and by Theodoritus as well (*Heptaplus* V, 4). The critique is brief and is directed specifically at divination, leaving the theoretical framework of celestial influence intact. But it marks a pivot in Pico's thinking that would deepen considerably over the next several years.

The *Expositiones in Psalmos* (1491–1492), Pico's unfinished commentary on the Psalms, represents the crucial transitional moment in his astrological thought. In the exposition of Psalm 18 (*Coeli enarrant gloriam Dei*, "the heavens declare the glory of God"), Pico develops a sophisticated account of two Suns — an intellectual Sun or the Good, and a natural Sun that receives its virtues from the intelligible one — and of the way in which divine light penetrates everything through this mediating channel. The philosophical problem Pico confronts here, as Akopyan has argued, is the incompatibility of Neoplatonic light (which remains incorporeal throughout the emanation process) with Aristotelian-scholastic physics (which requires the material transformation of any cause that affects matter). Pico's attempt to combine these two accounts in the *Expositiones* reaches an impasse: the natural Sun as a purely formal cause that transforms matter without being transformed is intelligible in Neoplatonic terms but not in Aristotelian ones, and the two frameworks cannot be harmonized in the way that Pico's syncretic ambitions demand. The failure of this reconciliation, Akopyan argues persuasively (Akopyan 2021, 27–28), is one of the principal internal philosophical motivations for the radical rethinking of celestial influence that the *Disputationes* undertakes.

The *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem* is Pico's most extensive and philosophically systematic work. The twelve-book treatise was left incomplete at his death in 1494 and published posthumously by his nephew Gianfrancesco Pico in 1496 in Bologna. The question of the authenticity and editorial history of the text has been central to scholarship on it. Stephen Farmer argued that the posthumous publication represents a substantial editorial intervention by Gianfrancesco, who had his own Savonarolan agenda and every motive to shape the text in a direction more hostile to the syncretic magical and astrological traditions of his uncle's earlier career (Farmer 1998, 151–76). Lucio Bellanti, the leading astrologer who composed a systematic response to the *Disputationes* in 1498, similarly accused Gianfrancesco and the physician Giovanni Mainardi of distorting the original text (Akopyan 2021, 35–36). Against these doubts, Akopyan's close analysis of the *Disputationes*' method demonstrates that its approach — deconstructing the tradition of astrological authorities by exposing the internal contradictions and mistranslations that accumulate as a tradition is transmitted across centuries — is methodologically identical to what Pico does in the *De ente et uno* with the Plato-Aristotle controversy. The continuity of method strongly suggests that the *Disputationes* represents Pico's authentic intellectual position, even if Gianfrancesco made minor stylistic corrections to an unpolished text (Akopyan 2021, 37–38, 152–53).

The argument of the *Disputationes* can be analyzed under four broad headings, corresponding to four distinct but interrelated modes of objection to judicial astrology. The first is historical and critical: Pico examines the claimed lineage of astrological authority from the Chaldeans and Egyptians through Ptolemy and the Arabic tradition and argues that at every stage the tradition is based on mistranslation, misattribution, and internal contradiction. No great philosopher or theologian — not Pythagoras, Plato, Aristotle, the Stoics, or the Church Fathers — genuinely endorsed judicial astrology, and the witnesses that astrologers claim for their science are either misread or fictitious. This deconstruction of astrological authority is, as Akopyan notes, the mirror image of what Pico does in the *De ente et uno* with the Neoplatonic tradition: just as he strips away centuries of Platonic commentary to reveal the authentic Plato who agrees with Aristotle, so in the *Disputationes* he strips away the accumulated tradition of astrological interpretation to reveal a lineage that was never coherent (*Disputationes* I, prooemium).

The second line of argument is natural-philosophical: in Book III, the philosophically most dense section of the work, Pico analyzes light, motion, and heat in Aristotelian terms and concludes that the proposed mechanisms of celestial influence are insufficient to ground the detailed, qualitatively differentiated effects that judicial astrology claims. Celestial bodies can affect the general conditions of the sublunary world — temperatures, seasons, tides — through their light and motion, but they cannot produce the specific, individuated effects on human character and destiny that astrology predicts, because the physical mechanism of celestial influence does not carry enough information to generate such specificity (Akopyan 2021, 32–33). This argument involves Pico's adoption of an Aristotelian rather than a Neoplatonic account of how celestial and sublunary bodies interact — a shift that Akopyan reads as the culmination of Pico's long philosophical struggle with the incompatibility of the two frameworks.

The third argument is epistemological and mathematical: the moment of birth cannot be precisely determined, and the mathematical calculations required to cast an accurate horoscope introduce errors that accumulate to the point at which any specific prediction derived from them is unreliable. Pico documents at length the disagreements among astrologers on technical matters — the number of planetary spheres, the divisions of the zodiac, the methods for calculating planetary positions — to show that the discipline lacks the internal consistency required of a genuine science. He adds to this the empirical observation that astrological predictions systematically fail: twins born at nearly identical moments have radically different fates, confirming that the celestial configurations at birth cannot determine individual destiny.

The fourth argument is philosophical-theological: if the stars determine human fate, free will and moral responsibility are destroyed, and divine providence is reduced to mechanism. This argument, which had deep roots in Patristic and scholastic theology (Augustine, Aquinas, and others had all argued against astrological fatalism on similar grounds), represents the most politically and theologically charged element of Pico's critique. It connects directly to the anthropology of the *Oration*: if human beings are defined by their capacity to determine their own nature — to become animals or angels, plants or gods, according to their own choice — then any doctrine that binds them to the stars at the moment of birth fundamentally misrepresents what human beings are. The *Disputationes* can thus be read as the philosophical and polemical completion of the anthropological argument the *Oration* had introduced.

The irony of Pico's late anti-astrological polemic was noted by his contemporaries and has continued to attract scholarly attention: the man who demolishes judicial astrology as a form of determinism antithetical to human freedom was himself deeply under the influence of Girolamo Savonarola's prophetic mission, having invited the Dominican preacher to Florence in 1490 precisely because he believed that Savonarola was divinely inspired. Gianfrancesco Pico's account of his uncle's intellectual development in the *Vita Johannis Pici* presents the *Disputationes* as the fruit of Savonarola's influence — a narrative that serves Gianfrancesco's own ideological agenda but that contains a real irony: the attack on the stars as determinants of human fate may have been accompanied by a susceptibility to prophetic authority as another form of externally imposed destiny. Akopyan documents this irony through the reception history: Savonarola's own *Contro gli astrologi* draws explicitly on Pico's arguments, and Gianfrancesco develops an anti-astrological position that is simultaneously indebted to his uncle and shaped by Savonarolan theology (Akopyan 2021, Chapter 7–8).

The reception of the *Disputationes* in the decades following its publication is itself a significant chapter in the history of astrology. Bellanti's *Responsiones in disputationes Ioannis Pici adversus astrologos* (1498) is the most substantial immediate response and attempts to rehabilitate judicial astrology by combining a defense of Ptolemy with an attack on the authenticity of Pico's text. Gianfrancesco Pico's *De rerum praenotione* (1507) and Maximus the Greek's epistles against astrology, composed after Maximus's arrival in Muscovy (where he had contact with Gianfrancesco's circle), extend the *Disputationes*' arguments in different directions and attest to the wide circulation of Pico's anti-astrological polemic in early sixteenth-century Europe. The Venetian Francesco Zorzi's *De harmonia mundi* offers a response to the *Disputationes* from within the Neoplatonic-Kabbalistic tradition that Pico himself had earlier inhabited, defending the compatibility of celestial harmony with Christian theology. Giovanni Pontano, the humanist and astrologer, composed the most technically sophisticated astrological response, arguing for astrology's genuine empirical and mathematical foundations against Pico's critique (Akopyan 2021, Chapters 10–11). Robert Westman has argued that Pico's *Disputationes* played a catalytic role in motivating sixteenth-century astronomers and astrologers — including Copernicus — to reform the mathematical foundations of their discipline in response to Pico's charges of mathematical incoherence (Westman 2011).

The scholarly rehabilitation of the *Disputationes* as a central rather than marginal text in Pico's corpus has been one of the significant achievements of recent Pico scholarship. Earlier historiography, influenced by Eugenio Garin's picture of Pico as a radical representative of humanist freedom, tended to treat the *Disputationes* either as the product of a late crisis in Pico's thinking or as substantially Gianfrancesco's fabrication. Copenhaver's position — that the *Disputationes* shows Pico working consistently across his career to distinguish legitimate from illegitimate forms of celestial influence, and that the late text is best read as the completion of an intellectual project begun in the *900 Conclusions* — has become increasingly influential, even if the precise relationship between the early magical endorsement and the late anti-astrological polemic remains contested. Akopyan's detailed reconstruction of the *Disputationes*' structure, sources, and argument has placed the text on a firm scholarly foundation and established that its methodological continuity with the *De ente et uno* constitutes strong evidence for Pico's authentic authorship.

The prooemium of the *Disputationes* (1557 Opera, pp. 412ff.) sets the tone with characteristic forcefulness: like Homer and Caecilius, Pico writes, the most dangerous enemies are those who approach under the guise of friendship. Astrology presents itself as a science, claims the authority of the most learned names in the tradition, and promises knowledge of the future — but precisely because it wears the costume of wisdom, it is more dangerous than open superstition. Pico distinguishes explicitly between mathematical astronomy, which measures the sizes and motions of the stars by mathematical reasoning and which is a certain, noble, and honorable art, and the astrological discipline that pronounces on future events — a fraud that exploits human curiosity and desire (*Astrologiam vero cum dico, non eam intelligo quae siderum moles et motus mathematica ratione metitur, artem certam et nobilem et suis meritis honestissimam... sed quae de sideribus eventura pronunciat*, Opera 412). The distinction was not new — Thomas Aquinas, Albertus Magnus, and many scholastic predecessors had made it — but Pico's execution is more systematic and philosophically better grounded than any predecessor, and it is this combination of philosophical rigor, historical criticism, and theological urgency that made the *Disputationes* the most consequential anti-astrological text of the Renaissance.
