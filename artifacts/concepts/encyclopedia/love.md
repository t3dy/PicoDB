# Love

Love — *amor* in the Latin philosophical tradition, *eros* in the Greek — is among the most contested and carefully delimited concepts in Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's philosophical vocabulary. It is most fully worked out in the *Commento sopra una canzona d'amore* (Commentary on a Love Song, composed c. 1486, published posthumously 1519), a vernacular Italian commentary on a philosophical poem by Pico's friend the Florentine poet Girolamo Benivieni (1453–1542). That the *Commento* is Pico's primary contribution to Renaissance love theory is well established. What scholars have spent more time demonstrating is how that contribution differs from the tradition it inherits, and specifically how it departs from the account of love offered by Marsilio Ficino in the *De amore*, the work that had defined Florentine Neoplatonic love theory in the preceding decade. The short answer is that Pico's love is more austere, more rigidly hierarchical, and less willing to acknowledge the lower forms of erotic attachment as genuine philosophy. The longer answer requires tracing the argument of the *Commento* across its three books, locating the sources Pico invokes and corrects, and attending to the specific metaphysical claims about beauty and desire that constitute his disagreement with Ficino.

The occasion of the *Commento* is Benivieni's canzone beginning *Amor, dalle cui man sospes' el freno / Del mio cor pende* ("Love, in whose hands the bridle of my heart is suspended and hangs"). Benivieni had written this poem as a versification of Ficino's love theory, drawing especially on the *De amore* — Ficino's Latin commentary on Plato's *Symposium*, composed around 1469 and increasingly influential in manuscript before its printing in 1484. The canzone is itself modeled formally on Guido Cavalcanti's *Donna me prega*, the Aristotelian love poem that Ficino mentions in the *De amore* in order to honor his own friend Giovanni Cavalcanti, a descendant of Guido's. By writing a philosophical commentary on Benivieni's poem, Pico was therefore inserting himself into a triple textual chain: his commentary stands to Benivieni's poem as Benivieni's poem stands to Ficino's prose, and as Ficino's prose stands to Plato's dialogue. The *Commento* is, in the most literal sense, a work that positions itself as a correction of everything that came before it in that chain.

Pico composed the *Commento* in the autumn of 1486, when he had retreated to his country estate at Fratta near Perugia while preparing for the Roman disputation. He had sent drafts to Ficino, who found in them a number of attacks on his own views — sometimes aimed directly at Ficino by name, sometimes directed at "a distinguished Platonist" who was mistaken on such and such a point, with the referent unmistakable. Ficino returned the manuscript with his own marginal remarks, including the curt annotation "This is a bad mistake," before Pico reincorporated some corrections and ignored others. As Michael Allen documents in his study of the *Commento* and *Heptaplus*, Pico eventually asseverated at the end of his second book that Ficino "made mistakes on every subject in every part of his treatise" (Allen 2017, 141). The surviving manuscripts preserve traces of this combative exchange, and the textual history of the *Commento* is itself a record of subsequent efforts to suppress the disagreement: Benivieni's expurgated version, published in the Giunti edition of 1519, excised the most pointed attacks on Ficino, and the posthumous editions in the Basel *Opera omnia* relied on this sanitized text. Sears Jayne's translation (1984) was the first English rendering based on the unexpurgated version, following the Garin critical edition of 1942, and Jayne's decision to restore the more combative ordering of the material was itself an editorial argument about the intellectual significance of Pico's anti-Ficinianism (Allen review, *Italica* 1986, 189–190).

The *Commento* is organized into three books plus a section of particular glosses on Benivieni's nine stanzas. The first book provides a general account of Neoplatonic metaphysics — an exposition of the three worlds (angelic, celestial, elemental), of God's relation to being and intellect, of the first mind or angelic intellect as God's unique direct creation — that largely recapitulates the framework of Ficino's *De amore* while already inserting corrections. The second and third books develop Pico's own distinctive positions, particularly his account of the three Venuses and the three kinds of love corresponding to them, and his treatment of the mythological episodes of the *Symposium* — the birth of Love in the gardens of Jupiter, the Orpheus and Eurydice story, the *mors osculi* or death by kiss — that provide his most original philosophical contributions. Scholars following Jayne's lead have typically described the movement from book one to books two and three as a movement from Ficinian summary to Piconian critique.

The core of Pico's love theory rests on a distinction among three Venuses that corresponds to a distinction among three kinds of *amor*. The celestial or Uranian Venus is identified with the beauty of the angelic intellect, the intelligible world, the pure Ideas as they exist in the first mind created by God. The second Venus — sometimes called the vulgar or generative Venus — is the beauty that presides over the world soul and over the production of corporeal forms in the sensible world. A third, lower still, governs the simple appetite of bodies for one another. These three Venuses correspond to three loves: a purely intellectual love that contemplates the beauty of the Ideas, a psychic love that governs the ascent of souls through the hierarchy of being, and a bodily love that is at best a degraded echo of genuine desire. The crucial point, and the point at which Pico diverges most sharply from Ficino, is his refusal to allow that bodily love — even a spiritualized attraction to the beautiful form of another person — constitutes a legitimate first rung on the Platonic ladder of ascent. For Pico, love (*amor*) has a formal object, which is beauty (*bellezza*), and beauty is always and essentially an intelligible, not a sensible, reality. Sensible beauty is at best a faint trace or participated image of real beauty, and the attachment that binds a lover to a beautiful body is not the beginning of philosophy but its counterfeit.

This is a sharper position than Ficino's, and understanding the sharpness requires understanding what Ficino had argued. In the *De amore*, Ficino had developed the Platonic ladder of ascent — the famous speech of Diotima in the *Symposium* (201D–212B), which describes how love of a beautiful body can lead, by stages, to love of beautiful souls, then beautiful activities, then beautiful forms of knowledge, and finally to Beauty Itself — as a theory of erotic *furore*, divine madness. Love of even a beautiful body is, on Ficino's account, the first movement of the soul toward its divine origin, because the beauty of the body is genuinely the reflection of divine beauty through a chain of participation: God is the Good, beauty is the splendor of the Good (*splendor boni*), the soul participates in this splendor, and sensible beauty in a beautiful person is a participated image of that participation. The soul that falls in love with a beautiful person is responding, however dimly, to the divine beauty that shines through that person's form. Ficino's is a cosmologically generous account; it finds divine presence distributed through all levels of being, and it assigns the erotic experience of beauty a genuine, if preliminary, philosophical significance.

Pico does not deny the hierarchy, but he imposes a stricter metaphysical audit on each of its rungs. Beauty, on Pico's analysis in *Commento* 2.8, is nothing other than the "friendly enmity or harmonious discord" (*una amica inimicizia, ovvero una discorde concordia*) that results from the ordering of diverse elements into a whole. This definition — drawn partly from Heraclitean and Empedoclean sources, partly from the Neoplatonic analysis of how unity and multiplicity coexist in Mind — means that beauty is fundamentally a property of the intelligible realm, where the Ideas constitute a unified multiplicity. The beauty of a sensible thing is therefore not beauty in the proper sense but an image of beauty; and love of sensible beauty is not love properly speaking but an image of love, or rather a confusion of love with something that resembles it. Allen traces how this argument puts Pico on a collision course with Ficino's treatment of the Symposium: where Ficino had used the *De amore* to argue that all three kinds of beauty — divine, angelic, corporeal — generate corresponding and legitimate loves, Pico insists that only the love oriented toward intelligible beauty constitutes *amor* in the sense established by Plato (Allen 2017, 186–214 passim).

The most striking expressions of Pico's severity appear in the glosses on the fourth stanza of Benivieni's poem, where he takes up the Orpheus and Eurydice episode from the *Symposium* (179A–D). Ficino, in the *De amore* 1.4, had declined to offer an interpretation of this passage, writing that he did not intend to inquire into the allegory of Alcestis and Orpheus. Pico is more intrepid. Orpheus, he argues, was unable to recover the true Eurydice because he refused to die to the sensible world: he tried to find her while still alive, still attached to the faculties of imagination and passive reason, and therefore he saw not Eurydice herself but a phantasm of her. For Pico, this is the allegory of what happens to anyone who thinks they can achieve genuine intellectual vision without severing themselves entirely from the sensible — without cutting off "the functions of the imagination and also the reason" (Allen 2017, 144). The intelligible Ideas cannot be grasped by a faculty that remains contaminated by the sensible; genuine love of beauty requires a death to the lower faculties, and Orpheus failed to die.

This brings Pico to the concept that gives the *Commento*'s account of love its most memorable form: the *mors osculi* or "death by kiss." The term is drawn from Kabbalistic sources — in Hebrew, *binsica*, the death of the fathers of Israel — and describes the soul's final union with incorporeal beauty in intellectual rapture. Pico distinguishes two deaths. The first is the ecstasy in which the soul, while still embodied, withdraws all its faculties from the body and gazes upon the heavenly Venus, the Idea of Beauty, face to face. The second death is more total: the body falls away from the soul entirely, and the soul unites with the heavenly Venus "in an intimate embrace," becoming "a single soul" with her. This second death is exemplified, says Pico, by Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, Aaron, and Miriam, of whom the Kabbalists say they died *binsica*, of kissing. It is also what Solomon longed for in the verse "Kiss me with the kisses of thy mouth" (*Canticles* 1:2), and it is what Plato alludes to when he speaks of the kisses of Agathon in the *Symposium*. The *mors osculi* is therefore the summit of the ascent program described in the *Oration*: the transformation of the soul from human existence into intellectual existence, from philosopher into angel, from the seeker of beauty into the one who is united with beauty itself.

Jayne's contribution to understanding this love theory was not only his translation but his attention to the trajectory of Pico's reception. The *Commento* was first published not among Pico's own works but in the 1519 Giunti edition of Benivieni's *Opere*, which circumstance alone ensured that readers would receive it through Benivieni's mediation rather than Pico's own. The French translation of the *Commento* by Guy Le Fèvre de la Boderie, based in turn on a French intermediary attributed to Claude Chappuys (1544–1548), brought Pico's love theory into the French and eventually English Renaissance literary traditions. The crucial complication, which Jayne documented and which Allen confirmed, is that this French transmission mixed Pico's argument with Ficino's in ways that made the anti-Ficinian edge largely invisible to subsequent readers. The love Platonism that influenced Pontus de Tyard, Ronsard, Edmund Spenser, and others was a Pico filtered through Ficino's categories, a Pico who seemed to be offering a variant on Ficinian love theory rather than a pointed critique of it. Recovering what Pico actually argued, as distinct from what his Ficino-shaped reception made of his argument, remains one of the central tasks of *Commento* scholarship (Jayne 1984, introduction; Allen 1986, 189–190).

The broader philosophical stakes of Pico's account of love extend beyond the *Commento* into his other major works. In the *Oration*, the ascent of the soul from the vegetative through the rational and angelic levels is driven by something like love — a desire to become, progressively, what one contemplates — though Pico does not use the vocabulary of *amor* systematically in that text. In the *Heptaplus*, the seven-book commentary on the Mosaic creation (1489), Pico explicitly quarried material from the *Commento*, and the account of the three worlds corresponds to the account of the three Venuses and their loves. The *900 Conclusions* contain a number of theses in the Platonic section that touch on love and beauty, though in the compressed form of the *conclusiones* these are not developed but merely asserted. Pico's claim in the *Commento* that God does not love us — because such a love "would be an imperfection" in God — is perhaps the most jarring implication of his account, one that Allen reads as a deliberate refutation of Ficino's theory of reciprocal love in the three Graces, and one that anticipates the scholastically informed distinction between erotic and agapic love that Pico never quite fully develops (Allen 2017, 146).

What is philosophically distinctive about Pico's *amor* is its combination of Neoplatonic hierarchy with something close to Aristotelian formalism. Love is not a feeling but a metaphysical appetite: the movement of a lower being toward the form that would perfect it, oriented always toward a formal object — beauty — that has its proper being in the intelligible realm. This means that love is properly speaking a single thing, graduated in its objects and in the clarity of the vision it enables, but unified by its essential reference to intelligible beauty. Bodily desire is not a form of love but a counterfeit of it; the love of beautiful souls is love in an impure but genuine form; and the intellectual love that terminates in the *mors osculi* is love in its fullness, the love that Plato described in the *Symposium* under the figure of Diotima and that the Kabbalists described in the figure of the patriarchs who died of kissing. Between these extremes, Pico's account has a precision and a harshness that set it apart from Ficino's more accommodating synthesis — and it is that harshness, as much as the metaphysical argument, that defines the *Commento*'s distinctive contribution to Renaissance philosophical psychology.
