# Poetic Theology

The category of *theologia poetica* — poetic theology — names one of the most consequential hermeneutical principles operating in Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's intellectual program, and also one of the most methodologically ambiguous. At its broadest, the principle asserts that the ancient poets — Homer, Hesiod, Orpheus, Pindar, Virgil — were not primarily storytellers or entertainers but theologians who encoded genuine metaphysical and theological truths in the deliberately veiled medium of mythological narrative. The myths of divine birth, divine conflict, divine transformation, and divine love that constitute the inherited treasury of Greek and Roman poetry are, on this reading, philosophical doctrines in a symbolically encrypted form — a form chosen not to please or entertain but to protect doctrines too profound or too sacred for common publication. To understand the myths philosophically is therefore not to add a layer of interpretation to poetry but to recover what the poets themselves intended. Pico employs this principle extensively, and understanding what he does with it — in the *Commento sopra una canzona d'amore*, in the *900 Conclusiones*, in the *Heptaplus*, and in the *Oration* — requires tracing it back to its Neoplatonic origins and forward through the Renaissance transformations that make it available to him.

The genealogy of poetic theology as a hermeneutical practice runs through late antiquity and reaches Pico through a complex of sources. The fundamental move — treating mythological narrative as philosophical allegory — predates Neoplatonism: allegorical reading of Homer appears already in the Stoics, and Plato himself in the *Republic* treats the narratives of Homer and Hesiod about the gods with critical seriousness, attacking their moral content precisely because he takes their theological implications seriously. But it is the Neoplatonists who systematized allegoresis into something approaching a method. Porphyry's *On the Cave of the Nymphs* offers a sustained reading of the famous passage in *Odyssey* 13 describing the cave on Ithaca as an allegory of the soul's descent into matter: the cave's two entrances (one for mortals, one for gods) encode the two movements of the soul — downward into birth and upward into liberation — and every detail of the passage, from the looms to the darkness, yields a metaphysical referent when the correct decoding procedure is applied. Iamblichus treats the theogonic myths — the stories of Kronos, Zeus, Ouranos, and the Titans — as symbolic accounts of the divine processions, the movement from the unified source downward through the levels of divine and cosmic reality. Proclus, in his *Theologia Platonica* and in his commentaries on the *Republic* and the *Timaeus*, brings this allegorical practice to its most systematic elaboration: the Orphic theogony, the violent births and dismemberments and the swallowing of children, encodes the structure of the hypostases; Night, Kronos, Zeus, and Dionysus name levels of the intelligible world whose relations the myths render in narrative form. Proclus is explicit that the ancient poets were theologians (*theologoi*), and that their use of mythological form was not a concession to popular taste but the proper medium for truths too elevated to survive being stated plainly to unprepared minds.

This Neoplatonic allegorical tradition enters the Renaissance primarily through Ficino's translations and commentaries. Allen has documented how Ficino's commentary on the *Symposium* — the *De amore*, composed around 1469 — treats the speech of Diotima, the myth of Poros and Penia, and the allegorized figures of Venus and Eros not as poetic embellishments on a philosophical argument but as philosophical argument in mythological dress (Allen 2017, passim). The point is not that Plato happens to use myths to illustrate doctrines he could have stated more straightforwardly but that the myths are the natural medium for certain kinds of truth — truths about the soul's movement, about the origins of love and beauty in the cosmic hierarchy, about the soul's relation to its divine source — whose proper presentation requires narrative form because they concern processes and movements rather than static propositions. Ficino's reading of Plato is therefore itself already a practice of poetic theology: it treats the mythological passages of the *Symposium*, the *Phaedrus*, the *Timaeus*, and the *Republic* as the deposit of the same doctrines that are stated more technically in the strictly dialectical passages, and it establishes the principle that myth and philosophy are alternate modes of the same truth.

The explicit articulation of this principle as *theologia poetica* draws on a Renaissance precedent that Ficino and Pico shared: Giovanni Boccaccio's *Genealogia Deorum Gentilium* (composed over several decades, completed c. 1374), the first major systematic treatment of classical mythology in the Renaissance mode. Boccaccio's *Genealogia* is fundamentally a defense of poetry against those who regard mythological narrative as morally dangerous or intellectually trivial. Boccaccio argues that the ancient myths encode genuine wisdom under the attractive covering of fiction — that the stories of the gods, correctly read, teach truths about natural philosophy, theology, and morality that the ancient poets deliberately concealed from the vulgar while revealing to the initiated. His title for the ancient poets — *theologi*, theologians — and his insistence that their fiction (*integumentum*) covers philosophical truth (*sensus allegoricus*) provide Ficino and Pico with the Renaissance vocabulary for what they are doing when they read the Orphic hymns or the *Symposium* allegorically. The key term in Boccaccio is *sensus allegoricus*: beneath the surface narrative (*littera*) lies an allegorical meaning (*allegoria*) that is the poem's true content. This is a medieval hermeneutical category applied to a new body of material — ancient poetry rather than scripture — and the implicit claim is that ancient poetry, like scripture, is a two-level text whose surface requires interpretation to reveal the depth.

Pico inherits this framework but extends it in several characteristic directions. The first and most important extension appears in the *Commento*, where the mythological canzone by his friend Girolamo Benivieni functions itself as a piece of poetic theology. Allen's review of Sears Jayne's translation of the *Commento* notes that Pico appeared to be working the text up into "a full-blown poetic theology" (Allen, *Italica* 1986, 189–190) — a treatise in which the myth-encoded philosophical love theory would be the central object of analysis. By choosing to write a philosophical commentary on a vernacular Italian love poem, Pico is implicitly claiming that Benivieni's canzone stands in the same relation to Florentine Neoplatonic philosophy as the Orphic hymns stand to ancient Neoplatonic theology: it is a poem that encodes philosophical content that the commentary will unlock. The commentary form is itself an act of poetic theology — a demonstration that philosophical truth inheres in poetic form and can be extracted from it by the trained reader.

This demonstration proceeds through the *Commento*'s sustained allegorization of the mythological episodes that Pico draws from Plato's *Symposium* and from the Orphic tradition. The myth of the birth of Venus in the gardens of Jupiter, when Poros (Plenty) lay with Penia (Poverty) and engendered Eros (Love), is read as an account of the metaphysical origin of beauty and love in the angelic intellect — an account whose philosophical content matches and is confirmed by the Neoplatonic analysis of how Nous constitutes itself by organizing the Ideas into a unified multiplicity. The Orpheus and Eurydice myth is read as the story of a philosopher who failed to achieve genuine intellectual vision because he had not undergone the philosophical death required to disengage from the faculties of imagination and passive reason. In each case, the myth is not an illustration of a philosophical point made independently but the primary vehicle through which Pico works out the philosophical point: the allegorical reading and the metaphysical argument are simultaneous acts.

The *900 Conclusiones* extend the practice of poetic theology into the domain of comparative religion. The historical section of the *Conclusiones* — what Farmer (1998) has analyzed as a reverse-chronological array of twenty-eight traditions or *gentes*, arranged from the most derivative (the Latin scholastics) to the most authoritative (the Hebrew Kabbalists) — is organized by the assumption that each tradition has encoded the same fundamental truths in the vocabulary appropriate to its cultural and historical location. The Orphic theses (section 10) treat the Orphic Hymns as encrypted philosophy, following the Proclean precedent: thesis 10.7 states that "whoever does not know how to intellectualize perceptible properties by the way of secret analogy will understand nothing sound from the Orphic Hymns." The Pythagorean theses treat the *acusmata* (the enigmatic sayings attributed to Pythagoras) as encoded philosophical and theological doctrine — what Pythagoras said in public about abstaining from beans or not looking in a mirror at lamplight hides deeper truths about the soul, the cosmos, and the divine, truths that only initiates could decode. The Chaldean theses treat the *Oracula Chaldaica*, the oracular verses attributed to Zoroaster and his circle, as a poetic theology of the divine triadic structure. In each case, Pico's procedure is the same: identify the surface form (myth, oracle, enigma), read through it to the philosophical content, and show that the philosophical content converges with the Neoplatonic and Kabbalistic doctrines established in the other sections.

Farmer's analysis of the *Conclusiones* is essential for understanding the structural function of poetic theology within Pico's syncretic program. Farmer (1998, 54–80) argues that the reverse-chronological arrangement encodes a theory of intellectual history: the earliest witnesses are the most authoritative because they stand closest to the primordial revelation, and the trajectory of history is one of progressive distortion and fragmentation from an original divine simplicity. Poetic theology in this framework is not merely a reading strategy but a theory about why the ancient poets encoded their doctrines in mythological form in the first place: because the primordial wisdom was too sacred and too demanding for common publication, the ancient theologians clothed it in the attractive veil of narrative and assigned its decoding to those who had undergone the appropriate philosophical training. The Pythagorean claim that Pythagoras wrote only a few sayings that he trusted to his daughter Dama — cited by Pico in the *Oration* — exemplifies this logic of esoteric transmission: the deepest truths are preserved by being encrypted in a form that reveals itself only to those prepared to receive it. Pico's citation of ancient parallels for this esoteric strategy — Plato's letter to Dionysius, Aristotle's remarks about the *Metaphysics* being "published and not published," Origen's claim that Christ revealed many things to the disciples that they did not write down, Dionysius the Areopagite's account of transmission "from mind to mind by means of speech" — is itself a piece of poetic theology's self-legitimation: the philosopher-reader who can decode the myths is the rightful heir of the ancient esoteric tradition.

The *Heptaplus* (1489) extends poetic theology into the domain of biblical hermeneutics in a way that reveals how far Pico extends the principle. The *Heptaplus* is a seven-book commentary on the first words of Genesis — *In principio creavit Deus caelum et terram* — that reads the Mosaic creation narrative as a philosophical and theological allegory encoding the same doctrines about the three worlds (angelic, celestial, elemental) that Pico had developed in the *Commento*. For Pico, Moses is not merely a lawgiver and narrative historian but a poetic theologian in the full Neoplatonic sense: he encoded in the sparse and apparently simple narrative of the opening verses of Genesis the entire structure of Neoplatonic cosmology, deliberately concealed from those without the philosophical preparation to receive it. Crofton Black's study of the *Heptaplus* and biblical hermeneutics shows how Pico's reading is in dialogue with the Jewish tradition of esoteric biblical interpretation — with the rabbinic *pardes* (the fourfold reading of Scripture) and with the Kabbalistic principle that every letter and word of the Torah conceals divine secrets. For Pico, Moses and Orpheus are doing essentially the same thing: the Torah and the Orphic poems are both poetic theologies, both forms of encoded wisdom whose surface narratives (*littera* or *cortex*) conceal metaphysical truth (*sensus allegoricus* or *nucleus*).

This equivalence between biblical and classical poetic theology is one of the most philosophically ambitious moves in Pico's program, and it is also one of the most methodologically risky. If Moses is doing what Orpheus is doing — encoding Neoplatonic cosmology in narrative form — then either the biblical revelation is not uniquely privileged or the Neoplatonic cosmology is itself a form of divine revelation. Pico characteristically wants both: he wants to honor the uniqueness of biblical revelation by showing that the most penetrating philosophical traditions independently confirm what Genesis teaches, and he wants to honor the philosophical traditions by showing that they were, despite their pagan origin, oriented toward the same truth. But the logic of poetic theology, once fully extended, tends to collapse the distinction it was meant to preserve: if all the ancient traditions encode the same truths, the question of which source is authoritative becomes harder to answer.

Fabrizio Lelli has noted that the concept of poetic theology, as it functions in Pico's Florentine context, provides the link between the Neoplatonic tradition and Kabbalistic interpretation: both are practices of reading encoded sacred wisdom, and Pico's synthesis draws on the hermeneutical structure that they share. The Kabbalists' practice of *peshat*, *remez*, *derash*, and *sod* — the four levels of biblical interpretation, culminating in the mystical sense (*sod*, secret) — runs parallel to the Neoplatonic practice of moving from mythological surface to philosophical depth. When Pico declares in the *Oration* that the Kabbalah teaches us to "read the Book of Law" in the same way that true astrology teaches us to read "the Book of God" (cited in Farmer 1998, 527), he is asserting that both are forms of the same hermeneutical operation: both decode a symbolically encrypted text to recover its deeper metaphysical content.

The historiographical question that poetic theology raises for modern scholarship is methodological as much as historical. If every mythological or poetic tradition can be decoded as encoding the same Neoplatonic and Kabbalistic doctrines, then the decoding method proves too much: it can make any tradition mean anything, because the set of philosophical truths it supposedly encodes is determined in advance by the interpreter's prior philosophical commitments. Farmer (1998) raises this methodological concern explicitly in his analysis of the *Conclusiones*' syncretic program: the convergences among traditions that Pico discovers are at least partly artifacts of the decoding procedure rather than discoveries about what the traditions actually teach. Copenhaver's scholarship on the *Oration* extends this concern to the rhetorical dimension: Pico's opening survey of ancient wisdom-traditions is not simply a scholarly report on what those traditions teach but a legitimation strategy, a construction of a philosophical genealogy that positions Pico himself as the heir and culmination of the entire chain of transmission. Poetic theology, on this reading, is as much a rhetorical as an intellectual achievement — a way of organizing authorities to authorize one's own conclusions.

None of this vitiates the philosophical interest of what Pico does with poetic theology. The insight that ancient mythological narrative can function as a medium for philosophical truth, and that decoding the myths philosophically is a legitimate intellectual operation, is not undermined by the observation that the procedure is susceptible to overextension. What matters for understanding Pico's practice is recognizing the multiple registers in which poetic theology operates: as a method of reading ancient texts (applied to the Orphic poems, the Pythagorean *acusmata*, the Chaldean oracles, and the Mosaic narrative alike), as a theory of the function of poetry in preserving esoteric wisdom, as a structural principle of the *Conclusiones*' historical organization, and as the implicit model for the *Commento*'s claim that Benivieni's poem and Pico's commentary together constitute a new act of philosophical-poetic encoding and decoding. To be a poetic theologian, for Pico, is to stand in a lineage that runs from Moses and Orpheus through Pythagoras and Plato to Ficino and, finally, to Pico himself — the latest, and he clearly hopes the most adequate, practitioner of the art of truth disclosed through veil.
