Celestial influence (*influentia caelestis*, *influxus*) — the causal action of heavenly bodies upon the sublunary world — was one of the foundational theoretical commitments of Renaissance natural philosophy, medicine, and astrology. For Giovanni Pico della Mirandola, this concept became the central battleground of his last major work, the *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem* (1493–94, published posthumously 1496). But the *Disputationes* represents only the final, polemically focused stage of a long philosophical engagement with celestial influence that runs through the entirety of Pico's career, from the astrological Neoplatonism of the early *Commento sopra una Canzone de Amore* (ca. 1486) through the Kabbalistic cosmology of the *Heptaplus* (1489) and the psalm commentaries of 1491–92. Tracing that trajectory reveals that Pico's anti-astrological turn is not a sudden conversion but the consequence of a philosophical impasse he reached in trying to reconcile two fundamentally incompatible theories of how heavenly influence reaches the material world.

The standard Renaissance account of celestial influence inherited from ancient and medieval sources held that the heavenly bodies — the fixed stars and the seven planets — continuously emit qualities downward through the cosmic hierarchy. These qualities act on the four sublunary elements and their combinations: they condition the weather, the growth of plants, the composition of minerals, the temperaments of animals, and — most controversially — the dispositions, fortunes, and fates of human beings. The Aristotelian formulation, as transmitted in the *Metaphysics* XII.5 and the *De generatione et corruptione* II.10, attributed the primary causal role in sublunary change to the sun, whose annual approach and recession along the ecliptic drives the cycle of generation and corruption. Aristotle was understood to have made the sun a "universal cause" of natural change in the lower world. Medieval Aristotelian commentators, particularly through the Arabic tradition, extended this framework to all seven planets, each of which was understood to radiate specific qualities through the medium of light and heat.

The Neoplatonic elaboration was more elaborate and metaphysically richer. In this tradition, each celestial sphere is governed by a planetary intelligence (*nous*) or soul that mediates between the pure, motionless First Intelligence (the One or Good) and the physical world below. The planetary intelligences do not merely radiate physical qualities but transmit ordered rational patterns — *logoi spermatikoi* or seminal reasons — that structure the forms of sublunary things. Plotinus in the *Enneads* had written movingly of the cosmic dance of sympathy (*sympatheia*) that binds all things to one another and to the heavens, describing the stars as "letters perpetually being inscribed on the heavens" that are signs rather than mere causes of earthly events — a formulation that carved some space for a non-deterministic reading of celestial influence (Allen 2017, 722). The Neoplatonic framework thus offered two faces: one that made celestial influence a vehicle for divine rational order and providential care, and another that threatened to make every sublunary event rigidly determined by the configuration of the heavens.

Ficino's *De vita coelitus comparanda* (1489), the third book of his *De vita triplici*, represents the most sophisticated Renaissance synthesis of these traditions. Ficino drew on Plotinus, Iamblichus, the pseudo-Ptolemaic tradition, and medieval Arabic astrology to construct a theory in which the magician-physician could attract favorable celestial influences through material correspondences — the right herbs, stones, music, talismanic images, and imaginative techniques — while the *spiritus mundi*, the world-soul's subtle pneumatic medium, provided the channel through which these influences flowed from the heavens into matter. For Ficino, celestial influence was not a brute mechanical causation but a form of divine communication mediated by the soul of the world and accessible to human intervention through the art of the *magus*. As Copenhaver has shown in his analysis of the Neoplatonic sources of Ficino's magic, particularly his reading of Plotinus *Ennead* 4.3–5, the theory of *logoi spermatikoi* embedded in the world-soul was central to Ficino's account of how magical operations could work (Copenhaver 1984, cited in Akopyan 2021, 964). The *De vita* thus integrated celestial influence, natural magic, medicine, and Neoplatonic cosmology into a single theoretical system.

Pico's engagement with celestial influence before the *Disputationes* shows a thinker working within this broadly Ficinian framework while introducing important modifications. The *Commento* (ca. 1486) presents a strongly astrological Neoplatonism: Saturn is explicitly correlated with the First Intelligence, Jupiter with the World Soul, and the planets are described as animated substances that radiate specific qualities into the sublunary world. Pico follows "well-established tradition" in attributing to Jupiter a positive influence on politicians and active people, while Saturn points toward "meditative moods" — the classic correlation of Saturn with contemplative withdrawal (Akopyan 2021, 12–13). In the *Commento*, Pico uses a scheme of ten celestial spheres that combines classical astronomical tradition with the Neoplatonic triad of One-Intellect-Soul and, implicitly, with the Kabbalistic doctrine of the ten *sefirot* that he was simultaneously developing through his study of Hebrew sources. This early Pico is unambiguously committed to celestial influence as a real cosmological mechanism.

The *900 Conclusions* (*Conclusiones nongentae*, 1486) include theses on natural magic that presuppose the reality of celestial influence and its accessibility to magical manipulation. Pico's famous conclusion that "the entire magic of this age has no other power than that of natural magic operating through celestial influences (*per influxus caelestes*)" situates him, at this early stage, as a defender of the legitimacy of natural astral magic against mere demonic conjuration. The *Heptaplus* (1489) continues in this vein: Pico describes the universe as ten spheres from whose highest empyrean the divine light descends through the ninth sphere to the planets and stars, and thence to the sublunary world (Akopyan 2021, 23–25). He speaks explicitly of the planets having "constellations" that "complement each other's effects" and recognizes that celestial bodies can produce opposite effects on the sublunary world, positive configurations balancing negative ones. He accepts, in short, the basic framework of judicial astrology — the idea that planetary configurations have specific, predictable effects on sublunary matters — while already expressing some skepticism about divination as such.

The philosophical problem that eventually destabilized this framework was one that Pico recognized himself. In both the *Heptaplus* and the *Expositiones in Psalmos* (1491–92), he worked to explain how intelligible, incorporeal celestial light could come into contact with material, corporeal matter without losing its incorporeal character or being transformed into mere material causation. This was a version of the general Neoplatonic problem of how the transcendent produces the immanent: how the One gives rise to Intellect, how Intellect gives rise to Soul, and how Soul acts on Body. In the *Heptaplus*, Pico proposed that the natural sun functions as the *imago* of the intelligible sun/Good, serving as a mediator between divinity and the physical world in a role he associated with the scholastic concept of the *intellectus agens* (agent intellect). As Akopyan reconstructs it, Pico found that "he had to combine the Neoplatonic doctrine of light with Aristotelian/scholastic physics, which postulated the transformation of the celestial influence received by matter"; the incompatibility of these two frameworks produced a philosophical impasse he could not resolve within the terms he had inherited (Akopyan 2021, 27).

This failure of reconciliation, according to Akopyan's influential analysis, was the primary philosophical motivation for Pico's radical reassessment in the *Disputationes*. By 1493, working through the methodological approach he had already developed in the *De ente et uno* (1491–92) — where he had "purified" Plato and Aristotle from the distortions of their Neoplatonic and scholastic interpreters — Pico applied the same critical method to the astrological tradition. His aim in the *Disputationes* was to demonstrate that the astrological tradition, from Ptolemy through its Arabic and medieval inheritors, had misread Aristotle and constructed a pseudo-Aristotelian legitimation for a discipline that neither the real Aristotle nor any serious philosopher had ever actually endorsed (Akopyan 2021, 39–41).

The natural-philosophical argument in Book III of the *Disputationes* is Pico's most sustained philosophical statement on celestial influence, and it requires careful reading. Crucially, Pico does not simply deny that heavenly bodies influence the sublunary world. His claim is more precise: that the spheres operate on the lower world through motion, light, and heat, and through nothing else (Akopyan 2021, 107). These three categories — *motus*, *lux*, *calor* — are the only legitimate media of celestial causation. Through motion, the prime mover gives its impulse to the celestial spheres, which in turn set sublunary matter in motion. Through light, the sun and the stars illuminate and warm the lower world. Through heat, this luminous causation produces its characteristic effects: it conditions bodies for life and supports generation. This is a limited and naturalistic account of celestial influence that Pico believed was consistent with genuine Aristotelianism, as opposed to the distorted pseudo-Aristotelianism that astrologers had constructed.

What these three categories explicitly exclude is the elaborate doctrine of specific planetary qualities and individual determining influences that judicial astrology required. Pico concedes that the sun warms and dries, that the moon affects tides and humors in fluid bodies, that celestial motion drives the cycle of seasons — all of this is legitimate natural science. What he denies is that Saturn specifically causes melancholic temperament, that Jupiter causes jovial disposition, that the configuration of the heavens at an individual's birth determines the character and fate of that specific individual. His argument, as Akopyan elucidates, is that celestial influence functions as a "universal cause" — it produces general effects applicable to species, not the particular differences among individuals within a species (Akopyan 2021, 107–8). A universal cause generates horses from horses and lions from lions; it does not generate this individual horse's particular disposition or fate. The philosopher born under Saturn does not have Saturn to thank for his contemplative temperament: that is a matter of proximate individual causes — constitution, habit, education, nutrition, and the choices of his will — that are beyond celestial determination.

The theological dimension of the argument reinforces the natural-philosophical one. Judicial astrology's claim to determine individual fates threatens divine providence and human free will simultaneously. If the stars determine what a person will do, then neither God's providential guidance nor the individual's own moral responsibility can be what Christian theology requires them to be. Pico's fourth line of argument in the *Disputationes* (alluded to by Akopyan's analysis of the work's structure) is precisely this theological-free will critique: astrology, by positing a deterministic chain from celestial configuration to human character and action, undermines both the Christian understanding of providence and the Aristotelian-scholastic account of rational free agency. This is not a purely negative argument: it reflects Pico's positive commitment, expressed in the *Oration*, to an understanding of human nature as essentially characterized by freedom — the capacity to choose one's own mode of being, which cannot be given or determined by the stars.

Pico's attack on the *prisca theologia* in Book XII of the *Disputationes* — the most textually problematic book, and one whose authorship Akopyan discusses at length — extends the argument to the cosmological and theological foundations that Ficino had used to legitimate his astrological synthesis. If the ancient theologians (Hermes Trismegistus, Zoroaster, Orpheus) were not reliable authorities for the truth of celestial influence but rather sources of corrupted and misinterpreted astrological speculation, then the entire edifice of Ficinian natural magic, which rested on the authority of these *prisci theologi*, loses its foundation. Pico's method throughout the *Disputationes* is, as Akopyan argues, a form of critical philology applied to the astrological tradition: showing that the tradition is built on mistranslations, misattributions, and misinterpretations, so that the accumulated weight of apparent authority dissolves when the original texts are read carefully (Akopyan 2021, 39–41).

This critique of celestial influence does not make Pico a mechanist or a materialist in any modern sense. He retains a robust cosmology in which the heavens are animated and in which divine rational order permeates the universe. What he denies is the specific astrological claim that this order is accessible to human predictive science through the calculation of planetary configurations — that the astrologer, by knowing the positions of the planets at a person's birth, can determine what that person will become and do. The soul is not, for Pico, subject to the stars in that way. Its vertical relationship to the divine intelligences above it, its capacity to ascend through the angelic hierarchies or to descend toward the demonic, its fundamental freedom to choose its own form of life — all of this lies outside the reach of celestial determination as Pico understands it.

The immediate reception of the *Disputationes* was complex and contested. Girolamo Savonarola and Gianfrancesco Pico della Mirandola (Pico's nephew) drew on the attack on astrology to support a more sweeping rejection of pagan learning that Giovanni Pico himself had not endorsed. Lucio Bellanti defended astrology against the *Disputationes* in his *Responsiones* (1498), questioning both the work's arguments and its authorship. Giovanni Pontano mounted a philosophically more sophisticated defense. As Akopyan documents, the *Disputationes* had European ramifications that reached as far as Maximus the Greek in Muscovy and resonated in the debates around Copernicus's astronomical reform — Rheticus, Copernicus's editor, apparently encountered a marginal note attributing the *Disputationes* to an "ancient book," suggesting that its arguments were being read in contexts far removed from their Florentine origin (Akopyan 2021, 112–13).

For PicoDB, celestial influence is one of the most conceptually rich entries in the encyclopedia, connecting to virtually every major theme in Pico's intellectual career: his early Neoplatonism in the *Commento*, his Kabbalistic synthesis in the *Heptaplus*, his natural philosophy and Aristotelianism, his theology of human freedom, and his polemical engagement with astrology in the *Disputationes*.
