No ancient philosopher is more continuously present in Pico's writings than Plato, and no figure is more difficult to reconstruct behind the mediating layers of Neoplatonic commentary, Ficinian translation, and Pico's own revisionary program. To speak of Pico's Plato is necessarily to speak of a Plato who arrives already interpreted — by Plotinus, by Proclus, by Pseudo-Dionysius, and above all by Marsilio Ficino, whose Latin translation of the complete dialogues, published in Florence in 1484, made the entire Platonic canon available to a Latin-reading audience for the first time since antiquity. Pico was one of that audience's most demanding and independently minded readers. He read Ficino's Plato with care sufficient to correct it — and the relationship between discipleship and philosophical independence that marks his reception of Plato is one of the most intellectually consequential tensions in his entire oeuvre.

The institutional precondition of Pico's Platonism is Ficino's editorial and translational achievement. As Michael J. B. Allen has documented, Ficino did not merely translate the dialogues but surrounded them with a vast apparatus of introductions, epitomes, and running commentaries, framing each dialogue within the Neoplatonic reading tradition and assigning them pedagogical positions within a curriculum culminating in the *Parmenides* (Allen 2017, 8–22). The *Parmenides*, in the Neoplatonic reading descending from Proclus, was understood not as an eristic exercise but as the supreme statement of Plato's theology — the apex of the teaching cycle, a summa of the One and its relations to being, mind, soul, and matter. Ficino accepted this Proclean hierarchy; his *Platonic Theology* draws its metaphysical architecture directly from the Neoplatonic reading of the *Parmenides*. When Pico came to Plato, he came through this interpretation; a direct, unmediated Plato was not available to him, and the question of what Plato *actually* said, beneath the Neoplatonic accretions, became one of the central philosophical problems his later work attempts to resolve.

The dialogues most consequential for Pico's major works can be identified with reasonable precision. The *Symposium* is the primary Platonic text behind the *Commento*, Pico's commentary on Girolamo Benivieni's canzone in the Petrarchan and Neoplatonic vein. Ficino had already produced his own elaborate commentary on the *Symposium* — the *De amore*, first composed in 1469 and republished in revised form in 1484 — which developed Plato's account of love as the soul's ascent toward the beautiful and the good. Pico's *Commento* opens with a pointed critical gesture: Ficino, he says, has made many errors in the first part of the *Banquet* (as Sophia Howlett notes, citing the Commentary directly; Howlett 2021, 66). This is not mere rhetorical posturing. Pico argues for the priority of Plotinus's account of love over Ficino's Platonizing adaptation of it, insisting on a more rigorous reading of the *Enneads* as the key to understanding what Plato meant in the *Symposium*. The quarrel is less about the *Symposium* itself than about the interpretive tradition through which it must be read. Already in the *Commento*, Pico's relationship to Ficino's Platonism is defined by productive antagonism rather than docile reception.

The *Phaedrus* occupies a structurally important position in Pico's philosophical psychology. The charioteer allegory — the soul as a charioteer driving two horses, one noble and one unruly, through the celestial spheres toward the vision of the ideas in the supercelestial place — provided Ficino, following Proclus and Plotinus, with his central account of the soul's ascent and its dependence on intellectual vision. Allen has traced how this imagery from the *Phaedrus* saturates Ficino's commentary on the *Symposium* and much of his *Platonic Theology* (Allen 2017, 18). In Pico, the *Phaedrus* material enters chiefly through the *Oration*, where the soul's capacity to assume any form and ascend through all levels of being is articulated in a language that combines Platonic soul-psychology with Dionysian purgation, illumination, and union. Howlett has observed that the triplex via described in the *Oration* is presented in Platonic-Dionysian terms — the threefold path of mystical ascent drawn from Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite — while the faculty structure that the soul must discipline on that ascent is fundamentally Aristotelian: appetite governed by moral virtue, intellect refined by philosophy (Howlett 2021, 170–172). This means that when Pico invokes "Plato" in the *Oration*, he is not simply opposing a Platonic mysticism to an Aristotelian rationalism; he is using "Plato" as a name for one register of a single philosophical vision in which Plato and Aristotle function as complementary, not antagonistic, authorities.

The *Timaeus* is crucial for the *Heptaplus*, Pico's sevenfold commentary on the six days of Genesis, composed around 1489. The *Timaeus* supplied the cosmogonic framework — the demiurge, the world soul, the four elements, the mathematical proportions governing the cosmos — that the Neoplatonic tradition had treated as Plato's most theologically rich statement about the structure of the created world. Allen has analyzed the "Ficinian *Timaeus*" and its role in Renaissance natural philosophy, showing how Ficino read the dialogue through a Neoplatonic lens that emphasized the descent of soul into matter and the return of all things to their source (Allen 2017, ch. 6). In the *Heptaplus*, Pico deploys this cosmological framework to show that Moses anticipated the Platonic and Aristotelian accounts of the three worlds — supracelestial, celestial, and sublunary — in his hexaemeral text. The strategy is characteristic of Pico's concordism: the authority of Plato is neither subordinated to Scripture nor placed above it, but shown to converge with it from a different angle of approach.

The Republic's account of the Good beyond Being (*epekeina tēs ousias*, Republic 509b) is the third Platonic text of critical importance, and its relationship to the Parmenides is the philosophical crux of Pico's most technically demanding work. In the Neoplatonic tradition, and for Ficino, the Good beyond Being in the Republic and the One that is "beyond being" in the first hypothesis of the Parmenides were read as identical: both expressed the radical transcendence of the first principle, which could not be predicated of being without being degraded to a lower ontological level. Plotinus had built his entire metaphysics on this identification; Proclus had systematized it; Ficino's Platonic Theology accepted it as the foundation of Platonic theology. From this perspective, Plato and Aristotle disagree fundamentally: Aristotle had identified the One and Being as convertible transcendentals, while Plato (on the Neoplatonic reading) had placed the One *above* being.

Pico's *De ente et uno* (1491) is a direct intervention in this debate, and it constitutes his most important and philosophically independent use of Plato. The work was composed to resolve a disagreement with Lorenzo de' Medici's circle, specifically with Angelo Poliziano's assertion that Plato and Aristotle disagreed on the relationship between the One and Being. Pico argues the contrary: Plato and Aristotle *agree* that the One and Being are convertible. The argument requires him to reread the *Parmenides* against the entire Neoplatonic commentary tradition. His strategy is to treat the first hypothesis of the *Parmenides* — where the One is shown to be "beyond being" — not as a positive metaphysical doctrine but as a dialectical or rhetorical exercise, a reductio demonstrating the consequences of an untenable abstraction (Howlett 2021, 131). This reading directly contradicts Ficino, who held with Plotinian orthodoxy that the One genuinely transcends being. Ficino's response was consequential: between 1492 and 1494, while Pico was still alive, Ficino composed his commentary on the *Parmenides* in a form that takes up Pico's arguments section by section in order to refute them (Howlett 2021, 133). The Parmenides controversy thus represents a collision between two coherent but incompatible Platonic traditions: the Neoplatonizing tradition running from Plotinus through Proclus and Ficino, and Pico's own revisionary Platonism that brings Plato into alignment with Aristotle.

The concordist claim that Plato and Aristotle agree is not merely one thesis among many in Pico's system; it is, as Sophia Howlett has argued, the organizing ambition of his philosophical project (Howlett 2021, 108–112). It appears in the *Oration* as one of the projects Pico intended to demonstrate at the Roman disputation of 1487 — he states explicitly that he will prove that the ideas of Plato agree with those of Aristotle. It appears in the historical section of the *900 Conclusions*, where the propositions *secundum Platonem* and *secundum Aristotelem* are organized to show their compatibility. And it culminates in the *De ente et uno*, which the work's own preface presents as a specimen of the larger *Concordia Platonis et Aristotelis* that Pico never completed. The concord project is thus the framework within which Pico's entire reception of Plato must be understood: Plato is always being read with one eye on Aristotle, and the question "what did Plato mean?" is always simultaneously the question "how does what Plato meant agree with what Aristotle meant?"

The specific mechanism by which Pico's Plato is filtered is complex. Pico uses Proclus more than any other ancient thinker in the *Conclusions* — some hundred of the nine hundred theses are drawn from Proclus (Howlett 2021, 74). This is significant because Proclus represents the most fully systematized version of late Neoplatonism, and Pico's engagement with Proclus is in many respects more detailed and technically competent than his engagement with Ficino's more synthetic Platonism. Allen has shown how Ficino's reading of individual dialogues was shaped by Proclus's commentary tradition, particularly for the *Parmenides*, the *Timaeus*, and the *Republic* (Allen 2017, ch. 6). Pico's corrections of Ficino are in several cases corrections that appeal to a more careful reading of Proclus himself, making Pico simultaneously a more thoroughgoing Neoplatonist than Ficino in his sources and a more revisionary one in his conclusions.

What Pico's Plato is not is simply a vehicle for Christian Neoplatonic piety. The Ficinian program — prisca theologia, the ancient theology as a preparation for and confirmation of Christianity — is present in Pico, but it is subordinated to a more rigorous philosophical program. When Pico argues in the *Apology* that the condemned theses are defensible within the framework of scholastic philosophy, and when he organizes the *Conclusions* according to the scholastic *disputatio* form, he is signaling that the authority of Plato must operate within the constraints of philosophical demonstration, not merely as an inspired witness to theological truth. Plato is one of the *principales philosophiae magistros*, the masters of philosophy, whose views are to be examined, compared, and where necessary corrected — not simply cited as confirmation of Christian doctrine. This is the spirit of the phrase Pico quotes in the *Oration*: *nihil aliud exercere philosophiam, quam de morte meditari* (Plato, *Phaedo* 64a). The philosopher's meditation on death is not a piety but a discipline, and Plato's authority is the authority of a philosopher who subjected his own doctrines to the rigors of dialectical demonstration.

The historiographical significance of Pico's Plato has been disputed. For Ernst Cassirer and the tradition he influenced, Pico's Platonism was primarily an expression of Renaissance humanism's emancipatory vision — the soul as self-creating, free to ascend through all orders of being. This reading, as Brian Copenhaver has demonstrated in extensive detail, is an anachronistic projection that imposes post-Kantian categories onto Pico's thoroughly medieval and scholastic philosophical framework (Copenhaver 2019, chs. 4–5). The Pico who reads Plato is not a proto-modern freedom-theorist but a late scholastic trained in Paduan Aristotelianism, Parisian via moderna logic, and Neoplatonic commentary who brings these resources to bear on questions that were live controversies in the academic theology of his day. Howlett's study reinforces this reading by showing that Pico's concordism between Plato and Aristotle is not an exercise in philosophical tolerance but a rigorous and technically specific philosophical claim about the convertibility of the transcendentals (Howlett 2021, 193–211).

Pico's Plato, then, is available to us only through a series of interpretive frames that must be distinguished rather than collapsed. There is the Plato of Ficino's translations and commentaries, which constitute the literary medium through which Pico actually read the dialogues. There is the Plato of the Neoplatonic commentary tradition — Plotinus, Porphyry, Iamblichus, Proclus — whom Pico reads in some cases directly and in others through Ficino's mediation. There is the Plato of the concordist program, who is made to agree with Aristotle through a revisionary reading of the *Parmenides*. And there is the Plato of the *Commento* and the *Oration*, who serves as authority for the soul's capacity for ascent through the intelligible world. These are not easily reducible to a single coherent "Platonic" position; they are strategic deployments of Platonic authority in service of different philosophical projects. Reading Pico's Plato requires tracking which project is in play, which dialogues are being invoked, and through which interpretive tradition they arrive — a task that remains one of the most rewarding and demanding in all of Renaissance philosophy scholarship.
