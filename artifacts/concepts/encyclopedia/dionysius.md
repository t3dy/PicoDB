Among the authorities whom Giovanni Pico della Mirandola regarded as witnesses to the universal truth underlying all philosophy and theology, Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite occupied a place of unusual honor. Pico called Dionysius "principe de' teologi cristiani" — the prince of Christian theologians — in the *Commento*, and his engagement with the Dionysian corpus shaped the structural spine of his most programmatic works. To understand why requires attending both to the Dionysian texts themselves and to what made them so compelling within Pico's particular intellectual project.

The figure known to Pico and his contemporaries as Dionysius the Areopagite was believed to be the Athenian convert mentioned in Acts 17:34 as having been won over by Paul's sermon on the unknown God at the Areopagus. This attribution gave the *Corpus Dionysiacum* an apostolic authority that made it among the most venerable texts in Christian theology. The corpus as Pico knew it comprised four treatises — *De divinis nominibus* (On the Divine Names), *De mystica theologia* (On the Mystical Theology), *De caelesti hierarchia* (On the Celestial Hierarchy), and *De ecclesiastica hierarchia* (On the Ecclesiastical Hierarchy) — together with ten letters. Modern scholarship, beginning with doubts raised by Lorenzo Valla in 1457 but not gaining broad acceptance until the sixteenth century and after, has established that the true author was an anonymous Christian thinker writing in Greek in the late fifth or early sixth century, working in close proximity to the Neoplatonist Proclus (412–485) and thoroughly saturated in his thought. In 1489, when Pico completed the *Heptaplus*, Valla's redating had not yet taken hold: the corpus retained its apostolic prestige. The reception of these texts in medieval Latin thought had in any case been enormous. As Crofton Black notes in his study of the *Heptaplus* (Black 2006, 122–123), at least six different Latin translations were made between the ninth and fifteenth centuries, over 120 manuscripts survive from before 1500, and nine printed editions appeared before the close of the century. The works were absorbed into scholastic theology through Albertus Magnus and Thomas Aquinas, and by Pico's time they circulated in the fifteenth-century Florentine translation associated with Ambrogio Traversari (1436). Marsilio Ficino was shortly to produce his own translations.

The intellectual substance of the *Corpus Dionysiacum* is a daring synthesis of Proclean Neoplatonism with Christian theology. Pseudo-Dionysius inherits from Proclus the structure of a hierarchically ordered cosmos in which being emanates from a first principle of absolute unity and returns to it through ordered stages, each stage characterized by a determinate mode of participation in the higher. This structure is Christianized: the first principle becomes the God of Scripture, the mediating levels become the angelic orders described in Paul, and the return to the first principle becomes the soul's ascent to union with God. The two hierarchical treatises — the *Celestial Hierarchy* and the *Ecclesiastical Hierarchy* — map this scheme onto first the angelic orders (Seraphim, Cherubim, Thrones; Dominations, Virtues, Powers; Principalities, Archangels, Angels) and then the orders of the church (bishops, priests, deacons; monks, the holy people, those undergoing purification). Each hierarchical order performs a triple office: it is purified, illuminated, and perfected (*katharsis*, *photismos*, *teleiosis*) by the level above it, and in turn purifies, illuminates, and perfects the level below. The *Divine Names* explores how the intellect can approach the divine through the names attributed to God in Scripture — Being, Life, Wisdom, Power, Beauty, Love — while recognizing that God is properly beyond all such names. The *Mystical Theology*, the shortest but philosophically most radical of the four treatises, traces the soul's ascent beyond all affirmation and all negation into the divine darkness (*skotos*) that Moses entered on Sinai — a union with the divine that transcends every concept and every predicate, a silence beyond both speech and its negation.

For Pico, this corpus offered something that nothing else in his reading could quite supply: a Christian framework for mystical ascent that was simultaneously philosophical in the Neoplatonic sense, scriptural in its authoritative grounding, and capable of being coordinated with the Kabbalistic and Platonic materials he was assembling. The most visible structural mark of Dionysian influence on Pico appears in the *Oration*, where the ascent program is articulated as a *triplex via* — a threefold path. Sophia Howlett's analysis of the *Oration* situates this pattern precisely in the Dionysian *Celestial Hierarchy*: the three stages of cleansing, then illumination, and afterward perfection — *purgatio, illuminatio*, and *unitio* (or *perfectio*) — provide the scaffolding for Pico's description of what the soul must do if it is to climb Jacob's ladder (Howlett 2021, 190–193). Pico tells his auditors that they must emulate the cherubic life on earth, "curbing the drive of the emotions through moral science, dispersing the darkness of reason through dialectics," and then "imbue our purified and well-prepared soul with the light of natural philosophy so that afterward we may perfect it with the knowledge of things divine." The three disciplines — moral philosophy, natural philosophy, theology — map precisely onto the Dionysian triad of purgation, illumination, and union. This is not a loose analogy: Pico names Dionysius directly as the authority for this structure in the *Heptaplus*, where he explicitly follows "the footsteps of Dionysius, or rather of Paul and Hierotheus" (*Dionysii vestigiis insistentes, aut Pauli potius et Hierothei*) in leading the soul upward beyond what it can receive in the weakness of its present condition.

The *Heptaplus* (1489), Pico's sevenfold exegesis of the first account of creation in Genesis, is saturated with Dionysian influence at both the macrostructural and microhermeneutical levels. At the macrostructural level, the tripartite cosmic model — angelic or intellectual world, celestial world, sublunar elemental world — is an adaptation of the Neoplatonic scheme as transmitted through Pseudo-Dionysius. Black demonstrates that Pico's theory of allegory in the *Heptaplus* derives primarily from the Dionysian corpus, specifically from the principles articulated in the *Celestial Hierarchy* about how material symbols function as vehicles for intellectual ascent (Black 2006, 161–175). Pseudo-Dionysius had argued that Scripture's use of material images for celestial and divine realities was not a category error but an epistemological strategy: the reader who understands the correspondence between levels of the hierarchy can use these images as "a launching pad" to rise to a comprehension of the simplicity that underlies them. Dionysius calls this movement *anagoge* (ἀναγωγή), "upward-leading," and describes the sacred texts as *anagogikai hierographiai* — upward-leading sacred symbols designed to lift the reader's mind toward the divine. Pico inherits this framework directly. His interpretive method in the *Heptaplus* proposes that the same words of Genesis simultaneously describe all three worlds because the worlds themselves are related through hierarchical correspondence, and the reader who grasps this correspondence performs, in the act of reading, an ascent through the worlds. Black concludes that "Pico used the writings of Pseudo-Dionysius to develop a theory of scriptural exegesis which was anagogical in the Dionysian sense — which led the reader upwards towards God" (Black 2006, 175).

The *Heptaplus*'s eighth and final book, an exposition of the single opening word of Genesis — *bereshit* ("in the beginning") — exemplifies this Dionysian hermeneutic at its most concentrated. Having completed the seven expositions that correspond to the seven days of creation and the seven levels of the threefold cosmic structure, Pico strips away all the accumulated framework and presents a reading of a single word that is itself an ascent beyond the multiplicity of the preceding expositions. The movement replicates the logic of the *Mystical Theology*, in which the soul first affirms all the names of God drawn from the *Divine Names* and then negates them one by one, ascending through denial into the divine darkness. Pico's anagogical reading of *bereshit* in the eighth book functions as the culmination and annulment of everything the preceding seven books have accomplished, pointing toward an intelligible unity that exceeds all the distinctions the commentator has drawn.

In the *900 Conclusions* (1486), Pico included a section of theses *secundum Dionysium* — "according to Dionysius" — placing him alongside Plotinus, Proclus, and the Kabbalists as one of the ancient *prisca theologia* authorities who, across cultural and religious difference, pointed toward the same underlying truth that found its completion in Christian revelation. Dionysius's authority as an ancient witness — still believed to have been Paul's convert — gave him an extraordinary position in this project of concordance. He was not a Platonist who happened to be later Christianized, as Ficino might have viewed Plotinus; he was a Christian who had stood in the direct presence of the apostle and had brought the highest Neoplatonic metaphysics into the faith from the beginning. This was precisely what made the *Corpus Dionysiacum* so indispensable to Pico's concordist program.

One dimension of Pico's Dionysian reception that merits particular attention is his modification of the Dionysian angelic hierarchy. Pseudo-Dionysius organized the angels into nine orders arranged in three triads. Pico, as Howlett observes, changed this number to ten — the number of the Kabbalistic sefirot (Howlett 2021, 115). This small but significant alteration illustrates the characteristically Piconian move of receiving an ancient authority while quietly transforming it to accommodate the concordance of traditions he was constructing. Dionysius provides the hierarchical framework; Kabbalah provides the numerological specificity; the resulting synthesis belongs to neither tradition alone. The coordination was also theological: the ten sefirot, as the divine attributes through which God acts in creation, could be mapped onto the Dionysian hierarchy of modes of divine self-communication, allowing Pico to claim that the Jewish mystical tradition and the Christian Neoplatonic tradition were describing the same ontological structure under different names.

Pico's use of the *Mystical Theology* in particular deserves notice. The *Mystical Theology* is the text in the Dionysian corpus most explicitly concerned with what lies beyond all hierarchy, all predication, and all intellectual grasp. Moses ascending into the divine darkness — *skotos* — that exceeds all knowing and all unknowing is its central image. Pico's *mors osculi* — the death by kiss that stands as the highest attainment of the *Oration*'s ascent program — evokes this Dionysian culmination, translated into Kabbalistic terms as *mitat neshikah* (death by divine kiss), but structured by the same logic: the soul that reaches the highest point dissolves into a union that cannot be conceptualized, named, or returned from. Howlett characterizes this as Pico's version of *henosis* — a union conceived not as Ficino's temporary vision of the angelic mind and return to the world, but as a solitary, ascetic, non-returnable absorption into the divine that corresponds precisely to the *Mystical Theology*'s culminating darkness (Howlett 2021, 191–208).

This last point also marks the limit of Pico's Dionysianism and the point at which his engagement with *De ente et uno* (1490) creates a tension. In that treatise Pico argued, against Ficino and the Platonist tradition, that the One is convertible with Being rather than beyond Being — a position that moderates the most radical strand of apophatic theology, including the *Mystical Theology*'s insistence that the divine transcends even the category of existence. Pico's theological metaphysics in *De ente et uno* is closer to Thomas Aquinas than to Plotinus or to Pseudo-Dionysius in their most apophatic registers. Whether this represents a genuine revision of his earlier Dionysian commitments or simply reflects the different rhetorical register of a scholastic disputation remains a question the scholarship has not fully resolved. What is clear is that the Dionysian legacy in Pico is not monolithic: the *Mystical Theology*'s mystical ascent beyond all categories sits in productive tension with the more affirmative theological framework of *De ente et uno*, and this tension is itself characteristic of the Renaissance encounter with Neoplatonism.

The critical significance of Black's and Howlett's studies lies in their independent convergence on the centrality of the Dionysian framework to Pico's most ambitious works. Where older historiography tended to present Pico primarily as a Platonist or a Kabbalist, these scholars restore Pseudo-Dionysius to his proper position as the structural mediator between the Greek philosophical tradition and the Christian mystical tradition in Pico's thought. The *Corpus Dionysiacum* provided Pico with a system in which hierarchy, hermeneutics, and mystical ascent were philosophically integrated — and that integration was precisely what his project required.
