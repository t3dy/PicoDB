*Ein Sof* (אֵין סוֹף, literally "without end" or "the Infinite") is the Kabbalistic designation for the utterly transcendent divine reality that precedes and exceeds the ten sefirot. In classical Kabbalistic theology, *Ein Sof* is God before and beyond any determination — not a sefirah, not a being among beings, not accessible through any of the divine names or attributes. The ten sefirot emanate from *Ein Sof* as the divine life descending into finitude, but *Ein Sof* itself remains without limit, without predicate, and in the strictest sense without name. Any name, including the Tetragrammaton (יהוה, *YHWH*), belongs to the sefirot and their operation within and toward the world; *Ein Sof* as such exceeds naming entirely.

This doctrine crystallized in the thirteenth-century school of Kabbalists at Gerona, whose most systematic representative was Rabbi Azriel of Gerona. Azriel's *Quaestiones super Decem Numerationibus* (Hebrew *She'elot u-Teshuvot be-'Inyan ha-Sefirot*), which Pico read in the Latin translation made by Flavius Mithridates, treats *Ein Sof* in terms of the ancient Neoplatonic problem of the One and the Many. As Chaim Wirszubski demonstrated in his posthumous study *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989), this text was a decisive source for Pico's own Kabbalistic theses on *Ein Sof*. Azriel's argument is that *Ein Sof* is the unity (*unitas*) that underlies all the sefirot, not as a superordinate member of their sequence but as their ground and source. In Mithridates' translation, Azriel writes that "omnes numerationes ipsae procedant ab ensoph extra quem nihil est" — all the enumerated powers proceed from *Ein Sof*, outside of which nothing exists (Wirszubski 1989, 237–238). Mithridates translated this phrase, which in the Hebrew original corresponds closely to John the Scot Eriugena's *praeter eum nihil est*, in the distinctive form "nihil est extra se" — "nothing is outside it" — a phrasing that fuses Azriel's Kabbalistic apophatics with the self-contained infinity of Neoplatonic philosophical theology.

Pico's engagement with *Ein Sof* appears most directly in his *900 Conclusions* (1486), specifically in Conclusio iv of the Kabbalistic theses *secundum opinionem propriam* — his own opinion, not merely a report of Kabbalistic doctrine. The thesis reads: "Ensoph non est aliis numerationibus connumeranda, quia est illarum numerationum unitas abstracta et incommunicata, non unitas coordinata" — *Ein Sof* is not to be counted among the other numerations [sefirot], because it is the abstract and uncommunicated unity of those numerations, not a coordinate unity among them (Wirszubski 1989, 235). The grammatical oddity that Pico writes *Ensoph* in the feminine, whereas the Hebrew noun is masculine, reflects the immediate mediation of Mithridates' translation: the feminine appears in the Latin translation of Abraham Axelrad's *Corona Nominis Boni*, which Wirszubski identifies as the probable direct source of Pico's second *secretam doctrinam* thesis, and which may have induced Pico's usage (1989, 235–236). This apparently minor philological detail illustrates the broader methodological finding of Wirszubski's study: Pico's Kabbalistic theses are not independent constructions but are closely tied to the specific vocabulary and grammar of the Latin translations he was reading, translations which were themselves sometimes contaminated, interpolated, or misread.

Pico returns to *Ein Sof* in Conclusiones xxxv and xxxvi, which concern God's self-concealment and the mediation of the sefirot. Conclusio xxxv articulates the apophatic extreme: when God is understood purely in himself as infinite, as one, and according to himself as such, nothing is understood to proceed from him — only a "separation from things and an utter closure of himself within himself and an extreme, most profound and solitary withdrawal into the remotest recesses of his divinity, concealing himself in the abyss of his own darkness and in no way manifesting himself in the diffusion and outpouring of his goodnesses" (Wirszubski 1989, 237). This is a remarkably precise formulation of the apophatic side of the *Ein Sof* doctrine: the absolute withdrawnness of the infinite God is not yet emanation, not yet creation, not yet revelation. Conclusio xxxvi then supplies the complementary affirmation: it is from the preceding conclusion that one can understand why the Kabbalists say that God clothed himself in ten garments when he created the world — the sefirot are the garments, the mediating divine powers, through which the hidden *Ein Sof* becomes accessible to creation.

Pico's syncretic reading mapped *Ein Sof* onto several distinct traditions simultaneously. The most obvious analogue is the Neoplatonic One-beyond-Being of Plotinus, which likewise exceeds predication, name, and number. Pico's acquaintance with the Geronese Kabbalistic tradition, particularly through Azriel, reinforced this alignment: Azriel is, as Wirszubski calls him, "the outstanding Jewish representative of the Neoplatonic doctrine that all opposites coincide in God" (1989, 238), and the similarity between Azriel's *Ein Sof* doctrine and Nicolaus Cusanus' *coincidentia oppositorum* was later noted by Johannes Reuchlin, who almost certainly drew on what Pico had initiated. Within Pico's own program, *Ein Sof* also maps onto the Pseudo-Dionysian divine darkness (*caligo* or *tenebrae divinae*) — that apophatic register in which the highest divine reality exceeds all positive theological predicates and is reached, if at all, only through the negation of negation.

However, the mapping between Kabbalistic and Neoplatonic apophatics is considerably more fraught than Pico's concordist program acknowledges. This tension is sharpened by Pico's own philosophical treatise *De ente et uno* (1491–1492), which argues against the Neoplatonic position — shared by Plotinus and by Marsilio Ficino — that the One is beyond being (*supra ens*). For Pico, following Thomas Aquinas and a certain reading of Aristotle, being and the One are convertible properties: the One is not beyond being but identical with it at the highest level. The implication is philosophically significant: if Pico's Aristotelian-scholastic ontology is correct, then *Ein Sof* cannot be simply identified with the Plotinian One-beyond-Being, because for Pico that Plotinian One is a mistaken abstraction. The Kabbalistic *Ein Sof*, which in Azriel's treatment is explicitly infinite and beyond the sefirot as their unmanifest ground, does not map cleanly onto either the Plotinian superessential One or onto the Thomistic identification of God with being itself (*esse ipsum*). Pico appears to have been aware of this complexity without fully resolving it: his *900 Conclusions* handles *Ein Sof* within a Kabbalistic-Neoplatonic register, while his *De ente et uno* argues a position that complicates the Neoplatonic side of that very register.

The transmission of *Ein Sof* through Mithridates' translations involved not only Azriel's Geronese material but also strands from other sources in the four Vatican manuscripts that constituted Pico's Kabbalistic library. The anonymous *De Proportione Divinitatis* (*Ma'areket ha-Elohut*), portions of Menahem Recanati's Commentary on the Pentateuch, and fragments of Joseph Gicatilla's *Sa'arey Sedeq* (Gates of Righteousness), all translated into Latin by Mithridates, provided additional angles on the sefirot and their relationship to the transcendent ground. Wirszubski's reconstruction of the four manuscripts — Codices Vaticani Ebraici 189–191 and Codex Chigi A.VI.190, amounting in their current state to roughly 3,500 pages in folio — makes clear that Pico's exposure to *Ein Sof* was not through a single authoritative text but through a multiplicity of texts representing different schools and historical strata of Kabbalistic thought (Wirszubski 1989, 10–16). The result is a composite Pichian *Ein Sof* that draws primarily on the Geronese tradition as mediated by Azriel, supplemented by the Abraham Axelrad tradition, with Mithridates serving as both translator and, in places, interpolator — inserting his own theological observations and at least once importing the language of Nicolaus Cusanus into an Abulafian context.

The Christian theological significance that Pico found in *Ein Sof* was primarily its confirmation of what he took to be the hidden Christological and Trinitarian content of the Kabbalistic doctrine. In his Kabbalistic theses, the doctrine that God clothes himself in ten garments — the sefirot — to create the world is read as a figure for the divine condescension into incarnation and creation. The apophatic moment of *Ein Sof* provides the background against which Pico can argue that the Jewish mystical tradition itself acknowledges a hidden God who becomes manifest through a structured mediation — a pattern he reads as anticipating, and confirming, the Christian theology of the Word through whom all things were made. This line of argument, which appears in both the *900 Conclusions* and the *Apology* written in defense of the condemned theses, became the template for subsequent Christian Kabbalists. Johann Reuchlin's *De Verbo Mirifico* (1494) and *De Arte Cabalistica* (1517) take up the *Ein Sof* doctrine directly, and Reuchlin is explicit about his debt to Pico's initiative. Paulus Ricius, Egidio da Viterbo, and later Guillaume Postel all worked within a framework that Pico had established: *Ein Sof* as the highest apophatic moment within a Kabbalistic cosmology that could be read as converging with Christian theology at its summit.

There remains a significant layer of historiographical caution that any engagement with Pico's *Ein Sof* must acknowledge. Pico was not a fluent reader of Hebrew in 1486 when he composed the *900 Conclusions*; by his own account in the *Commento*, his desire for Kabbalah was what drove him to begin studying Hebrew and Aramaic, and the study was recent — a matter of weeks or months, not years. The range and depth of his engagement with *Ein Sof* as a Kabbalistic concept was therefore determined not by independent access to the Hebrew sources but by what Mithridates translated, how Mithridates translated it, and what Mithridates added. Wirszubski's meticulous reconstruction of the translation manuscripts shows that Mithridates was himself a learned but sometimes idiosyncratic intermediary, capable of importing concepts from Cusanus or from Christian theological frames into the heart of a Hebrew Kabbalistic text. This means that what Pico encountered as "the Kabbalistic doctrine of *Ein Sof*" was in part Mithridates' own synthetic construction. The degree to which Pico's Conclusio iv and Conclusiones xxxv–xxxvi faithfully represent the Geronese Kabbalistic position, the degree to which they have been filtered and transformed by Mithridates' translation choices, and the degree to which Pico himself shaped the material to fit his concordist purposes — these remain matters requiring philological discrimination at every point.
