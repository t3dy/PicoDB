Abu Nasr Muhammad al-Farabi (c. 872–950 CE), known in the Latin West as "Alfarabius" or "Alfarabi," was the first systematic philosopher in the Islamic tradition to subordinate the legacy of Greek thought to a comprehensive account of knowledge, prophecy, and political order. Writing in Arabic under the Abbasid caliphate at Baghdad and later at the Hamdanid court of Aleppo, al-Farabi was honoured by later Islamic thinkers with the title *al-mu'allim al-thani*, "the Second Teacher," placing him after Aristotle in the hierarchy of philosophical authority. His work reached the Latin West piecemeal — the *De intellectu* circulated in a medieval Latin translation, his *Al-madina al-fadila* (On the Perfect State) was partially transmitted through Hebrew intermediaries, and his logical commentaries on the Organon were available in various scholastic compilations — but even in this fragmented form his influence on the intellectual environment that produced Giovanni Pico della Mirandola was considerable. The *900 Conclusions* of 1486 include eleven theses *secundum Alfarabium*, placing al-Farabi alongside Averroes, Avicenna, and Maimonides in Pico's formal taxonomy of the Arabic "nation" of thinkers who, in Pico's structural scheme, represent a middle station between the conflicted Latin scholastics and the more harmonious Platonists of antiquity.

The philosophical contribution for which al-Farabi matters most to the intellectual environment of the *900 Conclusions* is his elaboration of the Active Intellect (*al-'aql al-fa''al*, *intellectus agens*) within an emanationist cosmological hierarchy. Building on a Neoplatonized Aristotelianism — blended through his reading of Alexander of Aphrodisias, Themistius, Porphyry, and the Neoplatonic tradition that had been transmitted into Arabic partly through the pseudo-Aristotelian *Theology* (in reality an epitome of Plotinus) — al-Farabi proposed that reality flows in an ordered series of emanations from the First Being through successive cosmic intellects corresponding to the celestial spheres. The Active Intellect is the tenth and lowest member of this hierarchy, the intelligence governing the lunar sphere and standing at the boundary between the purely intellectual world above and the sublunary world of generation and corruption below. As Herbert Davidson's foundational study (*Alfarabi, Avicenna, and Averroes, on Intellect*, 1992) clarifies, the Active Intellect in al-Farabi's system is not an immanent power of the human mind but an external separate substance that illuminates the potential intellect of each human being when that intellect has been sufficiently prepared through the acquisition of intelligibles. This is a decisive point for the reception of al-Farabi's ideas in the Renaissance: the Active Intellect belongs to the cosmic rather than the psychological order, and conjunction with it (*ittissal* in the Arabic vocabulary, *continuatio* in the Latin translations) is not a merely internal mental event but a genuine encounter between a human cognitive capacity and a suprahuman intelligence.

In al-Farabi's *Al-madina al-fadila*, the hierarchy of intellectual states within the individual human being culminates in what he calls the "acquired intellect" (*al-'aql al-mustafad*), attained when the potential intellect "has been perfected" by apprehending "all intelligibles." As Davidson summarizes, the person who reaches this state "holds the most perfect rank of humanity and has reached the highest degree of felicity (*sa'ada*)" (Davidson 1992, 49, citing al-Farabi, *On the Perfect State*, ed. Walzer, 243–245). At this summit, the human soul is united in some sense with the Active Intellect, the soul no longer requires matter for its operations, and the question of immortality is answered affirmatively: the acquired intellect, precisely because it has been absorbed into the sphere of separate intelligences, can survive bodily death. Crofton Black's comprehensive analysis of Pico's intellect sources (*Pico della Mirandola's Heptaplus and Biblical Hermeneutics*, 2006) situates this Farabian account firmly within the larger discourse of *felicitas* that Black identifies as the unifying problematic of Pico's philosophical project. The spectrum of positions in the *900 Conclusions* — from the Averroist thesis that "the ultimate felicity of man is when the active intellect is joined to the potential intellect as its form" (*Conclusions*, 7.3 Averroes) to the Plotinian thesis that felicity consists in the particular intellect's conjunction with the first and total intellect — reflects a debate whose original shape was partly determined by al-Farabi's formulation.

The eleven theses *secundum Alfarabium* in the *900 Conclusions* are, as Stephen Farmer's critical edition notes (Farmer 1998, 268–270), drawn predominantly from al-Farabi's logical commentaries on Aristotle's *Posterior Analytics* and *Prior Analytics*, which Pico knew through medieval Latin translations. Thesis 9.1 corrects Aristotle's definition of the first premiss of demonstration; theses 9.2 through 9.5 engage with questions of genus, species, predication, and the structure of necessary inference. These logical theses are technically demanding and reflect the Farabian tradition of commenting on the Organon as the preparatory instrument for all further philosophical knowledge. Thesis 9.3 — that "man's highest good is perfection through the speculative sciences" — is the most philosophically charged of the set, and Farmer's note to it is crucial: "In the proem to *Heptaplus* 7 Pico tells us that al-Farabi dealt exclusively with 'natural' and not 'supernatural' happiness" (Farmer 1998, 269). This note discloses a subtle but important critical distance. In the proem to the seventh book of the *Heptaplus* (1489), written after the condemnation crisis had forced Pico into greater theological caution, he invokes al-Farabi in a context that limits the Arabic philosopher's authority precisely to the *felicitas* available within the natural order. Where al-Farabi's Active Intellect provides the highest intelligible illumination that philosophy unaided can reach, the *Heptaplus* insists that this natural summit is surpassed by a supernatural beatitude available only through Scripture and divine grace. The distinction marks a boundary that Pico draws between philosophical reason, whose resources he has assembled from the entire tradition, and theological *fides*, which alone can guarantee the ultimate end.

This boundary relates to one of the structural features of the *Apology* that Edelheit's study (*Ficino, Pico and Savonarola*, 2008) brings into relief. Pico's defence before the papal commission rests partly on a sustained distinction between *opinio* and *fides*, between what theologians hold as probable opinion and what the Church requires as necessary article of faith. The Arabic philosophical tradition — al-Farabi, Avicenna, and Averroes together — had developed the technical vocabulary and the argumentative structures for talking about intellectual ascent, conjunction with a separate intellect, and the summit of natural cognitive achievement without committing any of these claims to the register of theological necessity. This allowed Pico to draw on their accounts of *felicitas intellectualis* as a resource for understanding what natural reason and philosophical practice can achieve while insisting that such achievement remains on the side of *opinio* rather than *fides*. The Arabic tradition provides, in other words, not only a set of doctrines but a philosophical register — the register of the *probably true*, the *demonstrably possible*, the *defensible in argument* — that Pico exploits in the *Apology* to protect his more controversial theses from the charge of heresy.

Al-Farabi's specific contribution to the broader Arabic influence on Pico is clarified by contrast with the other figures in Pico's Arab section. Where Averroes pressed the unicity of the potential intellect in a direction that Thomas Aquinas found theologically catastrophic (denying individual immortality and personal moral accountability), al-Farabi's version of the conjunction doctrine was more accommodating to Christian concerns: the human individual who achieves the acquired intellect does not dissolve into a single intellect shared by all humanity but rather achieves a personal relation with a cosmic intelligence that does not eliminate individual persistence. Similarly, al-Farabi's political philosophy — his account of the *madina fadila*, the perfect state ruled by the philosopher-prophet who combines speculative wisdom with prophetic imaginative power — offered resources that Pico could draw on in the *Oration*'s account of the philosopher's ascent as simultaneously a contemplative and a political programme, an inner transformation that also reconfigures the thinker's relation to community and tradition. The philosopher-king of the *Oration*, who becomes first a Mosaic legislator and then a seraphic contemplative, carries traces of al-Farabi's synthesis of Aristotelian political science and Neoplatonic intellectualism.

Al-Farabi's doctrine of the Active Intellect also connects laterally with the Kabbalistic strand of Pico's syncretism. Chaim Wirszubski's meticulous reconstruction of Pico's Kabbalistic sources (*Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism*, 1989) shows that one of the central texts Pico received through the translations of Flavius Mithridates, the *Liber Redemptionis* (most probably an Abulafian commentary on the secrets of the *Guide of the Perplexed*), "contains the doctrine of the Active Intellect as 'the prince of the divine presence' (*sar ha-panim*) whose name is like the name of his master, with all that is involved in that identification and with all that it implies with regard to the actualization of the human intellect, its adherence to the active intellect, and its union with the latter" (Wirszubski 1989, 84–100). The prophetic Kabbalah of Abraham Abulafia, which proved central to Pico's reading of the Jewish mystical tradition, was itself deeply shaped by Maimonidean philosophy and, through Maimonides, by the Farabian and Avicennan tradition of the Active Intellect as the source of prophetic illumination. When Pico in the *Oration* describes the ascent through the angelic hierarchies toward seraphic union, the Active Intellect of al-Farabi — the separate substance that illuminates the prepared human mind with intelligibles — is one of the conceptual ancestors of that angelic illumination, even if Pico has reframed it through Dionysian and Kabbalistic registers.

The historiographical stakes of al-Farabi's presence in the *900 Conclusions* are considerable. Early readings of Pico that treated him primarily as a Christian Platonist or a proto-humanist tended to subordinate or ignore the Arabic strand of his sources. Farmer's edition brought the structural importance of the Arab section into focus, demonstrating that Pico organised the historical portion of the *Conclusiones* as a systematic genealogy from the most divided (the Latin scholastics) to the most harmonious (the ancient sages), with the Arabs representing a middle stage of increasing philosophical sophistication and decreasing sectarian conflict. Al-Farabi occupies position nine in Farmer's chart of the Arab nation, between Avicenna (eight) and the more obscure "Isaac of Narbonne" (ten), with eleven theses — fewer than Averroes's forty-one but more than the three assigned to Maimonides. This quantitative distribution already suggests how Pico read the relative philosophical importance of his Arabic sources: Averroes as the defining problematic of the intellect debate, Avicenna as the richest metaphysical and psychological resource, al-Farabi as primarily a logician whose doctrine of the acquired intellect and its *felicitas* provided a crucial conceptual complement.

What remains clear from all of the scholarship is that Pico's engagement with al-Farabi was not decorative. The eleven Farabian theses in the *Conclusiones* participate in a network of related claims about demonstration, predication, species, and cognition that runs across multiple sections of the historical part of the text and connects to the theses Pico advances in his own name on intellectual ascent and the summit of human perfection. The Farabian Active Intellect, stripped of any explicit identification with the Angel Gabriel of Islamic theology but retaining its function as the external illuminating intelligence of the prepared philosophical mind, becomes in Pico's synthesis one element of a layered account of angelic mediation between God and the human intellect — an account in which Neoplatonic henads, Dionysian hierarchies, Kabbalistic divine powers, and Arabic cosmic intellects are understood to be converging descriptions of the same structural reality.
