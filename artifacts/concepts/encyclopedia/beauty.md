# Beauty

Beauty — *bellezza* in the Italian of the *Commento*, *pulchritudo* in the Latin philosophical tradition — occupies an unusual and technically demanding position in Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's philosophy. It is the formal cause of love: the object that love aims at, the quality that draws the soul upward, the principle that gives the movement of *amor* its direction and its point. But beauty is also, for Pico, a deeply contested metaphysical category, one that he takes from Ficino and the Platonic tradition and submits to a more rigorous ontological analysis — with consequences that are sharper, more hierarchically severe, and ultimately less congenial to the phenomenological richness of Ficino's account of how beautiful things move us. The full argument is developed primarily in the *Commento sopra una canzona d'amore* (c. 1486, published 1519), where Pico's analysis of beauty constitutes the philosophical heart of his disagreement with Ficino's *De amore*.

The background is double: Platonic and Neoplatonic. In the *Symposium*, Plato had assigned beauty a privileged role in the ascent of the soul. The speech of Diotima (201D–212B) describes a ladder (*klimax*) that begins with love of a single beautiful body and ends with the vision of Beauty Itself (*auto to kalon*): an eternal, self-identical form that does not arise and perish with individual beautiful things but is the cause of their beauty. In the *Phaedrus* (249E–250E), Plato adds that beauty is the most visible of the Forms — the one that can be dimly perceived through the bodily senses in a way that justice or temperance cannot — and that the soul's first erotic response to a beautiful person is a kind of recognition, a memory of the perfect beauty it glimpsed before embodiment. These two accounts together gave Platonic love theory its distinctive shape: beauty is both the Form most accessible to perception and the one most directly connected with the erotic life of the soul. The Forms in general are objects of intellectual knowledge; beauty is the Form that provokes desire and draws the soul toward knowledge of the rest.

Plotinus, in *Ennead* I.6, "On Beauty," sharpened the Platonic analysis in a direction that would prove crucial for Ficino and Pico alike. Against the standard ancient definition of beauty as symmetry or proportion of parts (*symmetria*), Plotinus argues that symmetry cannot be the essence of beauty because beautiful things can be simple — a single gold object, a single tone, the beauty of knowledge or of virtue — and because the symmetry of parts is always beauty only derivatively, in virtue of a form that illuminates matter from within. Beauty is not the symmetry but the Form that imposes unity on the material plurality; it is the shining-through (*epiphainein*) of intelligible form in sensible matter. A face is beautiful not because its parts stand in certain proportions to one another but because a governing unity — an intelligible principle — has organized those parts into a coherent whole. When the Form has not fully penetrated the matter, the face is less beautiful; when the Form dominates, the face is beautiful in proportion to the Form's dominance. In the simplest cases, the beautiful thing is pure form without matter at all: the beauty of the soul, of intellect, of the Good itself.

Plotinus further argues that the soul recognizes beauty because it is itself of the same nature as the intelligible: the soul is formed by the same Ideas that constitute beauty, and it knows beauty by a kind of self-recognition (*Ennead* I.6.2–3). This account — beauty as intelligible form recognized by the soul's own formal nature — was the Neoplatonic inheritance that Ficino received and elaborated, and that Pico then subjected to critical revision. Ficino's formulation in the *De amore* is that beauty is the *splendor boni*, the radiance or splendor of the Good: it is the Good as it shines through the intelligible and sensible worlds, distributed through the three kinds of being (God, angel, soul, body) in a graduated hierarchy of participation. Divine beauty is the Good itself; angelic beauty is the intelligible reflection of the Good in the first mind; celestial beauty is the reflection of this in the world soul and the heavens; and corporeal beauty is the participated image of all these in the sensible world. Crucially, for Ficino, all four kinds of beauty are genuinely beauty: they differ in purity and degree but not in kind. A beautiful face participates in, and therefore genuinely instantiates, divine beauty, however dimly.

Pico's *Commento* accepts the hierarchy but redraws its ontological lines more strictly. The fundamental move is his definition of beauty — stated most explicitly in *Commento* 2.8 — as the quality that results from the ordering of diverse things into a concordant whole: *una amica inimicizia, ovvero una discorde concordia* ("a friendly enmity, or a harmonious discord"). This definition, drawn from Heraclitean and Empedoclean sources and from the Neoplatonic analysis of how unity and multiplicity coexist in the angelic intellect, has important consequences. Beauty is essentially a relational property: it is not the property of any single simple thing but the property of a manifold organized into unity. This means that beauty, properly speaking, is not a property of God, who is absolutely simple and without composition. Allen documents the remarkable moment in the *Commento* where Pico, glossing the lemma *Che poi che in sè* in the particular commentary on the seventh and eighth stanzas, argues that "there is no beauty in God, according to the Platonists, because of his infinite simplicity, beauty having been previously defined as an ordering of parts into a whole" — and therefore that we can only love God "in Himself" and not "as the author of ideal beauty" (Allen 2017, 146). This is a pointed departure from Ficino, for whom the Good and Beauty are almost interchangeable at the divine level, and it reflects a scholastic insistence on the distinction between God's absolute simplicity and the composite character of everything that proceeds from God.

Where, then, does beauty properly reside? For Pico, its primary locus is the angelic intellect: the first and noblest creature that God creates directly, identical with what Plotinus calls Nous (Mind) and what Christian theology calls the angelic world. This angelic intellect is, in Pico's analysis, the first composite being — the first being that consists of act and potency, of the limit and the unlimited, of unity and multiplicity. The Ideas or Forms of all things that will be created have their formal being in this intellect, and their collective ordering into the unity of Mind is what Pico identifies as the primary instance of beauty. The Ideas in God have only causal being: they are not yet beauty but the cause of beauty. The Ideas in Mind have formal being: they are beauty, the *grazia e decore* (grace and comeliness) that result from their diversity ordered into unity. This is the beauty of the celestial Venus, Urania, who in Pico's allegorization is born when the Ideas are first planted in the still-unformed substance of angelic Mind — the "natal day of Venus" on which beauty first comes into being as a formal property of an intelligible composite.

The birth of Venus, and the birth of Love that accompanies it, is the occasion for some of Pico's most technically dense and philosophically original allegoresis. Following the passage in Plato's *Symposium* (203B) in which Diotima describes Love as born in the gardens of Jupiter on the birthday of Venus, after Porus (Plenty) joined with Penia (Poverty) when the gods were seated at the banquet, Pico constructs an elaborate account of how beauty and love arise simultaneously in angelic Mind. In *Commento* 2.13, he argues that Porus signifies the plenitude of the Ideas in Mind — the multiplicity of intelligible forms — while Penia signifies not the essence of Mind per se but its still-unformed, deficient, or imperfect state before the Ideas have been fully organized within it. Beauty (Venus) is born when the Ideas first enter Mind, but at that moment beauty is still imperfect, because the Ideas are imperfectly ordered within the still partly unformed substance. Love is then born as Mind's desire to possess the Ideas "in their perfect form," a desire to achieve that complete ordering of multiplicity into unity that constitutes perfect beauty. Mind turns toward God, receives the perfecting of the Ideas, and Venus thereby grows from an infant goddess into a fully mature one — which is to say, beauty in the angelic intellect reaches its perfection when the angelic intellect achieves full intelligible unity (Allen 2017, 150–152).

This account has several important philosophical consequences. First, it means that beauty is essentially a dynamic property: it is not simply present in a thing but achieved through the thing's return to its source and perfection of its intelligible form. The angelic intellect becomes fully beautiful only when it turns back toward God and completes its circuit. Second, it means that love and beauty are co-originate and mutually implicating: beauty causes love by presenting itself as an object of desire, and love causes beauty to achieve its perfection by drawing Mind back to God. Third, and most important for the account of human love and ascent, it means that the beauty available to sense perception is not genuinely beauty in the primary sense but only a participated, degraded, and imperfect image of it. The beauty of a beautiful body is beauty insofar as it participates in the ordered unity that constitutes formal beauty in the angelic intellect, but it participates in this only weakly and through multiple removes, through the mediation of the world soul, the celestial spheres, and the generative powers of the lower Venus. It is, in the Neoplatonic technical vocabulary, a beauty "by participation" (*per participazione*) rather than beauty in its formal being.

The distinction between beauty by participation and beauty in its formal being is what grounds Pico's insistence that genuine philosophical love must orient itself toward intelligible rather than sensible beauty. Ficino, drawing on the same Plotinian source, allows that the soul's response to sensible beauty is the first movement of genuine ascent: the beautiful face awakens in the soul a memory of divine beauty, and this awakening is the beginning of philosophy. Pico's position is more severe. Because sensible beauty is not beauty in the proper sense but only a trace (*vestigium*) or participated image of real beauty, the soul that attaches itself to sensible beauty is not yet loving beauty at all — it is loving the image of beauty, which is a different thing. The three Venuses do not represent three legitimate levels of ascent so much as three metaphysical levels of beauty with correspondingly different relations to the ideal: only celestial beauty — the beauty of the angelic intellect — is beauty in its proper sense, and only the love oriented toward it is love in the philosophically serious sense of *amor*.

The Plotinian source is *Ennead* I.6, which Pico knows through Ficino's translations and through the general Neoplatonic framework he absorbed in Florence and in his Parisian studies. But where Plotinus treats the ascent to intelligible beauty as a gradual purification — the soul climbing from the beauty of bodies to the beauty of souls to the beauty of knowledge to the beauty of the One — Pico tends to draw a sharper line between the sensible and the intelligible, influenced in part by the scholastic distinction between participated and formal being that he took from his Aristotelian training. The result is that beauty in Pico's hands is less of a continuum and more of a hierarchical structure with clearly demarcated levels, each with its own ontological status and each grounding a correspondingly specific form of love.

The *Commento* also develops an account of beauty's connection to the ascent described in the *Oration*. In that text, Pico presents man as the creature placed at the center of the world, free to ascend or descend through the levels of being, and the philosophical life as a program of ascent through the disciplines, the Platonic purifications, and the angelic intelligences toward mystical union with God. Beauty in the *Commento* is the motor of this ascent: it is what draws the soul upward at each level, the visible face of the intelligible order that provokes love and thereby movement. The *mors osculi* — the "death by kiss" that terminates the ascent, when the soul in intellectual rapture separates entirely from the body and unites with the heavenly Venus, with the Idea of Beauty itself — is the completion of the journey that beauty initiates. At that summit, the soul no longer seeks beauty but becomes beautiful, absorbed into the intelligible unity that is Beauty's highest form. The soul's transformation into the angelic — what the Kabbalists described as Enoch's transformation into Metatron — is simultaneously a transformation into beauty: to become pure intellect is to participate fully in the intelligible beauty that constitutes the angelic order.

Several important qualifications must be kept in view when assessing the significance of Pico's account of beauty. Allen has noted that the *Commento* is an unfinished and in some respects inconsistent work: Pico raises difficulties and sometimes fails to resolve them, and his denial that God is beautiful sits uneasily with the Neoplatonic tradition's tendency to identify Beauty with the One or the Good at the highest ontological level (Allen 2017, 146). The suggestion that only intelligible beauty is properly beauty also generates questions about the status of celestial and sublunary beauty that the *Commento* does not fully address. And the reception history documented by Jayne shows that later readers did not always follow Pico's stricter hierarchical analysis: the French and English Neoplatonists who received the *Commento* through Claude Chappuys's translation (1544–1548) tended to absorb Pico's account into a broadly Ficinian framework in which sensible beauty retains a more positive role in the ascent of the soul. The critical edge of Pico's definition was blunted almost from the moment of the text's first wide circulation. Recovering it — understanding *bellezza* in the *Commento* as a technical concept with a precise and restrictive definition rather than a synonym for whatever is aesthetically pleasing — is one of the permanent methodological contributions of twentieth-century Pico scholarship.
