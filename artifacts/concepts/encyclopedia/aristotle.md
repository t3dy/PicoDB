Few questions in Pico studies have been more persistently contested than the question of what Aristotle actually does in his philosophy. Whether he should be read primarily as a Platonist who deploys Aristotelian vocabulary when convenient, or as a thinker whose deepest commitments are structurally Aristotelian, has divided scholars across more than a century of interpretation. The stakes are not merely biographical. They concern how Pico's concordist project is to be understood: whether the claimed harmony between Plato and Aristotle is a rhetorical gesture, a philosophical achievement, or something in between — a genuinely productive tension that neither resolution nor dismissal can fully capture.

Pico's engagement with Aristotle was grounded in an unusually thorough formation. He studied in Padua, the center of Aristotelian philosophy in the Italian Quattrocento, where the recovery of Aristotle was being prosecuted from two directions simultaneously. The older scholastic tradition, transmitted through Averroes and the commentaries that had made Aristotle the philosopher of the universities, was still very much alive in Padua in the person of Elijah del Medigo, the Jewish Averroist who taught Pico and wrote treatises on the intellect at Pico's explicit request (Howlett 2021, 63). Alongside this stood the new neo-Aristotelian movement represented by Nicoletto Vernia and Ermolao Barbaro, who sought to return to the Greek text of Aristotle and read him through the early peripatetic commentators — Themistius and Simplicius — rather than through Averroes's Arabic mediations. Pico studied with both tendencies, and his letters with Barbaro from 1485 show him already committed to a synthesis that would go beyond either camp. He told Ficino at one point that his aim had been to "compare Plato with Aristotle, and in turn Aristotle with Plato, in alternate studies" (Howlett 2021, 71), a formulation that suggests the concordist project was conceived from the start as a genuinely reciprocal philosophical exercise rather than an assimilation of one tradition to the other.

The most explicit monument to this project among Pico's surviving works is the *De ente et uno* of 1491, dedicated to Angelo Poliziano and circulated rather than published. Its stated purpose is to resolve a quarrel about the relation of Being and the One that had divided Platonic from Aristotelian metaphysics since antiquity and that in the fifteenth century had become a flashpoint between Ficino and the Paduan Aristotelians. Pico's solution proceeds by arguing that Plato and Aristotle actually agree: *ens* (being) and *unum* (unity) are coextensive and mutually convertible, and both philosophers, rightly read, hold this position. The argument is in two stages. First, Pico handles the embarrassing Platonic evidence — particularly the *Parmenides* and the *Sophist*, which seem to place the One above Being — by denying that these dialogues intend metaphysical claims at all. The *Parmenides* is *nihil aliud quam dialectica quaedam exercitatio* (nothing other than a dialectical exercise) (*De ente et uno*, c. 2). This hermeneutical move has been criticized: Michael Allen argued that Ficino's interpretation, which takes the dialogues as making genuine metaphysical claims, is more faithful to the texts as wholes (Allen, "Second Ficino-Pico Controversy," 430–32). But Pico's second argument is philosophically more substantial. He distinguishes being as that which stands outside nothing (*extra nihilum*) from being taken in the concrete sense, and shows through a scheme of participation, drawing on Thomas Aquinas's commentary on Boethius's *De hebdomadibus*, that the identity of being and unity can be preserved even while making room for the Neoplatonic claim that something can be said to be superior to being in a different, eminential sense. As Victor Salas has shown, Pico's metaphysics of participation in the *De ente et uno* is deeply indebted to Thomistic distinctions between *esse* and *essentia*, and the entire structure of the argument presupposes Aquinas's real distinction as the mediating term (Salas 2014, 357–62).

In the *900 Conclusions* of 1486, Aristotle figures in a more diffuse but equally revealing way. The first major section of the *Conclusions* is organized historically, moving backward through the tradition from recent scholastics to the ancient philosophers, and it contains a substantial cluster of theses *secundum Aristotelem* — positions Pico proposes to defend as authentic implications of Aristotle's philosophy. Stephen Farmer's edition makes clear that these theses engage not just Aristotle's texts directly but the accumulated scholastic commentary tradition: Averroes, Aquinas, Scotus, and the later peripatetics all appear as voices through whom Aristotle's positions are mediated and contested (Farmer 1998, 8–11). The sheer number and philosophical seriousness of these theses establishes that Pico's claim to be drawing out the genuine content of Aristotelian philosophy is not mere performance. He is making specific philosophical arguments about what Aristotle holds, and those arguments are often quite technical.

Three problems in Aristotle's philosophy were particularly consequential for Pico's project, and his handling of each illuminates both the depth and the limits of his Aristotelianism. The first is the problem of the active intellect and individual immortality, which arises from Aristotle's *De anima*. Aristotle's account of the *nous poietikos* (the active intellect) is famously unclear about whether it belongs to the individual soul or to a separate principle, and the Averroist reading of the text — that the active intellect is a single separate substance shared by all human beings — was deeply threatening to Christian doctrine, since it entailed the denial of individual immortality. Pico was drawn to this problem through his Paduan formation, and Elijah del Medigo's treatises on the intellect addressed it at length from an Averroist perspective. Howlett argues that one of Pico's distinctive contributions was to appropriate the Aristotelian active intellect for a mystical program: in Pico's universe, *henosis* — the mystical union with the divine — can be a noetic process in which the individual intellect makes contact with the attributes of the divine associated with mind and understanding (Howlett 2021, 417). This is a reading that neither identifies straightforwardly with Averroism nor abandons the Aristotelian framework: it is, rather, a creative transformation of the *nous* problem into a resource for mystical theology.

The second problem is the eternity of the world, which Aristotle argues for in the *Physics* and *Metaphysics*. For Christian theology, the eternity of the world is incompatible with creation *ex nihilo*, and the Averroists who maintained it — or who maintained it as a philosophical conclusion separable from the theological truth of creation — were suspected of double-truth doctrine. Pico's position on this issue is not straightforward. The *Apology* shows him navigating very carefully between philosophical speculation and theological commitment, using a distinction between *opinio* (philosophical opinion) and *fides* (faith) that allowed him to say that the eternity of the world is philosophically discussable without being theologically authoritative. Amos Edelheit's reading of the *Apology* is the most systematic account of how this distinction operates: Pico draws on Thomistic categories to argue that the opinions of philosophers — even when they conflict with Christian doctrine — operate at the level of probable speculation, and that error in philosophical opinion is not the same as heresy in matters of faith (*quia errare in opinione Thomae, non est errare in fide*, as the chapter title of Edelheit's analysis puts it: to err in the opinion of Thomas is not to err in faith) (Edelheit 2008, 286–349). This move permits Pico to discuss Averroist positions on the eternity of the world — and Kabbalistic doctrines, and Neoplatonic metaphysics — without immediately claiming they are theologically authoritative. They are *opiniones* held for debate, not *fidei* asserted as revelation.

The third problem is the one that the *De ente et uno* addresses most directly: the relation of Being and the One in *Metaphysics* book XII and in the confrontation with the *Parmenides*. As Salas's analysis makes clear, Aristotle's claim in *Metaphysics* book 4 that being and unity (*to on kai to hen*) are the same is the pivot of Pico's irenic argument. The difficulty is that the Neoplatonists, including Plotinus, Proclus, and Ficino, had insisted that the *Parmenides* places the One above Being, and had built entire metaphysical structures on this hierarchy. Pico denies that Plato intended this metaphysical subordination, arguing instead that the dialogues are dialectical exercises, and that when Plato's actual commitments are extracted from the dialectical framing, they turn out to be consistent with Aristotle's position on the identity of being and unity. The concordist claim that Plato and Aristotle actually agree is not, on this reading, an easy harmonization: it requires Pico to take a hermeneutical position against his own teacher-figure Ficino, and to adjudicate between competing readings of both philosophers on one of the hardest problems in ancient metaphysics.

Sophia Howlett's 2021 study, *Re-evaluating Pico*, offers perhaps the most sustained recent argument for the structural centrality of Aristotle in Pico's thought. Howlett insists that Pico's three pillars — Aristotelianism, Platonism, and Kabbalism — are genuinely coordinate rather than ranked, and that Aristotelianism provides his primary mode of argumentation throughout: the insistence on dividing causal from participatory being, the commitment to a rational and scientific universe, the interest in the Aristotelian intellect as the locus of the ascent program (Howlett 2021, 55–58). On this reading, even the *Oration* — often treated as a primarily Platonic or Neoplatonic text — draws on Aristotelian categories. The language of *virtus*, *actus*, and *forma* in the ascent program reflects an Aristotelian framework that shapes even the most apparently mystical passages. Howlett's argument pushes back against the Neoplatonist consensus that had dominated Pico scholarship for much of the twentieth century, and invites a rereading of the entire corpus with Aristotelian categories in view.

Whether Howlett's corrective goes too far is a question that remains genuinely open. Pico's universe is, at the macro level, predominantly a Platonic structure, inflected by Aristotle rather than grounded in it (Howlett 2021, 55). The hierarchy of worlds in the *Heptaplus*, the soul's ascent in the *Oration*, the theory of love in the *Commento*: all of these are organized by frameworks derived from Platonic and Neoplatonic sources, even when Aristotelian terminology appears within them. Edelheit's reading, which emphasizes the *Apology* as Pico's most important methodological statement, places the governing distinction between *opinio* and *fides* at the center of his philosophical project — and that distinction is, at its origin, a Thomistic move (Edelheit 2008, 282–349). On this reading, Pico is neither simply an Aristotelian nor simply a Platonist, but a humanist theologian who uses both traditions, along with Kabbalism and Hermetic sources, within a framework of philosophical inquiry that is kept structurally separate from revealed truth.

What is not in dispute is that Aristotle is indispensable to Pico's project in ways that go beyond cosmetic citation. The *De ente et uno* is explicitly a resolution of the Plato-Aristotle quarrel, and its argument — however dependent on Thomistic metaphysics for its execution — is framed as a vindication of Aristotelian commitments about the transcendentals. The *900 Conclusions* devote a substantial cluster of theses to positions drawn from Aristotle's philosophy, and Pico's engagement with the Paduan Aristotelians — del Medigo, Vernia, Nifo — is not that of a Platonist using opponents' vocabulary but of a philosopher who has internalized multiple Aristotelian traditions and is trying to articulate their genuine content. When Pico told Barbaro he had been "a scout" (as an Aristotelian exploring the Platonic camp), he was describing an early position he subsequently complicated rather than sustained. By 1491, in the *De ente et uno*, he had moved from exploration to adjudication, and the adjudication is one that claims to vindicate both traditions by showing their agreement on the deepest metaphysical question — the convertibility of Being and the One — from which all other metaphysical questions depend.
