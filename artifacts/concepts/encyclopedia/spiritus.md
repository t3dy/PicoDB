# Spiritus

The concept of *spiritus* — "spirit" in the sense of a subtle, refined, quasi-material substance mediating between body and soul — runs through the physiological, cosmological, and magical dimensions of Pico della Mirandola's thought without ever receiving a single, consolidated treatment. It belongs to several distinct traditions that Pico simultaneously inherits, and its different valences in medical, Neoplatonic, Ficinian, and Kabbalistic contexts are important to distinguish if one is to understand both what Pico takes for granted from his environment and where he departs from it.

The ancient medical background is Galenic. In Galen's pneumatic physiology, which remained authoritative throughout the medieval and Renaissance periods, the body is animated by three distinct pneumata or spirits produced at three sites and performing three distinct functions. The *spiritus naturalis* (natural spirit) is manufactured in the liver from blood and distributed through the venous system, governing nutrition, growth, and reproduction. The *spiritus vitalis* (vital spirit) is produced in the heart, where it is refined from the natural spirit and distributed through the arterial system; it governs pulse, heat, and life in the basic sense. The *spiritus animalis* (animal spirit, from *anima*, soul), refined from the vital spirit in the brain and distributed through the nerves, governs sensation and voluntary movement. These three spirits constitute a graduated series of increasingly refined corporeal substances linking the densely material processes of digestion and growth to the operations of sensation and cognition. They are not immaterial — they are, on the Galenic account, genuinely corporeal, composed of the finest and most tenuous matter — but they are far subtler than ordinary blood or tissue, and they serve as the indispensable bridge between the gross body and whatever immaterial faculty one wishes to posit as the seat of genuine rationality.

The philosophical tradition running parallel to the medical one is the Platonic-Stoic doctrine of pneuma as the vehicle (*ὄχημα*, okhêma) of the soul. In Plato's *Timaeus*, the Demiurge distributes portions of the World Soul into the stars and bodies of the planets; when souls descend from these regions into bodies, they require a vehicle that can carry them through the material world and that serves as the interface between the immaterial soul and its bodily instrument. The Stoics developed a related but distinct account in which *pneuma* — a mixture of fire and air — is the active principle pervading the cosmos and binding it into a sympathetic whole. In later Neoplatonism, especially in Porphyry and Iamblichus, the soul-vehicle (sometimes called the *astral body* or *luminous body*) becomes a crucial metaphysical entity: it is the semi-material shell in which the soul is wrapped as it descends through the celestial spheres, acquiring at each level a coating of the sphere's qualities, and it is through this vehicle that the soul is affected by material and celestial influences during its embodied life. The tradition is synthesized and transmitted to the Renaissance most powerfully through Marsilio Ficino.

Ficino's doctrine of the *spiritus* is the proximate intellectual environment for anything Pico says about the concept, and Ficino's treatment in the *De vita* (1489) — particularly the third book, *De vita coelitus comparanda* — is the most influential Renaissance formulation of how *spiritus* connects medicine, natural magic, and cosmology. For Ficino, the universe itself possesses a *spiritus mundi* (world spirit), a fine quasi-material substance that permeates the cosmos as the medium through which celestial bodies exercise their influence on sublunary things. The *spiritus mundi* is not the World Soul itself, nor is it the celestial matter from which the spheres are composed; it is an intermediary — a subtle vapor or effusion that carries the qualities, powers, and "signatures" of the planets and stars downward into the terrestrial world. Individual things in the sublunary world receive celestial influence through their share of or sympathy with this *spiritus mundi*. And in the human being, the *spiritus animalis* produced in the brain is the most refined of the Galenic spirits and the one most naturally attuned to celestial influences: it is, in effect, the individual's portion of the *spiritus mundi*, and its quality determines how receptive the individual is to favorable celestial configurations.

What Copenhaver has analyzed as the fundamentally pneumatic character of Ficino's natural magic follows directly from this doctrine. When Ficino recommends that one wishing to attract the influence of the Sun eat saffron, wear gold, drink wine in moderation, contemplate images of solar deities, and listen to music written in the Dorian mode, these recommendations are not arbitrary or merely symbolic. Each of these interventions acts on the *spiritus* — on the fine, subtle, light-seeking substance that is the medium of celestial influx. Saffron and wine refine and rarify the spiritus, making it more transparent to solar influence. Music in the Dorian mode — Apollo's mode — impresses a solar form on the vibratory medium of the air, and the *spiritus*, being itself a kind of refined air or vapor, is shaped by that form as it passes through the ear. The images, scents, and dietary practices all operate on the same medium. As Copenhaver argues, Ficino's whole system of astrological medicine and natural magic is coherent precisely because it identifies a single substance — the *spiritus* — as the universal medium of both celestial influence and magical manipulation (Copenhaver 2019, 421–24).

Pico's relationship to this Ficinian synthesis is complex, and the complexity is philosophically significant. On the one hand, Pico inherits the spiritus doctrine wholesale as a background assumption of his anthropology: the *Heptaplus* (1489) includes what is the clearest single statement in Pico's extant writings about the spiritus and its function. In the fourth exposition — devoted to the human world — Pico describes the constitution of the human being in terms directly continuous with Galenic and Neoplatonic pneumatology. "Man consists of body and rational soul," Pico writes. "The rational soul is called heaven, because it is celestial in nature. The body is called earth, because it is earthy and heavy substance. Between the earthy body and the celestial substance of the soul there needed to be a middle bond that could tie together such different natures. For this function was appointed that fine and spirital little body (*tenue et spiritale corpusculum*) which both doctors and philosophers call the spirit" (H. 4.1, Garin, 270–72, translated from Black 2006, 40). This *spiritus* mediates between the soul's celestial nature and the body's terrestrial nature, and — in a passage that explicitly cites Avicenna — all the soul's power (life, motion, sensation) passes into the body through the medium of this luminous spirit, just as all celestial virtue is transmitted to earth through the vehicle of light. The correspondence is programmatic: the macrocosmic structure in which light transmits celestial influence to the sublunary world is reproduced at the microcosmic level by the *spiritus*, which transmits the soul's influence to the body.

The *Commento sopra una canzone d'amore* (c. 1485–86), Pico's commentary on Girolamo Benivieni's Platonic love poem, deploys the *spiritus* in the register of love theory rather than physiology, but the doctrine remains structurally the same. Love, in the Florentine Platonic tradition that the *Commento* inherits from Ficino's *De amore*, operates through the *spiritus*: when two lovers exchange glances, the gaze of one carries fine spirital particles from the lover's eye to the beloved's, and these particles, entering through the eyes, take up residence in the beloved's heart and work their fascination through the spirital medium. The soul, for purposes of its embodied operations including love, requires the *spiritus* as its embodied vehicle and instrument. The Commento also makes the connection to the cosmic order explicit: the soul descends from its star carrying a celestial vehicle, and it is through this vehicle that it remains connected both to its celestial origin and to the material world it inhabits during embodied life.

On the other hand, Pico does not adopt the full Ficinian program of spiritus-magic, and the differences are diagnostic of his distinctive philosophical position. Farmer's detailed examination of the magic theses demonstrates that the *spiritus mundi* — the world spirit as the universal medium of celestial influence central to Ficino's *De vita coelitus comparanda* — appears nowhere in Pico's *900 Conclusions*, *Oration*, or *Apology*. Where Ficino grounds his entire system of natural magic in the manipulation of this cosmic spirital medium, Pico's mechanisms of natural magic are more diverse and less pneumatically unified: they include Neoplatonic cosmic sympathies, Pythagorean number theory, the sentience in nature theorized by al-Kindi and Roger Bacon, and musical resonance (Farmer 1998, 124–25). The *spiritus mundi* is implicitly present as a background assumption in Pico's account of how natural agents influence one another, but it is not the centerpiece of his magical theory as it is of Ficino's.

This divergence reflects a deeper difference in the two thinkers' programs. Ficino's magic is, in Copenhaver's phrase, fundamentally "spiritus-magic": it works by engineering the human *spiritus* to be receptive to particular celestial signatures. The entire system depends on identifying the *spiritus* as the universal medium of both celestial influence and human experience. Pico's magical program, by contrast, locates the highest and most important magical operations not in the realm where *spiritus* is the governing medium but in the supra-spirital realm of Kabbalah. The fifteenth of the magic theses declares that "no magical action can have any effect unless it has an act of Kabbalah, explicit or implicit, linked with it" (T2M15, Farmer 1998). And the eighteenth magic thesis establishes a limit: "Nature's magical powers rise only to the horizon between time and eternity" (T2M18). The *spiritus* is precisely the medium of the temporal, the natural, the sublunary order. What lies above the horizon between time and eternity — the sefirotic space of Kabbalah, the angelic world — is beyond the reach of any spirital mechanism. For Pico, the *spiritus* and the natural order it mediates are an important but subordinate domain; the truly significant operations occur at a level that no manipulation of the spirital medium can reach.

This hierarchical subordination of *spiritus* to Kabbalah connects to a further dimension of Pico's thought: the structural analogies between the Galenic-Neoplatonic spiritus doctrine and the Kabbalistic tripartition of the soul into *nefesh*, *ruah*, and *neshamah*. Wirszubski's analysis of the Kabbalistic texts made available to Pico through Flavius Mithridates's Latin translations demonstrates how extensively Pico worked with these concepts and how he mapped the three Kabbalistic soul-levels onto the Neoplatonic hierarchies. The *nefesh* (vegetative or vital soul), *ruah* (spirit, animating breath, moral and emotional life), and *neshamah* (highest intellective soul, divine breath) do not map perfectly onto the Galenic three spirits, but they rhyme with them in structurally suggestive ways. The *ruah* in particular — a word that can mean "wind," "breath," "spirit" in Hebrew, and that Mithridates sometimes renders as *spiritus* in his Latin translations — occupies a middle position in the Kabbalistic soul-hierarchy analogous to the *spiritus animalis* in the Galenic scheme and the Neoplatonic soul-vehicle in the Platonic scheme. Pico's syncretic reading of these convergences is less a philosophical argument than an interpretive method: he presents the structural parallels between traditions as evidence that all three are approaching the same underlying truth about the mediating function of the animated breath in the constitution of the embodied soul.

The *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinicatricem*, Pico's final and most polemically aggressive work (composed c. 1493–94, published posthumously), engages indirectly with the *spiritus* doctrine through its sustained attack on astrological determinism. One of the linchpins of astrological theory was precisely the spirital medium: celestial bodies exercise their influence on human affairs through the *spiritus mundi*, which carries their qualities downward into the sublunary world and into individual human *spiritus animalis*, thereby shaping temperament, health, and character. To undermine astrological determinism, Pico must also undermine the doctrine of celestial influence operating through spirital sympathy. His argument is not that the *spiritus* and celestial influence are unreal — he continues to accept natural sympathies as the basis for natural magic — but that they do not operate with the deterministic regularity that would make astrological prediction possible. The heavens influence the sublunary world through the spirital medium, but they do not determine it: the soul, at its rational and intellective levels, transcends the spirital mechanism and is therefore not subject to the fated causality that the astrologers claim (Copenhaver 2019, 456–60). The *Disputationes* thus preserves a role for the *spiritus* doctrine while stripping it of its deterministic implications, in a move that is characteristic of Pico's strategy throughout: he accepts the cosmological framework that makes natural magic possible while denying the determinist conclusions that would make human freedom impossible.

The scholarly literature on *spiritus* in the Renaissance context is anchored by Walker's *Spiritual and Demonic Magic from Ficino to Campanella* (1958) and Copenhaver's multiple analyses of Ficino's pneumatic magic. Copenhaver's *Magic and the Dignity of Man* (2019) provides the most comprehensive account of how Pico inherits and modifies the Ficinian program. Black's *Pico's Heptaplus and Biblical Hermeneutics* (2006) remains the essential analysis of the passage in the *Heptaplus* where Pico most explicitly defines the spiritus and its mediating function. Allen's studies in the Platonism of Ficino and Pico provide indispensable context for the Neoplatonic dimension of the soul-vehicle doctrine. What has not yet been done with sufficient precision is a close analysis of how the *spiritus* doctrine operates across the whole range of Pico's texts — not only the *Heptaplus* and *Commento* but the scattered references in the *Conclusiones* and the structural assumptions embedded in the *Oration*'s account of the soul's ascent. Such an analysis would need to attend to the genre-dependence of the concept: the medical register, the cosmological register, the love-theoretical register, and the magical register all draw on the same underlying pneumatology but deploy it for quite different purposes, and collapsing them produces precisely the kind of anachronistic synthesis that the best Pico scholarship now labors to resist.
