The doctrine of divine names (*shemot*, שמות) stands at the intersection of Hebrew grammatology, Jewish mystical theology, and Renaissance natural magic. For Pico della Mirandola, writing in the *900 Conclusions* of 1486 and defending himself in the *Apology* of the following year, divine names were not mere titles applied to God from without but operative realities — configurations of divine power that could be invoked, combined, and wielded. To understand what Pico meant by this claim it is necessary to trace a long arc from the ancient logic of Hebrew letter-mysticism, through the thirteenth-century development of theosophical and ecstatic Kabbalah, to the specific translations and interpolations that Pico read in the Latin manuscripts prepared for him by Flavius Mithridates.

The deepest root of the tradition lies in the Hebrew conviction that language is not a conventional system imposed on a pre-existing world but the very medium through which God creates and sustains creation. The *Sefer Yetzirah* (Book of Formation), a text of uncertain date but certainly pre-medieval in its core, teaches that God created the world by means of thirty-two paths of wisdom — ten *sefirot* (numerations or divine emanations) and the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet. The letters are not symbols for sounds but primordial instruments: God sealed the six directions of space with permutations of the divine name, and every creature is formed by specific letter-combinations. In this cosmology, the Hebrew alphabet is the grammar of being itself. The divine names that appear throughout Scripture — *El* (אל), *Elohim* (אלהים), *Shaddai* (שדי), *Adonai* (אדני), *YHVH* (יהוה) — are accordingly understood not as epithets but as concentrations of divine energy, each name corresponding to a different aspect or *middah* (measure, quality) of the divine nature.

This grammatological ontology reached its fullest medieval elaboration in the Kabbalistic tradition of thirteenth-century Spain and Provence. The key figures for Pico's own reading were Abraham Abulafia and Joseph Gicatilla, both working in the latter half of the thirteenth century. Abulafia, the founder of what Gershom Scholem called ecstatic or prophetic Kabbalah, systematized the *tzeruf* (combination) of divine names into a rigorous contemplative technique: by permuting the letters of the divine name according to precise methods — combining, transposing, substituting, and calculating their numerical values (*gematria*) — the practitioner could achieve a state of intellectual rapture equivalent to prophetic union with the Active Intellect. In Abulafia's system the divine names were simultaneously the object of contemplation and the technical instruments of the practitioner's transformation. The *tzeruf* of letters is not decoration on the page of religious life; it is the operative core of the mystical ascent.

Gicatilla's approach, represented in his *Sha'are Orah* (Gates of Light), offered a different but complementary map. Where Abulafia's Kabbalah centered on the permutation of letters as a meditative technique, Gicatilla's masterwork systematically correlated divine names with the ten *sefirot*, the ten divine attributes or emanations through which the infinite God (*Eyn-Sof*, the Endless) manifests in the world. Each of the sefirot bears a particular divine name as its proper designation: *YHVH* is the primary name of *Tiferet* (Beauty or Glory), the sixth sefirah and the heart of the sefirotic system; *Elohim* corresponds to *Binah* (Understanding) or to the principle of divine judgment; *El* to *Hesed* (Lovingkindness); *Shaddai* to *Yesod* (Foundation); *Adonai* to *Shekhinah* (Presence) or *Malkhut* (Kingdom), the tenth and lowest sefirah through which the divine influx reaches the created world. Knowledge of the proper name of each sefirah therefore means knowledge of the divine architecture itself, and prayer or invocation addressed by the correct name to the correct divine aspect becomes a form of theurgy — an act capable of influencing the flow of divine energy through the sefirotic channels.

Pico had access to these texts through Mithridates' Latin translations, completed primarily during the summer and autumn of 1486. As Wirszubski's study (1989) demonstrates through exhaustive manuscript analysis, Pico's encounter with Kabbalistic divine-name doctrine was filtered almost entirely through these Latin renderings of Abulafia, Gicatilla, Menahem Recanati, and others. The translations survive in four Vatican manuscripts (Codices Vaticani Ebraici 189–191 and Codex Chigi A.VI.190) amounting in their present state to nearly 3,500 pages in folio. These manuscripts are, as Wirszubski shows, written in Mithridates' own hand — he was translating directly onto the page — and they contain both accurate passages and systematic Christianizing interpolations inserted into the Hebrew originals.

The theological and operative framework that Pico derived from these materials is crystallized in the first of his Kabbalistic *Conclusiones secundum opinionem propriam* (theses according to his own opinion): "Whatever other Kabbalists may say, I would make the first division of the science of Kabbalah into *scientia sephiroth* and *scientia semot*, as into practical and speculative." This division — mapping the science of the *sefirot* onto speculative Kabbalah and the science of names onto practical Kabbalah — appears to invert what one would expect from familiarity with theosophical Kabbalism, in which the doctrine of the *sefirot* is the primary theoretical and contemplative achievement. Wirszubski traces Pico's unusual taxonomy to Abulafia's letter to his disciple Yehuda Salmon, translated in Cod. Vat. Ebr. 190, where Kabbalistic knowledge is divided between the "science of the divine name of the Tetragrammaton through the ten numerations" and the "science of the great name through the twenty-two letters" — speculative versus practical, sefirot versus names. Pico has transposed the categories, but the underlying source is identifiable.

The practical dimension — *scientia semot* — is what makes divine names more than contemplative objects. In his third Kabbalistic thesis Pico states: "The science that is the practical part of Kabbalah practises the whole of formal metaphysics and inferior theology." The phrase *practicat totam metaphysicam formalem* is dense, but Wirszubski's analysis suggests that it implies a theurgic operation on the *sefirot* themselves through the medium of their corresponding divine names: to address God by the correct name associated with the correct *sefirah* is to act on the divine structure of reality. This is not loose metaphor. In the twenty-first thesis of the *Conclusiones de modo intelligendi hymnos Orphei*, Pico writes: "The work of the preceding hymns is nothing without the work of Kabbalah, whose proper function is to practise every formal continuous and discrete quantity." The Orphic hymns, addressed to divine powers, require Kabbalistic divine-name invocation to activate their efficacy: names supply the operative principle that magic alone cannot furnish.

The most celebrated and controversial formulation of this principle appears in the *900 Conclusions*, Thesis 72 of the Kabbalistic section: "There is no science that gives us more certainty of Christ's divinity than magic and Kabbalah." This statement, which the papal commission found deeply suspect and which figured centrally in the condemnation of thirteen of Pico's theses by Innocent VIII in August 1487, does not merely assert that Kabbalistic learning confirms Christian doctrine in some general sense. It asserts that the operational dimension of Kabbalah — the practical science of divine names, letter combinations, and their application — provides a more rigorous epistemic ground for Christological claims than any other discipline. Copenhaver (2019) reads this assertion as transforming Kabbalah from a hermeneutic supplement to Christian theology into something more like a confirmatory science: Kabbalistic divine-name manipulation is an experimental procedure whose results demonstrate metaphysical truths.

The *Apology*, composed in haste in the spring and early summer of 1487 to defend his condemned propositions before a papal commission, contains Pico's most sustained public explanation of what he means by Kabbalistic divine names. He distinguishes carefully between his use of divine names and the demonic magic condemned by the Church. Demonic magic invokes fallen angels by their proper names to compel them to act; this Pico entirely condemns. Natural magic, by contrast, operates through the hidden sympathies of created nature. Kabbalah operates at a higher level still, through the divine names themselves, which are the proper operative instruments of angelic and ultimately divine power. In the *Apology* Pico argues that addressing God by his proper names is not conjuration but prayer of the highest order: "Haec est prima et vera Cabala, de qua credo me primum apud Latinos explicatam fecisse mentionem" ("This is the first and true Kabbalah, of which I believe I am the first among Latins to give a clear account"). Addressing God by *Shaddai* rather than by a generic title, on this account, is what the High Priest did in the Holy of Holies; it is the operation of sacred language at the level of divine reality rather than human convention.

Giulio Busi's interpretation, developed in the work on Pico's Kabbalah and its connections to what he calls "operative Kabbalah," supports reading the divine-name doctrine not as purely theoretical but as genuinely instrumental in Pico's conception. Busi argues that Pico understood the Kabbalistic operation not merely as exegesis but as a form of praxis capable of transforming the practitioner and acting on divine structures. This reading is consistent with the *Oration*'s account of the sixth stage of the philosopher's ascent, where Kabbalah appears as the culminating method before seraphic union: having moved through philosophy (including ethics, dialectic, and natural philosophy), the philosopher who has reached the summit of rational inquiry then requires Kabbalistic knowledge to complete the transformation. The *Oration* describes Kabbalah at this stage not as book-learning but as an operative discipline whose subject matter is the divine names and whose effect is participation in divine wisdom.

A crucial complication in assessing all of this is the mediation of Mithridates. Wirszubski demonstrates that Mithridates systematically inserted Christianizing glosses into his translations — rendering the Hebrew pronouns and symbols in ways that made Jewish Kabbalistic texts appear to anticipate or confirm Christian doctrine. The transliteration of the Hebrew pronoun *ze* (זה, "this"), for example, was used by Mithridates to create Christological implications: since *ze* is a name of *Yesod*, the ninth sefirah, and since Mithridates was familiar with the tradition that equated *ze* with the figure of Christ, he rendered it consistently in a way that allowed Pico to read the Kabbalistic texts as speaking of Jesus. More broadly, Mithridates inserted the word *trinitas* into passages where no such term appears in the Hebrew originals, and constructed equations between Hebrew letter-numerical values and Christian theological claims. This means that many of Pico's convictions about what divine names "teach" about Christian doctrine were in fact products of his translator's interpolations — the convergences Pico found between Kabbalistic name-doctrine and Christian theology were in significant measure manufactured in the act of translation.

The *Oration*, written as a preface for the projected public disputation of January 1487 that was never permitted to occur, provides the most rhetorically vivid account of what divine names meant to Pico in the larger context of his encyclopedic program. Having described the philosopher's ascent through the disciplines, Pico reaches the Kabbalistic climax: "We shall fly up with winged feet, like earthly Mercuries, to the embraces of our blessed mother and enjoy that wished-for peace. Holy peace, wholesome peace, the undivided and genuine friendship through which all souls not only accord in the single mind which is above all minds, but in some inexpressible fashion become altogether one." This is the telos of the science of divine names, not a magical operation performed on the world from outside but a transformative praxis that reconfigures the practitioner's relationship to the divine architecture of reality. Whether Pico's understanding of this telos owed more to Abulafia's ecstatic Kabbalah, to Gicatilla's theosophical system, or to the Christianizing interpolations of his translator is a question that scholarship since Wirszubski has been slowly — and with growing nuance — addressing.

The concept of divine names in Pico thus operates on at least three distinct registers: a cosmological register inherited from the Kabbalistic tradition (the names as structural components of divine reality and creation), an operative register (the names as instruments of practical Kabbalah and Kabbalistic magic), and an apologetic-theological register (the names as proof-texts for Christian doctrine derivable from within the Jewish mystical tradition). These registers do not always cohere, and the specific content of any given thesis touching on divine names must be evaluated against its probable source in the Mithridates translations, against what can be established about the Hebrew original that Mithridates was working from, and against Pico's own philosophical priorities as expressed across his works. The entry that PicoDB maintains for this concept should therefore track not only the explicit appearances of the term *nomen divinum* or *semot* in Pico's texts but also the network of specific theses, their identifiable sources, and the scholarly debate about whether Pico's divine-name Kabbalah is best understood as operative magic, contemplative theology, or Christian apologetics — or, as seems most likely, an irreducible combination of all three.
