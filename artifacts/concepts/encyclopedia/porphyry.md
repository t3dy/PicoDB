Porphyry of Tyre (c. 234 – c. 305 CE) occupies a peculiar double position in the history of Western philosophy: as the pupil, biographer, and literary executor of Plotinus, he transmitted the most important body of late ancient philosophical thought to subsequent centuries; as the author of the *Isagoge* (*Eisagoge*, "Introduction"), he generated the most consequential logical controversy of the medieval period. Both aspects of this double role are relevant to Pico della Mirandola, who engaged Porphyry's Neoplatonic metaphysics through the Greek texts of the *Sententiae ad intelligibilia ducentes* (Sentences Leading Toward the Intelligible) and the *De abstinentia animalium* (On Abstinence from Animal Food), incorporated Porphyry's views on the Timaeus into twelve theses of the *900 Conclusions*, and inherited the problem of universals posed in the *Isagoge* as part of the basic intellectual formation he received from the Latin scholastic tradition through Boethius.

The *Isagoge*, composed by Porphyry as an introductory treatise for students approaching Aristotle's *Categories*, opens with one of the most famous undecided questions in the history of philosophy: whether genera and species (*i.e.*, universals) exist in reality (*in rebus*) or only in the mind (*in intellectu*), and if they exist in reality, whether they are corporeal or incorporeal, and whether they exist separately from sensible things or only in and through them. Porphyry explicitly declines to settle these questions, noting that they belong to a deeper philosophical inquiry than his introductory purpose requires. But by posing them in this way and then deferring them, he ensured that they would haunt the entire subsequent tradition of Latin philosophy. The *Isagoge* was translated by Boethius in the sixth century (together with Boethius's two commentaries on it), and this Boethian text became the standard gateway into the study of logic throughout the medieval period. Every student who progressed through the scholastic *trivium* encountered Porphyry's five predicables — genus (*genos*), species (*eidos*), differentia (*diaphora*), proprium (*idion*), and accident (*symbebêkos*) — as the fundamental categories through which the relations between terms in any syllogism must be analyzed. The so-called "tree of Porphyry" (*arbor Porphyriana*), a hierarchical diagram descending from the most general category (Substance) through successive differentiations to the individual (for example, Substance → Body → Animate → Animal → Rational → Man → Socrates), became a standard pedagogical tool for visualizing the logical relations of inclusion and subordination among terms. When Copenhaver summarizes the intellectual formation that Pico and his contemporaries received in logic, he identifies as central "the logic invented by Aristotle, explicated by Porphyry, and Latinized by Boethius" (Copenhaver 2022, 57), with Porphyry's *Isagoge* serving as the mediating text that gave Aristotelian logic its specifically scholastic form.

The question of universals posed in the *Isagoge* had generated a millennium of controversy by the time Pico encountered it, encompassing the great disputes between William of Ockham and the realist tradition, the terminism of the late medieval *via moderna*, and the revival of Platonist realism in the Florentine circle around Marsilio Ficino. Pico's *De ente et uno* (On Being and Unity, 1491), composed in response to objections raised by Angelo Poliziano concerning the relationship between Plato's One and Aristotle's Being, engages directly with the question of whether the most general terms of metaphysical predication — Being and the One — are convertible or distinct. This is in part a universals question: the convertibility of Being and the One (the thesis Pico defends, following Thomas Aquinas and against Ficino's reading of the Platonic transcendence of the One beyond Being) requires a careful analysis of how the most general predicates relate to the things they are predicated of. Pico's scholastic training in the tradition that Porphyry's *Isagoge* had structured gave him the analytical vocabulary — genus, species, predication, univocal and equivocal terms — without which the argument of *De ente et uno* could not have been conducted.

In the *900 Conclusions*, Porphyry appears not as a logician but as a Neoplatonist, and specifically as a philosopher of the post-Plotinian Platonic tradition. Farmer's authoritative edition and commentary identifies the twelve theses ascribed to Porphyry (22.1–22.12) as drawn from three sources: reports of Porphyry's views found in Proclus's commentary on the *Timaeus* (edited by Diehl in three volumes), Porphyry's fragmentary *Sententiae ad intelligibilia ducentes*, and the *De abstinentia animalium* (Farmer 1998, 306). The theses themselves are grouped around Porphyrian interpretations of the Platonic cosmogony: who or what is "the father" and "the maker" in Plato's *Timaeus*, what is the demiurge of the world, what is the exemplar that the demiurge imitates, and how souls are distributed through the cosmic hierarchy. The first three theses (22.1–22.3) propose a Porphyrian reading of the Timaeus in which "by the father in Plato we should understand the cause which from itself produces every effect; by the maker that which receives matter from the other" — the demiurge is thus identified as the supermundane soul, and the exemplar the demiurge imitates is the intellect of that demiurgal soul. These positions are, as Farmer notes (1998, 307), drawn from Proclus's *In Timaeum*, which reports Porphyry's views in the context of the debate between Porphyrian and Iamblichan readings of the *Timaeus*. The astrological theses (22.4–22.8) adapt material from the same source and connect Porphyrian doctrines about soul-distribution through the lunar sphere to a set of symbolic correlations: Apollo as the solar intellect, Asclepius as the lunar intellect — a symbolic system connecting Neoplatonic cosmology to the mythological figures that Pico would also use in his Orphic and magical theses.

The final group of Porphyrian theses (22.9–22.12) turns to demonology and metaphysics. Theses 22.9 and 22.10, as Farmer establishes, derive from *De abstinentia* 2.38–40, and propose a distinction between two kinds of evil daemons — those that are souls and substantial daemons, and those that are material powers and accidental daemons — correlating this with Paul's reference to "the powers of darkness" in the Pauline letters. Thesis 22.12, the most philosophically striking of the group, is a doctrine about omnipresence and location: "God is everywhere because he is nowhere; intellect is everywhere because it is nowhere; soul is everywhere because it is nowhere — but God is everywhere and nowhere with respect to all things that come after him; intellect in God is everywhere and nowhere with respect to what comes after it; soul in intellect and God, but everywhere and nowhere with respect to body." This graduated account of how each level of the cosmic hierarchy is simultaneously everywhere and nowhere with respect to the levels below it is a characteristically Neoplatonic formulation, and its inclusion in the Porphyrian section reflects Pico's access to the Greek Porphyrian texts directly: Farmer notes that the key texts — the *Sententiae* and the *De abstinentia* — were available to Pico in Greek before Ficino translated Porphyry into Latin (Ficino's translations of Iamblichus, Porphyry, and Proclus were not completed until 1488, two years after the *Conclusions* were published, and were not printed until 1497, three years after Pico's death).

The question of Pico's textual access to Porphyry is significant for understanding the broader intellectual ambition of the *900 Conclusions*. Farmer notes that Pico "relied heavily on post-Plotinian Greek scholastics (especially Porphyry, Iamblichus, and Proclus) who had not yet been systematically discussed by Ficino; he also drew heavily on the Greek text of Plotinus's *Enneads*, which Pico thought that Ficino interpreted especially badly" (Farmer 1998, 1142–43). This is a key point about Pico's intellectual self-positioning: in 1486, Ficino had not yet translated the Porphyrian, Iamblichan, and Procline texts that would come to define the later Florentine Platonist program. Pico's staking out of these texts as his own philosophical territory — his reading of the Greek texts directly and his incorporation of their doctrines into the *Conclusions* — represents a deliberate competitive move against Ficino's Platonic authority. By constructing a Neoplatonism that went beyond Ficino to include Porphyry, Iamblichus, and Proclus in their Greek originals, Pico was claiming a more complete and more rigorous mastery of the Platonic tradition than his senior rival had yet demonstrated.

Within the history of Neoplatonism, Porphyry occupies a transitional position that is philosophically important for understanding Pico's selection of sources. Porphyry was Plotinus's closest pupil and his literary executor: he arranged Plotinus's treatises into the six groups of nine that we know as the *Enneads*, and his *Life of Plotinus* (*Vita Plotini*) — the biographical introduction to that edition — became the essential text through which Renaissance readers encountered the human context of Plotinian philosophy. Porphyry's own philosophical positions, however, differ from Plotinus's in ways that generated the subsequent divisions within Neoplatonism. Where Iamblichus argued that theurgical ritual practice was a necessary complement to — or even superior to — philosophical contemplation as a means of the soul's ascent, and where this "theurgy question" became the central division within later Neoplatonism, Porphyry occupied a more ambiguous position. In some works (*De abstinentia*, *Letter to Marcella*) he appears to endorse contemplative philosophy as the primary path of ascent; in others he acknowledges the validity of theurgical practice. Farmer identifies Porphyry's *De mysteriis* (actually Iamblichus's, presented as a response to a letter from Porphyry to Iamblichus's disciple Anebo) as a text that records "civil warfare in the Platonic camp" that caught Pico's attention (Farmer 1998, 1672), and the debate between Porphyrian and Iamblichan Platonism — between a more intellectualist and a more theurgical model of the ascent — is built into the architecture of the *Conclusions* themselves.

For Pico's purposes, Porphyry represents the intellectualist wing of post-Plotinian Neoplatonism: a thinker whose commitment to philosophical contemplation as the primary vehicle of ascent aligns him more closely with the broad tradition of rational philosophy than the theurgical radicalism of Iamblichus. This made Porphyry a useful figure in Pico's program of demonstrating the concordance between pagan philosophy and Christian theology: Porphyry's emphasis on the soul's intellectual return to the divine, his sophisticated account of the cosmic hierarchy, and his demonology could all be read as anticipating Christian doctrines in ways that Iamblichus's ritual-heavy system made more difficult. The *Apology* lists Porphyry among the ancient magicians, but with a distinction: Pico draws his information about ancient magic from "Pliny, Apuleius, Porphyry, and similar late-ancient sources" (Farmer 1998, 6743–46), using Porphyry as a historical witness to ancient magical tradition rather than as an authority for magical practice in his own right. This deployment of Porphyry as a historical source, alongside his use of Porphyrian doctrine in the metaphysical theses of the *Conclusions*, illustrates the flexibility with which Pico put his ancient authorities to work: as a witness to magical tradition, as a metaphysician of the cosmic hierarchy, as a theologian of the soul's ascent, and — through the *Isagoge* — as the transmitter of the logical framework within which all scholastic predication was conducted.
