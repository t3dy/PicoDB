# Abraham Abulafia

Abraham Abulafia (1240–c. 1291) was the Spanish-Jewish mystic whose writings constitute the deepest and most technically demanding stratum of Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's Kabbalistic inheritance. The distinction between the two major types of Kabbalah that organize Pico's *900 Conclusions* — the Kabbalah of the sefirot and the speculative Kabbalah of names (*scientia semot*) — was recognized by Pico only after reading Abulafia, and it was from Abulafia that Pico derived the understanding that Kabbalah was not merely a body of doctrine but a systematic practice for achieving union with the divine. Wirszubski, whose *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989) remains the indispensable philological account, identifies Abulafia's texts as second in importance only to Menahem Recanati's *Commentary on the Pentateuch* among the major Kabbalistic sources for Pico's *Conclusiones Cabalisticae secundum opinionem propriam*. But in terms of structural influence — the architectonics of Pico's understanding of what Kabbalah is at its highest level — Abulafia may be the more consequential figure.

Born in Zaragoza, Abulafia spent his life in restless movement through Italy, Greece, and the Levant, absorbing Maimonidean philosophy and developing an idiosyncratic mystical synthesis that set him at odds with the dominant Kabbalistic schools of his day. His major works include *Or ha-Sekhel* (The Light of the Intellect), *Imrei Shefer* (Words of Beauty), and *Sefer ha-Zeruf* (Book of Letter Combination). These texts develop what Abulafia himself called *kabbalat ha-nevu'ah* — prophetic Kabbalah — a systematic method for achieving the state of prophecy through concentrated manipulation of the letters and names of God. He distinguished this approach sharply from what he dismissed as *kabbalah numerationalis* (Kabbalah of the sefirot) — the theosophical system of divine emanations that dominated the Spanish and Geronese Kabbalistic schools. In Abulafia's view, the Kabbalah of the sefirot risked a kind of divine pluralism: by locating different divine attributes in ten distinct emanations, it came dangerously close to asserting ten gods. His own system, grounded in the contemplation of the unified divine name, avoided this trap by making the letters of the Tetragrammaton (YHWH), rather than the sefirot, the primary objects of mystical attention.

Abulafia's system was built on a synthesis of Maimonidean rationalism and prophetic technique that was unprecedented in medieval Judaism. In Maimonides' *Guide of the Perplexed*, the Active Intellect (*sekhel ha-po'el*, in Latin *intellectus agens*) serves as the last and lowest of the cosmic intelligences, the one that governs the sublunar world and that makes possible human intellectual cognition and, in prophets, inspired knowledge. Abulafia took this Aristotelian-Maimonidean framework and transformed it into a mystical psychology: the goal of his letter-combination exercises was to activate the human intellect to the point where it merged with the Active Intellect, achieving a state that Abulafia described as prophetic overflow from above. The *tzeruf ha-otiyot* — combination of letters — was not magic in any mechanical sense but a discipline of total cognitive concentration: by permuting the letters of the divine names through carefully prescribed sequences, breathing exercises, and postures, the practitioner could dissolve the ordinary boundary between human and divine intelligence and receive genuine prophetic knowledge. Abulafia wrote extensively about the preparatory stages — purification, fasting, withdrawal from sensory distraction — and about the phenomenology of the prophetic state itself, including the experiences of light, of inner speech, and of apparent confrontation with another self that turns out to be the practitioner's own intellect in its activated state.

It is this practical and experiential dimension of Abulafia's Kabbalah that makes him so significant for understanding Pico's *Oration*. The famous ascending sequence that Pico describes in the *Oration*'s central movement — philosophy, dialectic, theology, natural magic, and finally Kabbalah as the culminating stage — is not merely programmatic. When Pico says "if you summon Kabbalah" (*si Cabalam in conclave vocaveris*), the invocation is of precisely the Abulafian technique: a practice of concentrated attention to divine names that actualizes the intellect's capacity for union. Wirszubski notes that the distinction between *scientia sephiroth* and *scientia semot* that opens the second set of Kabbalistic theses in the *Conclusiones* — the distinction between doctrinal and speculative Kabbalah — derives from Abulafia and represents Pico's recognition, achieved only after encountering Abulafia's texts, that the two orientations constituted genuinely different epistemic projects (Wirszubski 1989, 53–65).

Pico's access to Abulafia's writings was entirely mediated through Flavius Mithridates, the Sicilian Jewish convert who translated a Kabbalistic library for Pico in the summer and autumn of 1486. The texts by Abulafia that Mithridates translated include the *Summa Brevis Cabale* — identified by Wirszubski as a letter from Abulafia to his disciple Yehuda Salmon, in which Abulafia defines his own prophetic Kabbalah in contradistinction to the Kabbalah of the sefirot (*kabbalah numerationalis*) — and the *Liber de Secretis Legis* (fols. 336v–469v of Cod. Vat. Ebr. 190 plus quires of Cod. Chigi A.VI.190), Abulafia's mystical commentary on thirty-six secrets embedded in Maimonides' *Guide of the Perplexed*. A third Abulafian text was the anonymous *Liber Combinationum*, which Scholem identified as a fourteenth-century popular version of Abulafia's combinatory and philosophical Kabbalah; Wirszubski establishes it as the direct source of the third thesis in the first set of Kabbalistic theses (Wirszubski 1989, 19–20). Together, these texts gave Pico not only Abulafia's doctrine but his characteristic idiom: the associative, cryptic, isopsephic mode of argument that Wirszubski describes as "scarcely indicated gematric equations" — a style of thinking in numerical equivalences that is nearly invisible in plain Latin translation without recourse to the Hebrew letters.

Mithridates' admiration for Abulafia was, by the evidence of his own marginal notes, exceptional. Where he treated most subjects and authors with what Wirszubski calls "reckless disrespect," Mithridates' marginal notes on Abulafia's texts are marked by something approaching reverence. At the end of the *De Secretis Legis*, when Abulafia wrote of the one who "directed me to write you what was lawful for me to write" and mentioned his own name Abraham, Mithridates noted: "Know, Pice, that he does not mean Abraham the patriarch alone but also himself, because that was his name, and he was truly a great man" (Cod. Vat. Ebr. 190, fol. 445v). Mithridates also records a family tradition: his father, Nissim Abul-Farag, had known traditions about Abulafia in Palermo, where Abulafia's "miraculous" acts were still celebrated in the Hebrew literary monuments of the Sicilian community. This biographical proximity between Mithridates and Abulafia's legacy — Sicily was the last place Abulafia operated before his death around 1291 — gave the translations a quality of engaged familiarity rare in the production of translated texts.

What Mithridates' translations also did — and this is the aspect of the transmission that most complicates simple accounts of Pico "reading Abulafia" — was insert Christianizing glosses at strategic points. In the *Summa Brevis Cabalae*, Mithridates interpolated a remark about Christians who "founded imperfectly on this science, although their founders knew it perfectly" — a sentence absent from any surviving Hebrew manuscript tradition of the epistle, as Wirszubski established by comparing multiple Hebrew copies (Wirszubski 1989, 111–113). In the *De Secretis Legis*, the Cusan principle *quodlibet est in quolibet* — a phrase from Nicholas of Cusa's *De Docta Ignorantia* — was inserted into Abulafia's account of his letter-combinatory method, manufacturing a structural homology between Abulafian technique and Cusan speculative theology. These interventions mean that the Abulafia Pico read was already, in significant respects, a Christianized Abulafia — a thinker whose prophetic union with the Active Intellect had been glossed, at the translation level, to suggest convergence with contemplative traditions Pico already valued.

It is this manufactured convergence that Busi, in the Einaudi volume *Giovanni Pico della Mirandola: Mito, magia, Qabbalah* (2014), reframes in terms of an "operative" dimension. Busi argues that Pico's investment in Abulafian technique was not merely theoretical: the Kabbalistic section of the *Oration*'s ascending program presents letter combination as an actually performable practice, not simply as a philosophical doctrine. From this perspective, the *alphabetaria revolutio* (alphabetic revolution) that Pico describes in his *Apology* as the art of combining letters — *hokmat ha-tzeruf*, the Abulafian combinatory art — was understood by Pico as a real transformative technique, not a metaphor. Busi observes, moreover, that in the *Conclusiones* the traditional hierarchy of Jewish Kabbalah was inverted: where most Kabbalists treated letter-combination as preparatory to the higher contemplation of the sefirot, Pico placed *scientia semot* above *scientia sephiroth*, elevating Abulafian technique to the summit of the Kabbalistic path (Busi-Ebgi 2014, 46n). This inversion reflects Pico's philosophical training: for someone formed by Aristotelian epistemology and Neoplatonic metaphysics, a system that promised union with the cosmic Active Intellect through intellectual practice was more philosophically satisfying than a system based on the symbolism of divine emanations.

Wirszubski also noticed a structural homology between Abulafian ecstatic technique and Iamblichan theurgy — a homology that sheds light on why Pico's syncretic project could accommodate both simultaneously. Both traditions involve a graduated preliminary purification of the practitioner; both focus concentrated attention on specific names or symbols as vehicles of ascent; and both aim at union with a divine intelligence — for Iamblichus the One or the divine Henads, for Abulafia the Active Intellect and ultimately the divine name. The structural parallel is not accidental from Pico's perspective: it suggests that distinct cultural traditions had arrived, by different paths, at a common account of the mechanics of mystical union. This is precisely the kind of convergence that the *prisca theologia* framework and the program of *concordia* sought to document. The Abulafian technique, read through Mithridates and refracted through Neoplatonic and Iamblichan categories, provided Pico with what he needed: a Kabbalistic practice that could plausibly be presented as the Jewish equivalent of Platonic theurgic ascent.

The textual history of Pico's engagement with Abulafia thus traces a path from Spanish-Jewish ecstatic mysticism through Sicilian Jewish-Christian mediation, Latin translation with interpolated Christianizing commentary, and Pico's own synthetic reformulation within a Neoplatonic and scholastic framework. What emerges at the end of this chain is not Abulafia's prophetic Kabbalah as Abulafia would have recognized it, but something that is genuinely shaped by Abulafia — the bipartite structure of Kabbalah, the primacy of the divine name, the possibility of intellective union — while being organized around very different ultimate aims: not the preparation of a Jewish prophet for the coming of the Messiah (as in Abulafia's own urgent eschatological framework) but the confirmation of Christian doctrine from Hebrew sources, and the demonstration that the great mystical traditions of antiquity converged in a single universal wisdom.

The Abulafian strand in Pico's Kabbalah was recognized but not fully disentangled by scholarship before Wirszubski. Gershom Scholem had noted the Abulafian origin of the distinction between sefirot-Kabbalah and name-Kabbalah (Scholem, "Zur Geschichte der Anfänge der christlichen Kabbala," 1954, 164). Wirszubski's contribution was to trace this recognition down to the level of specific manuscripts, specific theses, and specific interpolations, transforming a vague impressionistic connection into a documented textual genealogy. For readers of PicoDB, the practical implication is that any interpretation of Pico's claim that Kabbalah provides a "science of names" that surpasses the Kabbalah of emanations — and any reading of the *Oration*'s Kabbalistic climax as a call to actual practice — must engage with the Abulafian corpus and with Wirszubski's reconstruction of how that corpus reached Pico. Abulafia's *De Secretis Legis* and *Summa Brevis Cabalae*, now available in Giulio Busi's critical editions published by the Istituto Nazionale di Studi sul Rinascimento (Turin, from 2004), are the primary texts; Mithridates' Latin versions, preserved in Cod. Vat. Ebr. 190, are the medium through which Pico actually read them; and the divergences between the two are the historical record of how the encounter actually happened.
