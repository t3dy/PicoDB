# Heptaplus

The *Heptaplus* is Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's sevenfold commentary on the opening verse of Genesis — "In principio creavit Deus caelum et terram," "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth" — completed in 1489 and published in Florence in the autumn of that year. It is the first work Pico brought to completion after the catastrophe of 1486–87, when thirteen of his nine hundred theses were condemned by a papal commission and his subsequent *Apologia* provoked an outright ban from Innocent VIII. The *Heptaplus* accordingly represents not a repetition of the syncretic ambitions of the *Conclusiones* but their recasting: where the theses had been an encyclopedic miscellany of compressed aphorisms drawn from every source tradition Pico could master, the *Heptaplus* is a single unified structure, architecturally coherent, focused on a single verse of Scripture, and framed as the most orthodox of literary genres — the biblical commentary. As Crofton Black has argued, it is the pre-eminent text for understanding Pico's theory of allegory and the single most elaborated expression of his biblical hermeneutics.

When Gianfrancesco Pico della Mirandola edited his uncle's *Opera omnia* in Bologna in 1496, he placed the *Heptaplus* at the very beginning of the collection. The work maintained this position in all subsequent editions, so that for early modern readers the *Heptaplus* was in effect the introduction to Pico. The placement was partly strategic — Gianfrancesco was concerned to rehabilitate his uncle's reputation by presenting him foremost as a sober biblical commentator — but it also reflects a genuine judgment about the work's maturity. Ermolao Barbaro, writing to Pico on 9 September 1489, reported that he had read the *Heptaplus* in a single day without stopping, and that three things delighted him above all: its capacity to teach and to move, its combination of sacred letters with natural philosophy in the manner of the Pythagoreans and Platonics, and something he called its singular modesty. Cristoforo Landino, writing to a mutual friend, declared that scholars had treated the same topics in Greek and Latin and Hebrew without apparently exhausting them, but that Pico had begun from an almost new argument and arrived at the most familiar conclusions by an unaccustomed road, making everything seem new and unheard-of even to the most learned readers.

## Structure: Seven Books and an Eighth

The title — from the Greek *hepta*, seven — names the central structural principle. The work consists of two general proems followed by seven expositions, each of which reads the six days of creation as a revelation of one of seven distinct "worlds" or ontological registers, and each of which has its own brief proem followed by thirteen chapters. After the seven expositions comes an eighth section: a separate and brief exposition of the single opening word of Genesis, *bereshit*, "in the beginning." The *Heptaplus* thus has a peculiar relationship with completion: its seventh exposition is followed by something that is not an eighth exposition in the sequence but a kind of metaexposition, a turn to the word that precedes all the other words of the verse, the word that precedes even "God" in the Hebrew text.

The seven worlds that structure the seven expositions are the following: the elemental world of terrestrial matter; the celestial world of the heavens and the stars; the angelic or invisible world of pure intelligences; the human world, in which man is understood as a union of all three preceding worlds; a fifth exposition treating all the worlds in their successive order; a sixth exposition dedicated to the affinities and correspondences among the worlds and all the things within them; and a seventh exposition treating *felicitas*, the happiness or blessedness that is eternal life. This last, as Black emphasizes, is not a conventional theological appendix but the hermeneutic goal toward which the whole work moves: the Heptaplus understands Genesis not as a historical account of cosmological origins but as a text designed to lead its reader toward the intellectual ascent that constitutes human perfection.

The structure thus encodes multiple levels of meaning simultaneously. The first four expositions correspond to four ontological orders — elemental, celestial, angelic, human — that Pico treats as the three chief zones of creation plus man as their microcosmic synthesis. The fifth and sixth expositions then move from reading each world separately to reading their mutual correspondences, moving from analysis to the perception of cosmic unity. The seventh exposition transcends cosmology altogether to treat the eschatological goal. This movement from multiplicity through correspondence to unity is itself the anagogical arc of the work.

## The Dedicatory Epistle to Lorenzo de' Medici

Pico's dedicatory letter to Lorenzo de' Medici opens with a claim that has been read with appropriate caution by scholars: "The emulation of your studies, Lorenzo de' Medici, has moved me to review the hidden books of Moses" (*Movit aemulatio me studiorum tuorum, Laurenti Medices, ut arcana Moseos volumina recenserem*). Black notes that this attribution of the work's origins to Lorenzo's influence is a conventional rhetorical gesture of the dedicatory epistle and should not be taken entirely at face value. Lorenzo's role in securing Pico's release from arrest and providing him with hospitality in Florence was, however, entirely real, and the dedication has an obvious political dimension: it announces Pico as the client of the Medici, kept out of Rome and out of trouble, devoted to quiet scholarship in the Platonic mode.

What the epistle nonetheless announces is a genuinely ambitious hermeneutic claim. Pico positions the *Heptaplus* as an unveiling of hidden meaning in the Mosaic text — *arcana Moseos* — not a philological commentary but an allegorical penetration to the deeper wisdom that Moses encoded in his six-day narrative. He makes it clear that Moses was not merely recording events but concealing truths accessible only to those who read allegorically, and that the seven expositions are the method by which this concealment can be systematically undone. The dedication thus announces the first proem's argument before it is made.

## The Two Proems: Esotericism and Cosmic Structure

The first proem — the longest single section of the work — argues for the legitimacy of esoteric reading. Pico's claim is that Moses wrote for two audiences simultaneously: the unlearned, who receive the literal account, and the initiated, who perceive the hidden doctrine. This is not, Pico insists, a heterodox reading of Scripture. It is the reading of the Church Fathers themselves. And it is confirmed by the practice of every ancient wisdom tradition: the Greeks concealed their philosophy in myth, the Pythagoreans concealed it in number, and the Hebrews — above all Moses — concealed the deepest truths in the literal surface of the Torah.

Black traces this argument through three successive redactions in Pico's writings. The earliest form appears in the *Commento sopra una canzone d'amore* (c. 1485–86), where Pico defends poetic theology. A second form appears in the *Apologia* of 1487, where the argument serves a defensive function after the condemnations. The *Heptaplus* proem represents the third and most elaborated form, in which the argument has been systematically grounded in both Christian and Jewish traditions of commentary. Crucially, Pico's position in the *Heptaplus* differs from the mainstream medieval Christian consensus about allegorical reading. Mainstream medieval exegesis (following Augustine and the tradition of the four senses — literal, allegorical, tropological, anagogical) treated allegory as one mode among several, always grounded in the literal sense. Pico instead argues for esotericism in a more radical sense: the literal text is a concealment designed to protect truth from the unworthy, and the interpreter's task is to penetrate behind it, not to supplement it.

The second proem addresses Pico's model of cosmic structure and develops the theory of allegory that will govern the seven expositions. Here Pico articulates the principle that has been called the most distinctive element of his hermeneutics: the proposition that every world contains all other worlds within itself, each in its own mode (*omnia sunt in omnibus, sed in unoquoque secundum propriam naturam*, "all things are in all things, but in each according to its own nature"). This principle, drawn ultimately from the Neoplatonic tradition and particularly from Proclus, transforms the relationship between the seven expositions. Each exposition is not reading a different *part* of Genesis. Each is reading the *same* verse through a different ontological lens, perceiving the same total reality in a different mode. The Genesis verse, as a microcosm of divine creation, contains all the worlds within its seven words; and the seven expositions reveal how each world is present in every other.

## Kabbalistic Dimensions

The *Heptaplus* is not an explicitly Kabbalistic text in the way that the Kabbalistic conclusions of 1486 are explicitly Kabbalistic. Pico does not cite Kabbalistic sources by name in the body of the expositions. But the work encodes Kabbalistic patterns throughout, and the final section on *bereshit* draws on specifically Kabbalistic exegetical traditions in ways that Black, Wirszubski, and Giulio Busi have all examined in detail.

The word *bereshit* — "in the beginning" — was for Kabbalistic interpreters a word of extraordinary density. Rabbinic tradition held that the Torah began with *bereshit* rather than with *aleph* for reasons concealed within the word itself; and Kabbalistic exegesis found in *bereshit* encoded references to the sefirot, to the divine names, and to the structure of creation. For Pico, the final exposition of *bereshit* is the culmination of the whole work: having read the verse seven times over through seven ontological registers, he now turns to the single word that precedes all the others, the word that is the beginning of the beginning, and finds within it the entire structure of creation compressed into a single linguistic act. The analysis Pico offers draws on the letter-by-letter decomposition of *bereshit* as practiced in Kabbalistic texts — particularly those made available to him through Flavius Mithridates's Latin translations of the Zohar, the commentaries of Recanati, and other Kabbalistic sources — and at the same time integrates this analysis with Pseudo-Dionysian Christian theology and Neoplatonic metaphysics.

Black argues that the structure of the *Heptaplus* as a whole — its sevenfold division — reflects the Kabbalistic doctrine of the forty-nine gates of understanding. This doctrine, known from texts Pico had read (Moses had reached forty-nine of the fifty gates; the fiftieth was inaccessible to any human), provides the numerological logic that governs the work: seven expositions each of seven chapters, producing forty-nine units of commentary before the final, eighth section that reaches toward what lies beyond the forty-nine gates. The Sabbath structure — seven as the number of rest and completion — provides a second, complementary numerological frame: the Heptaplus enacts a hermeneutic Sabbath, a sevenfold labor that points toward the divine rest of eternal contemplation.

Wirszubski's *Pico's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989) remains the definitive account of the specific Kabbalistic texts available to Pico through Mithridates's translations and of the ways Pico deployed them. Wirszubski demonstrates that Recanati's commentary on the Pentateuch — the single most influential Kabbalistic source for the *Conclusiones* — remained a central point of reference for Pico's subsequent work, including the *Heptaplus*, even as Pico's engagement with Kabbalistic material became less explicit and more structurally embedded.

## The Post-Crisis Context

The *Heptaplus* appeared in the autumn of 1489, three years after the *Conclusiones* and two years after the papal ban and the *Apologia*. The Roman crisis had been severe: arrest, flight to France, capture, imprisonment, release through Medici intervention, and then the enforced retreat to Florence. Lorenzo's letter of 13 June 1489 to his ambassador in Rome described Pico as living like a monk, constantly engaged in theological work, saying the canonical hours, observing fasts, living simply and without ostentation. The letter reads, as Black notes, like a rehabilitation effort. It was unsuccessful: the ambassador reported back that the pope found Pico's continued theological work intolerable and would prefer him to write poetry.

The pope's response to the *Heptaplus* itself was similarly hostile. The papal secretary Lanfredini reported that Roman theologians had examined the work on Genesis and condemned it because Pico was in many places repeating the same heresies from the *Conclusiones*, merely introducing them indirectly. Lorenzo again defended Pico vigorously. The *Heptaplus* was enthusiastically received by humanist and intellectual circles in Florence and elsewhere, and it went on to be translated into Italian in 1555 and French in 1578.

What the Roman response reveals is that the *Heptaplus*'s strategy of recasting syncretic doctrine within a biblical commentary format did not succeed in neutralizing ecclesiastical suspicion. But it did succeed in a different sense: it transformed Pico's project from the exposed, polemical form of the *Conclusiones* — a list of theses inviting dispute and inviting scrutiny — into the more sheltered form of sustained exegesis, where the doctrinal commitments are embedded in layers of exposition rather than stated baldly as theses. The *Heptaplus* is not a retreat from the positions of 1486 but a reorganization of them, and that reorganization has its own intellectual coherence, quite apart from its apologetic function.

## Scholarly Interpretation

Black's monograph *Pico's Heptaplus and Biblical Hermeneutics* (2006) is the most thorough modern study. Black's central argument is that the *Heptaplus* articulates a theory of allegory that is genuinely distinctive and that differs from the mainstream medieval Christian tradition in specific and important ways. For Black, Pico's concept of esotericism — the idea that a hidden meaning is structurally separate from the literal sense and designed for a select audience — diverges from Augustinian tradition and draws instead on a combination of Neoplatonic, Pseudo-Dionysian, and Kabbalistic sources. The *Heptaplus* is best understood not as an orthodox biblical commentary that incorporates heterodox elements but as an autonomous hermeneutic theory for which the biblical commentary form is a vehicle.

Black also insists on the importance of the work's epistemological commitments. The *Heptaplus* is concerned throughout with *felicitas*, with the proper end of human intellectual life, and the seven expositions constitute a kind of guided intellectual ascent. The reader who follows Pico through all seven expositions of Genesis 1:1 does not simply accumulate information about seven worlds; she is guided to perceive the unity of the whole, to understand the principle of correspondence that links all the worlds, and thereby to ascend toward the intellectual contemplation that is, for Pico, the beginning of eternal happiness. This makes the *Heptaplus* continuous with the *Oration on the Dignity of Man* in a fundamental way: where the *Oration* describes human dignity as the capacity to ascend through all the levels of being by transforming oneself, the *Heptaplus* provides a hermeneutic enactment of that ascent — reading Scripture is, for Pico, itself a form of intellectual self-transformation.

For an educated reader encountering the *Heptaplus* for the first time, the essential point of orientation is this: it is not a conventional commentary, and its relationship to Genesis is not that of exegesis in the ordinary sense. It reads a single verse seven times and each reading is complete. The text of Genesis 1:1 is not explained by the seven expositions so much as it is used as a mandala — a structured object whose every element contains the whole and whose contemplation is itself the goal. The verse is a microcosm of Scripture; Scripture is a microcosm of creation; creation is a microcosm of God. To read the *Heptaplus* is to follow Pico's demonstration that these equations hold.
