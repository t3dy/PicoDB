# Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem

The *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem* is the longest and most systematically organized work Pico della Mirandola undertook. Composed in twelve books, it mounts a comprehensive attack on judicial or divinatory astrology — the practice of reading celestial configurations to forecast contingent human events — and engages that practice at every level at which it could claim rational authority: the credibility of its founding sources, the soundness of its natural-philosophical premises, the reliability of its predictive record, and its compatibility with Christian theology and moral philosophy. Pico began the work probably in 1493 and was still writing it at his death on 17 November 1494, at the age of thirty-one. It was published posthumously in 1496 by his nephew Gianfrancesco Pico della Mirandola, working in close proximity to Girolamo Savonarola and the community of San Marco in Florence. The only modern critical edition is Eugenio Garin's two-volume text published in 1946–52 and reissued by Aragno in 2004. For PicoDB, the *Disputationes* is simultaneously Pico's most extended single philosophical composition and one of the most textually complicated items in his corpus, requiring a distinction between what Pico wrote, what Gianfrancesco published, and what later readers received.

**The intellectual context: what was at stake in Renaissance debates about astrology**

Astrology in the late fifteenth century was not a marginal or simply superstitious practice. It was taught at Italian universities as a branch of natural philosophy, practically continuous with mathematical astronomy; it was practiced by court physicians, political advisers, and humanist scholars; it occupied a long and respected tradition of medieval Latin learning that synthesized Arabic, Greek, and Latin materials into a coherent technical discipline. The tradition distinguished at its core between two kinds of inquiry: the mathematical description of celestial motion, which was theoretical and largely uncontroversial, and the application of that mathematics to the prediction of events in the sublunar world, which was philosophically contentious. The second domain — judicial astrology, sometimes also called divinatory astrology — included nativities (predictions based on the configuration of the heavens at an individual's birth), elections (the choice of auspicious moments for undertaking action), horary astrology (answering questions from the positions of the heavens at the moment the question is asked), and the interpretation of great conjunctions (the rare meetings of Saturn and Jupiter, taken to herald historical transformations). All of these rested on a common premise: that the celestial bodies, through their positions relative to one another and to the zodiacal signs, exercise a determining or strongly conditioning influence on particular outcomes in the lives of individuals and nations.

The philosophical problem this premise raised was acute. Medieval theologians had accepted that the heavens might exert influence over natural processes in the sublunar world — weather, generation, the tempering of matter — while insisting that divine providence and human free will were not subject to celestial necessity. But the line between influence and determination was always contestable in practice. If the stars at the moment of birth could reliably predict what a person would do in life, what profession they would follow, whether they would be violent or peaceful, whether they would die young or old, then the freedom of the will was either illusory or so hedged by celestial conditioning as to be practically unimportant. Theologians like Thomas Aquinas had navigated this by holding that the celestial bodies influenced the passions and the body but could not compel rational choice; the stars, on this account, inclined without necessitating. But practicing astrologers pressed against that boundary constantly, claiming predictive power over the very outcomes the theological tradition assigned to free will.

This was the landscape into which Pico intervened — not as someone ignorant of the tradition he attacked, but as someone who had spent nearly a decade working with celestial natural philosophy, Platonic accounts of the soul's descent through the spheres, Kabbalistic mappings of angelic hierarchies onto celestial orders, and the natural-magical theory of celestial images and sympathies. His attack on astrology was the work of an insider.

**Pico's central arguments**

The *Disputationes* does not make a single argument but assembles a battery of them, organized so that even a reader who rejected some would encounter others. Akopyan's study, the first to read all twelve books with systematic attention, identifies the major lines.

The first and in many ways most devastating line of attack is directed at the foundations of the astrological tradition itself. Pico argues that astrology claims an ancient and authoritative heritage — Chaldean, Egyptian, Arab — but that the texts in which this heritage is preserved are either fraudulent attributions or cannot support the doctrines derived from them. The *Centiloquium* attributed to Ptolemy is a particular target: Pico contests both its authenticity and the interpretations placed upon it by later astrologers. The authority of Abu Ma'shar, the great medieval Arabic synthesizer, is disputed. The entire appeal to a venerable *prisca* astronomical science, a sacred ancient wisdom about the heavens, is shown to be historically groundless. Here the *Disputationes* connects directly to Pico's broader critical project across his career. In *De ente et uno*, Pico had argued that medieval interpreters had distorted the Platonic tradition. In the *Disputationes*, he makes an analogous argument about astrology: the tradition is not what it claims to be, the sources do not say what the practitioners say they say, and the discipline has been built on a chain of misattributions and corruptions.

The second line of argument targets celestial causation at the level of natural philosophy. Pico concedes that the heavens have general influence over the sublunar world — light, heat, the tides, the generative processes of nature. What he denies is that this general influence can be specified down to the particular: that from the position of Mars at this degree of Scorpio and Saturn at that degree of Capricorn, at this hour of this day, one can reliably infer that a particular individual will have a particular fate. The gap between general celestial influence and the particular determinations divinatory astrology claims to read is not a gap astrologers can bridge, because they have no coherent causal account of how celestial bodies would act differentially on individuals. Moreover, Akopyan shows that Pico's natural-philosophical argument here involves a sophisticated engagement with Iamblichus and Ficino on the theory of celestial light: Pico is not simply asserting Aristotelian limits on celestial causation but proposing a position that mediates between Platonic and Aristotelian accounts.

The third argument is empirical in character: the predictive record of astrologers is a catastrophe. When twins are born at nearly identical celestial moments and go on to have entirely different lives, the astrological prediction cannot account for the divergence. When historical events are checked against the conjunctions supposed to have forecast them, the correspondence collapses under examination. Pico presses the empirical case not because he thinks astrology could in principle be validated by better data but because he wants to show that practitioners have never subjected their own claims to the test their predecessors said the discipline required.

The fourth argument is theological and moral. Astrological determinism compromises the freedom of the will, and this compromise is not merely an abstract philosophical problem but a practical danger to moral life and Christian theology. If a person's wickedness is written in the stars, the basis for sin, merit, repentance, and judgment is undermined. The theological stakes are continuous with what Pico had argued, from different angles, in the *Oration* and in his defense of the condemned theses: human dignity rests on the capacity for self-determination, and any account of the world that assigns our actions to external causes external to the will is a diminishment of that dignity.

**The Disputationes and Pico's broader philosophy**

The relationship between the *Disputationes* and Pico's earlier work is more complicated than it appears. The obvious reading — that the late polemic against astrology marks a conversion from his earlier embrace of celestial natural philosophy, magical correspondences, and Kabbalistic-astral hierarchies — is precisely what Akopyan challenges. The *Disputationes* targets judicial astrology: the claim that specific celestial configurations determine specific individual outcomes. It does not target every account of how the heavens shape or influence natural processes, nor does it abandon the framework of ordered celestial hierarchy that Pico deploys in the Heptaplus and elsewhere.

This distinction is especially important for reading Pico's natural magic. In the *Apology* defending the condemned theses, and in the theses themselves, Pico had distinguished natural magic sharply from demonic magic and from forbidden arts. Natural magic, for Pico, is the completion of natural philosophy: it works by understanding the hidden sympathies and antipathies in nature, including the ways in which natural objects below stand in correspondence with celestial influences above. This is not the same as divinatory astrology. Natural magic, as Pico defines it, does not claim to read the future from planetary positions; it uses knowledge of natural powers to act in the world. The *Disputationes*' attack on judicial astrology does not therefore commit Pico to abandoning natural magic, and reading the late polemic as a general rejection of all engagement with celestial forces is a mistake the text does not require.

Similarly, Pico's 900 Conclusions had included theses on astrological and magical topics that gave the heavens a role in natural processes and in the theurgical operations linking the human soul to higher orders. These theses operate within a different conceptual frame than judicial astrology. In the 900 Conclusions, celestial orders are part of a Neoplatonic-Kabbalistic metaphysics in which the sefirot, the angelic hierarchies, and the spheres correspond across levels of being; influence moves within this system as part of the ordered downward emanation of divine power. That framework is not divinatory: it does not pretend to read contingent individual destinies from planetary degrees. Akopyan insists on this continuity because it alone explains why the *Disputationes* looks like a rupture only if one equates every form of celestial thinking with the judicial astrology the text attacks.

**The editorial problem**

Any serious engagement with the *Disputationes* must confront the fact that the text Garin edited and that readers use is not a manuscript Pico prepared for publication. It is a posthumous print made under conditions that Stephen Farmer has argued raise significant questions about editorial intervention. Farmer's chapter on Pico and Anti-Pico in his edition of the *Syncretism in the West* volume establishes the general principle: no text printed or reprinted by Gianfrancesco can be assumed free of adulteration. Gianfrancesco's editorial program was not neutral scholarship; it was the construction of an image of his uncle that served his own theological and institutional interests, operating in the shadow of Savonarola's execution and in the context of Florence's ongoing conflicts over prophecy, astrology, and reform.

The specific issues for the *Disputationes* are several. The text contains what looks like a palinode: passages in which Pico apparently repudiates the prisci theologi, natural magic, and esoteric tradition he had celebrated in 1486. Farmer asks whether this repudiation is Pico's or Gianfrancesco's. The absence of any substantial engagement with Kabbalah in the *Disputationes* — remarkable given Pico's sustained investment in Kabbalistic study across the preceding decade — is a further puzzle; it might reflect Pico's own decision to leave the Kabbalistic framework aside in a work focused on divinatory astrology, or it might reflect editorial removal. Savonarola himself used the *Disputationes* in his *Contro gli astrologi*, and the political utility of the text to the Savonarolan reform movement gave Gianfrancesco strong incentives to publish it in a form that emphasized its prophetic and anti-magical implications at the expense of whatever else Pico's working manuscript contained.

Akopyan is more cautious than Farmer on the tampering question. He does not deny the editorial problems but does not make the text's philosophical coherence depend on resolving them. His argument is that the major lines of the *Disputationes* — the historical criticism of astrological authority, the natural-philosophical critique of celestial causation, the moral-theological objection to determinism — are genuinely Pico's, continuous with his method and concerns in the works whose textual history is less compromised. On this reading, the *Disputationes* can be read and analyzed as a document of Pico's late thought while maintaining appropriate caution about passages that seem to serve specifically Savonarolan or anti-magical purposes. PicoDB follows Akopyan's methodological position while flagging the Farmer concern: the transmission status of the *Disputationes* is COMPLEX, and doctrinal conclusions drawn from passages that stand alone in the text — especially those repudiating earlier positions without philosophical argument — should carry a lower confidence than conclusions drawn from the extended argumentative sections.

**Reception**

The *Disputationes* was not a text that simply ended a debate. It opened one, or rather several. Savonarola's *Contro gli astrologi*, composed in direct response to Pico's work, used the anti-astrological argument in the service of prophetic theology: the critique of astrology was the counterpart of the defense of true prophecy. Gianfrancesco's own treatise *De rerum praenotione* and his *Quaestio de falsitate astrologiae* developed the anti-astrological position further within a framework that made prisca theologia into prisca haeresis — ancient theology into ancient heresy — sharpening the anti-magical implications at the expense of the Neoplatonic and Kabbalistic dimensions of Giovanni Pico's original project.

Outside Italy, Maximus the Greek, who had been present in Florence at San Marco and worked for Gianfrancesco, absorbed the Pico-Savonarola anti-astrological tradition and deployed it upon his return to Muscovy, where he wrote *Epistles Against Astrology* that framed judicial astrology as a characteristically Latin corruption, unworthy of Orthodox Christianity. This is perhaps the most unexpected vector of the *Disputationes*' influence.

On the other side, Lucio Bellanti's direct refutation, addressed to Lorenzo de' Medici and working through Pico's arguments one by one, represents the first sustained attempt to defend judicial astrology against the *Disputationes*' attacks. Bellanti accepted more of Pico's framework than he wished to: the very point-by-point structure of his response demonstrated that Pico had shifted the burden of proof. Giovanni Pontano, poet and statesman, replied to Pico from a different position: as a humanist and courtier who saw in astrological practice a kind of learned culture irreducible to the philosophical claims Pico had dismantled, and who defended the discipline's cultural value even while conceding much of the natural-philosophical case. Francesco Zorzi's *De harmonia mundi*, a massive Franciscan synthesis of Neoplatonism, Kabbalah, and biblical hermeneutics, engaged the *Disputationes* more obliquely, absorbing some of its critique while constructing a vision of celestial harmony that retained the positive valence of astral correspondence.

By the mid-sixteenth century, the *Disputationes* had become a standard reference in European debates about the status of astrology, used in academic disputations, cited in theological controversy, and invoked wherever the question of celestial determination and free will required a philosophical touchstone. Its influence can be traced into Kepler, who knew the work and engaged with the anti-astrological tradition Pico had helped to define, even as Kepler himself sought to distinguish a purified mathematical astronomy from the pseudo-scientific apparatus of judicial prediction. The *Disputationes* did not kill astrology; it changed the terms under which astrology could be defended.
