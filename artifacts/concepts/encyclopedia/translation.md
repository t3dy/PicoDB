Translation, in the intellectual world that Pico della Mirandola inhabited, carried two distinct but related meanings that his work constantly sets in play. The first is the familiar linguistic sense: the movement of a text from one language to another, from Greek into Latin, from Hebrew into Latin, from Latin into Italian vernacular. The second is the wider cultural and historical sense implied by the medieval concept of *translatio studii* — the movement of learning from one civilization to another, from East to West, from Hebrew antiquity through Greek philosophy to Latin Christianity. In Pico, these two meanings are structurally interdependent. His entire encyclopedic project of universal concordance — the project announced by the *900 Conclusions* and given its most grandiose statement in the *Oration* — presupposes that wisdom originally lodged in ancient Eastern sources can be translated, in both senses, into the Latin philosophical present. The history of scholarship on Pico has made clear that this presupposition was itself shaped, enabled, and in important ways distorted by the specific translation activities that put texts in his hands.

The foundation of Latin Renaissance Neoplatonism is a body of translations, and that body was almost entirely the work of Marsilio Ficino. Before Ficino, European readers had extremely limited access to Plato: part of the *Timaeus* in the fourth-century Latin of Chalcidius; two dialogues in the literal, often unreadable versions by Henricus Aristippus; sections of the *Parmenides* embedded in Proclus's commentary as translated by William of Moerbeke. Platonic concepts were known through Cicero, Augustine, Pseudo-Dionysius, and Boethius, but direct access to the dialogues was fragmentary and unreliable. Allen's account of Ficino as the primary architect of Renaissance Platonism emphasizes this point: "Plato's recovery was distinctly a Renaissance achievement and mainly the work of a single person" (Allen 2017, 327). Under Cosimo de' Medici's patronage, Ficino translated the complete Platonic canon — all the dialogues and letters — in a publication that appeared in Florence in 1484. This was not merely a scholarly edition but a cultural event. Ficino accompanied the translations with introductory *argumenta*, summaries, and commentaries, including his long commentary on the *Symposium* that became the central document of Renaissance love theory. He also translated the *Corpus Hermeticum* into Latin in 1463 at Cosimo's dying request, completed the *Enneads* of Plotinus in 1492, and produced partial translations of Proclus, Iamblichus, Porphyry, and Pseudo-Dionysius.

This body of translation created the textual basis for everything Pico could do with the Platonic and Neoplatonic traditions. When Pico read Plotinus's *Enneads*, he read Ficino's Latin. When he engaged with Proclus's *Elements of Theology*, he did so through versions Ficino had prepared. The relationship between Pico and Ficino as a relationship between a reader and the translator who made his reading possible has never been adequately theorized, partly because the two figures are typically presented as intellectual peers rather than in a patron-dependent relationship. Farmer has documented the evidence that Pico was in fact highly critical of Ficino as a philosophical commentator — he described the "sweating Platonists" (*sudantes Platonici*) who barely understood Plotinus, a characterization widely taken to be aimed at Ficino — and that this criticism was at least partly motivated by a dispute about how to interpret specific Plotinian texts (Farmer 1998, 35 n. 35). The irony is that these arguments were conducted entirely within the linguistic frame that Ficino had constructed: Pico disagreed with Ficino's reading of Plotinus in a Latin that Ficino had translated.

The problem of translation as distortion rather than mere conveyance becomes most acute in Pico's encounter with Kabbalah, which was mediated entirely by Flavius Mithridates, a Sicilian Jewish convert whose Latin renderings of Hebrew and Aramaic Kabbalistic texts constituted virtually the only point of entry Pico had into that tradition. Wirszubski's meticulous investigation of the Mithridates manuscripts in the Vatican Library — Codices Vaticani Ebraici 189–191 and Codex Chigi A.VI.190, amounting in their present state to nearly 3,500 folio pages — established definitively that these translations are not neutral renderings but systematic theological interventions (Wirszubski 1989, 69–120). Mithridates introduced Christianizing interpolations throughout: christological readings of passages that had no such meaning in Hebrew; glosses that identified the Kabbalistic *sefirot* (the ten divine attributes or emanations in the Kabbalistic system) with the persons of the Trinity; occasional notes reminding Pico that certain material was "impossible to understand" without knowing the Hebrew letters themselves, while simultaneously providing the reader with a heavily doctored Latin that obscured precisely those features of Kabbalistic reasoning that depended on the Hebrew text.

Wirszubski's chapter "Christianizing Interpretations" documents this process in granular detail (Wirszubski 1989, 106–13). The effect is not merely that Pico received an inaccurate text; it is that the act of translation had already performed the theological argument Pico believed he was discovering independently. When Pico claimed in the *Apology* that the Kabbalah confirmed the divinity of Christ and the doctrine of the Trinity — "there is no science," he wrote, "that more firmly establishes the divinity of Christ than magic and Kabbalah" — the "science" in question was a Latin text that had been pre-shaped to confirm exactly that conclusion. The translation was simultaneously a theological argument, and the discovery of concordance between Hebrew wisdom and Christian doctrine was an artifact of the translation process rather than a finding in the Hebrew texts themselves. In this sense, Pico's most celebrated intellectual achievement — the demonstration that the most ancient Eastern wisdom converges with Christian truth — was built on a foundation of motivated, systematically distorted translation.

Wirszubski also documents a feature of Kabbalistic reasoning that makes any translation into non-Hebrew languages philosophically problematic in a way that goes beyond questions of accuracy or theological motivation. Kabbalistic interpretation is deeply embedded in the visual and combinatory properties of the Hebrew alphabet: arguments depend on isopsephic equivalences (the numerical values of Hebrew letters and words), on letter-combinations, on the visible structure of individual characters. "Arguments or interpretations that are inseparable from the letters of Hebrew words tend to become lost — to be literally invisible — or become unintelligible in a plain translation" (Wirszubski 1989, 7). Mithridates occasionally inserted the Hebrew text alongside his Latin rendering and addressed notes directly to Pico — "O Pice, magna sunt hec sed impossibile est ut intelligantur a te nisi in propriis literis et verbis hebraicis" (great are these things, but it is impossible for you to understand them without the proper letters and Hebrew words) — acknowledging the fundamental untranslatability of a tradition whose deepest operations depend on features of Hebrew that disappear in any other script (Wirszubski 1989, 7–8). Pico's knowledge of Hebrew was real but limited — sufficient, by October 1486, to "dictate a letter not yet with praise, but without fault," as he himself admitted — and the structural impossibility of fully translating Kabbalistic reasoning into Latin is one reason why scholars like Edelheit have insisted that Pico's encounter with Kabbalah must be understood as a highly mediated reception rather than a direct engagement with the tradition (Edelheit 2022, 2–3).

Translation in the sense of *translatio studii* — the movement of wisdom from earlier to later, from East to West — is the explicit framework of the *Oration*'s first major movement. Pico's historical scheme presents learning as emanating from the Hebrews and Chaldeans, flowing to the Greeks, and passing from the Greeks to the Latin West. "Almost all wisdom emanated from the barbarians to the Greeks, from the Greeks to us," he writes in an early draft of the *Oration*, and the structure of the *900 Conclusions* is organized to mirror this historical movement: the Latin scholastics, whose theses begin the text and whose tradition is most recent and most divided, are followed by the Arabs, the Greeks, the Chaldeans, Egyptians, and Hebrews, whose concord increases the further back in time one goes (Farmer 1998, 33–34). The *translatio studii* is thus not merely a background assumption but a governing structural principle of Pico's entire encyclopedic project. To synthesize all wisdom is, in his scheme, to reverse-translate: to move back through the linguistic and cultural layers through which truth has been successively transmitted — and increasingly distorted — in order to reach the original, harmonious source.

Pico's own texts are themselves acts of translation in this expanded sense. The *Heptaplus* (1489) is a sustained hermeneutical effort to translate the meaning encoded in the Hebrew word *bereshit* ("in the beginning," the opening word of Genesis) into the philosophical languages of Neoplatonism and Kabbalah. Pico presents himself as recovering the esoteric senses — the anagogical, the mystical, the natural-philosophical — hidden beneath the literal surface of Moses's text. The *Heptaplus* thus operates as a translation from one register (biblical narrative) to multiple registers simultaneously (Neoplatonic cosmology, Kabbalistic doctrine, Christian theology), claiming that these registers are not alternatives but dimensions of a single, inexhaustibly rich originary text. Whether this procedure succeeds depends entirely on whether the sources Pico uses to illuminate Moses are themselves trustworthy translations of an ancient wisdom — and Wirszubski's work makes clear that at least the Kabbalistic component of that hermeneutic is built on Mithridates' distorted Latin, not on the Hebrew originals.

The *Commento* (1486, published 1519) is a translation in a different and more explicit sense: it is Pico's move from the Latin scholastic and Neoplatonic tradition into Italian vernacular philosophical commentary. The decision to write a philosophical commentary in Italian — rather than in Latin, the language of the schools — was itself a cultural statement, associating philosophical reflection with the new Italian literary culture being fashioned around Dante, Petrarch, and the Florentine Platonic Academy. Pico was translating Platonic love theory, which he had absorbed primarily through Ficino's Latin commentary on the *Symposium*, into a register accessible to readers who were educated but not necessarily schooled in Latin philosophy. This is translation as cultural transmission: the *Commento* moves Neoplatonic metaphysics of love from a Latin clerical to an Italian literary context, in the process requiring Pico to find Italian equivalents for technical Latin and Greek terms and to make explicit what a scholastic text could leave as shared assumption. Allen's essay "The Birth Day of Venus: Pico as Platonic Exegete in the *Commento* and the *Heptaplus*" has explored the ways in which Pico's Platonic exegesis in the *Commento* differs from Ficino's and represents a distinctive interpretive position, shaped by but not identical to the tradition of Ficinian translation and commentary that made Platonic texts available in Italian Latin (Allen 2017, 170–205).

The *De ente et uno* (1492) enacts translation in a third sense: it "translates" a philosophical dispute — the long-standing controversy between Platonists and Aristotelians about whether Being (*ens*) is prior to the One (*unum*) or the One prior to Being — into a resolvable scholastic question about the convertibility of the transcendentals. Pico argues that Plato and Aristotle are in agreement about the relationship of Being, Unity, Goodness, and Truth if each philosopher is read correctly within his own philosophical context. The translation here is interpretive: Pico is proposing a hermeneutical frame within which the apparent contradiction between the two traditions dissolves. That this "translation" is itself controversial — Edelheit's account of Pico's scholastic formation shows in detail how Pico's reading of Aquinas, Scotus, and Henry of Ghent shapes the *De ente et uno*'s arguments in ways that are not simply reducible to either Platonism or Aristotelianism (Edelheit 2022, 214–73) — illustrates how the concept of translation, in Pico's work, names a practice always entangled with the philosophical stakes of what is translated.

The recognition that translation in Pico's world was not a neutral technical operation but a field of contested interpretation, doctrinal motivation, and cultural power transforms how we read the entire edifice of his concordist program. The ancient wisdom that Pico claimed to synthesize — Chaldean, Hebrew, Greek, Arabic, Latin — was never directly available to him. It reached him through translators with their own agendas: Mithridates, whose Christianizing interpolations performed in advance the theological argument Pico thought he was independently discovering; Ficino, whose Platonic translations created the conceptual vocabulary Pico both used and argued against; the Byzantine manuscript tradition that transmitted the Orphic Hymns and the *Corpus Hermeticum* to Renaissance Italy under false pretenses of antiquity. To read Pico on universal concordance is to read a text built from translations, and the question the scholarship has gradually forced into the open is whether the concordance Pico found was a genuine discovery in the sources or a function of the translational medium through which those sources came to him.
