# Benivieni, Girolamo

Girolamo Benivieni (1453–1542) was a Florentine poet, scholar, and religious reformer whose long life spanned almost a century of intellectual and political upheaval in Italy, from the height of Medici cultural patronage through the Savonarolan reform movement and beyond. In the context of Pico della Mirandola studies, Benivieni matters above all as the author of the *Canzone dello amor celeste e divino* — the philosophical love poem on which Pico composed his *Commento sopra una canzona d'amore* — and as the close friend through whose literary activity Pico's most sustained and original philosophical contribution to the theory of love was provoked into existence. But Benivieni is also more than a pretext for Pico's philosophy. He was a significant cultural figure in his own right, a member of the innermost circle of the Florentine Platonic Academy, a poet of sufficient prestige to be called "the foremost living Florentine vernacular poet" in his own time, and a man whose subsequent religious conversion and decades-long work of editorial preservation shaped — and in important respects distorted — the way that Pico's thought was transmitted to posterity.

Benivieni came to cultural prominence as a teenager in the Medici circle, delighting the company with a gift for spontaneous verse recitation and musical performance that earned him the nickname "the Other Orpheus" — a sobriquet that carried pointed overtones in a milieu where Marsilio Ficino was himself celebrated under the Orphic name, and where the figure of Orpheus served as the patron saint of a particular conception of the poet-theologian who encodes divine wisdom in musical verse (Roush 2002, 50n). He developed friendships with Ficino, Angelo Poliziano, Pandolfo Collenuccio, and other leading figures of the Florentine intellectual world, and he surpassed many of his contemporaries in the study of Hebrew, a distinction that his later century would recognize by commissioning him — a task he never completed — to produce an Italian vernacular translation of the Bible. In the late 1470s and early 1480s he circulated Petrarchan-inflected love lyrics and published pastoral poems that earned him a reputation in the vernacular tradition; he also cast a Boccaccian novella in verse, demonstrating a range that moved comfortably between lyric, narrative, and philosophical modes.

It was in the context of this broad literary activity, and through his deep immersion in Ficino's philosophy, that Benivieni composed the *Canzona d'amore* — more precisely the *Canzone dello amor celeste e divino*, whose incipit is *Amor, dalle cui man sospeso el freno* ("Love, in whose hands the bridle [of my heart] is suspended") — probably around 1486. The poem was an ambitious undertaking: a vernacular Italian love canzone in the technical formal tradition of the *dolce stil novo*, but with philosophical content derived almost entirely from Ficino's *De amore*, the Latin commentary on Plato's *Symposium* that had been completed around 1469 and was circulating with increasing influence in the years before its printed publication in 1484. Benivieni was essentially versifying Ficino: translating the metaphysical framework of Florentine Neoplatonism — the two Venuses, the circle of divine love, the ascent of the soul through beauty toward the Good, the cosmic bond of *amor* that holds the universe together — into the inherited forms of Italian lyric poetry. The result was a poem that combined formal elegance with philosophical seriousness in a way that reflected Benivieni's dual formation in the vernacular literary tradition and the Platonic Academy.

Pico's decision to write a commentary on Benivieni's canzone was simultaneously an act of friendship, a philosophical intervention, and a literary gesture of some complexity. The friendship between the two men is among the most thoroughly documented intimate intellectual relationships in the Italian Quattrocento. Pico was eight years younger than Benivieni, but the age difference does not appear to have produced a hierarchical dynamic; the surviving evidence suggests a relationship of genuine mutuality, sustained by shared philosophical interests and deepened by the intensity of the religious conversion that both men underwent under Savonarola's influence in the final years of Pico's life. Benivieni's later work to preserve and transmit Pico's writings, including his collection of Pico's letters, was an act not merely of editorial duty but of personal devotion. The *Commento* itself is addressed to Benivieni in a prefatory dedication that frames the whole enterprise as an act of friendship — Pico writing for his friend, on his friend's poem, in order to give that poem the philosophical commentary that would reveal the full depth of the ideas it contained.

The literary gesture embedded in Pico's project was itself significant. By writing a prose commentary on a vernacular poem, Pico was participating in a tradition with distinguished precedents: Dante had commented on his own *canzoni* in the *Convivio*, Cavalcanti's *Donna me prega* had generated a commentary tradition, and Ficino himself had linked the love-poem tradition to the philosophical tradition by invoking Giovanni Cavalcanti in the *De amore*. Pico's *Commento* inserts itself into this lineage while simultaneously converting the commentary from a literary to a predominantly philosophical exercise: the poem is the occasion, not the object, and what the commentary actually does is work out the metaphysical argument that Pico wants to make about love, beauty, and the conditions of genuine philosophical eros. Benivieni's poem thus functions in the *Commento* somewhat as the *Symposium* functions in Ficino's *De amore*: as the ostensible subject that gives the writer permission to develop his own philosophical positions.

Those philosophical positions, as Sears Jayne established in his translation and introduction to the *Commento* (Jayne 1984), represent a significant and pointed departure from the Ficinian framework that Benivieni had versified. Pico does not simply elaborate or deepen what Benivieni's poem says; he corrects it at the philosophical level, departing from the *De amore* in ways that amount to a sustained polemic against Ficino. Pico attacks Ficino directly by name in the unexpurgated text, faults his logic, complains of his ignorance of specific authorities, and disagrees with him about the metaphysical status of beauty and love, the correct interpretation of the *Symposium*'s two Venuses, and the meaning of the Orpheus and Eurydice episode that Ficino had chosen not to interpret at all (Allen 1986, 189–190). The *Commento* is thus, at the same time, a tribute to Benivieni's poem and an argument that the poem, as Ficino-derived verse, requires philosophical correction before its full meaning can be disclosed.

This produces a structural irony in the relation between Pico and Benivieni that has received insufficient critical attention. Benivieni had written the canzone as a loving amplification of Ficino's ideas, expressing in lyric form the philosophical vision that Ficino had developed and that the Platonic Academy had made central to its intellectual identity. Pico, in his commentary on that canzone, proceeds to dismantle significant portions of Ficino's account and to replace them with his own more rigorous and more severe analysis. This means that the poem and its commentary are, in a certain sense, in philosophical tension with each other: the verses embody a Ficinian philosophy that the prose commentary argues against. Whether Benivieni noticed this tension, or whether he was troubled by it, the surviving evidence does not conclusively establish. What is established is that the expurgated version of the *Commento* that Benivieni edited after Pico's death removed the direct attacks on Ficino — a choice that may reflect personal loyalty to the older philosopher, institutional prudence about antagonizing the Ficinian tradition, or simply the desire not to wound Ficino's memory and standing in the philosophical community.

The publication history of the *Commento* is inseparable from Benivieni's subsequent editorial activity, and understanding that history is essential for any reader who approaches the *Commento* through the texts that circulated in the sixteenth century and beyond. Pico died in November 1494, at the age of thirty-one, shortly after the death of Lorenzo de' Medici and amid the social upheaval caused by Charles VIII's invasion of Italy and the consequent preaching campaign of Girolamo Savonarola. He had never published the *Commento*, and indeed had been revising and redistributing its material through the last years of his life; he also quarried it for the *Heptaplus*, his 1489 commentary on the Mosaic account of creation, which incorporated several of the *Commento*'s arguments about the three worlds and the soul's ascent. After his death, Pico's nephew Gianfrancesco compiled and published an *Opera omnia* in 1496 that notably omitted both the *Commento* and the *900 Conclusions* — the two works most directly implicated in Pico's confrontations with papal authority and with Ficino — and this omission helped to establish the image of Pico as a harmonizing orthodox Neoplatonist that dominated early reception.

The *Commento* reached print only in 1519, when it appeared not among Pico's own works but in a Giunti edition of Benivieni's collected poetry. As Michael Allen's review of Jayne's translation establishes, the text that appeared in this edition had been worked over by Pico's friends — Benivieni prominently among them — in the period after Pico's death: the offensive references to Ficino had been excised, some of the more combative passages removed, and the material reordered (Allen 1986, 189–190). This expurgated text then served as the basis for subsequent editions in 1522, 1524, and 1731, and eventually appeared in the Basel editions of Pico's *Opera omnia* in 1557, 1572, and 1601. The result was that the *Commento* as posterity knew it for almost four centuries was Benivieni's version — a diplomatically altered text that suppressed the intellectual edge of Pico's argument in order to present a more accommodating, less combative philosophical document.

Benivieni also mined the *Commento* for his own later literary purposes. A 1500 edition of his own poems included marginal glosses drawn from or influenced by the *Commento*, even though that edition did not reprint the canzone that had originally occasioned it. His grand-nephew later drew on the *Commento* when drafting a proposal for yet another edition of Girolamo's collected works. The *Commento* thus passed through multiple stages of Benivienic mediation before it reached modern readers, and recovering Pico's original intentions requires reading through and against that mediation.

The recovery of the unexpurgated text — the text in which Pico's polemic against Ficino appears in its full form — was the achievement of twentieth-century Italian scholarship, above all the work of Eugenio Garin, whose critical edition of 1942 drew on manuscripts representing the earlier stages of the text before the expurgations. Garin's edition established the basis for modern Pico scholarship in Italian. In English, Sears Jayne's translation of 1984 — based on Garin's edition and supplemented by two subsequently identified manuscripts — made the *Commento* accessible in a form that restored, as far as possible, the combative order and content of the unexpurgated text. Jayne's interpretive decisions were themselves the subject of debate: Allen's 1986 review noted that Jayne's choice to follow the unexpurgated order against Garin's editorial judgment — Garin had adopted the order found in the expurgated manuscripts and the 1519 and later editions — was intellectually defensible but created difficulties for readers using the standard Italian reference text. The stemmatic reconstruction Jayne proposed, which postulated a complex set of lost drafts and elevated the status of Benivieni's own adaptation of the *Commento* as a textual witness, was described by Allen as controversial.

Beyond these editorial and textual questions, the relationship between Benivieni and Pico underwent a significant transformation in the last years of Pico's life. Both men came under the influence of Girolamo Savonarola, the Dominican friar whose apocalyptic preaching in Florence from 1490 onward attracted a devoted following among those who combined a serious religious sensibility with dissatisfaction at the ethical limitations of Medici court culture. Savonarola's movement — whose followers were called *piagnoni* ("weepers") for the copious tears his sermons provoked — called for moral austerity, republican liberty, and a radical reorientation of cultural life away from pagan antiquity and toward Christian devotion. Benivieni became one of the most committed *piagnoni*, composing lauds for the religious processions of 1496 and 1497, translating several of Savonarola's works into Italian, and in 1506 editing Dante's *Divine Comedy* with a proem — the "Cantico in laude di Dante" — that appropriated Dante's authority for the Savonarolan program (Roush 2002, 49–50). Pico's conversion to the Savonarolan movement in the early 1490s was more compressed in time, given that he died in 1494, but contemporary witnesses recorded that Pico had been profoundly moved by Savonarola's preaching and intended a radical reorientation of his scholarly life toward devotional study.

This shared conversion created what Sherry Roush has called a "piagnone" cultural identity that informed Benivieni's subsequent literary career long after Pico's death (Roush 2002). For Benivieni, the conversion produced a complicated retrospective relationship to the erotic philosophical content of the *Canzona d'amore*. The poem had celebrated, in the Ficinian framework, the power of divine love to draw the soul upward through beauty toward God — a vision that could, in principle, be compatible with Christian devotion. But the poem also participated in a literary and philosophical tradition that celebrated the erotics of the Platonic ascent in terms that a Savonarolan sensibility might find uncomfortably close to the pagan love poetry the preacher had condemned. Benivieni's later work to compile Pico's letters and preserve his memory was, in this context, an act of reconciliation: a way of honoring a friend who had shared both the Platonic intellectual world and the Savonarolan conversion, whose work therefore embodied the tension that Benivieni's own long life had to negotiate.

The influence of the *Commento* beyond Florence — and thus beyond the sphere of Benivieni's direct editorial control — was substantial but mediated. Gabriel Chappuys produced a partial French translation between 1544 and 1548, and it was this French version, rather than the Italian or the Basel Latin edition, that carried Pico's love theory into the intellectual currents of the French and eventually English Renaissance. Edmund Spenser, George Chapman, and Ben Jonson all show traces of the *Commento*'s influence, though the French transmission mixed Pico's argument with Ficinian elements in ways that blunted the anti-Ficinian edge. Art historians have found the *Commento*'s allegorizations of Venus, Porus, Penia, and the birth of Love useful for interpreting Botticelli and Michelangelo, though here again the influence operates through a text that is already Benivienic in its mediation rather than straightforwardly Piconian.

For the reader of PicoDB, Benivieni matters as both a figure in Pico's intellectual biography and an agent of Pico's reception history. He is the friend who provided the poem that occasioned Pico's most sustained philosophical treatise. He is the editor who softened that treatise's most combative passages for posterity. He is the *piagnone* who shared Pico's final religious reorientation and carried that reorientation into his own literary career for four decades after Pico's death. And he is the poet who exemplifies the ambition of Florentine Neoplatonism to unite vernacular lyric poetry with Platonic metaphysics — an ambition that Pico accepted as the premise of the *Commento* while subjecting the Ficinian philosophy that Benivieni had versified to a rigorous and sometimes harsh philosophical audit. The *Commento* emerges from the collaboration and the tension between these two men, and understanding it fully requires attending to both.
