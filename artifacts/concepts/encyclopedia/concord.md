# Concord

Among all the intellectual ambitions that shaped Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's brief career, none was more central or more programmatically explicit than the project of philosophical concordance: the demonstration that the great traditions of human wisdom — Greek, scholastic, Platonic, Hermetic, Kabbalistic, theological — ultimately agree with one another, not superficially and not through borrowing, but because they all reflect the same underlying truth that has been distributed across human history by divine Providence. Pico did not merely hold this as a private conviction. He proposed to demonstrate it publicly, at Rome, in January 1487, before an audience that was to include the pope, the cardinals, and learned representatives of every tradition. The 900 *Conclusiones* were the instrument prepared for that demonstration, and their suppression by papal condemnation became the central crisis of Pico's intellectual life. But the project of concordance survived the crisis, continued to shape the *Heptaplus*, and found its most concentrated philosophic expression in *De ente et uno* (1491), which Pico described as a fragment of the larger work on the concordance of Plato and Aristotle that he never completed.

The Latin word Pico uses is *concordia* — in the *Conclusiones*, in the *Oration*, and in the description of the planned *De concordia Platonis et Aristotelis*. The word carries connotations both of agreement in argument and of peace between parties, and this double register is not accidental. For Pico, the demonstration that philosophical traditions agree is at the same time a political act: it offers the conditions for intellectual peace among the scholars and traditions that have been at war. When he announces in the *Oration* that he has read not "one man's books" but the books of "all the philosophers," he is simultaneously announcing a pedagogic fact and a vision of human intellectual solidarity.

## The 900 Conclusions as Enactment

The *Conclusiones nongentae* of November 1486 are the most extraordinary document Pico produced, and they are the practical enactment of the concordance project. Pico divided them into two large categories. The first — the *conclusiones secundum opinionem alienam*, "conclusions according to the opinion of another" — is organized by source tradition and represents Pico's distillation of what he took to be the essential doctrines of each tradition: Albertus Magnus, Thomas Aquinas, Duns Scotus, and other scholastics; Plato, Plotinus, Porphyry, Iamblichus, Proclus, and other Platonics; the Chaldean Oracles, Orpheus, Pythagoras, Zoroaster; the Arab philosophers Al-Farabi, Avicenna, Averroes; Mercury Trismegistus; and twenty-six conclusions from Kabbalistic sources. The second category — the *conclusiones secundum opinionem propriam*, "conclusions according to my own opinion" — represents Pico's own synthesis and his attempts at original doctrine.

The structure of the historical section itself performs concordance: by presenting Averroes and Avicenna alongside Thomas Aquinas and Duns Scotus, by placing the Chaldean Oracles alongside Plotinus and Orpheus alongside Kabbalah, Pico implies that these traditions belong on the same page — that they are answering the same questions and that their answers can be compared. S. A. Farmer, who produced the first full critical edition and translation of the *Conclusiones* with commentary, has described this as the work's fundamental claim: that all the philosophical and theological systems of antiquity form a single corpus of interconnected doctrine in which any given thesis in one tradition implies, echoes, or can be shown to agree with a thesis in another.

Farmer calls this the *occulta concatenatio* — the hidden concatenation — borrowing the phrase from Pico's own preface to the *Conclusiones*, where Pico writes that he has brought together theses "from many sources" but with a "hidden concatenation" (*occulta concatenatio*) that, if he had been permitted to hold the debate, would have become visible through the act of disputation itself. The claim is thus not merely that the traditions agree but that the agreement is structurally hidden and that the debate itself — the Socratic encounter of trained minds across traditions — is the mechanism by which it can be made visible. Concordance, on this reading, is not a static assertion but an epistemic event.

## The Planned Concordance of Plato and Aristotle

The most concrete expression of Pico's programmatic concordance is the work he announced and never completed: *De concordia Platonis et Aristotelis*. What has survived is the short treatise *De ente et uno* (1491), which Pico himself described as a fragment or preliminary demonstration. In *De ente et uno* he takes up one of the most ancient and contentious points of disagreement between the two philosophical traditions: whether, as Plato maintained, the One is beyond being, or whether, as Aristotle argued, being and the one are really the same thing and differ only in their manner of expression. Pico's resolution — which relies on a careful analysis of what Plato means by "beyond" and what Aristotle means by "same" — claims to show that the apparent disagreement dissolves once the terms are correctly defined.

The larger *De concordia* was never written, at least not in any form that has survived. Black notes that Pico managed only this single brief tract of what he described as a projected concordance before his death in 1494 at the age of thirty-one. The posthumous history of the project is itself significant. Pico's nephew and literary executor Gianfrancesco Pico della Mirandola, who edited the *Opera omnia* published in Bologna in 1496, was deeply ambivalent about his uncle's syncretic legacy. Gianfrancesco was himself a committed critic of pagan philosophy and a sceptic about the possibility of harmonizing it with Christian truth. The evidence — both from the silences of the *Opera omnia* and from Gianfrancesco's own later writings — suggests that he may have had more material relating to the concordance project than he published. Some scholars have argued that Gianfrancesco actively suppressed portions of his uncle's work that he regarded as philosophically or theologically dangerous. Whether or not suppression is the right word, the *De concordia Platonis et Aristotelis* exists in the canon only as an announced intention, and that announcement itself — the knowledge that Pico planned such a work and that it remained incomplete — has shaped the reception of everything else he wrote.

## The Oration and the Universal Audience

The *Oration on the Dignity of Man*, written as a prefatory address to the planned Rome disputation, articulates the concordance project in its most rhetorically elevated form. Pico announces that he has studied not one school but all schools: "I have set myself to master not the doctrine of one man but the books of all philosophers; I have reviewed all works, become acquainted with all schools." The programmatic claim is deliberately universal: he has read Plato and Aristotle but also the Arabic commentators; he has studied the scholastics of Paris and the Platonics of Florence; he has read the ancient theologies — Zoroaster, Orpheus, Hermes Trismegistus, Pythagoras — and the Kabbalistic tradition that he believes descends from Moses.

The *Oration*'s announcement of this universal curriculum is not mere display. It is a methodological claim about where truth is to be found: everywhere, distributed across all traditions, inaccessible to any one tradition in isolation. When Pico writes that he has aimed to hold a disputation before a genuinely universal audience — one that would include representatives not only of the Latin theological tradition but of Greek, Arabic, Hebrew, and ancient wisdom traditions — he is proposing that concordance can only be demonstrated, not merely asserted, and that the demonstration requires the public encounter of live representatives of the traditions whose agreement he claims to reveal.

The Rome disputation of January 1487 was designed to be that public demonstration. Pico appears to have hoped that showing the convergence of the traditions before a mixed audience would constitute, in itself, a kind of proof by enactment. The suppression of the debate by the papal commission thus deprived the concordance project not only of its moment of public vindication but of the one formal occasion on which Pico's method could be seen at work.

## The Hermeneutic Key: Omnia in Omnibus

Underlying the concordance project is a specific metaphysical principle that Pico articulates most explicitly in the second proem of the *Heptaplus*: *omnia sunt in omnibus, sed in unoquoque secundum propriam naturam* — "all things are in all things, but in each according to its own nature." This is the Proclean principle of universal participation adapted to Pico's correlative hermeneutic. Applied to the philosophical traditions, it means that every genuine tradition of wisdom contains the whole of truth, but expresses it according to the particular mode, language, and conceptual apparatus specific to that tradition. Aristotle's being and Plato's One are the same reality expressed in different modes. The Kabbalistic sefirot and the Neoplatonic emanations are the same divine procession expressed in different traditions. The Mosaic creation narrative and the Platonic Timaeus are the same cosmological truth expressed in different genres.

This principle is what distinguishes Pico's concordance from simple eclecticism — from the view that one should pick the best ideas from each tradition. For an eclectic, the traditions are a menu; one selects according to taste or need. For Pico, the traditions are different languages for the same reality, and the task is not selection but translation — or more precisely, demonstration of the structural identity of doctrines that appear different on the surface. Pico genuinely believes that once you have correctly understood what Aristotle means by "being" and what Plato means by "the One beyond being," you will see that they are saying the same thing. The disagreement is terminological, not substantive. Concordance is not imposed on the traditions from outside; it is revealed within them by sufficiently rigorous reading.

## Concordance and the Prisca Theologia

The deepest foundation of Pico's concordance project is his commitment to the *prisca theologia*, the ancient theology — the belief that a single divine revelation of philosophical and theological truth was given at the beginning of human history and was then transmitted, variously encoded and partially obscured, through all the major intellectual traditions that followed. This belief, which Pico shared with his older Florentine contemporary Marsilio Ficino, underwrites the concordance project by explaining why the traditions agree: not because they borrowed from one another (though Pico was aware that borrowing occurred) but because they all drink from the same original spring.

Ficino had identified the lineage of the *prisca theologia* running from Hermes Trismegistus through Orpheus and Pythagoras to Plato. Pico accepted this lineage but extended it, characteristically, to include sources Ficino had not systematically engaged: above all the Kabbalah, which Pico treated as the esoteric tradition of biblical revelation — the secret wisdom given to Moses on Sinai alongside the written Torah and transmitted orally through the generations of Jewish sages. In Pico's version of the ancient theology, the Kabbalistic tradition and the Platonic tradition are parallel transmissions of the same primordial wisdom, one through the line of Moses and the other through the line of Hermes. Their structural agreement, which Pico attempted to demonstrate in the Kabbalistic conclusions of 1486 and which informed the *Heptaplus*, is not surprising once one accepts this genealogical premise. It was predicted by the very premise of the *prisca theologia*.

## Scholarly Debates

Modern scholarship has been divided on whether Pico's concordance is a genuine philosophical achievement or a sophisticated rhetorical performance that disguises its own inconsistencies. Farmer, in his monograph *Syncretism in the West* (1998), is the most searching sceptic. Farmer argues that Pico's method of demonstrating concordance consistently involves distorting his sources: he reads Plato through Neoplatonic lenses that Plato himself would not have recognized, and he reads Aristotle through scholastic interpretive traditions that modernize and Christianize the Stagirite in ways that the text does not require. The concordance that Pico reveals is thus in large part a concordance that Pico himself constructed by reading his sources tendentiously. The *occulta concatenatio* is hidden, on Farmer's account, not because the deep structure of reality is difficult to perceive, but because Pico has buried it there himself.

Farmer also raises the question of genuine pluralism. A truly pluralist philosopher would allow the traditions to differ and would examine the differences as philosophically significant. Pico's concordance, by contrast, tends to dissolve differences by reinterpreting them as agreements. This can be experienced as a form of philosophical violence rather than philosophical generosity: it effectively denies each tradition its own integrity by insisting that it really means what Pico says it means.

Deborah Howlett's work on Pico's three-pillar synthesis offers a more sympathetic reading. Howlett emphasizes that for Pico, Aristotelianism, Platonism, and Kabbalah function as genuinely equal pillars of his synthesis — not as resources to be subordinated to a Christian theological framework, but as independent traditions each of which contributes something irreducible. In Howlett's reading, Pico's insistence on the equality of the three pillars is itself a philosophical commitment, not a rhetorical gesture, and it generates genuine philosophical constraints: Pico cannot simply reduce Kabbalah to a Jewish prefiguration of Christian theology, because he treats it as a source of philosophical insight that is independent of its confessional origin. Whether or not Howlett's reading is ultimately more charitable than the evidence warrants, it captures something important about Pico's method: his Kabbalistic conclusions are not decorative additions to a Platonic framework but are meant to be genuinely co-ordinate with it.

Amos Edelheit, in his study of Pico's scholastic commitments, has argued that the scholastic tradition — Thomas Aquinas, Duns Scotus, the Paris disputation culture — is not merely the methodological vehicle for Pico's eclecticism but is one of the substantive philosophical traditions whose agreement with Platonism and Kabbalah he is attempting to demonstrate. This reading corrects a longstanding tendency in scholarship to treat Pico as primarily a Platonist who uses scholastic method instrumentally. For Edelheit, the tripartite concordance — Aristotelianism, Platonism, Kabbalah — is already implicit in the structure of the *Conclusiones* and is not compromised by Pico's evident debt to the scholastic logical culture in which he was trained at Padua and Paris.

The debate between these positions is unlikely to be fully resolved, because it turns on a deep interpretive question that admits of degrees: to what extent is Pico's concordance a genuine philosophical discovery and to what extent is it a prior conviction that directs his reading? This is not a question unique to Pico — every philosopher who attempts a synthesis must confront the charge that the synthesis reveals what the synthesizer already believed — but in Pico's case it is particularly acute because his method requires distancing from any single tradition. The scholar who maintains that Pico distorts his sources to fit them into his schema and the scholar who maintains that Pico achieves genuine cross-traditional insight are both reading the same texts; what separates them is a prior judgment about whether the structural similarities Pico reveals are found or imposed.

What is not in doubt is the ambition and the originality of the project. No previous philosopher in the Latin tradition had attempted so explicitly to bring Kabbalah, Aristotelian scholasticism, and Platonic philosophy into a single framework and to argue that their agreement was philosophically demonstrable rather than merely asserted. Pico's concordance project, whatever its ultimate success or failure as philosophy, transformed the intellectual landscape of the Renaissance and made available, for the century that followed, a model of cross-traditional synthesis that no single tradition could have generated from within itself.
