Menahem ben Benjamin Recanati (c. 1250–c. 1310) was an Italian Kabbalist whose *Peirush al ha-Torah* (Commentary on the Torah) became the single most important theosophical Kabbalistic source available to Giovanni Pico della Mirandola. Born and active in central Italy, Recanati occupied a distinctive position in the history of medieval Jewish thought: he was among the first Italian scholars to transmit and systematize the doctrines of the Spanish Kabbalah, and in particular the theosophical tradition centered on the ten sefirot (*sefirot*, the divine emanations or attributes) and their relationship to the biblical text. His work synthesized the teachings of Nahmanides, the Gerona Kabbalists, and the *Zohar*, presenting them in the accessible form of a running biblical commentary. A second major work, *Ta'amei ha-Mitzvot* (Reasons for the Commandments), extended this framework to halakhic practice, grounding each commandment in its sefirot correspondence. For Pico, working through Latin translations provided by his Sicilian collaborator Flavius Mithridates in the summer and autumn of 1486, the lost translation of Recanati's *Commentary on the Pentateuch* — now identified with the manuscript described by Jacques Gaffarel as present in Pico's library — was the fountainhead of the thick, doctrinally elaborate theosophical Kabbalah that animates the first set of the Kabbalistic theses in the *900 Conclusions*.

The documentary basis for Recanati's primacy among Pico's sources was established definitively by Chaim Wirszubski in his posthumously published *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989). Wirszubski undertook the painstaking work of deriving, thesis by thesis, the demonstrable direct sources of the forty-seven *Conclusiones Cabalisticae secundum secretam doctrinam sapientum Hebraeorum Cabalistarum*, the first set of Kabbalistic theses that Pico presented as representative of Jewish esoteric tradition rather than as his own opinions. The evidence is decisive: with more than half of these theses deriving directly from Recanati's Commentary, Recanati occupies what Wirszubski calls "an unrivaled predominance" among the subsidiary sources (Wirszubski 1989, 59). The remaining theses draw on a cluster of texts including Joseph Gicatilla's *Portae Iustitiae* (Gates of Justice), the *Book Bahir*, two commentaries on the *Sefer Yetzirah* (Book of Creation), and Abraham Axelrad's *Corona Nominis Boni* — but none of these sources approaches Recanati's weight or frequency. Wirszubski's argument proceeds through internal derivation, matching the compressed Latin of each thesis against the translated passages of the Hebrew originals, and the convergence is sufficiently precise to establish Recanati's text, even in translation, as what Pico was actually reading.

To understand why Recanati mattered so much, one must grasp the distinction between theosophical and ecstatic Kabbalah — the two major currents available to Pico through Mithridates' translations. Theosophical Kabbalah, represented by Recanati and by the Zoharic tradition he transmits, concerns itself with the inner structure of the divine: how the *Ein Sof* (the infinite, boundless divine ground) manifests through a chain of ten sefirot, how these sefirot interact in patterns of *hishtalshelut* (literally "chain-linking," the cascade of emanation), and how each narrative event in the Torah is an allegory of transactions within this divine structure. The biblical text, on this view, is not primarily a record of human or historical events but a symbolic grammar encoding the life of the divine attributes. Every proper name, every ritual, every narrative episode has a sefirot referent: the "gathering of the waters" in Genesis 1:9 signifies the ninth sefirah, Yesod (Foundation), which draws all being toward the tenth sefirah, Malkut (Kingdom) or Shekinah; the sexual love of man and woman allegorizes the union of Tiferet (the sixth sefirah, Beauty or Glory) with the Shekinah; Abraham's journeys southward encode his adherence to the sefirah of Hesed (Charity), which is the right side and the south. These equations are not fanciful associations but systematic readings that Recanati inherits and elaborates from Nahmanides and the Zohar.

Pico absorbed this framework with remarkable precision. Wirszubski demonstrates, for instance, that Conclusion IV — "The sin of Adam was the truncation of the kingdom from the other plants" — derives directly from Recanati's Commentary on the Pentateuch (fol. 19vb–20ra), where Recanati quotes the Zohar's interpretation of Adam's transgression as the "severance of the plants," meaning the separation of the lower sefirot from their proper hierarchy. Conclusion VIII — "Souls descend from the third light to the fourth day and thence to the fifth" — traces to Recanati's comment (fol. 9vb) on the descent of souls from the third sefirah (Binah, Intelligence) through the intermediate sefirot to the tenth (Malkut), from which they flow into the sublunar world. Conclusion XVIII — "He who will unite with Tiferet at midnight, all his offspring will be prosperous" — derives from Recanati (fol. 213rb–va), who interprets the proper hour for marital union as midnight because at that hour the Holy Blessed One (identified with Tiferet) unites with the Shekinah. The pattern is consistent throughout: Pico's most specific theosophical theses about the sefirot, their symbolism, and their relationship to biblical narrative almost invariably derive from Recanati.

Ecstatic Kabbalah, by contrast, is what Abraham Abulafia represents in Pico's source library. Abulafia's program centers on techniques for achieving mystical union — the combination and permutation of divine names (*ars combinandi*), the achievement of intellectual rapture, and ultimately the *mors osculi* (death of the kiss), the soul's final adherence to the Active Intellect. Pico uses both traditions, but assigns them different functions. For the doctrinal architecture of the sefirot, for the account of how divine names correspond to divine attributes, and for the exegetical demonstration that specific biblical passages encode specific sefirot — this is the domain of Recanati and theosophical Kabbalah. For the ascending practical method, the emphasis on combination of letters as a vehicle for contemplative ascent, and the mystical goal of union with God — this is Abulafia's contribution. The *Conclusions* hold both traditions simultaneously, which is one reason why their Kabbalistic section is so difficult to interpret without identifying the distinct source streams.

S.A. Farmer's edition and commentary on the *900 Conclusions* (1998) confirms and extends Wirszubski's source analysis. Farmer emphasizes that the Recanati translations gave Pico access not merely to individual doctrines but to the entire structural logic of theosophical Kabbalah: the sefirot as a complete symbolic system in which every term connects to every other, and through which the Torah as a whole can be read as a single extended allegory of the divine life. The theses derived from Recanati are thus not isolated propositions but nodes in a network whose coherence depends on the Recanati framework. When Pico claims in the *Oration* that Kabbalah "confirms" Christianity — that the ancient Hebrew wisdom secretly foretells and supports Christian doctrine — it is largely the Recanati translations that supply the specific sefirot equations through which this argument is made. Farmer also underscores that the compressed telegraphic form of the theses conceals their derivativeness: what looks like a bold original proposition is frequently a direct transposition of a passage from Recanati into scholastic Latin.

The transmission of Recanati's text to Pico is complicated, and this complication has direct consequences for evaluating Pico's Kabbalah. Mithridates' translation of the *Commentary on the Pentateuch* is now lost: the manuscript described by Gaffarel as the "first manuscript" of Pico's Kabbalistic library, and identified by Gaffarel as containing Recanati's Commentary with many personal notes by the translator, has not been recovered since the seventeenth century. What Wirszubski was able to do was to compare the Hebrew original of Recanati (using the printed Venice 1545 edition as a control) against the evidence of the theses themselves, inferring the content and character of the lost translation. This reconstruction yields a finding of fundamental importance: the translations of Mithridates are shot through with systematic interpolations, and the Recanati translation was no exception.

Wirszubski's chapter on "Christianizing Interpretations" (Chapter 9 of *Encounter*) documents a pattern of deliberate theological tampering evident across the extant manuscripts. Mithridates inserts the word *trinitas* into contexts where the Hebrew original discusses the three upper sefirot (Crown, Wisdom, Intelligence) but says nothing about the Trinity. In the translation of the *Commentary on the Prayers* (*De Secretis Orationum*, Cod. Vat. Ebr. 190, fol. 319v), a clause explaining that the word *Amen* indicates "the Trinity of the three superior sefirot in one unity" has no equivalent in the Hebrew original. In the *Liber de Radicibus* (Cod. Vat. Ebr. 190, fol. 226r), the word *az* (תאז, "then") is glossed as indicating "the Trinity of the three superior sefirot — Crown, Wisdom, and Intelligence — united in the unity of essence," which again is Mithridates' invention. The practical consequence is direct: Pico's twentieth Kabbalistic thesis *secundum opinionem propriam* — "If Kabbalists consider their own interpretation of the word *az*, meaning 'then,' they will be greatly illuminated regarding the mystery of the Trinity" — derives from this interpolated passage. In the lost Recanati translation, similar Christianizing insertions must be assumed to have been at work. The theosophical Kabbalah that Pico received through Mithridates was thus a Christianized theosophical Kabbalah, one already partially shaped toward the apologetic conclusions Pico would draw from it.

The surviving manuscripts also record Mithridates' occasional intrusions in the margins addressing Pico directly — sometimes playful, sometimes informative, sometimes impatient. At one point (Cod. Chigi, fol. 75r) Mithridates writes: "These are great things, but it is impossible that you should understand them except in the proper Hebrew letters and words." The comment acknowledges a deep structural problem: gematria, letter symbolism, and the isopsephic equations at the heart of theosophical Kabbalah are essentially untranslatable, because their force depends on the numerical and formal properties of specific Hebrew characters. The Latin translation can convey the doctrine — that the gathering of the waters signifies Yesod, that circumcision on the eighth day encodes a specific sefirot relationship — but it cannot easily convey the argumentative mechanism that gives the doctrine its Kabbalistic authority. Pico read Recanati, in other words, in a medium that preserved the conclusions while partially obscuring the methods.

This does not mean the transmission was without value. Wirszubski's analysis of the forty-three Kabbalistically impeccable theses among the forty-seven of the first set shows that Mithridates' translations, despite their interpolations, faithfully conveyed the major doctrines of Recanati's Commentary with remarkable accuracy. The high proportion of impeccable theses is itself an index of the translations' quality. But the four theses that are Kabbalistically problematic — including Conclusion VIII with its confusion of "five lights" and "five days" due to a variant reading in the Hebrew manuscript Mithridates used — show how textual accidents in the lost Hebrew original or in the process of translation could introduce errors that Pico, unable to check the Hebrew directly, had no means of detecting.

For interpreting Pico's later works, the Recanati translations have specific relevance to the *Heptaplus* (1489), the sevenfold commentary on the six days of creation. Crofton Black's study of the *Heptaplus* (2006) shows how the three-world cosmological framework of that work — elemental, celestial, and angelic — derives in significant measure from a Kabbalistic three-world structure that itself traces back to the sefirot doctrine Pico absorbed from Recanati and related sources. Wirszubski had already shown that this three-world doctrine, which Pico presents as a harmony of Platonic, Aristotelian, and Kabbalistic teaching, "is of Kabbalistic origin" (Wirszubski 1989, xi). The *Heptaplus* thus represents a hermeneutical application of the theosophical framework absorbed from Recanati: the seven expositions of the Genesis text proceed by mapping the structure of the text onto the structure of the three worlds, in a procedure that replicates, at the level of Christian biblical commentary, the Kabbalistic practice of reading the Torah as a coded map of divine ontology.

The reception-historical significance of Pico's use of Recanati lies in what it inaugurated. Pico was, as Wirszubski and Kristeller both emphasize, the first Christian scholar to attain a detailed and accurate understanding of genuine Kabbalistic texts. Prior to Pico, Christian awareness of Kabbalah was fragmentary and mediated through summaries or isolated fragments. Recanati's Commentary, precisely because it is systematic and exhaustive in its application of sefirot doctrine to the biblical text, provided Pico with something qualitatively different: a comprehensive theosophical grammar that could generate new readings indefinitely. Johannes Reuchlin, Egidio da Viterbo, and the sixteenth-century Christian Kabbalistic tradition built on the foundation Pico had laid with the Recanati translations; but they were working, often, with translations or paraphrases themselves derived from Mithridates' corpus, making the influence of the lost Recanati translation — Christianized, interpolated, partially garbled — a formative episode in the entire subsequent history of Christian Kabbalah.
