# Scholar Profile: Ovanes Akopyan

Ovanes Akopyan is a historian of Renaissance intellectual culture whose work centers on the intersection of natural philosophy, astrology, and the history of science in late fifteenth- and early sixteenth-century Italy. Trained at the University of Warwick under Maude Vanhaelen and Paul Botley, he subsequently held positions at the University of Innsbruck and the Ludwig Boltzmann Institute for Neo-Latin Studies before moving to Oxford, where he is affiliated with Wolfson College and the Bodleian Library. His major monograph, *Debating the Stars in the Italian Renaissance: Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem and Its Reception* (Brill, 2021), published in the prestigious Brill's Studies in Intellectual History series, represents the first comprehensive scholarly study of the *Disputationes* as both a philosophical text in its own right and as a cultural force with a traceable afterlife.

The *Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem* is Pico's most extended single work: twelve books of systematic argument against judicial or divinatory astrology, composed in the final year or two of his life (1493–94) and left unfinished at his death in 1494. It was published posthumously in 1496 by his nephew Gianfrancesco Pico della Mirandola, who also saw several of Pico's other incomplete projects into print. The work takes as its principal target the practice of using celestial configurations to predict individual human destinies — nativities, elections, horary judgments, historical conjunctions — and mounts against it a sustained critique on grounds of natural philosophy, evidential reliability, historical authority, and moral theology. It has long been recognized as a significant document in the history of astrology's decline as a legitimate learned discipline, but until Akopyan's study it had never been read as an internally coherent and philosophically serious piece of writing. It tended instead to appear in scholarship as a useful source for historians of science who needed evidence of anti-astrological polemic, or as a symptom of Pico's Savonarolan turn in his final years, or as a monument to the dangers of reading an unfinished and posthumously edited text as though its author had tidied every argument.

Akopyan's central argument is that this reputational situation has persistently misled modern readers. The *Disputationes* has been treated as peripheral — as a text that matters less than the *Oration*, the *900 Conclusions*, the *Heptaplus*, and *On Being and Unity* — partly because its posthumous character raises editorial anxieties and partly because its polemical purpose makes it look more like a pamphlet than a treatise. Akopyan disputes both reasons. The *Disputationes*, he argues, is Pico's most methodologically consistent work. When it is read carefully and compared with Pico's earlier writings, the method on display turns out to be exactly the method Pico develops in *De ente et uno*: the attempt to purify a corrupted tradition by distinguishing what the founding sources actually said from the distortions introduced by their successors. Just as Pico argues in *De ente et uno* that medieval philosophers had misread Aristotle and the Neoplatonists had misread Plato, so in the *Disputationes* he argues that the astrological tradition had systematically misrepresented its own foundational texts and overstated the theoretical grounds on which its predictions rested. Far from being a rupture in Pico's intellectual development — a late capitulation to Savonarola's influence or a fideist retreat from his earlier synthetic ambitions — the *Disputationes* is the continuation and culmination of the same philosophical impulse Akopyan traces across the entire career.

This reframing has several important consequences for how Pico's later thought is read. One of the persistent puzzles in the scholarship is the apparent contradiction between Pico's early writings, in which astrology appears in the context of natural philosophy, Kabbalistic-celestial correspondence, and poetic explanation, and the *Disputationes*, in which he seems to condemn the whole enterprise. Scholars have explained this either as an evolution or a conversion, usually by invoking Savonarola or Pico's increasing religiosity. Akopyan addresses the contradiction differently. By tracing Pico's astrological language from the *Commento* on Benivieni through the 900 Conclusions, the *Heptaplus*, and the late polemic, he argues that the development is not a reversal but a gradual tightening of focus. What Pico criticizes in the *Disputationes* is judicial, divinatory astrology specifically: the claim that planetary configurations at the moment of birth or decision determine particular human outcomes. That critique does not commit him to rejecting every form of celestial influence, every account of how the heavens might shape sublunary nature, or the kind of celestial natural philosophy visible in his earlier works. Akopyan insists on this distinction because conflating these things produces the apparent paradox; keeping them separate reveals a philosopher who moved, with increasing clarity and specificity, toward the elimination of a narrower target.

A second major consequence follows from Akopyan's engagement with the natural-philosophical arguments in the *Disputationes*. He argues that Pico, probably under the influence of Iamblichus's *De mysteriis Aegyptiorum* and Marsilio Ficino's commentary on that work, proposes in the *Disputationes* a compromise between Platonic and Aristotelian theories of celestial light — and that this compromise is philosophically significant rather than merely polemical. Pico does not simply assert that the stars do not influence human affairs; he examines the models under which celestial influence could be coherently theorized and shows that no such model can sustain the specific causal claims divinatory astrology requires. This is engagement with the medieval and Renaissance tradition of mathematical astronomy and philosophical astrology from the inside — the kind of engagement Akopyan's book is the first to map in full.

The second major contribution of *Debating the Stars* is its reception history. Akopyan does not stop with Pico's text but traces the immediate aftermath of the *Disputationes* across several decades and into several distinct intellectual contexts. Girolamo Savonarola's *Contro gli astrologi* draws on Pico's anti-astrological arguments, but Akopyan shows that Savonarola's use is an ideological appropriation rather than a faithful application: the prophetic context and the political function of Savonarola's anti-astrology differ fundamentally from Pico's philosophical one. Gianfrancesco Pico, the nephew who edited the *Disputationes* and who stood between Savonarola and his uncle's legacy, complicates the picture further; his own works on cognition, prophecy, and astrology rework the *Disputationes* material within a Savonarolan frame that Giovanni Pico himself might not have recognized. Beyond Italy, Akopyan traces the remarkable case of Maximus the Greek, an Orthodox monk who had become a Dominican novice at San Marco in Florence, worked for Gianfrancesco, absorbed both Savonarola's and Giovanni Pico's arguments, and then carried them to Muscovy, where he used them to attack astrology as a "Latin vice" in his epistolary writings. On the other side, Akopyan reads the major defenses of astrology composed in explicit response to the *Disputationes*: Lucio Bellanti's direct refutation, Giovanni Pontano's courtly and literary rebuttal, and Francesco Zorzi's incorporation of Pico-derived arguments into a broader Franciscan Neoplatonism that sought to rehabilitate the positive value of celestial correspondence while accepting some of Pico's critique.

What emerges from this reception history is not the story of a book that ended astrology or liberated Renaissance thought from superstition. It is the story of a text that functioned as a versatile intellectual resource: usable by prophets, by skeptics, by reformers who wanted better astronomy, and by apologists who wanted to distinguish licit from illicit celestial science. The *Disputationes* had enough philosophical substance to be taken seriously by people who disagreed with its conclusions, and that seriousness — recognized by Akopyan's study and largely neglected before it — is the ground for rereading Pico's later thought as a coherent philosophical project rather than as a set of symptoms.

For PicoDB, Akopyan functions as the indispensable guide to the *Disputationes*. He establishes the developmental chronology of Pico's astrological thinking, provides the natural-philosophical analysis needed to read the late polemic with precision, and opens the reception archive that shows how the text was used, modified, and contested after Pico's death. He also writes in full awareness of the editorial problems the text presents: the posthumous edition, Gianfrancesco's editorial agenda, and the proximity of Savonarolan politics to the manuscript's fate are all part of his account. On the textual transmission problem he is more measured than Stephen Farmer, who presses harder on the possibility of posthumous doctoring, but Akopyan shares Farmer's methodological caution: the *Disputationes* cannot be read as transparent evidence of Pico's final views without acknowledging the conditions under which it reached print. That caution combined with Akopyan's positive demonstration of the text's philosophical coherence makes his work the necessary starting point for any serious engagement with the late Pico.
