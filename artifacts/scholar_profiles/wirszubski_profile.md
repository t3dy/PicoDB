# Scholar Profile: Chaim Wirszubski

Chaim Wirszubski (1915–1977) produced what remains the indispensable foundation for all serious study of Pico della Mirandola's relationship to Jewish mysticism. Born in Vilna, Poland, he emigrated to Palestine in 1933 and completed his education at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, where he studied classical languages alongside Jewish philosophy and Kabbalah under Julius Guttmann and, most crucially, Gershom Scholem. Scholem's influence on Wirszubski was, as the foreword to his posthumous volume records, "particularly profound and decisive." It oriented the younger scholar toward the history of Jewish mystical traditions as a legitimate subject of rigorous philological inquiry — an orientation that would define the most important work of his career. Wirszubski died in 1977 before he could see his Pico study through to publication; the manuscript was brought to completion by Moshe Idel, with additional annotations and footnotes, and appeared in 1989 under the auspices of both the Israel Academy of Sciences and Humanities and Harvard University Press, with an introduction by Paul Oskar Kristeller of Columbia University.

Wirszubski's scholarly life was unusual in its breadth. He began as a classicist: his first book, *Libertas as a Political Idea at Rome during the Late Republic and Early Principate* (Cambridge, 1950), is a philologically meticulous study of a Roman political concept, and his 1954 article on Cicero's "cum dignitate otium" displays the same textual precision. He also translated Spinoza's *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* into Hebrew, an undertaking that required him to hold together high philological exactitude with idiomatic clarity. These exercises in classical and early modern philology were not diversions from his Pico work; they were its preparation. When Wirszubski turned to the Latin manuscripts that Flavius Mithridates produced for Pico in 1486, he brought to bear the same combination of textual close-reading, multilingual command, and historical contextual awareness that had shaped his classical scholarship. Paul Oskar Kristeller, in his introduction to the posthumous volume, described him as "uniquely qualified" for the task: an excellent philologist and intellectual historian who was "at home in both Hebrew and Western scholarship and had the rare ability to read, interpret, and compare difficult texts in several languages."

The central discovery of *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* concerns the figure of Flavius Mithridates, a Sicilian Jewish convert to Christianity who served as Pico's translator of Kabbalistic texts in the summer and autumn of 1486. Before Wirszubski's work, the importance of Mithridates had been partially recognized, but the full extent of his mediation — and the nature of his interventions in the texts he translated — had never been systematically demonstrated. Wirszubski established, first, that the four surviving manuscripts in the Vatican Library (Codices Vaticani Ebraici 189–191 and Codex Chigi A.VI.190) are autographs written by Mithridates himself in the act of translating from the Hebrew originals into Latin. He proved this not by palaeographic comparison alone but by a method of internal textual criticism: he identified corrections in the manuscripts that could only have been made by someone who was reading and misreading the Hebrew originals at the moment of writing — errors occasioned by the visual similarity of Hebrew letters, for instance, that a copyist could not have generated independently. The manuscripts thus date from the period May to November 1486, when Mithridates was working at a furious pace, occasionally noting in parentheses that he was producing two quires per day.

The manuscripts constitute, Wirszubski showed, a small Kabbalistic library in Latin translation: approximately 3,500 surviving pages in folio, representing some forty books of varying authorship and Kabbalistic orientation, with an estimated further 2,000 or more pages lost. Among the texts translated were works by Menahem of Recanati (whose *Commentary on the Pentateuch* emerged as the single most important source for Pico's first set of Kabbalistic theses), Joseph Gicatilla's *Portae Iustitiae*, the ancient Kabbalistic text known as the *Bahir*, the *Sefer Yesirah* with commentaries, Rabbi Azriel's *Quaestiones super Decem Numerationibus*, and multiple works by Abraham Abulafia. Wirszubski's patient reconstruction of which specific manuscripts Pico actually used for his theses — the *Conclusiones Cabalisticae* of 1486 — is accomplished by what he calls "derivation of texts": comparing each thesis, phrase by phrase, against the Latin translations in the manuscripts to establish the likeliest direct source. This method is the heart of Part One of the book and represents one of the most rigorous exercises in source criticism in the scholarship of Renaissance thought.

The second major contribution of the work concerns Mithridates himself as a translator and interpolator. Wirszubski demonstrated that Mithridates did not simply render the Hebrew texts faithfully into Latin. He systematically inserted Christianizing glosses, interpolations, and marginal notes into the translations. In some instances these were minor theological nudges — the word *trinitas* introduced into a passage about the three upper sefirot where the Hebrew has no such term, or the symbol *Ze* (the ninth sefirah, Yesod) rendered in ways that invited Christological reading. In other cases the interpolations were more elaborate: Mithridates transformed Abulafia's mystical commentary on Maimonides' *Guide of the Perplexed* so that Maimonides himself appeared to be speaking as a Kabbalist in philosophical guise, by systematically replacing phrases like "the teacher said" (referring to the *Guide*) with first-person direct speech, making it seem as though Maimonides was personally disclosing secrets to the reader. These textual transformations were not always Mithridates' own invention — Wirszubski was careful to note that Christianizing interpolation in Jewish texts had predecessors, and the authorship of each interpolation requires independent establishment — but the systematic pattern across the manuscripts is unmistakable. The practical consequence for Pico was that he was reading Jewish Kabbalah through a series of Christian lenses installed by his translator, often without knowing it.

The third major contribution of *Encounter* is its account of what Kabbalah meant to Pico himself. Wirszubski traces the doctrines Pico derived from Abulafia (notably the distinction between the Kabbalah of the sefirot and the speculative Kabbalah of divine names), from Recanati (the dominant source of the first set of Kabbalistic theses, with its symbolical exegesis of Scripture), and from the minor sources. He analyzes the second set of Kabbalistic theses — the *Conclusiones secundum opinionem propriam*, Pico's own Kabbalistic positions — showing how Pico selected, recombined, and Christianized the materials he had read, linking Kabbalistic doctrines to the Chaldean Oracles, to Orphic hymns, to Proclus, to Cusanus, and to Ramon Lull in syntheses that had no precedent in the Hebrew sources themselves. Wirszubski's central conclusion on this point — that it was Pico, not his Jewish sources and not his Christian predecessors, who laid the foundations of Renaissance Christian Kabbalah — became the standard view that subsequent scholars have elaborated, qualified, and debated.

Wirszubski's methodological disposition is as important as his specific findings. He consistently refuses to allow uncertainty to collapse into assertive generality. Where sources cannot be positively identified, he says so. Where evidence permits multiple interpretations, he presents them. His judgment that the theses secundum opinionem propriam are "for the most part Pico's own speculations which cannot be traced to identifiable sources" is a scholarly caution that later scholars have sometimes too quickly set aside. The same restraint governs his treatment of Mithridates' interpolations: he does not claim more certainty about authorship than the evidence warrants.

Every subsequent scholar working on Pico and Kabbalah — Moshe Idel, Giulio Busi, Brian Copenhaver, Stephen Farmer, Stéphane Toussaint — stands in explicit debt to Wirszubski's reconstruction. The manuscripts he identified and described remain the primary documentary basis for understanding Pico's Kabbalistic education. The method he established — tracing individual theses back to specific passages in the Mithridates translations, which in turn must be compared to their Hebrew originals — is the only method that yields firm conclusions. Wirszubski made Christian Kabbalah, and Pico's version of it in particular, a precise philological object rather than a diffuse cultural atmosphere, and he did so without diminishing the genuine strangeness of the encounter he was documenting.
