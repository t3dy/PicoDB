# Scholar Profile: Giulio Busi

Giulio Busi holds the chair of Jewish Studies (Ebraistica) at the Freie Universität Berlin and is one of the most distinguished authorities on medieval and Renaissance Judaism in the world. His research spans Jewish mysticism from its medieval Sephardic roots through its Renaissance transformations, with major works including *Simboli del pensiero ebraico*, *Qabbalah visiva*, and an edition of the *Zohar*, all published by Einaudi in the prestigious Millenni series. As director of the Kabbalistic Studies project at the Freie Universität's Institute for Jewish Studies—a project that has produced, in collaboration with the Istituto Nazionale di Studi sul Rinascimento in Florence and the publisher Nino Aragno, the ongoing critical edition of Flavius Mithridates' Latin translations of Hebrew Kabbalistic texts for Pico della Mirandola—Busi stands at the center of a major international effort to reconstruct the precise documentary basis of Pico's encounter with Jewish mysticism. His 2014 volume *Giovanni Pico della Mirandola: Mito, magia, Qabbalah*, co-authored with Raphael Ebgi and published by Einaudi, represents the most comprehensive Italian-language account of Pico's Kabbalistic and magical learning yet written, and it marks a significant advance upon the foundation laid by Chaim Wirszubski's *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (Harvard/Cambridge, 1989).

To understand Busi's contribution, it is necessary first to understand the Wirszubski baseline he is building upon and complicating. Wirszubski, the late Israeli scholar of Renaissance Kabbalah who died in 1987 before completing his work, demonstrated through painstaking manuscript analysis that Pico's knowledge of Kabbalah was not drawn from independent reading of Hebrew sources but was almost entirely mediated through the Latin translations and glosses of Flavius Mithridates—a Sicilian Jewish convert whose full name was Semu'el ben Nissim, baptized as Guglielmo Raimondo Moncada. Wirszubski showed that Mithridates frequently interpolated Christianizing readings into his translations, inserting Trinitarian glosses and Christological hermeneutics that had no basis in the Hebrew originals, and that Pico's celebrated *Conclusiones* on Kabbalah sometimes reflected these distortions rather than authentic Jewish tradition. This was a founding discovery for modern Pico scholarship: it meant that the relationship between Pico's Christianized Kabbalah and authentic Jewish Kabbalistic tradition was far more complicated than earlier scholars had assumed, and that any serious study of Pico's Kabbalistic learning required working through Mithridates' translations against the Hebrew originals.

Busi's project picks up precisely where Wirszubski left off, and extends it in several directions that Wirszubski was not in a position to pursue. The Mithridates editions produced under Busi's direction—including *The Great Parchment* (2004), *The Book of Bahir* (2005), editions of Menahem Recanati's commentary on the daily prayers, Yosef Gikatilla's *Book of Punctuation*, and *The Gate of Heaven* (2012)—provide the first published texts of the actual Latin translations Mithridates made for Pico, accompanied by the Hebrew originals and English translations. This editorial work makes it possible, for the first time, to assess the relationship between what Mithridates gave Pico and what authentic Kabbalistic tradition contains, and to distinguish, with much greater precision than Wirszubski could, between the elements that Pico received accurately, the elements that were deliberately distorted, and the creative syntheses Pico himself made from the material he received.

In the 2014 Einaudi volume, Busi and Ebgi present a new critical Italian edition and translation of Pico's major Kabbalistic and magical texts—the *Oration on the Dignity of Man*, the relevant sections of the *Conclusiones nongentae*, the *Commento sopra una canzone d'amore*, the *Heptaplus*, and *On Being and the One*—organized not as a straightforward critical edition but as what the authors call a "dizionario," a lexicon of key terms that structures Pico's synthetic vision: myth, magic, and Kabbalah as the three highest rungs of the ladder of wisdom. Each entry in the lexicon—from *Bacio* (Kiss) to *Vino* (Wine), through *Bacco*, the Muses, and the Venuses—selects and presents textual passages from Pico's writings on that theme, accompanied by Busi's commentary that illuminates the interpretive background in Jewish mysticism, Neoplatonic philosophy, and Renaissance magic. The lexical organization is itself an interpretive statement: it proposes that Pico's thought is not best understood through the traditional genre categories of philosophical treatise, biblical commentary, or disputed conclusions, but rather through the symbolic vocabulary that runs across all his writings and that constitutes his distinctive hermeneutic method.

Busi's central interpretive thesis is that Pico's Kabbalah is not primarily theoretical or doctrinal—not, that is, a learned appropriation of sefirot speculation for the purpose of Christian apologetics—but is fundamentally operational and practical. The 900 *Conclusiones* are, in Busi's reading, a raw debate machine (*macchina da disputa*) in which scholastic syllogistic, ancient symbolic logic, Platonic metaphysics, magical formulas, astrological observation, and Kabbalistic invocation coexist in an unstable but energetic mixture. What unifies the whole is not a single philosophical system but a principle of symbolic inclusion: the belief that all things contain all other things when the correct proportions and correspondences are known, that the scale of correspondences (*scala delle corrispondenze*) makes it possible to ascend from any starting point to the same ultimate truth. Pico's own formulation, cited by Busi from thesis 3.52 of the *Conclusiones*: "The science of God, of man, and of prime matter is the same, and whoever has knowledge of one will have it of the others, provided the proportion from extreme to extreme, from middle to extremes, and from extremes to middle is maintained." This is the principle that allows Kabbalah, Platonic emanation theory, Christian theology, and natural magic to inhabit the same intellectual space in Pico's system—not because they are identical, but because the scale of symbolic correspondence allows them to be mapped onto each other.

Busi's treatment of Flavius Mithridates goes considerably beyond Wirszubski's in its attention to Mithridates as a personality and cultural mediator. Where Wirszubski focused primarily on the textual distortions in Mithridates' translations, Busi reconstructs, through a combination of archival research and philological analysis, the complex figure of the man himself: a Sicilian Jew converted to Christianity, trained in theology and medicine at the University of Naples, ordained as a priest, subsequently entangled in a criminal case that forced him to leave Rome, and eventually arriving in Florence in 1485 to encounter Pico. Busi stresses that while Mithridates was undoubtedly capable of inserting false or tendentiously Christianized readings into his translations, in the majority of cases—which amount, across the Mithridates corpus, to thousands of pages—his translations are accurate and technically sophisticated. The Kabbalistic library Mithridates created in Latin for Pico remains, Busi notes, the largest corpus of Kabbalistic material available in any Western language even today, and its scholarly importance cannot be reduced to the distortions that Wirszubski rightly identified. Mithridates was, for Busi, a "contradictory genius" (*genio contraddittorio*): translator, teacher of Hebrew, Aramaic, and Arabic; occasionally a forger and manipulator of texts; but also a genuine polymath of remarkable linguistic range whose mediation was, for all its impurities, the indispensable channel through which Pico accessed the tradition.

Busi makes an important and original argument about the relationship between Pico's Kabbalah and his magic. While earlier scholarship—including Wirszubski's—had treated Pico's Kabbalistic theses and his natural magic theses as partially overlapping but analytically distinct domains, Busi argues that in Pico's system the two are inseparable. The *Conclusiones* invert the traditional Kabbalistic hierarchy: for Jewish Kabbalists, theosophical speculation on the sefirot normally takes precedence over, and provides the theoretical foundation for, the more practical operations of divine-name mysticism. Pico reverses this. For him, it is the operative Kabbalah of divine names—the manipulation of the Hebrew letters and names through which God created the world—that stands at the apex of the hierarchy of wisdom, above both natural magic and theoretical sefirot speculation. Kabbalah in Pico's system is *opus*, praxis, a technique of transformation that acts on reality by acting on its linguistic substrate. This emphasis on the operative dimension of Kabbalistic learning distinguishes Pico's appropriation of Jewish mysticism from the more purely theoretical use that later Christian Kabbalists, following the tradition Pico inaugurated, would make of it.

In terms of the broader landscape of Kabbalah scholarship, Busi occupies a position alongside Moshe Idel—the Hebrew University scholar whose work on Kabbalah across multiple books, including *Kabbalah: New Perspectives* (1988) and the monumental studies of Abraham Abulafia and prophetic Kabbalah, transformed the field by insisting on the ecstatic and operative dimensions of Jewish mysticism against earlier scholarship's tendency to emphasize its theosophical and cosmological dimensions—as one of the definitive authorities on the Renaissance transmission of Kabbalistic tradition. Busi and Idel share a commitment to recovering Kabbalah in its historical specificity, against the tendency of comparative religious studies to dissolve its specific character into a generic "mysticism." Where Idel has focused primarily on the Hebrew sources and their internal development, Busi has focused on the moment of transmission: the extraordinary encounter between Jewish esotericism and Renaissance humanism that took place in Pico's Florentine circle and that gave birth, through Pico's creative synthesis, to the Christian Kabbalah tradition.

The Einaudi volume also includes what Busi calls "Pico visivo"—commentary on the relationship between Pico's ideas and the major masterworks of Quattrocento art, with particular attention to paintings and frescoes that illuminate the visual imagination of the world Pico inhabited. This dimension of the book, though it cannot substitute for art-historical analysis, reflects Busi's conviction that Pico cannot be understood as a purely textual phenomenon: he was a figure of his culture in the fullest sense, and the symbolic vocabulary of his thought was continuous with the symbolic visual culture of late Quattrocento Florence.

For PicoDB, Busi's work is the primary guide to several crucial areas. His analysis of the sefirot theses in the *Conclusiones*—Pico's Kabbalistic *Conclusiones secundum opinionem propriam*—supplies the framework needed to read those theses in relation to authentic Kabbalistic sources, to understand what Mithridates' translations gave Pico and what Pico made of them, and to assess the originality of Pico's Christianized synthesis. His treatment of the relationship between Kabbalah and magic in Pico's system clarifies why Pico placed Kabbalistic operation above natural magic in the hierarchy of sciences, and what that placement meant for the structure of his concordist project. And his contextualization of Pico within the broader landscape of Mithridates scholarship makes the Mithridates translations themselves legible as primary sources for Pico studies—which they are, in ways that the history of scholarship has not always recognized.
