# Scholar Profile: Crofton Black

Crofton Black is a historian of ideas associated with the Warburg Institute, University of London, where he completed the doctoral research that would become his principal monograph. His academic formation placed him at the intersection of classical scholarship, the history of medieval philosophy, and Renaissance intellectual history, under the supervision of Jill Kraye, with significant assistance from Charles Burnett and contributions from Brian Copenhaver and Peter Adamson. His examiners were Stephen Clucas and Anthony Grafton — a pairing that signals the book's double ambition: to satisfy the rigorous philological standards of Warburg scholarship while also engaging the broader history of ideas. The result, *Pico's Heptaplus and Biblical Hermeneutics*, published by Brill in 2006 as volume 116 of the *Studies in Medieval and Reformation Traditions* series, is the most sustained scholarly account in English of Pico della Mirandola's 1489 commentary on the opening chapter of Genesis, and it stands as an indispensable guide to what Black himself calls, in his opening sentence, a study in "the history of allegory."

The *Heptaplus* is a peculiar and structurally audacious work. Pico takes the six days of creation in Genesis 1 as his text and subjects it to seven successive expositions, each organized around a different cosmic register: the elemental world, the celestial world, the angelic and invisible world, the human world, a fifth exposition treating all worlds in sequence, a sixth on their mutual correspondences, and a seventh on *felicitas*, or the eternal happiness that constitutes man's proper end. This sevenfold structure is not decorative; it is, as Black argues throughout, a deliberate exegetical architecture whose symbolic and hermeneutical meaning is inseparable from the content of each exposition. The number seven evokes the Sabbath and the jubilee, and Black devotes his final chapter to demonstrating that the architecture of the whole reflects the forty-nine gates of understanding — a kabbalistic concept according to which Moses ascended through successive degrees of intellectual illumination that he then concealed within the Hebrew text of the opening of Genesis. The Heptaplus is not, in other words, merely a commentary; it is an enactment of the theory of reading it propounds.

Black's central contribution is to situate this claim within Pico's intellectual biography and within the wider field of fifteenth-century biblical hermeneutics. He argues that the *Heptaplus* represents Pico's sustained response to the crisis of 1486–1487, when the papal commission found thirteen of his nine hundred *Conclusiones* heretical, Innocent VIII issued a bull condemning the entire collection, and Pico was forced to flee Rome and eventually seek asylum with Lorenzo de' Medici in Florence. The three works generated by that crisis — the *Conclusiones* themselves, the *Apologia*, and the *Oratio* commonly subtitled "On the Dignity of Man" — are formally as different from the *Heptaplus* as it is possible to imagine. Where the *Conclusiones* are a miscellaneous collection of bare aphorisms arranged for scholastic disputation, the *Heptaplus* is, as Black observes, "a complete and perfected structure." The earlier work never became the public debate it was designed to provoke; the later work presents itself as a finished literary and philosophical object, one that Lorenzo de' Medici felt confident enough to defend against renewed Roman suspicion and that was received enthusiastically by humanists including Ermolao Barbaro, Cristoforo Landino, and Bartolomeo Fontio. The difference is not merely one of genre but of intellectual strategy. After the Roman debacle, Pico relocated his most ambitious theoretical claims — about the nature of biblical interpretation, the hidden structure of the cosmos, and the intellect's ascent toward God — within a form that would be harder to atomize and prosecute than a list of theses.

Black's method is to read the *Heptaplus* through its two prefatory proems before approaching the seven expositions, and this sequence produces his most important analytical moves. The first proem contains Pico's justification for esoteric or non-literal biblical interpretation: his argument that Moses, like the ancient philosophers, concealed the highest truths beneath the letter of the text, revealing them only to the initiated. Black traces this argument through its successive redactions in the *Commento sopra una canzone d'amore* (1485–86), the *Apologia* (1487), and the *Heptaplus* itself, demonstrating a deliberate evolution in Pico's presentation. In the context of the fifteenth century, this argument was unusual. Black's third chapter, a survey of the state of biblical hermeneutics during the second half of the Quattrocento, establishes that the dominant model remained the fourfold sense of Scripture as inherited from medieval commentary tradition, and that the nascent humanist interest in philology, while beginning to make itself felt, had not displaced this framework. Pico's esoteric theory differs sharply from both: it is not a modification of the four senses but a challenge to the entire Christian consensus on the purpose and nature of allegorical reading. Black locates the intellectual sources of this challenge not in mainstream Christian hermeneutics but in the confluence of later Neoplatonism, the works attributed to Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, and kabbalah — traditions that share a commitment to the idea that the deepest knowledge is incommunicable by ordinary discourse and must therefore be wrapped in concealment.

The second proem introduces Pico's model of cosmic structure and his theory of allegory proper, and it is here that Black's engagement with the intellectual traditions becomes most detailed. He argues that Pico's allegorical theory derives primarily from the Neoplatonic tradition and, above all, from its Christian articulation in Pseudo-Dionysius, whose concept of anagogy — the soul's intellectual ascent through symbolic vehicles toward the divine — provides the operative logic of the *Heptaplus* as a whole. The seven-level structure of the commentary is not, on this reading, an arbitrary scheme of parallelism but a formal representation of the ascent itself: to pass through the seven expositions is to undergo a version of the intellectual journey that leads from the elemental world to the contemplation of eternal felicity. This is why Black argues, in his sixth chapter, that the *Heptaplus* should be understood as an expression of the role of the intellect in man's progress toward *felicitas*. He traces this concept through medieval philosophy, particularly through the tradition of commentary on Aristotle's *De anima* and the controversy surrounding Averroes's interpretation of the agent intellect — a controversy that had been a live issue since the thirteenth century and remained intensely contested in the Paduan Averroist circles where Pico had studied. The epistemological dimension of the *Heptaplus* thus connects the work to Pico's Paris years and to his Paduan formation, placing the commentary at the intersection of scholastic Aristotelianism, Neoplatonic epistemology, and biblical exegesis.

The kabbalistic dimension of the *Heptaplus* receives close and careful treatment throughout. Black's first chapter includes a substantial excursus on Pico's Hebrew studies, tracing his relationships with the three scholars who gave him access to Jewish learning: the Averroist Elijah Delmedigo, who provided Arabic philosophical texts via Hebrew intermediaries; the converted Sicilian Jew Flavius Mithridates, who produced over 3,500 pages of Latin translations of kabbalistic and other Hebrew texts specifically for Pico in the years around 1486; and Johanan Alemanno, whose interest in biblical exegesis and the idea of intellectual ascent closely paralleled Pico's concerns in the period of the *Heptaplus*. Black's discussion of Mithridates's translations is particularly important: he notes, following Chaim Wirszubski's foundational work, that marginal marks in the surviving Vatican manuscripts demonstrate that Pico actually read and annotated these texts, sometimes reproducing passages almost verbatim in the *Conclusiones*. The kabbalistic sources to which Pico had access — including Recanati's commentary on the Pentateuch, identified by Wirszubski as the single most influential source for Pico's interpretation of kabbalah in the *Conclusiones* — are therefore directly relevant to the *Heptaplus*, which was composed in the years immediately following this intensive engagement with translated Hebrew learning.

Black's final chapter brings together the structural and kabbalistic dimensions of his argument in its most original move. He demonstrates that the opening word of Genesis, *bereshit*, receives its own independent exposition at the end of the *Heptaplus* — standing apart from the seven expositions as a kind of conclusion — and that this choice reflects a kabbalistic hermeneutic in which the initial word of Scripture concentrates within itself the totality of divine wisdom. The Sabbath, moreover, which governs the number of the expositions, carries in the kabbalistic sources Pico read a specific symbolic burden: it is associated with the fiftieth gate of understanding, the gate that Moses alone approached but did not enter, the gate whose approach is traced through forty-nine degrees of illumination symbolized by the seven times seven of the *Heptaplus*'s structure. The jubilee, too — the fiftieth year — resonates through this numerological system. Black's argument is not that Pico was simply transposing a kabbalistic scheme into a Christian commentary; it is that Pico synthesized Jewish, Neoplatonic, and Christian hermeneutical traditions in a way that was novel in fifteenth-century Europe and that placed him in deliberate opposition to the mainstream commentary tradition of his time.

For PicoDB, Black's book functions as the primary scholarly lens through which the *Heptaplus* should be read and through which every artifact touching on that work should be evaluated. His methodological priorities are clear: structure before paraphrase, hermeneutics before theme-tagging, source ecology over source hierarchy. He insists that the *Heptaplus* cannot be understood without simultaneous attention to Greek Neoplatonism, Pseudo-Dionysian mystical theology, scholastic epistemology (especially the Averroist controversy over the intellect), and kabbalistic biblical exegesis — and that the relationship among these traditions in the *Heptaplus* is not a matter of mechanical syncretism but of genuine intellectual synthesis. He also insists on the continuity between the *Heptaplus* and Pico's earlier works, arguing that the Heptaplus should be read as a deliberate re-articulation, in a new and more stable form, of claims first formulated in the *Commento* and defended, under fire, in the *Apologia*. The post-crisis Pico is not a different thinker from the pre-crisis one; he is the same thinker who has learned, from a painful encounter with ecclesiastical authority, to embed his most heterodox commitments within a structure of such formal completeness that they become harder to isolate and condemn.

Black teaches us, above all, to read the *Heptaplus* as a work of biblical hermeneutics in the fullest sense: a theory of how sacred texts mean, not merely an account of what they say. Every artifact in PicoDB that touches on Genesis, on biblical allegory, on the Sabbath or the jubilee, on the concept of *felicitas*, on Pico's relationship to kabbalistic sources, or on the relationship between the *Heptaplus* and the *Conclusiones* or *Apologia* should be read through the conceptual framework Black provides. His is not the only lens — questions of Pico's Latin style, his relationship to Ficino, or his place in Florentine social history require other guides — but for the hermeneutical architecture of Pico's most sustained biblical work, Black's 2006 monograph remains the indispensable point of departure.
