# Scholar Profile: Sophia Howlett

Sophia Howlett obtained her M.A. from Cambridge University and her Ph.D. from the Medieval Studies Center of York University, where she specialized in Renaissance philosophy and literature. Her earlier scholarly career included a permanent lectureship at the University of Teesside, visiting professorships at the National University of Kiev Mohyla Academy and Kaliningrad State University, a deanship and professorship at Central European University in Budapest (during which she was an Open Society Institute fellow and a visiting scholar at Harvard), and an associate vice-presidency at Kean University in New Jersey. Her 2016 book, Marsilio Ficino and His World (Palgrave Macmillan), established her as a scholar of Florentine Neoplatonism, and her subsequent work on Pico della Mirandola represents both a continuation and a deliberate departure from that earlier focus. By the time she published Re-evaluating Pico in 2021, she was serving as president of the School for International Training in Vermont — a position that, as she describes in her preface, forced the book through a protracted gestation that ultimately transformed it from a planned survey of Pico's syncretism into something more critical and revisionary in temper.

Howlett's intellectual trajectory into Pico is itself instructive. She had initially understood him through the lens of Ficino's Platonic Academy, as something like an heir-apparent to the revival of Platonism. The discovery that "Pico Studies" — the scholarly subfield she distinguishes from Ficino scholarship — had moved decisively away from this framing, and indeed had largely sidelined Ficino altogether in favour of the Kabbalistic dimensions of Pico's work, prompted a fundamental reorientation. What began as an introduction to Pico for English-language readers became, in her own description, a "reevaluation" entailing considerable critical distance from its subject.

Re-evaluating Pico is published in Palgrave Macmillan's series Critical Political Theory and Radical Practice, edited by Rutgers political scientist Stephen Bronner. The series, which "hopes to help reinvigorate what is fast becoming a petrified field of study," aims to connect theoretical inquiry to practical and political concerns. This framing shapes Howlett's book in subtle ways: she situates Pico's thought within the social and political circumstances of late quattrocento Italy with unusual care, attending to his aristocratic class position, his "power network" of patrons and allies, and the knowledge network of translators and scholarly dependents whose labour underwrote his work. Her attention to Pico's social position as a wealthy nobleman — someone who could challenge the pope and hire Flavius Mithridates to translate Kabbalistic texts at his own expense — represents a significant corrective to a scholarly tradition that has tended to mythologize his genius as entirely solitary.

The central intellectual claim of Re-evaluating Pico is that the philosopher's work rests on three roughly equal pillars: Aristotelianism, Platonism, and Jewish Kabbalism. These are not merely sources he draws upon selectively but constitutive foundations of a syncretic vision of the universe that Howlett calls a "christo-syncretism." The three pillars converge in Pico's ambitious project of concordance, which she traces through the 900 Conclusions of 1486, the Heptaplus, On Being and the One, and the Oration. Howlett argues that this corpus, though fragmentary and cut short by Pico's death in 1494, is more coherent than critics like Eugenio Garin and Louis Valcke have maintained: there is, she argues, a sustained and consistent philosophical vision across the major works, unified by a concern with the ascent of the individual soul toward a form of henosis with the divine.

On the question of Pico's relationship to Ficino, Howlett occupies genuinely original ground. Against the tradition — stretching from Ficino's own self-fashioning through Kristeller's influential scholarship — that treats Pico as essentially Ficino's closest protégé and successor in the Platonic Academy, Howlett argues that this framework obscures more than it illuminates. The "Academy" itself, she notes, was largely a Ficinian construction, and Pico's repeated polemical engagements with Ficino — over the theory of love in the Commentary on a Canzone, over the metaphysics of Being and the One, and indirectly over astrological magic in the Disputations — suggest not discipleship but principled rivalry. "Pico wishes to take on Ficino," she writes, "Ficino wishes to 'correct' Pico or smooth over the cracks" (p. 68). The structural difference she identifies between the two thinkers is one of mission: Ficino's is a project of renovatio, a return to a Golden Age mediated by a community of philosophers under his guidance; Pico's is an exceptionalist, solitary, and ultimately ascetic journey of the individual soul to henosis, with no envisioned return.

This contrast with Ficino informs Howlett's treatment of the Oration on the Dignity of Man. She is sharply critical of the twentieth-century reception that transformed this brief preface to the 1486 Rome disputation into a humanist manifesto and herald of modern individualism. She notes that the work was not given its present title during Pico's lifetime — the subtitle de hominis dignitate first appeared in the 1504 Strasbourg edition — and that it was composed in stages during the autumn of 1486 as an introduction to the Conclusions, with a substantial portion later cannibalized for the Apology. Its famous opening passage on Adam's indeterminate nature and capacity for self-transformation is not, she insists, an announcement of modern liberal self-creation but a prelude to a rigorous program of ascetic discipline, philosophical purification, and Kabbalistic ascent. The "dignity" at stake is not the dignity of the species in the abstract but "the mystical vocation" of exceptional individuals capable of undertaking that journey.

Howlett's reading here converges with and draws on Brian Copenhaver's 2019 monograph Magic and the Dignity of Man, which she cites as one of the most important recent interventions in Pico scholarship. She accepts Copenhaver's central claim that the Oration "is silent about human dignity" in any modern sense and that the association of Pico with proto-modern individualism owes more to Burckhardt's anecdotal framing and Garin's mid-century existentialist investment than to the text itself. Garin's eclipse of Kabbalah in favour of Pseudo-Dionysius and Hermeticism, and his consequent foregrounding of the Oration as Pico's masterpiece at the expense of the Conclusions, represents for both Howlett and Copenhaver a serious distortion of Pico's priorities. Where Howlett differs from Copenhaver, or rather supplements him, is in the sustained attention she gives to the Aristotelian dimensions of Pico's thought — dimensions that Pico scholarship has sometimes underplayed relative to Platonism and Kabbalah.

Howlett's treatment of Pico's Aristotelianism is one of the more distinctive contributions of Re-evaluating Pico. She argues that Aristotelianism provides not merely a secondary mode of argumentation but a structuring framework for Pico's metaphysics and theory of ascent: "Aristotelianism or more specifically Aristotelian logic and theory of causality provide the primary mode of argumentation" (p. 55). Crucially, she traces the specific form of Aristotelianism operative in Pico's work to his Paduan formation, particularly his study with Elijah del Medigo, the Cretan Jewish Averroist who introduced him to a tradition in which Aristotelian rationalism had already been placed in dialogue with Jewish mysticism through Maimonides. This Aristotelian-Kabbalistic synthesis, mediated through Averroist categories of causality and the theory of the Active Intellect, is for Howlett the key to understanding how Pico's ascent differs from Ficino's: where Ficino's henosis is primarily Platonic and affective, Pico's is also noetic, a potential union of human intellect with the Aristotelian divine Active Intellect.

Her treatment of Pico's political and social position — the fourth dimension that the series framing encourages — is less fully developed than her philosophical analysis, but it raises genuine questions about the mythology of individual genius that has surrounded Pico since his nephew Gianfrancesco published the first edition of his collected works in 1496. Gianfrancesco's erasure of the contributors — translators, teachers, philosophical interlocutors — who made Pico's output possible is, for Howlett, a founding act of mystification that subsequent scholarship has too often accepted. The result is that "the role of those around Pico — the entourage of translators, philosophers, commentators, mentors — was lost" (p. 29). Restoring that collective dimension is one of the minor but real scholarly contributions of Re-evaluating Pico.

The book is addressed to English-language readers who may not have easy access to the Italian and French scholarship that has substantially reshaped the field since the 1990s. In this respect it performs a genuinely useful function. Its explicit indebtedness to scholars including Moshe Idel, Giulio Busi, Chaim Wirszubski, Pier Cesare Bori, and Brian Copenhaver signals the directions in which serious Pico scholarship has moved. For readers of this encyclopedia, Howlett is most useful as a synthesizing guide to the three-pillar framework, the contrast with Ficino, the critique of the modern myth of the Oration, and the exceptionalist logic of Pico's ascetic mysticism.
