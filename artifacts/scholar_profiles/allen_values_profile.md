# Scholar Profile: Michael J. B. Allen

Michael J. B. Allen is Professor Emeritus of English at the University of California, Los Angeles, where for more than four decades he was the preeminent Anglophone authority on the thought of Marsilio Ficino. His career, which produced a succession of monographs on Ficino's major philosophical works—including *Marsilio Ficino and the Phaedran Charioteer* (1981), *The Platonism of Marsilio Ficino* (1984), *Icastes: Marsilio Ficino's Interpretation of Plato's Sophist* (1989), and a two-volume Variorum collection, *Plato's Third Eye* (1995)—culminated in a collaborative editorial achievement of the first order: the I Tatti Renaissance Library edition of Ficino's *Platonic Theology*, which Allen co-edited with James Hankins across six volumes. The appearance of *Studies in the Platonism of Marsilio Ficino and Giovanni Pico* (Routledge, 2017), a second Variorum collection gathering eighteen essays written over two decades, solidified Allen's position as an indispensable guide not only to Ficino's metaphysics but to the intellectual ecology in which Giovanni Pico della Mirandola formed his own philosophy. Although Allen is principally a Ficino scholar, two essays in the Routledge collection—"Cultura hominis: Giovanni Pico, Marsilio Ficino and the Idea of Man" and "The Birth Day of Venus: Pico as Platonic Exegete in the Commento and the Heptaplus"—represent the most rigorous application of Ficinian Platonism to Pico's thought yet produced in the Anglophone scholarly tradition.

Allen's methodological signature is close, technically demanding philological engagement with Platonic and Neoplatonic texts as Ficino understood them. He does not approach Pico as an eclectic or as a Kabbalist—those dimensions of Pico's project are present in Allen's work only at the periphery—but rather as a thinker who was decisively shaped by, and who deliberately contended with, the Platonic commentary tradition that Ficino had reconstructed in Florence. In this Allen differs sharply from Chaim Wirszubski, whose landmark *Pico della Mirandola's Encounter with Jewish Mysticism* (1989) illuminated the Kabbalistic dimensions of Pico's thought through analysis of Flavius Mithridates' translations, and from S. A. Farmer, whose structural and comparative analysis of the nine hundred *Conclusiones* in *Syncretism in the West* (1998) emphasized the architecture of Pico's concordism over the content of any single tradition. Allen's Pico is, above all, a Platonic exegete: someone who had absorbed Ficino's readings of the *Phaedrus*, *Symposium*, *Timaeus*, *Philebus*, *Sophist*, and *Parmenides*, and who entered into philosophical dispute with those readings at the highest level of technical precision.

The essay "Cultura hominis" is Allen's most sustained exercise in Platonic source-analysis applied to Pico. Beginning from the famous opening of the *Oration on the Dignity of Man*, Allen identifies in Pico's portrait of Adam a dense intertextual matrix drawn from the later Platonic dialogues, especially the *Philebus* and the Neoplatonic commentaries on the *Parmenides*. Pico's description of Adam as a work of *indiscretae imaginis*—a being of indeterminate form—recalls, Allen demonstrates, the doctrine of to apeiron, the indeterminate or infinite, which Plato introduces in the *Philebus* (23C ff.) and which Ficino analyzed at length in his *Philebus* Commentary. For Ficino, and for the Neoplatonists before him, to apeiron signifies the chaos of infinite matter awaiting the imposition of formal determination; but in the Neoplatonic interpretation it also points toward a more positive notion: the soul's infinite potentiality, its capacity to take on the forms of all things because it is not yet limited to any one of them. Allen argues that this reading of Adamic indeterminacy places Pico's anthropology squarely within a Plotinian metaphysical framework, against the influential reading advanced by Eugenio Garin—and subsequently by Cassirer, Kristeller, and Trinkaus—that Pico articulated a distinctively modern doctrine of human freedom standing outside or above the Neoplatonic hierarchy of being. For Allen, Adam is not "outside" the hierarchy but moving within it, exercising a freedom that is ultimately consonant with Neoplatonism's concern for the soul's procession from and return to the One.

This interpretive move—correcting Garin's influential existentialist reading by restoring the Platonic metaphysical context—is characteristic of Allen's approach throughout his engagement with Pico. He shows, for instance, how the *Commento sopra una canzone d'amore* of 1486, Pico's commentary on a love lyric by Girolamo Benivieni, draws directly on Ficino's *De Amore* and on the Neoplatonic commentaries on the *Symposium*, even as it attacks Ficino sharply and claims to correct his errors. The *Commento*'s account of love, beauty, and the intelligible hierarchy—Venus Urania and Venus Vulgaris, the procession of beauty from the One through Intellect and Soul into matter, the ascent of the soul through eros toward the divine—is, Allen shows, thoroughly Ficinian in its metaphysical architecture, however much Pico insists on departing from Ficino in particular theses. As Allen puts it with characteristic precision, Pico "writes like one in haste, a brilliant and animated lecturer who knows his subject better than himself and who speaks without notes and without restraint." The critique is acute, but the apparatus borrowed—the chain of being, the Neoplatonic hypostases, the soul's erotic ascent—comes from Ficino's translations and commentaries, above all from his reading of the *Phaedrus* and *Symposium*.

In "The Birth Day of Venus," Allen reads both the *Commento* and the *Heptaplus* as exercises in Platonic exegesis and poetic theology. The essay traces the genealogy of Pico's interpretive method—the idea that the ancient poets concealed philosophical truths beneath the veil of myth—through Ficino, Cristoforo Landino, and the long tradition of allegorical theology stretching back through Boccaccio's *Genealogie deorum gentilium* to Stoic allegorism. Pico's *Commento*, Allen argues, is not simply an anti-Ficinian polemic: it is a demonstration of what poetic theology should look like when practiced at the highest level of Platonic rigor. Where Ficino and Landino had read classical myths through the lens of Christian allegory, Pico insists on a more strictly Platonic exegesis: the ancient poets—Orpheus, Homer, Hesiod—were themselves Platonists before Plato, and their myths encode the metaphysical truths of emanation, procession, and return. The *Heptaplus*, Pico's sevenfold interpretation of the Mosaic creation narrative, extends this method to Scripture: Genesis becomes a Platonic cosmogony in which each of the seven days corresponds to a level of being in the Neoplatonic hierarchy, and in which Christ appears as the perfection and fulfillment of the Idea of Man—the second Adam who redeems what the first Adam left incomplete.

Allen's treatment of the textual history of the *Commento* is among his most important contributions to Pico studies. He notes that the first printed edition of the *Commento*, published in Florence in 1519 by Giunta, was prepared after both Pico and Ficino were dead, and that Benivieni—who had commissioned the commentary—suppressed the most pointed attacks on Ficino, creating an artificially harmonious picture of the two philosophers' relationship. The recovery of the full polemical text by Eugenio Garin in the mid-twentieth century revealed the true sharpness of Pico's critique. Allen situates this textual history within the broader context of what he calls "the second Ficino-Pico controversy"—a dispute over the interpretation of the *Parmenides* and the *Sophist* that underlies both the *Commento* and the later treatise *On Being and the One* (*De Ente et Uno*). In *On Being and the One*, Pico takes the Aristotelian-Thomist position that being and unity are convertible, against Ficino's Proclean reading that the One is prior to being. He dismisses the *Parmenides* as a "dialectical game" (a remark that, Allen notes, particularly annoyed Ficino), and in doing so rejects two texts that were central to Ficino's entire Platonic theology.

For the purposes of PicoDB, Allen's work defines several key research horizons. His essays establish the precise contours of Pico's engagement with the Ficinian Platonic tradition and supply the tools needed to interpret the *Commento* as a document in the history of Platonic exegesis rather than as an anomalous episode in Renaissance love poetry. Allen provides indispensable guidance to the philosophical vocabulary of the *Commento*—the theory of emanation, the Neoplatonic account of beauty and eros, the concept of the intelligible world (*mundus intelligibilis*) and its relationship to the Ideas, the Plotinian hypostases and their Ficinian equivalents. He also clarifies Pico's position within the broader landscape of Renaissance Platonism: not a faithful disciple of Ficino but a contentious co-Platonist who absorbed Ficino's Plato deeply enough to contest it with genuine philosophical force.

Allen's scholarly genealogy locates him in a tradition that runs from Paul Oskar Kristeller's foundational work on Ficino's philosophy through the I Tatti scholars of the late twentieth century. His collegial relationship with Brian Copenhaver—Pico's foremost Anglophone translator and interpreter, and Allen's "beloved colleague" as he describes him in the preface to the Routledge volume—created an intellectual partnership that shaped much of the current understanding of Ficino and Pico in Anglophone scholarship. For Copenhaver's work on Pico's magic and Hermetic sources, Allen's Platonic analysis provides the metaphysical background; conversely, Copenhaver's attention to the *Conclusiones* and the *Oration* fills out the dimensions of Pico's project that Allen treats more briefly.

What Allen cannot provide—and does not attempt—is coverage of Pico's Kabbalistic learning, of the precise role of Flavius Mithridates in shaping Pico's Hebrew sources, or of the sefirot doctrines that animate the Kabbalistic *Conclusiones*. Those questions belong to Wirszubski and to Giulio Busi. Allen's Pico is a philosopher of the Platonic tradition: technically masterful, combative, sometimes hasty, always brilliant in flashes, and impossible to understand without the long Ficinian shadow behind him. It is a portrait that complements rather than competes with the Kabbalistic Pico of Wirszubski and Busi, and together these traditions of scholarship constitute the major axes around which serious interpretation of Pico's project must orient itself.
