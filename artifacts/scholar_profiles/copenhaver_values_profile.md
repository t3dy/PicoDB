# Brian P. Copenhaver as a Scholar of Pico della Mirandola

## Intellectual Formation and the Long Road to Pico

Brian P. Copenhaver arrived at Giovanni Pico della Mirandola by a circuitous route that itself mirrors the complexity of the Renaissance thinker he would spend decades reassessing. His scholarly formation began in earnest at Loyola College in Baltimore in 1960, where Jesuit teachers still delivered a curriculum shaped by the sixteenth-century Ratio Studiorum, drilling students in Thomist scholasticism at the very moment their secular counterparts were reading Freud and Skinner. This early immersion in the technical vocabulary of Aquinas and the schools left a permanent mark, equipping Copenhaver to read Pico's most forbidding texts with a fluency that his contemporaries in Renaissance studies often lacked. After moving to UCLA, he migrated formally into the Philosophy Department in 2003, where extended seminars with the medieval logician Calvin Normore taught him how to bring the "honored philosophical dead back into living philosophical conversations." This double allegiance — to the history of ideas and to technical analytic philosophy — defines the distinctive method of his two major Pico books.

His earlier work on Hermetica, Kabbalah, and magic in Renaissance thought grew from a seminal article on Kabbalah in the Warburg Journal in 1977, research conducted at the Warburg Institute under the influence of D. P. Walker, and a decades-long collaboration with Moshe Idel, whom he credits alongside Gershom Scholem and Chaim Wirszubski for whatever he understands of Kabbalistic thought. Two synthetic volumes published in 2015 — a source anthology and an interpretive history of magic in Western culture — left a conspicuous gap: both bypassed Kabbalah and bypassed Pico, the man who first attempted to explain Kabbalah to Christians. The two Pico books of 2019 and 2022 fill that gap.

## The Modern Myth of Pico and Its Genealogy

The central achievement of *Magic and the Dignity of Man: Pico della Mirandola and His Oration in Modern Memory* (Harvard University Press, 2019) is the systematic destruction of what Copenhaver calls "the usual stories about Pico and his Oration." He begins from a simple, verifiable observation: the speech universally titled the *Oration on the Dignity of Man* was never given that title by its author. Pico never published it. He never delivered it. The title was imposed by a later editor who drew hasty conclusions from the speech's opening pages, and the phrase "dignity of man" has since been read backward onto a text it fundamentally misrepresents.

The misreading hardened into a myth through a chain of historiographical moves Copenhaver traces with forensic care. Jacob Burckhardt, in his 1860 *Culture of the Renaissance in Italy*, awarded Pico roughly two hundred decisive words, placing the opening fable from the Oration in the context of Italian peasants' social mobility and the Renaissance artist's capacity to shift perspectives — "on this topic, the loftiest ideas were expressed by Pico in his speech on the dignity of man." Burckhardt's reading was conditioned by his thesis about Renaissance individualism, and the fit between Pico's fable of Adam's free self-fashioning and Burckhardt's sociological argument was convenient rather than philologically justified. After Burckhardt, the myth passed through the Italian idealist tradition: Giovanni Gentile, who in 1916 read the Oration as an incipient naturalist idealism still shackled by God's transcendence; and then Eugenio Garin, whose 1937 book on Pico became the standard scholarly account for the rest of the twentieth century. Garin's Pico is "the last thinker of the fifteenth century, tormented by the discomfort of a dying era," who "experimented with the power of the Spirit that is never content" and took from that experiment "the theme that celebrates human dignity." Copenhaver credits Garin with real scholarly achievement and genuine engagement with Pico's texts, but also shows that Garin systematically downplayed Kabbalah, omitted the 900 Conclusions from his 1942 anthology, and inherited from Gentile a reading of the Oration that was ideologically overdetermined — a Hegelian-inflected image of self-creating human spirit that the text cannot support.

The myth received its postwar popularization through American textbook culture. The 1948 anthology *The Renaissance Philosophy of Man*, still in print, included the first complete English translation of the Oration and placed it in the context of humanist individualism. Dozens of general history textbooks followed with boxed excerpts introducing Pico as the man who "stated the Renaissance image of man" and described human beings as "free to become whatever they choose." By the end of the twentieth century, the Oration had become, in Wikipedia's formulation, the "Manifesto of the Renaissance." Copenhaver documents this celebrity with the wry thoroughness of an intellectual historian who has watched a philosophical falsification become a cultural reflex.

## What the Oration Actually Says

Against the myth, Copenhaver performs a close reading of the full Oration in the book's third and longest section, showing that the speech is not about human dignity and freedom in any modern sense. The word *dignitas* appears exactly twice in the text. On its first occurrence it is paired with *gloria* as a prize held by angels, not humans — it denotes angelic rank higher than anything human could attain. On its second occurrence it describes the value of philosophy as a preparatory exercise, a comparative and relative term. In neither case does *dignitas* apply to human persons as an inherent or inalienable endowment. Kant's moral concept of Würde — an unconditional, incomparable, inalienable value belonging to every rational being — is nowhere in Pico's speech, and Copenhaver argues at length in his first chapter that it could not be, because that concept did not exist in Pico's world. It was the late Enlightenment that produced the modern English word "dignity" with its Kantian freight, and it was the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries that first projected it onto Pico's text: Copenhaver traces this move to the historian of philosophy Wilhelm Tennemann, who read the Oration in German translation and described its *dignitas* as Würde.

The speech Pico actually drafted is an exhortation to ascetic mysticism. Its most extended passage, translated in full by Copenhaver, instructs its audience to "scorn things of earth, let us despise those of heaven, and then, leaving behind whatever belongs to the world, let us fly up to the hypercosmic court nearest the most exalted divinity." The ladder of ascent is Jacob's ladder, the angelic orders are Cherubim and Seraphim, and the goal is not human self-realization but self-annihilation: "we shall be ourselves no longer, but shall be him, the very One who made us." The body, far from being celebrated, is denounced as the noose around the soul's neck. The Oration, Copenhaver argues, is an esoteric speech whose Jewish Kabbalistic allusions — encoded in scriptural fragments scattered through the text — were unintelligible to the Roman cardinals who were its intended audience. Pico drafted it to introduce the 900 Conclusions and never published it, probably because after the debacle of 1487 he was embarrassed by its grandiose ambitions. To read its opening fable of Adam's free self-determination as a charter of Renaissance humanism is to mistake a rhetorical exordium for the message of the whole.

## The Trial, the Thirteen Condemned Theses, and the Apology

*Pico della Mirandola on Trial: Heresy, Freedom, and Philosophy* (Oxford University Press, 2022) shifts focus from the Oration's reception to the juridical and philosophical crisis that nearly ended Pico's life. In late 1486 Pico published the 900 Conclusions — a vast compendium of theses drawn from Latin scholastics, Greek Platonists, Arabic commentators, the Hermetic corpus, and Kabbalistic texts — and invited all comers to Rome to debate them at his expense. Pope Innocent VIII responded within weeks by appointing a commission of sixteen judges to examine the book. By early March 1487 the commission had returned unanimous verdicts condemning thirteen propositions. Copenhaver shows that the speed of the proceedings — roughly three weeks from first meeting to final verdict — and the documented behavior of the judges make clear that the outcome was predetermined. The commission was a prosecutorial instrument, not a neutral tribunal.

The thirteen condemned theses range across the central dogmas of Catholic Christianity: Christ's descent to Hell and the metaphysics of his soul's spatial location; the doctrine of the Incarnation and the problem of how an unchanging divine nature could take on a human body; three theses on the Eucharist, including questions about transubstantiation and the logical supposition of the words of consecration; Pico's Kabbalistic thesis defending Kabbalah as confirmation of Christian doctrine; a philological-patristic question about Origen; a question about the relationship between belief and will; and further questions touching worship, miracle, the nature of God, and the soul. Copenhaver analyzes six of these Questions at length, using the tools of analytic philosophy to reconstruct Pico's arguments and assess their validity.

The most technically demanding of these analyses concerns the eucharistic theses. Pico's Q6 proposes that Christ's body can be present on the altar "by the sustaining of breadness (*paneitas*)" — using a scholastic technical term, *suppositare*, that is not found in any classical dictionary. His Q9 argues that someone who holds that accidents cannot exist without "existing-in" a subject can still maintain orthodox eucharistic doctrine without claiming that the substance of bread persists after consecration. His Q10 claims that the words of consecration — *hoc est enim corpus meum* — are "taken materially, not as signifying," invoking the medieval logicians' theory of material supposition to explain the puzzling demonstrative *hoc*. Copenhaver shows that all three theses rely on a body of scholastic logical and semantic theory — the theory of suppositio, including distinctions between personal, simple, and material supposition — that was both technically sophisticated and, by 1487, already under assault from humanist critics who saw it as barbarous jargon. Pico's judges applied the principle *ex vi verborum* — "from the meaning of the words" — to find his propositions heretical, and repeated this finding unanimously in four of their verdicts. Copenhaver shows that they were following a predetermined script and in some cases reasoning badly, but he also shows that Pico's own defenses were hastily assembled and sometimes incoherent.

Pico composed the Apology — his formal self-defense addressed to the Pope — in approximately twenty nights, dictating in haste and relying on scholarly assistants. Copenhaver identifies Jean Cabrol's four-volume *Defenses of the Theology of Thomas Aquinas* as a primary source for Q6, taken almost verbatim in places; Guido the Carmelite's *Summa on Heresies* as a source for Q1; and various quodlibetal and Sentences commentaries for the other questions. The prince cited dozens of medieval authorities — Albert the Great, Thomas Aquinas, John Duns Scotus, William of Ockham, Robert Holcot, Jean Quidort, Pierre de la Palud, Henry of Ghent, Durand de Saint-Pourçain, and others — sometimes naming them, sometimes not, following normal scholastic practice. The Apology shows Pico not as a humanist displaying classical philology or emulating Ciceronian eloquence, but as a scholastic theologian fluently deployed within a tradition of medieval disputation. When he did switch to a more rhetorical register in the Apology's introduction, he immediately caught himself: "I must change my way of speaking — I'm talking to barbarians."

Copenhaver also provides vivid transcription of the courtroom confrontations recorded in the Apology itself, including Pico's exchanges of insults with commissioners who could not read Greek, his punning on *testis* (witness/testicles), and his dismissal of the *Fasciculus Temporum* — a popular illustrated chronicle consulted by one judge — as a book that "can go straight to Hell since no one trusts it." These episodes reveal an arrogant young aristocrat whose combativeness served him badly but whose underlying philosophical arguments were often sound.

## Historiographical Position: Against Garin, Against the Humanist Canon

Copenhaver's revision of Pico scholarship stands in deliberate opposition to the dominant interpretive tradition established by Garin and ratified by Ernst Cassirer, Paul Oskar Kristeller, and the postwar humanist scholarship they inspired. That tradition, while differing in important ways among its practitioners, consistently reads Pico as a representative figure of Renaissance humanism — a thinker who synthesized ancient wisdom, celebrated human freedom and creative self-making, and pointed toward modernity. Cassirer's 1942 analysis of Pico in *The Individual and the Cosmos in Renaissance Philosophy* treated the Oration as evidence of the Renaissance discovery of human subjectivity and freedom. Kristeller, more circumspect about labels, nonetheless placed Pico within the tradition of Platonic philosophy that he saw as definitive of Renaissance high culture.

Copenhaver's counter-argument is methodological as much as historical. He insists that reading only the opening of the Oration — as Burckhardt did, as textbooks did, as most subsequent commentary has done — produces a radically incomplete picture. The full speech exhorts its audience toward self-annihilation in God, Cherubic transformation, and Kabbalistic angel magic. The *actual* books Pico published in his lifetime — the 900 Conclusions, the Apology, and the Heptaplus — are scholastic through and through, written in "not the splendid Roman tongue but the way the most famous disputants talk in Paris." Pico's letter to Ermolao Barbaro, in which the prince defends the barbarian scholastics' philosophical Latin against humanist classicists, makes explicit that Pico chose not to be a humanist in the philological and rhetorical sense that Valla, Poliziano, and Poggio exemplified. The Apology provides the most extensive written record of Pico's actual philosophical practice, and that practice is "medieval and scholastic" — words that Copenhaver deploys not as insults but as precise historical descriptions.

This does not mean that Pico was simply a medieval scholastic. Copenhaver is careful to distinguish between the different texts and registers of Pico's output. The Oration, the Commento, the letter to Barbaro, and the Heptaplus are written in elegant humanist Latin very different from the Conclusions and Apology. His ambition was genuine synthesis — concordia — the harmonization of Plato and Aristotle, of scholasticism and Platonism, of Christian theology and Kabbalah and Hermeticism. But this was an original and idiosyncratic project, not the reduction of Christianity to Enlightenment values. And the execution, in the published texts, was thoroughly scholastic. What Copenhaver argues, ultimately, is that the familiar "progressive humanist Pico" who did not write the Apology was also not the author who produced the works he chose to make public during his lifetime.

## Method and Scope

Copenhaver's method in both books is unusually wide-ranging. The first book operates at the scale of cultural history, tracking Pico's celebrity across five centuries through textbooks, journalism, hagiography, Italian nationalist and Fascist intellectual politics, Victorian and Edwardian literary culture, American Cold War pedagogy, and internet culture. It is a history of how a philosopher becomes a cultural icon and how that process necessarily distorts the philosopher's ideas. The second book contracts to close philosophical analysis, reconstructing the logical and metaphysical arguments of individual Questions in the Apology with the precision of an analytic philosopher reading a medieval text. Copenhaver is explicit about his methodological ambition: to make Pico "closer to current philosophical conversations by clarifying his thought with tools of analytic philosophy."

The two books together constitute a scholarly project that is unusual in its refusal to separate intellectual history from philosophy. Copenhaver reads primary sources with the rigor of a classicist, the contextual sensitivity of a historian of ideas, and the argumentative precision of an analytic philosopher. The result is a Pico who is simultaneously more inaccessible — because deeply embedded in medieval scholastic traditions largely foreign to modern readers — and more historically specific than the textbook icon he has displaced.

## Significance

Copenhaver's two Pico books represent the most thorough and technically sustained challenge to the humanist interpretation of Pico yet produced in English. They displace Garin's 1937 book as the starting point for serious engagement with Pico's thought, though Copenhaver is careful to acknowledge Garin's real scholarly contributions, particularly his treatment of magic. They make the Apology — largely unread for five centuries and never before the subject of book-length philosophical analysis — central to understanding what Pico actually believed and how he actually argued. And they restore to Pico's biography the crisis of 1487, which prior scholarship had treated as an episode rather than a defining moment: Copenhaver argues that the heresy trial and the Apology it produced reveal more about the prince's philosophical commitments than the famous and carefully crafted speech he never delivered.

For the study of Renaissance philosophy, Copenhaver's work represents a sustained methodological argument: that the history of philosophical ideas must be grounded in the full range of a thinker's texts, in the specific technical languages those texts deploy, and in the institutional and theological contexts that shaped what could and could not be said. Giovanni Pico della Mirandola was not a herald of Enlightenment. He was a brilliant, arrogant, theologically audacious young man who tried to debate the whole of human knowledge at once, provoked a papal condemnation, and spent twenty nights arguing for his life in the technical idiom of medieval scholasticism. That Pico is more interesting, and considerably stranger, than the icon of the textbooks.
