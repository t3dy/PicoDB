# Scholar Profile: Amos Edelheit

Amos Edelheit is a historian of Renaissance intellectual and religious life whose scholarly formation spans several of Europe's leading research institutions. His doctoral work was completed at Tel-Aviv University under the joint supervision of Rivka Feldhay and, until his untimely death in 2002, Salvatore I. Camporeale O.P. — the Dominican historian of humanism and theology to whom Edelheit's principal monograph is dedicated. The book was substantially revised during a postdoctoral fellowship at the De Wulf-Mansion Centre for Ancient, Mediaeval, and Renaissance Philosophy at the Catholic University of Louvain, and it benefited additionally from a Saxl Fund Award at the Warburg Institute, London. These overlapping institutional affiliations — Italian Dominican intellectual history, Flemish scholastic philosophy, Warburg classical scholarship — left a visible mark on Edelheit's method: he reads Renaissance humanists with simultaneous attention to their classical learning, their scholastic formation, and their practical theological commitments, refusing the disciplinary walls that have often separated these dimensions in modern scholarship.

His principal work, *Ficino, Pico and Savonarola: The Evolution of Humanist Theology 1461/2–1498*, published by Brill in 2008 as volume 78 of the *Medieval Mediterranean* series, proposes an interpretation of late-Quattrocento Florentine intellectual life that is both historiographically ambitious and deliberately corrective. The book argues that the intellectual productions of Marsilio Ficino, Giovanni Pico della Mirandola, and Girolamo Savonarola — figures usually treated separately, or as representatives of incompatible intellectual programs — should be understood together as participants in a single evolving movement that Edelheit names "humanist theology." This term, which he takes from and extends beyond the usage of Camporeale, Charles Trinkaus, and John O'Malley, designates a new kind of religious-intellectual practice that emerged in Florence from the early 1460s onward in response to a pervasive sense of spiritual crisis: a mode of thinking that was humanist in its classical formation and rhetorical sensibility, theological in its subject matter and doctrinal commitments, and philosophical in its engagement with both Platonic and scholastic traditions. Edelheit argues that "humanist theology" should replace the common scholarly vocabulary of "syncretism," "fideism," and "Christian apologetics" as the operative framework for understanding this generation of Florentine thinkers.

The book's historiographical argument, laid out in a long and detailed introduction, is directed simultaneously against four major interpretive traditions in Renaissance studies. Against Hans Baron's civic humanism, Edelheit argues that the religious dimension of Florentine intellectual life has been systematically underweighted: the humanists' engagement with theology was not a byproduct of their political commitments but a primary and generative concern in its own right. Against Ernst Cassirer's and Frances Yates's emphasis on the mystical and Hermetic elements in Ficino and Pico, he argues that these approaches, however illuminating on their own terms, divorce the ideas they discuss from the religious and theological context that gives them their meaning: Cassirer's "mystical vision" and Yates's "Renaissance Magus" become, in the absence of that context, essentially secular notions, untethered from the questions about Christianity, grace, and ecclesiastical reform that actually animated the thinkers in question. Against Paul Oskar Kristeller's identification of humanism with the specifically rhetorical and grammatical disciplines of the *studia humanitatis*, Edelheit argues that such a definition excludes by stipulation precisely the figures — Ficino, Pico, Poliziano — who were recognized by their contemporaries as the most important humanists of the age, and produces the absurdity of calling Pico, who engaged more deeply with scholastic Aristotelianism than almost any humanist of his generation, a non-humanist because he "stressed content rather than form." And against Eugenio Garin's more capacious dialectical account of humanism — which Edelheit regards as the most historically productive of the four traditions, and from whose lineage he acknowledges himself to descend — he argues that Garin and his followers, including Cesare Vasoli, sometimes emphasize the novelty and the heterodox elements of Ficino and Pico at the expense of the more traditional and scholastic dimensions of their religious thinking.

The concept of "humanist theology" that Edelheit proposes in response to these limitations is defined by several linked characteristics. It is a theology that takes as its reference points not the summae and quaestiones of the medieval schools but the classical texts of pagan philosophy, the writings of the Church Fathers (read with humanist philological tools), and Scripture itself as a historical document. It treats religion not as a fixed doctrinal system to be elaborated by trained specialists but as a phenomenon central to human nature, with a history that can be recovered and evaluated by humanist methods. It uses Latin not in the technical idiom of scholastic disputation but in a prose style shaped by classical models. And it is prepared to draw on heterodox sources — Neoplatonic, Hermetic, kabbalistic, Arabic — without assimilating them to a predetermined syncretic scheme, since the criterion of Christian truth remains operative and normative for all the thinkers in Edelheit's account. The crucial point is that this theology is not simply an application of humanist rhetoric to inherited theological content: it involves genuinely new ideas about what Christianity is, what religion means for humanity, and how the tradition of sacred learning should be critically assessed.

Edelheit's chapter on Pico, the fourth and longest in the book, is organized around the *Apologia* of 1487 — the defense Pico wrote in response to the papal commission's condemnation of thirteen of his nine hundred *Conclusiones*. This choice is itself a historiographical intervention: Edelheit notes that no scholar from either the school of Kristeller or that of Garin had, at the time of his writing, devoted a sustained study to the *Apologia*, even though Garin himself had translated into Italian only the *De hominis dignitate*, the *Heptaplus*, and the *De ente et uno*, leaving the *Apologia* to one side. For Edelheit, this neglect has distorted the picture of Pico's thought in a fundamental way, because the *Apologia* is the text in which Pico most explicitly articulates his attitude toward philosophy, toward the scholastic tradition, and toward the boundaries between *opinio* and *fides* — opinion and faith. The chapter's title names the central tension: "Between *opinio* and *fides*."

What Edelheit finds in the *Apologia* is a Pico who deploys scholastic argumentation with great technical competence not because he is a scholastic philosopher at heart but because he needs to refute the theologians of the papal commission on their own ground. Pico demonstrates his mastery of Albert, Aquinas, Scotus, and the other scholastic authorities in order to show that the propositions condemned as heretical are in fact defensible within the tradition: that, as one section heading puts it, "to err in the opinion of Thomas is not to err in the faith." But when Pico speaks in his own voice about the nature of theology and religion, the register changes. Edelheit argues that Pico connects the skepticism of the ancient Academy — drawing specifically on Cicero's *Academica* and Augustine's *Contra Academicos* — with a theological position that locates certainty exclusively in Scripture and in the primitive Church, while treating the subsequent tradition of patristic and scholastic commentary as a domain of probability rather than of faith. This is a genuinely novel position, and it distinguishes Pico sharply from Ficino: where Ficino brings into his discussions of religion the full resources of Neoplatonic cosmology, Pico at this moment emphasizes the primacy of revelation and the radical difference between the certainties of faith and the probabilities of philosophical opinion.

Edelheit's broader argument about the relationship between Ficino and Pico is one of the most useful contributions his book makes to Pico studies. He resists the common tendency to treat Pico as a member of "Ficino's Platonic Academy" — a phrase whose historical accuracy has been questioned by James Hankins and others — and instead presents the two thinkers as engaged in a shared project of humanist theology while differing fundamentally in their methods, their sources, and their theological emphases. Ficino's approach is constructive and systematic: he builds a new theological universe by synthesizing Platonic, Neoplatonic, Hermetic, and Christian materials within a framework of prisca theologia. Pico's is more critical and adversarial: he uses the scholastic tradition as a foil against which to define the limits of philosophical certainty, and he draws on a far wider range of sources — Arabic, Jewish, kabbalistic, as well as Greek and Latin — without assimilating them to a single systematic framework. The relationship between the two men is best understood, on Edelheit's account, not as that of master and disciple but as that of colleagues within a shared intellectual environment who are pursuing related but distinct versions of the humanist theological project.

The institutional and social dimensions of this project receive sustained attention throughout Edelheit's book, in ways that distinguish his approach from both Cassirer's idealist history of ideas and Kristeller's history of professional disciplines. He insists that humanist theology is not simply a set of ideas but a practice embedded in specific institutional contexts — the Florentine circle around Lorenzo de' Medici, the Dominican convent of Santa Maria Novella, the papal court in Rome — and that these contexts shaped both the form and the content of what the humanists wrote. His first chapter, on Giovanni Caroli, the prior of Santa Maria Novella, is designed precisely to make this point: by examining the detailed local institutional crisis through which Caroli lived, Edelheit establishes the concrete historical texture within which the more speculative humanist theology of Ficino and Pico unfolded. The spiritual crisis that humanist theology responded to was not an abstraction; it was felt by specific individuals in specific institutions, and the intellectual responses it generated bore the marks of those specific contexts.

For PicoDB, Edelheit's principal contribution is methodological and corrective. He teaches us to notice the scholastic theological dimension of Pico's thought as something more than background noise or a rhetorical strategy: it is, he argues, a genuine and productive engagement with the tradition of medieval philosophy that shaped the very terms in which Pico posed his questions about faith, reason, and the limits of human knowledge. He draws our attention to the *Apologia* as a primary document for understanding Pico's philosophical theology, a document that the major interpretive traditions had largely passed over. He makes visible the Florentine institutional context as a shaping condition of Pico's intellectual life, resisting the tendency to treat Pico as a free-floating genius of pure syncretism. And he offers a framework — humanist theology — that allows us to see Pico in productive comparison with Ficino and Savonarola without collapsing the important differences among them. Where Crofton Black illuminates the hermeneutical architecture of the *Heptaplus*, Edelheit illuminates the theological and philosophical commitments that govern what Pico was trying to do when he read Scripture at all. The two accounts are complementary: Black gives us the structure of Pico's biblical exegesis; Edelheit gives us its theological stakes.

Edelheit's position in the field places him in productive tension with the other major interpretive traditions. His debt to Garin is explicit; his departures from Garin are carefully argued. His critique of Kristeller is sharper than his critique of Garin, but it is not dismissive: he acknowledges the importance of Kristeller's institutional history of humanism even as he argues that it systematically misses the religious dimension of Florentine intellectual life. His engagement with Cassirer and Yates is respectful but pointed: the mystical and Hermetic dimensions of Ficino and Pico are real, but they cannot be properly understood when separated from the theological context that makes them meaningful. Against S. A. Farmer's cross-cultural and comparative approach to the nine hundred *Conclusiones*, Edelheit implicitly argues for a more contextual and less typological reading: what matters is not that Pico assembled theses from many traditions but what he thought he was doing theologically when he did so, and that question can only be answered by attending to the specific theological problems — about faith, reason, heresy, and the authority of Scripture — that the *Apologia* addresses. Edelheit is, in short, a historian of theology as well as of ideas, and his insistence on taking the theological dimension of Pico's work seriously, rather than reducing it to a rhetorical strategy or a mystical attitude, is his most durable contribution to the field.
