# S. A. Farmer

Stephen Alan Farmer is an independent scholar whose 1998 monograph *Syncretism in the West: Pico's 900 Theses (1486): The Evolution of Traditional Religious and Philosophical Systems* (Medieval and Renaissance Texts and Studies, vol. 167; Tempe: Arizona State University, 1998; second printing 2003) remains the standard critical edition and the most ambitious synthetic interpretation of Pico della Mirandola's *Conclusiones nongentae*. The book is simultaneously a Latin edition with facing English translation, an introductory monograph of roughly 180 pages, and a running commentary on every thesis in the text. No comparable edition or interpretive synthesis existed before it; Farmer's work superseded Bohdan Kieszkowski's 1973 edition and Albano Biondi's 1995 Italian translation, both of which Farmer judged unreliable on textual grounds.

Farmer's scholarly formation was deliberately cross-cultural and comparative. His research began in the early 1980s under fellowships from the National Endowment for the Humanities and Harvard's Villa I Tatti center for Italian Renaissance studies in Florence, where the Berenson Library's unusual collection of Eastern alongside Western texts first prompted the comparative dimensions of the project. His acknowledged intellectual debts include the sinologist John B. Henderson, whose studies of Chinese cosmology, scriptural canon, and orthodoxy provided the conceptual vocabulary of "correlative systems" that Farmer applies to Pico's work, and a range of Platonic scholars, Arabists, and Hebraists, including Paul Oskar Kristeller, François Secret, and Chaim Wirszubski, who preceded him in mapping the individual source traditions of the *Conclusiones*. By training and disposition Farmer belongs to no single disciplinary home; his method is explicitly comparative rather than conventionally philological, and *Syncretism in the West* is accordingly framed as a contribution to the study of the evolution of premodern thought in general rather than to Italian Renaissance scholarship in particular.

## The Central Interpretive Argument

The governing thesis of *Syncretism in the West* is methodological as much as historical. Farmer's principal claim is that the nine hundred theses constitute a systematic whole and must be read as such: that Pico himself said, in the *Apology* for his aborted debate, that "in all my conclusions there is always a certain hidden connection" (*occulta concatenatio*) that his opponents might not notice. Farmer takes this claim seriously in a way that previous scholars had not. He argues that five centuries of Pico scholarship have resulted in a "large family of warring Picos" precisely because historians read the *Conclusiones* in piecemeal fashion, lifting theses out of their systematic context and treating them as direct evidence of Pico's views without considering the structural logic that ties individual theses to one another across the text. The book's central ambition is to reconstruct that *occulta concatenatio* by analyzing the organizational principles of the whole text rather than the contents of any single section.

Farmer contends that those organizational principles are primarily correlative and numerological. Pico's "new philosophy" (*philosophia nova*)—the set of seventy-two paradoxical conclusions presented in his own name in the second part of the text—constitutes a rigidly hierarchical emanationist metaphysics in which every property at every level of reality reappears "in some mode" (*modo suo*) at every other level. This principle, expressed in the formula *omnia sunt in omnibus modo suo* (all things exist in all things in their own mode), Farmer identifies as the operative key to Pico's syncretic strategy: apparent conflicts between authorities are resolved by assigning each conflicting statement to a different level of a cosmic hierarchy, so that Plato and Aristotle, Averroes and Avicenna, the Neo-Platonic henads and the kabbalistic *sefirot*, can all be harmonized by correlative hierarchical distinctions rather than direct contradiction. What looks like pluralism is in fact a single metaphysical system imposing itself across all traditions.

This argument has implications for how Pico's *syncretism* should be understood. Farmer argues against treating Pico as a simple reconciler who valued all traditions on their own terms. Pico radically distorted his sources—he knew this and did it deliberately. The theses attributed to Averroes deny individual immortality, which Averroes genuinely denied; but Pico planned to argue at Rome that those same theses, properly understood through the proportional logic of his *philosophia nova*, were actually compatible with Christian doctrine and with Avicenna's contrary position. The same strategy governs his treatment of the kabbalistic *sefirot*, Neo-Platonic henads, and Orphic hymns: these traditions are not valued for what they actually say but for the analogical raw material they supply to Pico's prior correlative schema. Farmer's Pico is not a Renaissance pluralist but an extreme systematizer who absorbed everything into a totalizing metaphysical architecture.

## Source Ecology and Reading Methodology

One of Farmer's most consequential methodological contributions is what might be called a "source ecology" for the *Conclusiones*. Rather than pursuing the traditional Renaissance "source hunt"—tracing each thesis back to its immediate literary antecedent—Farmer pays attention to the organizational structure within which sources are grouped, the order in which gentes (nations of thinkers) appear in the first or historical section of the text, and the way theses from different source clusters are designed to be collated at Rome. He demonstrates that the historical section of the text was organized in *reverse* historical order, with the most corrupt Latin scholastics appearing first and the most ancient traditions—Chaldeans, Egyptians, and Hebrew Kabbalists—appearing last, embodying the progressive discovery of wisdom nearer its primal source. This structure, Farmer argues, directly mirrors the emanationist cosmology of the *philosophia nova*: just as reality emanates from divine unity through progressive multiplicity toward matter, so the flow of wisdom through history moves from primordial unity (Moses, Zoroaster, Orpheus) through gradual corruption to the warring schools of the moderns.

The implications for reading magic and Kabbalah in the *Conclusiones* are significant. Farmer argues that the extensive scholarly effort since the sixteenth century to decode Pico's kabbalistic *sefirot* has been misdirected because it ignores the equally extensive series of theses on Neo-Platonic henads that occupy a parallel position in the text. Pico planned to collate these two series at Rome, not to validate either tradition independently but to use them as complementary symbolic vocabularies for a single correlative ontology. The magic theses, similarly, are not a separate province of Pico's thought but are grounded in the same hierarchical metaphysics: natural magic works by tapping cosmic correspondences between levels of reality, and Pico's claimed discovery of magic in the Orphic Hymns was a claim about the structural consonance of Orphic symbolism with his own philosophical system, not an endorsement of theurgic practice in the tradition he found in those hymns.

Farmer also challenges Frances Yates's influential account of Pico as completing a Ficinian Hermetic-magical synthesis. He points out a chronological problem that had been systematically overlooked: Ficino's *De vita coelitus comparanda*, his only explicitly magical work, was written roughly two and a half years *after* Pico published the *Conclusiones*. If there is an influence, it runs from Pico to Ficino rather than the reverse. Moreover, Farmer notes that Pico's lists of authoritative magicians in the *Oration* and *Apology* conspicuously omit Hermes Trismegistus—the figure Ficino placed at the center of Renaissance magic—because Pico closely associated Hermes with Ficino's circle and pointedly declined to endorse Ficino's interpretation of the Hermetic corpus as magical.

## Gianfrancesco and the Editorial Fraud

The fourth chapter of *Syncretism in the West* advances one of the most controversial claims in the book: that substantial portions of the later works attributed to Pico were heavily doctored—and in some cases substantially composed—by his nephew and editor, Gianfrancesco Pico, in collaboration with Giovanni Mainardi and with the tacit or active involvement of Savonarola and the Dominican community at San Marco in Florence, who controlled Pico's papers after his death in 1494.

Farmer's evidence for this argument is cumulative and circumstantial but extensive. Gianfrancesco rushed to Florence immediately after his uncle's death, gained access to Pico's unpublished manuscripts even though he was not named as heir, and by his own later admission possessed "perhaps three hundred chapters" of disordered material which he proposed to "polish" and publish, adding materials of his own. In a letter to Ercole Strozzi written in late 1496—the year the first collected edition of Pico's works appeared—Gianfrancesco boasted that he would give Pico's material "my form," supplement what was omitted, and "draw together what is diffuse," cementing the whole with "my glue." The 1496 *Opera omnia* conspicuously omitted the *Conclusiones* and the *Commento*, the two texts most centrally representative of the philosophical project of the Vatican debate, but included the *Disputationes against Divinatory Astrology*—the one work whose anti-syncretic, anti-magical stance aligned with Gianfrancesco's own Savonarolan commitments.

Farmer finds numerous errors in Gianfrancesco's *Vita* of his uncle—errors ranging from false chronologies and the suppression of Pico's humiliating flight to France, to the transformation of the vehemently anti-Thomistic Pico into a pious defender of St. Thomas—which he attributes partly to ignorance and partly to deliberate ideological falsification. He notes that the *Disputationes*, transcribed by Gianfrancesco and Mainardi from Pico's papers with remarkable speed in the weeks immediately after Pico's death, never once uses the word "Cabala," even though Pico's Cabalistic interests were a defining feature of the Vatican project; Farmer suspects Savonarolan editors removed references to Cabala from that text. More provocatively, he argues that the *Disputationes* as received can be read not simply as Pico's late anti-astrological polemic but as a text heavily shaped by posthumous editors in a direction calculated to make the dangerous syncretic magician of 1486 look like a proto-Savonarolan enemy of pagan wisdom. Lost works—most importantly the *Concord of Plato and Aristotle*, which was apparently near completion at Pico's death and which Gianfrancesco admitted to possessing large portions of—appear to have been systematically suppressed by an editor whose philosophical and religious commitments made him his uncle's inverse.

Farmer is careful to note that not all the changes in Pico's later thought need be assigned to editorial tampering; the *Disputationes* is a polemical text designed to exploit the conventions of academic disputation, and some of its apparent anti-syncretic tendencies may reflect genre rather than conversion. But the broader evidentiary picture he assembles supports the conclusion that the Pico known to posterity has been substantially shaped by an interested editorial hand, and that the recovery of Pico's actual thought requires constant critical attention to the circumstances of transmission.

## Reception and Place in the Field

*Syncretism in the West* has had an ambivalent reception. Its textual edition and translation are widely acknowledged as valuable contributions, and Farmer's structural observations about the organization of the *Conclusiones* have influenced subsequent scholarship. His challenge to Yates's Hermetic-Ficinian model for Pico's magic has been largely accepted. His cross-cultural comparative framework, however, and his ambitious claims about the systematic character of Pico's project, have prompted debate among scholars skeptical of totalizing readings of a text deliberately designed as a puzzle. The book's treatment of Gianfrancesco's editorial interventions, which requires the reader to entertain evidence of widespread literary fraud, has been noted with interest but remains contested.

The work remains indispensable for any serious study of the *Conclusiones*. Its importance lies not only in providing the first reliable Latin text and English translation of the nine hundred theses but in demonstrating, with close philological and analytical work, that the text rewards and indeed demands reading as a whole.
