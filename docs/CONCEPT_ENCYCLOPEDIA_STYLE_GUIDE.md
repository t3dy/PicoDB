# Concept Encyclopedia Style Guide

- Artifact ID: `concept_encyclopedia_style_guide_pass021`
- Status: ACTIVE

## Purpose

The concept encyclopedia turns PicoDB's reading artifacts into reference essays for the website. Every entry should be a 2,000–6,000 word academic encyclopedia article written in **continuous scholarly prose**. The essay defines the concept, maps it across Pico's works, identifies source traditions, summarizes scholarly debates, and draws on primary texts and scholarship.

## Format Requirements

**Prose, not bullet points.** Write as continuous paragraphs throughout. Do NOT use section headings (##, ###) within the essay body itself. The concept's definition, primary-text coverage, source genealogy, scholarly debate, and historiographical cautions should all emerge organically in flowing sentences, not as labeled sections with headers.

**No reading questions.** The essay should answer the relevant questions in its prose. Study questions, "open problems" sections, and "next reading tasks" are internal scaffolding and must not appear in final essays.

**No seed-template boilerplate.** Remove all language like "Future close reading should identify…", "This seed should be expanded…", "In PicoDB this concept is…". Write as if for an educated reader encountering the concept for the first time.

**YAML frontmatter only** for metadata: artifact_id, type, status, primary_pico_loci (list), guide_scholars (list), cross_references (list), word_count_approx.

## Content Coverage (embedded in prose, not as headed sections)

The essay should cover all of the following in its flowing paragraphs:
1. Definition: what the concept is, in Pico's vocabulary and in modern scholarly terms
2. Primary-text map: where and how the concept appears across Pico's corpus
3. Source genealogy: which traditions (Platonic, Aristotelian, scholastic, Kabbalistic, Hermetic, Arabic, etc.) supply Pico's formulation
4. Argument and function: what work the concept does in Pico's specific texts
5. Scholarly positions: what key scholars argue about this concept, including disagreements
6. Historiographical cautions: anachronism risks, reception distortions, editorial problems

## Citation Style

Cite scholars parenthetically: (Copenhaver 2019, 47) or name in sentence ("Farmer argues…"). Cite Pico's works by short titles (*Oration*, *900 Conclusions*, *Heptaplus*, *Apology*, *Commento*, *De ente et uno*). Quote Latin phrases when exact wording matters; always translate or gloss them.

## Quality Standard

The target register is the *Dictionary of Gnosis and Western Esotericism* or the *Cambridge History of Renaissance Philosophy* — authoritative, precise, readable by an educated non-specialist. The essay should stand as a self-contained reference that explains not just what a concept means but why it matters for reading Pico and what scholars have argued about it.

## Style

Write in an academic but readable encyclopedia style. Avoid promotional prose, vague Renaissance generalities, and unmarked speculation. Prefer precise distinctions: direct source versus mediated source, Pico's term versus modern label, doctrine versus practice, primary text versus reception, and secure claim versus interpretive hypothesis.
