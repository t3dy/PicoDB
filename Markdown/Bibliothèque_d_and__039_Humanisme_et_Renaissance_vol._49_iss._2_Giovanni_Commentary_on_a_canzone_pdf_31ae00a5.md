     In good  time for the five hundredth  anniversary  of  its composition,
Professor  Sears  Jayne has produced  an English   translation  of Giovanni
 Pico's   Commento,    buttressed  by   a   substantial   and  very  helpful
 introduction and  notes. He  has  chosen not  to reproduce  the Italian  text,
 assuming  that the interested reader will be referring constantly  to Eugenio
Garin's   edition, published  in 1942, departures from which  are explained  in
 the notes.  Although   Professor   Jayne makes   use  of  two manuscripts
 discovered  since Garin's   edition, he assures  the reader that they do not alter
 the text significantly. This   is the first complete English  translation  to be
 published;   the only other complete  translation, by Stephen  Salchenberger,
 is  in an  unpublished   doctoral   dissertation   (John Hopkins   University,
 1967).

    The  Introduction, which  occupies  the first quarter  of the book  relates
 the circumstances   in which  the Commento   was  composed,    traces  the
 history of themanuscripts  and  early published   texts, analyses  the subject
matter and  structure, and  explains  themethod  and  criteria adopted  by the
 translator. This   is all done very clearly, and  is accompanied  by thorough
 and helpful  notes. The  translation  itself occupies  some ninety pages,  and
 the accompanying  notes nearly as much  again. The  translator refers to the
 annotation   as ? heavy-handed  ?, but  readers  can only be grateful  for his
 scholarly  industry. It redresses the lack of any explanatory  notes  inGarin's
 edition. A  useful  bibliography  completes  the work. One   slip here  is the
 omission  of P.O.  Kristeller's  The Philosophy   ofMarsilio   Ficino,  while  the
 reviewer owes   it to his publisher  to point out  that his study of Pico was
 published   in Geneva,  not Toronto.
   One   especially  valuable   contribution   in the Introduction   is the clear
 analysis  of the different elements  in this very confusing work.   It was  not
 one work  but  three, masquerading    as  a  fourth.  It incorporated  elements
 intended for a Poetic  Theology,   a Commentary  on  the Symposium  and  a
Treatise  on Love,  and  represented  them as  a commentary  on  a canzone
which  Pico  had  Beni vieni  change  to  suit his purposes. A  point which
Professor  Jayne brings out clearly in both his Introduction and his notes  is
 that the Commento  was  a headlong  attack on the views of Marsilio   Ficino.
Pico  judged  Ficino's  De  amore wrong  in itsmethodology,    itsmythology
 and   its discussion  of  love. Much   of  the Commento     is taken up with
 expositions   of   classical  myths  which   Pico   says  Ficino   has   totally
misunderstood.   The  arrogance  shown  in Pico's  comments may  help  to
 explain  his success  inmaking  enemies  in Rome  in themonths  to follow. At
 the same  time,  the Commento   underlines   the depth  of  disagreement
between  Pico  and Ficino,  and shows again how wrong   is the conventional
 linking of the two as master  and  disciple  or as kindred  spirits within  the
Florentine  Platonic Academy.
   The  translation  itself follows  faithfully the principles  Professor  Jayne
 has  enunciated.  As  he  justly observes,  any  translation of the Commento
must  be an  interpretation, but he also  gives  the literal translation  in the
 notes. One  of his principles was  to translate  technical philosophical   terms
 literally, and one might wish  that he had adhered more  strictly to this with
 terms like ?natura?  and ?fine?. He  translates ?concilio  nostro?  (referring
 to Pico's  proposed  disputation   in Rome)   as ?my Council?,   and  refers to it
 also   in the  Introduction.  This,   surely,  is too  arrogant  even  for Pico;
 besides,  one did not talk lightly about summoning  a Council,   least of all in
Rome.
     In translating one notorious  sentence Professor  Jayne gives a less-than
 literal version and,  surprisingly, no explanatory  note or comment.  This   is
 the sentence  in which  Eugenio  Garin  found  an  approximation   to the
 ?double  truth? (Giovanni Pico  della Mirandola,    Florence,   1937, p. 28).  In

 created a multiplicity  of angels,  as Dionysius  and  the Christian  theologians
