         ASTROLOGY IN THE LOW COUNTRIES

The Limits of Influence: Pico, Louvain, and the Crisis of Renaissance Astrology.
   Steven Vanden Broecke (Brill, Leiden, 2003). Pp. xvi + 316 + 12 plates. €113/
   $153. ISBN 90-04-13169-8.
The limits of influence represents an important contribution to the history of early
modern astrology.  Its stated aim is to investigate the reasons behind the rise and
decline of astrology in the Renaissance by focusing on astrologers and astronomers
in the Low Countries. The volume opens with an introductory chapter setting the
background and providing key definitions, while the rest ofthe book traces the study
and practice of astrology from the founding of the University of Louvain in 1425 to
the end of the sixteenth century. Despite its focus on university culture, this book
offers much more than a narrow institutional history of universities. While covering
almost two hundred years of dense history, the author presents a well-documented,
insightful history of the interactions between Louvain's astrologers and their patrons in
the different contexts ofthe university, the town and northern European courts. What
emerges is a fascinating picture of the intellectual (and to a small extent, political)
life of the Low Countries and of astrology within this context.
  At the centre of the book is the reception of Pico della Mirandola's influential
Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem (1496) at Louvain, which Vanden
Broecke sees as the pervasive motivation behind the attempts to reform astrology by
Northern European astrologers. He argues convincingly that Pico's rejection of astrol-
ogy was motivated by the rise and popular success of conjunctionist astrology on the
model of Albumasar's De magnis coniunctionibus. Inthe wake of fifteenth-century
political unrest in Italy and in Europe and the unprecedented spate of conjunctionist
prognostications that accompanied it, such an hypothesis seems eminently plausible.
That Pice's intention was to reform astrology and not disprove it (as Vanden Broecke
seems to argue in chap. 3), however, seems more difficult to substantiate (especially
as Pico's publication was a posthumous affair), and indeed may not be so crucial for
his argument. What is significant, instead, is how sixteenth-century astrologers reacted
to Pico's work and what they did to lend renewed credit to their discipline.
   Like Pico, a number of Louvain's academic astrologers became increasingly keen
to distance themselves from the popular practice of conjunctionism. Using an interest-
ing series of examples, Vanden Broecke shows how various authors reacted to Pice's
critique, how they approached the problem of astrological reform, and what their
proposed solutions were. One approach was to embrace the study of other related
disciplines such as cosmography (Gemma Frisius and Gerard Mercator), cartography
(Mercator), and geography (Gemma and Mercator), while  still integrating certain
astrological topics and techniques within the teaching of these subjects. Related to this
approach was a wider reform of 'astrological physics', which Vanden Broecke defines

as the study of "astrological effects on earth independently of predictive interests" (p.
18). This approach led most academic astrologers to shun annual prognostications
and natal astrology in favour of mundane astronomy and astronomical observation
and calculation. As Cornelius Gemma stated in his Ephemerides (1561), "that which
concerns kings and the casual state of public matters, is no part of our art or inten-
tion" (p. 187). Embracing Copernicanism became another important way to reform
astrology. Copernican astronomy came thus to serve Louvain astrology by offering
a counterweight to Pico's critique of issues such as the inaccuracy of astronomical
measurements and the problem of planetary distances. Similarly, Gerard Mercator
attempted to reform judicial astrology by proposing new, more accurate astronomical
and astrological instruments (chap. 5), while John Dee made use of optical principles
to explain celestial influence (chap. 6).
  There is much to praise inVanden Broecke's book, not least its remarkable concen-
tration on the primary sources. The author examines a considerable body of under-
studied literature to unveil a complex network of intellectual exchange and patronage
support geared towards the promotion and the study of the 'science of the stars'.
The attention to lesser-known figures (less studied, but, as Vanden Broecke amply
demonstrates, not less interesting or significant) is indeed one of its most valuable
aspects. In reading this work one gets a vivid sense of sixteenth- and seventeenth-
century scientific culture, and not just of single major figures and their intellectual
advancements. The drawback of this approach, however, is that the reader gets often
only an impressionistic view of some of these people. This fact, however, hardly
diminishes the value of Vanden Broecke's excellent study: one certainly hopes that
it will become the essential starting point for further research on these figures.
  No book is perfect and this is no exception. Its flaws, however, are relatively minor
and, to some extent, unavoidable. Vanden Broecke's study covers much ground and
at times its author fails to draw some important conclusions from his results: for
instance, one may have hoped to see a chapter that drew more overt conclusions
about the relationship between scientific patronage and the reform of astrology, or a
deeper sense of the connections between the astrological interpretation of historical
events and their impact on society and politics. Given the book's breadth, this syn-
thesis will have to be done to a large extent by the reader interested in these issues.
Yet this is a small price to pay for the rich material examined here. To conclude,
The limits ofinfluence certainly deserves an important place in the present literature
on the history of astrology and it will become an essential reading for all scholars
interested in Renaissance astrology. Those not daunted by the breadth of its topic
and the complexities of the subject will find it enormously rewarding.
