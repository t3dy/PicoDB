      WING TO his desire for knowledge of Hebrew wisdom, Giovanni Pico della
         Mirandola sought out a small but diverse group of scholars who could acquaint
        him with  its language,  philosophy and methods  of biblical  exegesis, and with
kabbalah, which he considered to be as important a link in the chain of ancient theology as
the writings associated with, among others, Hermes Trismegistus,  Pythagoras, Orpheus
and Plato. The Hebrew scholars of greatest influence on Pico are thought  to have been
Elijah del Medigo,  Flavius Mithridates and Jochanan Alemanno.  This article will focus
upon the third and least known of these, and will attempt to present a clearer picture of
Alemanno and his relationship  to Pico than has hitherto been available.1
    In trying to piece together an account of Alemanno's  life, we must rely on statements
scattered  throughout  his writings; he remains  for the most part unmentioned  by his
contemporaries. Some caution, however, must be employed. There is first the caveatwhich
applies to material of this sort in any writer: pride and the passage of time can affect the
recollection  of events.  With Alemanno  in  particular  a second  problem  arises:  the
autobiographical  sketch in his Hjai ha-Olamim, (Eternal  Life), upon which we are most
dependent  for information,  is clearly modelled after the Ijayy Ibn Yaqzanof Abu Bakr Ibn
Tufayl:  events  in both take place in seven-year  cycles.2 This can do nothing  but cast
doubts on the veracity of Alemanno's  chronology.

and political events of the time. As a Jew, Alemanno was periodically  subject to certain
restrictions on where he could or could not live, study and work. As a migrant to Italy he

   The period from 1470 to 1488 remains a blank. Lack of surviving  literary output
suggests a period of travel, and Alemanno  does mention an encounter  in Bologna;16  it
may also be inferred from his writings  that he was in a Venetian  territory.17 But the
likelihood that he simply remained for some or all of this period in Mantua should not be
ruled out. He now had a family, which would have restricted his mobility.18  It is also
known that Judah  Messer Leon founded a studio in Mantua  in the second  half of the
fifteenth century, which included philosophy in its curriculum.19 He hired teachers for it,
and it is possible that Alemanno,  his former pupil, could have found employment  there.

    Pico's  acquaintance  with Hebrew  language  and  letters  did  not  begin  with  his
encounter with Alemanno. During his stay in Padua, 148o-82, he met Elijah del Medigo
 (c. 1460-93).28 Del Medigo's Judaism was of less interest to Pico than his knowledge  of
Averroes and his ability to translate into Latin certain works of the Islamic philosopher,
hitherto available only in Arabic and Hebrew. In Padua del Medigo translated portions of
the commentaries  on the Meterologia and the Metaphysics, and wrote two treatises on
Averroes's view of the intellect, at Pico's request. The two met again in Florence, 1484-85.
There del Medigo translated the Epitome ofPlato's Republic and ThreeQuestionson the Prior
Analytics, and wrote  lengthy  annotations  on the Middle Commentaryon the Physics. In
addition, a later commentary on De substantiaorbis resulted from discussions with Pico.29
Del Medigo was also known to other members of the Platonic Academy:  in a letter to
Domenico  Benivieni,  Ficino  recalls  their presence  at Pico's house  to attend  debates
between del Medigo and Abraham, aJewish doctor, and Flavius Mithridates, on whether
Old Testament  prophecies referred toJesus.30 Their final meeting took place in Perugia,
May 1486, after Pico's return from France and before his journey to Rome to attempt to
present  his nine hundred  theses. A letter written  in December  of that year from del
Medigo to Pico recalls that at Perugia they discussed logic, physics and metaphysics.31  It
was  only  in  this  letter  that  specifically  Jewish  topics  became  the  subject  of their
interchange. After discussing Averroes's view on being and unity, and Islamic writings on
logic and physics,  del Medigo  explained  that Neoplatonic  thought was the basis  of
kabbalah, but that Aristotelian-Averroistic  conceptions presented a more valid cosmolo-
gical  picture.  Del Medigo  had elsewhere  expressed  his preference  for Aristotle  over
Plato,32  and had been  critical  of kabbalistic  thinkers.33 Nevertheless,  presumably  in
deference to Pico, he then listed the major kabbalistic works, including the Zohar, the Sefer

Flavius Guillelmus Ramundus  Mithridates and Guilelmus  Siculus; Reuchlin knew him

    Mithridates was a convert to Christianity,  the son of a learned Sicilian Jew. He was a
man of some learning, as was attested to by Rudolph Agricola, who called him
a man very knowledgeable in all languages, in Latin, Greek, Hebrew, Chaldaean and Arabic...  in
addition a theologian, philosopher and poet...  36
Mithridates was in Rome from 1477 until he had to flee the city for some unspecified crime
in I483.37 For some of this period he was a lecturer in theology at the Sapienza. On Good

Raymund  Martini's  Pugio Fidei.39 In  1484-85  Mithridates  was  in Germany,  at the

For after a whole month of days and wakeful nights with the Hebrew language, I have devoted

    It has been suggested that Alemanno was more stable both as a person and as a
scholar than either del Medigo or Mithridates, and that he moderated in Pico some of the
extremist positions that the latter may have acquired from them.s50 These conclusions
must have been drawn from the details of Pico's life: during the period of his acquaintance
with his first two teachers he was involved in disputes with the Church over some of his
Conclusiones,had to flee to France, and wrote his similarly controversialApologia;whereas
during the six years he knew Alemanno he lived for the most part a withdrawn and
increasingly pious life in Florence. They cannot have been drawn from anything we know
about the relationship between Pico and Alemanno, for unfortunately we have no direct
evidence of this sort. Nor do the details of Alemanno's nomadic life support a conception
of him as 'stable', although it is true that as far as we know he was not involved in any
raging controversies. Whether he was more psychologically stable than either del Medigo
or Mithridates is of course impossible to know, but it is worth noting that his own
description of his physical ailments and difficulties with peoples51might suggest some-
thing less than complete emotional harmony. In any event it is perhaps wiser to base a
discussion of the relationship of Pico and Alemanno on more solid evidence, scanty as it
may be.
   At the beginning of the Shir ha-Ma'alotli-ShlomoAlemanno describes what was
presumably his first meeting with Pico.s2 He had come to Florence in the Hebrew year
5248, or 1488. The virtues of the Florentines, which he found to be seven in number,s3 led

Florentine Platonism, nor should conclusions about one be drawn from the other. These
