  The  papers  vary  as  to  language.   In Volume     I,  two  are  in English

  The  durableness  and universality   of Pico's   reputation  was   reflected   in
  the  meeting's        large    attendance        of    foreigners       as  well     as    Italians.     The
  titles  of  the papers   indicate  the  influence  of  his works.   For   the non
  specialist    who     senses     Pico's     continuous         significance,         these    volumes,     on
 the whole,   represent  much   of  the  best   international   Pico   scholarship
 brought  up  to date. The   largely dominant  themes  are Pico's  900 Theses
 and  their prefatory  Oration  on  the Dignity   of Man;   his  thoughts  on  the
 universality   of  truth  in the great   religions  and philosophical    traditions;
 his work  against  astrological    foretelling;   his  emphasis  on man's  freedom
 to choose  his  destiny;   his   interest   in the Cabbala;    less prominent,   but
 sometimes  considered  most   important,  are his philosophy   of  love and his
 concern   to  interiorize   religion.  Provoking    the most   thought  of  this  re?
 viewer were   : Miss  Yates's    identification   of Pico's man  of  dignity  as  the
magus,  and Garin's  and Kristelier's   divergent   definitions   of  the Renais?
 sance humanist. Was   Pico  a philosopher  qua humanist   (Garin),   or was
 he a humanist who was  also a philosopher    (Kristeller).   The  distinction
   is more     than    verbal.      Kristelier's         view,      in  any    case,     leads    to  a   sharper
 distinction   between   the  humanist's    profession   and   the  philosopher's
 concern.          -  _

   Taking    full advantage   of  the numerous   studies  of Heinrich   Cornelius
Agrippa   von Nettesheim     (1468-1535),    his  thought,  and  his  age which
 have   appeared       in  this   century,     Professor      Nauert     has   written     an   interest?
  ing and  exhaustive   study  of  this  legendary  Renaissance  man.  This  new
 biography   far exceeds  the earlier,  standard  biographies  by Henry  Morley
 and Auguste   Prost.    It should  remain  the definitive   study  for some  time.
 By  carefully   delineating   the mind  of Agrippa,    this book  rightly  empha?
  sizes     that    the   Renaissance       was     not     just    a   period      of   optimism,       con?
  fidence,    and   "rebirth,"     but   also   one   of   pessimism,         fear,   and   complaint.
