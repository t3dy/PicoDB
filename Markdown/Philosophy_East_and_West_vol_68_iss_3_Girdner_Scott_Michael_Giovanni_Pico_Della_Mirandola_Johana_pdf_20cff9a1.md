Introduction and Summary of Previous Article

This is the second of two articles describing the inﬂuence of Abū Ḥāmid
Muḥammad  al-Ghazālī  (Ghazali,  d. 1111 C.E.) on Giovanni  Pico  della
Mirandola  (d.  1494). The  ﬁrst  article  critically analyzed common and
contrasting images of Ghazali, often presented as the religiously motivated
destroyer of philosophy in Islamic  traditions,1 and Pico della Mirandola
(Pico, d. 1494), who is romantically associated with philosophy’s “rebirth”
in the Italian Renaissance.2 In fact, both Pico della Mirandola and Ghazali
attempted  to create a coherent  synthesis  of philosophical and  religious
tradition; and the ﬁrst article demonstrated that Ghazali actually inﬂuenced
Pico  della  Mirandola  in  this  respect.  Supporting  Craig  Truglia’s  prior
conclusion  that Ghazali was an important source  for  Pico,  the  article
examined manuscripts of Hebrew translations of Ghazali’s The Niche of
Lights (Mishkāt al-Anwār) and illustrated their reception and transmission by
Joḥanan Alemanno (d. after 1504), an associate of Pico della Mirandola.3
The article also discussed conceptions of human dignity shared by Ghazali,
Alemanno, and Pico della Mirandola as part of a larger project that seeks to
understand both how Ghazali could have inﬂuenced Pico della Mirandola,
as well as the nature of such inﬂuence.
    This article continues the project of understanding Ghazali’s inﬂuence
by examining additional conceptual parallels in the thought of Alemanno
and Pico della Mirandola. In particular,  this  article describes a two-fold
conception of human felicity that includes both the intellectual actualization
that was the aim of human life in philosophical tradition, as well as a more
mysterious felicity attained in mystical religious experiences. The article also
explores a  shared  treatment  of a  perceived  philosophical dilemma  in
traditional religious understandings of divine love for humans. The Niche of
Lights  remains  as an  inﬂuential  source  for Alemanno and  Pico  della
Mirandola in this article, but the article will also argue, supporting a prior
suggestion by Moshe Idel, that The Book of Love (Kitāb al-Maḥabbah) from
Ghazali’s  Revival  of  the  Religious Sciences  (Iḥyāʾ ʿUlūm  al-Dīn)  is an
important source  for Alemanno and Pico della Mirandola as well. Both
articles conclude that Ghazali inﬂuenced Giovanni Pico della Mirandola

     Human Felicity

         Felicity means the telos, aim, or end of human existence; it is our ﬁnal cause,
        the purpose for which we exist. Ghazali, Giovanni Pico della Mirandola, and
       Johanan Alemanno shared a conception of human felicity that was related to
         their conception of humanity’s variable nature, discussed in the previous
         article. Humans might actualize their physical vegetative and bestial capa-
          cities, but these do not lead to the happiness that is our true aim and end. For
        that happiness or felicity, our ﬁgures agree on two broad possibilities. One is
        the  philosophical  actualization  of  the  intellect. The  other  is a  mystical
        identiﬁcation with God, expressed by Pico and Ghazali in On Human Dignity
      and The Niche of Lights as contemplation of the face of God.4
           Ghazali, Alemanno, and Pico della Mirandola received the neoplatonic
        Aristotelian tradition as  it had been elaborated by ﬁgures such as al-Farabi
      and Ibn  Sina. Avoiding the many  details and  subtle  variations, human
        existence in this tradition begins and ends with the Active Intellect, who is
        the last in a series of cosmic intellects that emanate from God. The Active
         Intellect  gives  form  to  material  existents  in  the  terrestrial  realm and
        actualizes the human potential to know. Such intellectual actualization  is
        the true aim of human life as the rational animal; and  it was expressed as
       conjunction with the Active Intellect in the philosophical worldview. In this
       way, the Active Intellect both originates human life, by informing its material
        existence, and serves as the aim of human life that is the felicity of human
        intellectual actualization and identiﬁcation with the Active Intellect.
           Ghazali, Alemanno, and Pico della Mirandola adapt and modify this
        philosophical tradition, but largely accept its conception of human felicity;
        that is, they do not exclude intellectual actualization from their conception
        of  felicity, but they do add to  it.5 They assert that the human ascent to
         felicity reaches greater heights, beyond the intellectual actualization and
        identiﬁcation with the Active Intellect that is the aim of the philosophers. In
       doing so, they create space for conceptions of the aim and end of human
          life  that can be more  readily  reconciled with  traditional and  mystical
        religious doctrines. Signiﬁcantly, Pico makes an explicit reference to Islamic
        tradition as he describes the two levels of  felicity, marked below, in his
       Heptaplus:

           There is, as the theologians assert, [1] one felicity which we can attain through
            nature and  [2] another which we can  attain through grace   . . .  although
              different philosophers hold different opinions, nevertheless all have kept within

the narrow bounds of human capacity, limiting the felicity of man either to the
   mere [1.a] search of  truth, as the Academics do, or to [1.b]  its attainment
    through the study of philosophy, as Al-Farabi said. [1.c] Avicenna, Abu Bakr
    (Ibn  T. ufayl), Alexander, and the  Platonists seem to allow somewhat more,
    basing our reason   . . . on the active intellect or on some greater one which is
    nevertheless related to us; but they lead man neither to his beginning, nor end.
    [2] The true and perfect felicity, however, carries us back to the contemplation
    of the face of God   . . . and leads us to perfect union with the beginning from
   which we sprang  . . .  to this level man cannot go, but can be drawn.6

The highest felicity leads to an apparently mystical unitive experience of the
divine that is expressed in terms of grace, a return to the source, and the
contemplation of God’s face [2, above]. In  this scheme, the intellectual
actualization of the philosophers accounts for a lower “natural” felicity [1,
above]. Pico subdivides the lower felicity according to various degrees of
intellectual actualization: some identify  it with the search for intellectual
truth [1.a]; others identify  it with the attainment of truth [1.b]; and others
still identify this felicity as conjunction with the Active Intellect, or another
cosmic intellect [1.c]. Generally, though, the philosophers present intellec-
tual actualization as the goal and identiﬁcation with the Active Intellect, or
another cosmic intellect, as the highest attainment.
    Similarly, one may readily discern the two general categories of felicity,
philosophical and mystical, organizing Alemanno’s presentation of felicity,
even as he enumerates his conception of human felicity in a variety of ways
that display his Kabbalistic preference for the number seven. For example, in
the ﬁnal chapter of The Song of Solomon’s Ascent, Alemanno identiﬁes the
ultimate good for humans ﬁrst in terms of attachment to the Active Intellect,
which philosophy  facilitates; but then he identiﬁes  felicity with a higher
form of attachment to the divine attributes (Seﬁrot) of Kabbalah such as
Divine Presence (Shekhina), Kingship (Malkhut), and Adornment  (Tiferet).
Intellectual actualization and attachment to the Active Intellect are clearly
part of felicity for Alemanno, but mystical and religious practice, and not
intellectual actualization, facilitates the highest attainment, which is attach-
ment to the divine attributes. Alemanno says that such an attachment  is
impossible “except through knowledge of the secrets of the Torah and the
performance of its commandments.”7
  A few passages stand out as representative of Ghazali’s conception of
two levels of felicity, when we restrain our analysis in order to focus on The
Niche of Lights.8 Ghazali presents the felicity of the philosophers, which is
intellectual actualization, as both an exalted and penultimate end. As with
Alemanno and Pico, the highest aim is a felicity that Ghazali consistently
associates  with  the  contemplation  of God’s  face,  as  well  as  mystical
practices and states that are additional to such philosophical  felicity. For
example, consider a passage from The Niche  of  Lights  that obviously
interested Alemanno, as he copied the Hebrew  translation  of a related

     One may experience the truth of God’s necessary existence, expressed here as
        seeing nothing but God, in two ways: the ﬁrst way is philosophy; it entails the
       experience of  realizing God’s necessary existence by means of discursive
        reasoning, or “cognitive knowledge.” The second, and more complete, way of
      knowing is religious mysticism.11 Ghazali routinely expresses this mystical state
        in terms of the experiential knowledge of “tasting” (dhawq) in the Niche of Lights
      and other works. At this point in The Niche of Lights, after introducing this
        mystical way of knowing, Ghazali turns to a lengthy elaboration of the proper
       understanding of such “tasting” by mystics absorbed in an ecstatic state known
        as extinction (fanā’) in Suﬁtradition.12 He associates this state with the highest
          felicity; it is an experience of the dissolution of the self in the act of contemplating
      God.  Subjectively,  the  mystic may  experience and  express  this  state  as
        uniﬁcation with God, but Ghazali rejects such actual identiﬁcation of God and
        creature as both unorthodox and logically impossible.13
          Ghazali returns to a consideration of two levels of attainment at the climax
        of The Niche of Lights in the third chapter. He situates the two degrees of
         felicity relative to other philosophical and theological attempts to contemplate
      God. The precise meaning of this section of The Niche of Lights has long been
        controversial, in terms of some of the detailed allusions to various philosophi-
        cal and religious traditions.14 Regardless of such controversy, it is clear that
       Ghazali outlines a hierarchy of ways to know or contemplate God, which is
        the aim of human life that is felicity. Most attempts to attain felicity fall short.
       Ghazali depicts these ways of knowing as veiled from true contemplation of
       God’s face: those who take their appetites as their aim do not progress beyond
         their  bestial nature; those who worship  idols have  fallen prey  to  their
       imaginative  faculty. Many  are  veiled, but Ghazali organizes them  into
        categories that correspond to the various faculties described by the philosophi-
        cal psychology of his time, which he outlines earlier in The Niche of Lights.15
       “Those who arrive” at the true contemplation of God’s face, which is human
          felicity,  fall into the two categories we encountered above: some achieve
        intellectual actualization, while others achieve something greater that Ghazali
       identiﬁed with a mystical-prophetic faculty that he added to his discussion of
        the philosophical psychology;  this  faculty accounts  for the existence  of
       prophecy and the mystic’s experience of mystical states.

As  in  Pico’s  discussion  of  philosophical  felicity  after him, Ghazali
distinguishes  between  philosophical  and  mystical  felicity,  while  also
distinguishing some subcategories within philosophical felicity according to
various schools. Though many scholars have apparently missed the allusion
and its signiﬁcance, the most important distinction is between the Peripate-
tics and the school of Ibn Sina, which Ghazali indicates by reference to
famous arguments associated with these schools. Ghazali alludes to the
Peripatetics by describing those who understand kinematic arguments for
God as the cause of motion in the cosmos, as with Aristotle’s unmoved
mover. He situates this Aristotelian approach beneath Ibn Sina’s conception
of God as the necessary existent, identifying it as superior to the concept of
the unmoved mover  for two  reasons.  First,   it  is  superior  because   it
establishes God as the cause of all existence and not simply  all motion.
Second, Ibn Sina’s argument is superior because God’s necessary existence
is subject to conﬁrmation in mystical experience.16 The Niche of Lights
repeatedly describes such mystical conﬁrmation as the contemplation of
God’s face.  It is the highest felicity and Ghazali explicitly identiﬁes  it with
the mystical state of extinction and the mysteries of divine revelation, while
situating it beyond the reach of human reasoning and philosophy.17
    Ghazali, Alemanno, and Pico della Mirandola each expressed a concep-
tion of two types of felicity. Ghazali and Pico della Mirandola both contrasted
the end attained by various philosophical schools with a mystical felicity that
they expressed in terms of the contemplation of the divine face and unitive
experiences. In this typology, they promote and subordinate philosophy by
identifying  it as the penultimate  felicity. Ghazali emphasizes this two-fold
conception of felicity in The Niche of Lights, as did Ibn Tufayl in Hayy ibn
Yaqzan, following him.  It was clearly one of the aspects of The Niche of
Lights that interested Alemanno: he copied a closely related prior section in
his notebook, during the period of his association with Pico della Mirandola.
Pico could have known the two types of felicity from Alemanno, or their
shared study of Ibn Tufayl, or both. However, the resemblance of Pico della
Mirandola’s presentation of two types of felicity in his Heptaplus to Ghazali’s
discussion at the end of The Niche of Lights suggests that Pico knew the work
directly. Both  broadly  contrast  philosophical and  mystical  felicity;  both
subdivide  philosophical  conceptions  according  to  distinctions  between
Aristotelians and the more neoplatonic school of Ibn Sina; and both depict
the highest felicity as contemplation of the face of God, while specifying that
such a felicity is attained by divine grace and not human reasoning.18

suggested that Pico della Mirandola and Johanan Alemanno were inﬂuenced

by Ghazali. Idel noted a signiﬁcant adaptation that Alemanno and Pico della
       Mirandola made to the thought of the Andalusian philosopher Ibn al-Sayyid
        al-Bat.alyawsī (Batalyawsi, d. 1127), author of The Book of Circles (Kitāb al-
        Hadā’iq).19 Idel brieﬂy noted that this adaptation of Batalyawsi by Alemanno
      and Pico della Mirandola was inﬂuenced by Ghazali:

            Like Pico, Alemanno believes in a circle stemming from God and ending in
           him, which can be completed by man ascending to the source. Alemanno
            explains the force which causes this movement from God and back to Him, by
            the Divine Love. This is an addition to [Batalyawsi] based on a quotation from
            al-Ghazzali [ . . . ]20

      The passage makes general reference  to  theological adaptations  of  the
       neoplatonic cosmology  in which human existence emanates from God,
       through the mediation of the cosmic intellects, and returns back. In terms of
        the two felicities above, the passage describes the second felicity that moves
      beyond conjunction with the Active Intellect towards a unitive experience
      and the contemplation of the face of God. In the passage cited above, Idel
         asserts that a quotation by Ghazali was the source of the adaptation of
       Batalyawsi by Alemanno and Pico della Mirandola. Unfortunately, Idel did
       not develop his analysis further; and he did not identify a source for the
       quote beyond suggesting an examination of The Book of Love in Ghazali’s
       Revival of the Religious Sciences: this section of this article provides an
        analysis of that work in support of Idel’s conclusion that Ghazali was an
       inﬂuence for Alemanno and Pico della Mirandola.
         The passage to which Idel refers, regarding Ghazali as a source for Pico
      and Alemanno, occurs in Alemanno’s The Song of Solomon’s Ascents:

            but the divine spirit circulates around and around, from Him and toward Him,
            as is well explained in [Batalyawsi’s] Book of the Imaginary Circles  ( . . . ) the
            divine desire is the desire which the supernal [entity] has for the one emanated
           from it. For just as the lower desires of the supernal that the latter will emanate
                  its good upon him, so also the supernal desires to emanate upon the lower as
         Abu Hamid [Ghazali] said: “in reality, a part [of it] desires the other part. The
           lower desires the supernal so as to become similar to  it, and the supernal
             desires the lower so as to emanate upon it and see its perfection within it.”21

      Alemanno deploys  this apparent quotation from Ghazali to characterize
        divine love as the motive force in the emanation of the cosmos, elaborating
       a reciprocal desire between hierarchic levels of the emanating cosmos.
         The fact that the above quote describes a reciprocal desire between
         different levels of the cosmos is important. According to the philosophical
       cosmology, for example, the heavenly souls move the heavenly bodies in
       accordance with the Aristotelian conception of ﬁnal causation: the heavenly
        souls desire to be perfectly actualized, like the intellects above them; so
       they imitate them to the best of their ability, moving the heavenly bodies in

seemingly eternal, perfectly circular, motions. The normative philosophical
position  is that the higher levels of reality cannot desire anything below
them. In terms of the quote, the motion of the heavens  is caused by the
lower’s desire for the higher. As more fully actualized beings, God and the
heavenly  intellects do not await any temporal developments  for  further
actualization, such as the fulﬁlment of an earthly temporal desire would
necessitate. From this perspective, the superior beings do not desire anything
beneath  them;  their  desires  are  already  fully  actualized and  fulﬁlled.
Alemanno associates this cosmology with Batalyawsi’s philosophy with his
citation  of The Book  of  Circles; however, he adapts the philosophical
position by describing higher beings as desiring the lower beings that they
emanate. This is the novel, or adapted position, that Alemanno attributes to
Ghazali in the above quotation.
   Of course, strict religious traditionalists reject the philosophical cosmol-
ogy on the basis of its plain contradiction of scriptural descriptions of God,
and other celestial beings such as angels, desiring speciﬁc terrestrial events.
In fact, Ghazali incorporates this traditionalist concern in his critique of the
philosophers in The Incoherence of the Philosophers. Ghazali argues against
the  philosophical  conception  of  creation  as a  process  of  the cosmos
necessarily emanating from a God conceived of as too transcendent to love
creatures,  or  will  speciﬁc  events  in  time. Ghazali  asserts,  against  the
philosophers, that God must be characterized as willing temporal events in
order to be truly an agent of creation.22
   Across his works, Ghazali seems to accept, and even promote, aspects
of the emanationist cosmology, while making alterations to the philosophical
presentation of the doctrine, in order to preserve space for more traditional
understandings  of God’s  attributes, such  as knowledge,  will, and  love.
Similarly,  the above quote from Alemanno  cites Ghazali and employs
philosophical concepts such as emanation and actualization, while present-
ing these in terms of a mutuality of desire that the philosophers reject and
the more  traditionally  religious advocate. The “supernal”  desire  for the
lower, described in the quote, reﬂects Ghazali’s adaptation of the philoso-
phical conception of emanation, presenting  it as compatible with a divine
agent who wishes and desires speciﬁc terrestrial events.23
   The passage  citing Ghazali and Batalyawsi occurs  in the context  of
Alemanno’s discussion of passionate love (ḥesheq) in The Song of Solomon’s
Ascents. We shall see that  it derives from Ghazali’s discussion of passionate
love (‘ishq). Alemanno asserts the reality of mutual love between lower and
higher beings, but recognizes the philosophical difﬁculty this position entails:

   we cannot deny the existence of this mutual passionate love, even though we
    cannot understand its essence because of the extreme opposition between our
    nature and the uniqueness  of God. This  is  like what  Aristotle says about
    affection between the beautiful and the ugly: although the ugly one may love

the beautiful one only for his beauty, the beautiful one will not love the ugly
          one because  of  his  ugliness.  Just  so, although we love God  passionately
           because of his perfection, He will not love us because of our imperfection.24

      Alemanno emphasizes that any attribution of a quality, such as passionate
        love,  to  both God and humans  is  ultimately  a  mystery:  this  is  the
         traditionalist position that asserts that such mysteries may be understood
       only imperfectly and by means of  revelation. However, Alemanno also
       engages philosophy, explaining the philosophical impropriety of attributing
       mutual love to God and creatures, when the objects of God’s love are ugly
      and imperfect relative to the divine. Indeed, Alemanno goes on to develop a
        philosophical argument that he employs to support the traditionalist position
        that God does love imperfect and ugly creatures:

          Between the extremes of difference between God and us  is a middle term.
              Aristotle said that every affection between those who are different is due to a
         common  quality which each one  loves  ( . . . )  Let us  say,  then,  that  the
         common or median quality in the passionate love between God and us, his
             opposites, is the good. The good for man is not to become absolutely separate
           from matter, but only this way to a degree; and the good for God is not, perish
            the thought,  that He become material, but only  that  his overﬂow become
            attached  to matter  in an attachment  that  perfects  the ﬂaws  in everything
            outside of Him.25

       Creatures love God as the good that all things seek as their ultimate end and
       purpose. In contrast, God also loves the good, which is both the divine self
      and the end of all creatures. In terms of the prior quote, God “desires the
       lower [creature] so as to emanate upon [it] and see (His) perfection within
          it.”26 In terms of the above quote, God loves the good that is, again, both
        his self and the “overﬂow” from him that “perfects the ﬂaws in everything
        outside of Him.”27 While offering this philosophical resolution, Alemanno
        also asserts the traditionalist position that one cannot deny divine love for
       humanity,  because  scripture  plainly  asserts   it.  Whatever  philosophical
        difﬁculties divine love for humanity entails, Alemanno  is characteristically
       conﬁdent that these can be resolved based on both the testimony of the
       prophets in scripture and a keen understanding of Aristotle.
          These passages from Alemanno have interesting parallels in Ghazali’s
       discussion of passionate love and desire (ʿishq, shawq) in The Book of
      Love (Kitāb al-Maḥabbah), in his Revival of the Religious Sciences. Both
       Ghazali and Alemanno address the difﬁculty of attributing love to God,
        identifying essentially the same problem as the cause of this difﬁculty.28
      They both are concerned as well with reconciling any philosophical
        difﬁculty with prophetic authority. Most signiﬁcantly, they both present
      God’s love of creatures in terms of love for the good by both creatures
      and God and some mysterious and perfecting identiﬁcation of the creature
       with God.

Ghazali begins his book on love and desire by mentioning that there are
those among the scholars (ʿulamāʾ) who deny that God loves creatures,
because love can only exist among members of the same species.29 This
comment points to the same problem that is Alemanno’s dilemma regarding
God’s love of creatures.30 However, as is typical for the popular audience of
this work, Ghazali does not further articulate the problem of the impossi-
bility  of  love between God and humans  with a  formal  philosophical
exposition. Rather, he asserts the prophetic authority of passages from the
Qur’an and  various  sayings  of  the prophet Muhammad  (ḥadīths).  Like
Alemanno, he dismisses the possibility of this issue as a genuine philosophi-
cal dilemma partly on the basis of the authority of tradition. Ghazali uses
the rest of his discussion to show what the real understanding of love should
be. Of particular interest for us is the section entitled “On the love of God
for the servant and its meaning.” There, Ghazali explains how only God is
the most worthy object of love for both himself and humans. Like Alemanno
after him, Ghazali explains how God’s love of creatures can be understood
as God’s love for himself as the good that creatures seek.
   To identify God’s love of the creature with God’s love of himself as the
good, Ghazali quotes the famous divine saying (ḥadīth qudsī)  that: “the
servant continues to draw near Me [God] by supererogatory acts, until I love
him; and when I love him I am his hearing by which he hears, and his sight
by which he sees.”31 Ghazali takes care to reject unorthodox interpretations
that understand the saying in terms of divine incarnation, or actual identity
between humans and God. However, he does ﬁnd an allusion to the Suﬁ
state of extinction in the enigmatic language of the saying.  It asserts that
when God loves the servant he becomes the servant’s hearing and sight.
God’s love of the servant corresponds to God’s qualiﬁed identiﬁcation with
the servant. In a paraphrase of the language that Alemanno attributes to
Ghazali above, “the supernal God loves the lower creature so as to emanate
upon the creature and see His perfect hearing and sight within the lower
creature’s hearing and sight.” Paraphrasing Alemanno’s own language, God
loves the good that is both his self and that which “perfects the ﬂaws in
everything outside of Him” including the servant’s hearing and sight.32
    In  this section, Ghazali further elaborates God’s love  for humans in

   Alemanno makes reference to the thought of Ghazali in the place where

provide detail and support to Moshe Idel’s basic observation: Ghazali was
an important source for Johanan Alemanno and Pico della Mirandola. This
is particularly the case with respect to an adaptation they made to the
philosophy of Batalyawsi, regarding the philosophical dilemma of divine-
human love. This adaptation entails a break from the normative philosophi-
cal position  that, nonetheless, employs philosophical content in favor of
more traditionalist understandings of God and scripture. Ghazali, Alemanno,
and Pico della Mirandola share a common sympathy with this approach.
Idel describes the conception of divine-human love by Pico della Mirandola
and Johanan Alemanno  as an  important  part  of  their  legacy. Ghazali
contributed to that legacy. Indeed, as we have seen, Ghazali’s contribution
to the philosophy of Pico della Mirandola and Johanan Alemanno  is not
limited to just this engagement with the philosophical dilemma of divine-
human love.

Conclusion: Ghazali’s inﬂuence on Pico della Mirandola and Alemanno

Ghazali inﬂuenced Giovanni Pico della Mirandola and Johanan Alemanno
in a variety of ways. For example, in the ﬁrst article we saw that Ghazali
articulated inﬂuential conceptions of the variability of human nature. Indeed,
Pico della Mirandola expresses the dignity that interests him in his famous
Oration on Human Dignity in terms of humanity’s uniquely variable nature.
Pico della Mirandola remains famous for this conception of human dignity
that was inﬂuenced, by Pico’s own admission, by the writings of the Arabs.
We have seen that Ghazali’s works, and The Niche of Lights in particular,
are among such inﬂuential Arabic writings. In support of Craig Truglia’s
prior conclusion, we saw in the ﬁrst article that Pico della Mirandola and
Johanan Alemanno knew The Niche of Lights from its Hebrew translations.
   Among the various attributions and quotations from Ghazali found in
Alemanno’s works and notebooks, we found a passage from The Niche of
Lights related to Ghazali’s presentation of a two-fold conception of human
felicity discussed above. This two-fold felicity includes both the philosophi-
cal felicity of intellectual actualization, as well as a higher felicity associated
with the contemplation of God’s face and religious mysticism. As observed
above, this two-fold conception of felicity later expressed by Alemanno and
Pico della Mirandola shows the inﬂuence of this passage from Ghazali’s The
Niche of Lights. In addition, as suggested by Moshe Idel, we have seen that
Ghazali’s synthesis of philosophical and mystical interpretations of scripture

       both philosophical and religious traditions. In Ghazali, Pico della Mirandola
      and Johanan Alemanno found a profound resource for such reconciliation.
        One still encounters—too often, unfortunately—depictions of Ghazali as a
       dogmatic enemy of philosophy tout court, as well as depictions of Pico della
       Mirandola as a heroic champion of modern ideals against an obsolete religious

       philosophy in the Muslim world; but he did contribute something to philosophy’s
         rebirth in the West through the inﬂuence of his synthesis of philosophy and
