is presented as the golden-haired Platonist, passing leisurely hours with his Floren-
tine friends, in another as "the marvel-mongering Cabbalist feverishly searching
after hidden meanings in ancient Oriental manuscripts"; and by a third group "as
the author of an original idealist philosophy, expressive of the Renaissance and em-
bodying the germs of Hegelianism." Mr. DULLES does not hold with any of these
three groups. He feels that the true nature of Pico's doctrine has been largely ob-
scured in the controversies of the above three schools of thought, all of which have
failed to note Pico's essential dependence upon and agreement with the main lines
of medieval scholastic philosphy. However, it must be stated that in thus classifying
the works of "modern writers" on PIco, Mr. DULLEShimself obscures the fact that
none of the schools or categories that he presents account for other recent works on
PIco which he himself has utilized. For example the present reviewer is at a loss to
see how one can fit into this system of classification the writer, EUGENIOGARIN,
who, Mr. DULLESstates (Appendix p. 171), "has written by far the most complete
and scholarly study of Pico that has yet appeared." According to Mr. DULLEStoo,
GARINis said to have seen "Pico as a representative of the scholastic movement"
(p. 171). Nor do any of the categories appear to apply to EUGENIOANAGNINE,whom
Mr. DULLES commends "for recognizing that PIco had some affiliations with
scholastic thought" (p. 172), and "who asserts that scholasticism, Neo-Platonism,
and Cabbalism were all constant characters of Pico's philosophy" (p. 12). One might
further question where to place Professor THORNDIKE'Scharacterization of PIco as
"more of a scholastic disputant than a humanist," contained in the opening para-
graph of the first of his two chapters on Pico in the History of Magic and Experi-
mental Science (1934, IV, 485). Also my survey of Pico's intellectual interests as re-
flected in his library can surely not be put into any of the above categories. In the
chapter on Latin books (Libraryof Pico della Mirandola, 1936, Chap. VI and passim),
 it is pointed out that the books in the library bear out the preference expressed in
Pico's own writings for the general content and main currents of medieval scholastic
thought.  It  is difficult therefore to understand why Mr. DULLESshould seek to
convey the impression (for so he does imply) that he is the first to break with cur-
rent traditions which hail Pico as a precursor of modern thought and philosophy.
Mr. DULLES'specific contribution lies in the detailed analysis of Pico's philosophical
doctrines as developed in his writings. But the conclusions are not new. They were
clearly presaged in the works of Mr. DULLES' predecessors. The results of the
present investigation merely serve to substantiate or corroborate the conclusions of
previous works which have utilized other avenues of approach to the subject. Mr.
DULLES'assertions would have borne considerably more weight if he had acknowl-
edged this fact.
