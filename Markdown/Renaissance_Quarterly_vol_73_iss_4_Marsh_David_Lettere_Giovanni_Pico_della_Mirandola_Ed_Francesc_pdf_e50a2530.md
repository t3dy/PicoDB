       This critical edition may surprise the reader for two reasons: ﬁrst, its modest dimensions
        (seventy-four letters, ﬁlling less than a hundred pages [81–171]), and second, its long
         gestation. As Francesco Borghesi points out in his introduction (3–4), the project of
        editing Pico’s letters dates back to 1939, when Alessandro Perosa made a list of
         forty-three humanists whose correspondence merited publication. Borghesi notes that
        there are only two signiﬁcant witnesses for the collection: the Vatican codex Capponi
       235, a miscellany of nearly two hundred humanist epistles, and the editio princeps of
         Pico’s works (Bologna, 1496), edited by his nephew Giovan Francesco Pico. The vol-
      ume concludes with a bibliography, an index of names, and an index of correspondents.
        An edition of 121 letters is already available in the complete works of Pico compiled
      on CD-ROM by Francesco Bausi (46). Though not a critical edition with apparatus, it

       Complete in the English edition of the Collected Works of Erasmus (CWE) since 2016,
        the Paraphrases on the New Testament had been stalled in the Amsterdam (ASD) edition
        of the Opera Omnia since 1997, when John J. Bateman’s edition of the paraphrases on
        the Epistle to the Hebrews and the (seven) Catholic Epistles (ASD VII-6) opened the
        sub-series for the Apostolic Epistles with the last of its designated volumes. Jan
       Bloemendal’s edition of the Paraphrase on Luke (ASD VII-2), the second of three vol-
      umes designated for those on the Gospels and Acts, has relaunched the publication of
        the Ordo Septimus of the Opera in ﬁne style.
         Thanks in no small part to Bateman and his CWE collaborators, the past twenty
         years have seen a ﬂorescence of scholarship on Erasmus’s New Testament paraphrases,
      now widely seen as the supreme expression from his own pen of the philosophia evan-
          gelica sketched in the Enchiridion (1503) and expounded in the New Testament edition
       (1516–35). Bloemendal’s general introduction to the Paraphrases in ASD VII-2 updates
       and expands Bateman’s in ASD VII-6 without replacing it. He situates the Paraphrase
       on Luke (1523) in the composition and publication history of the Paraphrases as an
       emergent whole, gives an account of Erasmus’s narrative and expository strategies in
        the work (broken by a summary of his altercation with the Paris theologian Noël
       Béda, and muddied by a nonce distinction between exegesis and hermeneutics), then
        turns to the question of the paraphrast’s patristic and other sources, with special refer-
      ence—and building on the insights of Jane E. Phillips, translator and annotator of the
       same text for CWE—to his virtuoso expansion of Luke 24:27 (“And beginning with
       Moses and all the prophets, he interpreted to them in all the scriptures the things con-
        cerning himself”). The introduction closes with a list of early editions and an explana-
        tion of editorial principles.

        P. F. Hovingh’s edition of the Annotations on Luke in ASD VI-5, we can now follow
        Erasmus’s revoicing of the evangelical text at every turn. The moment is worth marking.
