Crofton Black’s Pico’s Heptaplus and biblical hermeneutics is a well-researched study of
Giovanni Pico della Mirandola’s seven-chapter commentary upon the  biblical
creation story, entitled the Heptaplus. Itself cleverly organised into seven chapters,
Black’s study explores the many philosophical disciplines and religious traditions that
combined to form Pico’s allegorical reading of Scripture. Black eﬀectively traces the
roots of Pico’s hermeneutics to the writings of early church Fathers, neo-Platonic
traditions and Jewish mystical and philosophical teachings. He contends that Pico’s
esoteric hermeneutics are an indication of his participation in the Jewish studies of
his day, since esoteric hermeneutics such as those of Clement and Origen had long
been abandoned by Christian exegetes, though they remained popular among
Jewish mystics and philosophers. The coupling of esotericism and elitism in Pico’s
work is further indication of his indebtedness to Jewish studies since that coupling,
though a hallmark of Jewish esotericism, tended not to accompany Christian biblical
hermeneutics. Black illuminates another aspect of Pico’s biblical interpretation,
namely the presence of neo-Platonic traditions such as those of Proclus and Pseudo-
Dionysius  in  his work and  his break from the dominant mode  of Christian
hermeneutics. Black’s study therefore provides an important perspective on the
development of medieval Christian hermeneutics more generally, that is its break
from the earliest stage of esoteric readings in late antiquity, the development of a
distinctly medieval Christian exegetical tradition and the emergence of a new
exegetical discourse during the Renaissance. Black’s extensive use of the manuscripts
and translations that were available to Pico is a serious contribution. His examination
of dozens of original manuscripts allows him to arrive at well-grounded conclusions
regarding the texts and translations to which Pico had access, most signiﬁcantly the
translations from Hebrew by Flavius Mithridates. Black’s sober conclusions regard-
ing Pico’s ability to work directly with Hebrew texts is refreshing in light of Pico’s
great fame as a Christian Kabbalist. Black’s presentation of the neo-Platonic
traditions that informed Pico’s work  is also highly informative and includes the
notion that neo-Platonic exegesis was not only a mode of reading, but a mode of
ascent, an action of the intellect. Pico’s exegetical activity is therefore part of the
magical and neo-Platonic activity of the Renaissance. Black’s presentation of Jewish
esoteric hermeneutics, however, could have beneﬁtted from the more nuanced
characterisations of Jewish esoteric hermeneutics presented most notably by Elliot
Wolfson. Wolfson has emphasised that Jewish mystical hermeneutics were not pre-
dicated upon a dualistic notion of biblical language as comprised of a surface and its

In addition to two substantial and absorbing surveys of the history of Christianity
in South Africa, ambitiously covering the precolonial period to the 1830s, this
CD-ROM, produced by the Research Institute on Christianity in South Africa at
the University of Cape Town, includes 186 archival documents (relating to the
footnotes) and an index of more than 5,000 documents from relevant archives. Part I
comprises ‘Christianity and the colonisation of South Africa, 1487–1870’, by Charles
Villa-Vicencio and Peter Grassow; part II John de Gruchy’s study of ‘Christianity
and the modernisation of South Africa, 1867 to 1936’. Both are synoptic treatments
by leading scholars  of South African  religious history and serve as excellent
introductions to the ﬁeld. A third section contains a study entitled ‘Dark days to
liberation’, by James Cochrane and Basitienne Klein which takes the story from
1936 to the end of apartheid. The latter piece has has already been published in the
Journal for Theology in Southern Africa (March 2004). The presentation of the material
and the quality of the CD is basic and represents a hybrid between new and older
publication formats, but it is certainly adequate for scholarly and teaching purposes.
The ability to search these documents in PDF form is a bonus, although many would
still prefer to read these texts in a more traditional form.
