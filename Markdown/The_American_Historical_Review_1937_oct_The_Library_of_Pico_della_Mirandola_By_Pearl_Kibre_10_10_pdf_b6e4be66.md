   The book consists of three parts. The first deals with monuments of art
and their themes and has several divisions: Imperial portraits a mi-corps,
standing, sitting on the throne, or sometimes in groups (coemperors, co-
rulers, emperor and empress); monuments expressing in various ways the
idea of imperial victory (the emperor triumphant); the theme of the em-
peror and his subjects, as the adoration of the emperor, the emperor pre-
siding over councils, etc.; finally, monuments representing the emperor in
relation to Christ, the emperor in adoration, the emperor making offerings
to Christ or the Holy Virgin, most frequently the model of a church founded
by the emperor. One very popular theme is Christ crowning the emperor.
The second part of the book, entitled "An Historical Study", is an attempt
to show briefly the connection between the themes mentioned above and the
historical evolution of the Byzantine Empire beginning with the fourth
century. Finally, the third part, entitled "Imperial Art and Christian Art",
deals with the very interesting problem of the reciprocal influences of these
two. Imperial art, deriving from the art of the pagan Roman Empire, which
in its turn goes back to the art of the Hellenistic and Oriental monarchies,
considerably affected Christian art, both as to the formation of Christian
iconography and as to the treatment of certain Scriptural subjects, especially
in the fourth and fifth centuries. But the equilibrium between these two
branches of art apparently disappeared towards the end of the Empire, when
imperial art was in a state of definite decadente. Examination of the works
of imperial art confirms the fact, which perhaps has not yet been adequately
emphasized, that in the domain of the plastic arts the "renaissance of the
Palaeologi" manifested itself almost exclusively in religious painting.
   Forty beautiful platen reproduce over sixty monuments of art and a
number of coins. Most of these monuments are well known; there is very
little here that is new. But the author has shown great discrimination in
selecting the subjects for reproduction and gives several new interpretations.
His book is based on his profound knowledge of written and monumental
material and is a very important contribution to the bistory of Byzantine
art in particular and of Byzantine civilization in general.

The Library of Pico della Mirandola. By PEARL KIBRE. (New York:
   Columbia University Press. 1936. Pp. 33o. $4.00.)
   IN this study of one of the most celebrated privately owned libraries of
the fifteenth century Miss Kibre examines the literary resources of a scholar
whose short but brilliant career made a profound impression upon his own
and later generations. While Pico's priceless manuscripts and rare editions
were destroyed, probably by fire in 1687, it is possible, nevertheless, thanks

 to the literary detective work and competent scholarship of Miss Kibre, to
 become acquainted with this magnificent collection of 1190 volumes and to
 estimate its value. The reconstruction of the library is based upon records
 of its contents preserved in catalogues or inventories, one of which, hitherto
 unpublished, is reproduced in the appendix.
   The Greek collection, the first division of the library to be considered,
 though smaller than that of the Greek scholar, Cardinal Bessarion, and that
 of the Medici, compared favorably with the collections of other Italian
 scholars of the day and totally eclipsed those of scholars outside of Italy.
Greek classics in philosophy and literature occupied a prominent place in
 the library, and classics in science were well represented. The major portion
 of the philosophical treatises, it should be noted, pertained to the school of
 Aristotle, whom Pico, like most of his literary contemporaries, preferred to
 Plato or to any other Greek philosopher.
   Of particular interest to students of the Scriptures is the Oriental division
of the library, for Pico had the distinction of owning one of the largest
Hebrew collections in all Italy. His enthusiasm for Oriental languages,
 particularly Hebrew, suggests the new interest exhibited by fifteenth century
Humanists in the textual study of the Bible and the Talmud.
   But even more impressive than the Greek and Oriental sections of the
 library was the Latin division. It is this collection of nine hundred volumes,
moreover, ranging chronologically from the era of the Roman Republic to
Pico's own day, that reveals, as Miss Kibre points out, the chief interests of
Pico as a writer and as a scholar. The numerous books and manuscripts of
Latin literature testify to his enthusiasm for contemporary Humanism,
while those of theology and philosophy reflect his interest in medieval
Scholasticism.
    In the field of the occult the library contained a number of treatises by
early Arabic and Jewish astrologers in Latin translation and some of later
date by European writers, and in the field of natural science many repre-
sentative volumes. Among the scientific books were several by Euclid, who,
in Pico's opinion, was a dangerous companion for theologians. Treatises on
medicine were more numerous than any other class of scientific works,
,though Pico wrote little on the art of healing except in its relation to
astrology.
    In reconstructing this famous library and connecting each division with
the owner's writings and with contemporary literary tastes, Miss Kibre has
shed light upon the intellectual history of the fifteenth century. Students
of the Humanistic revival in Italy will welcome this study, which will
enrich all future research in a great cultural movement. It is peculiarly
fitting, finally, that the modern literary world should be united in thought
and spirit, not only through his writings but through knowledge of the
masters who inspired him, with this earnest and gifted young Humanist.

History of Parliament: Biographies of the Members of the Commons House,
   1439-1509. By Colonel the Right Honourable JosIAH C. WEDGWOOD,
  M. P., in collaboration with ANNE D. HOLT. (London: H. M. Stationery
   Office; New York: British Library of Information. 1936. Pp. lv, 984.
    $10.25.)
  THE scope of the History of which this is the first volume to appear has
been indicated in this journal (XLII, 205). The publication may extend to
some forty volumes and will require years for its completion. It has the
official support of parliament, to one of whose members, Colonel Josiah C.
Wedgwood, its initiation is due. Appropriately the introduction to the
volume before us is written by him, and his zealous devotion to an institu-
tion in which "freedom and experience ever grow" is apparent throughout.
However the History may develop, his name should always be associated
with it.
   In 1932 the committee appointed "to report on the materials available
for a record of the perronnel and politics of past members of the House of
Commons from 5264 to 1832 and on the cost and desirability of publica-
tion" presented a valuable interim report (Cmd. 4530). Recommending the
undertaking because of the world-wide influence and prestige of parliament,
it enumerated topics to be elucidated and reviewed existent literature, ex-
plaining that the Official Return of 1878, compiled from sheriffs' returns
and writs de expensis, had hitherto served as the accepted list of members.
But it revealed in one of several useful appendixes that six to seven thousand
names have since been recovered. Of these nearly fourteen hundred are
from the so-called "blank period", 1459-1545, for which, except in the case
of a few parliaments, the sheriffs' returns have been lost. The committee
noted the manuscript material which might profitably be examined to build
up around all names brief biographies. The outcome of the examination of
such sources for the seventy years, 1439-1509, is now in part before us.
   For this period, as for others earlier and later, the findings are to be
arranged in three volumes. One volume is biographical, giving whatever
 pertinent matter can be found about the lives of all known members of the
Commons. The second is to contain "lists and analyses of all Members of
 both Houses in each successive Parliament with brief identification—offices,
 party, age, constituency and experience". The third will analyze the other
 two, making deductions about parliamentary activity and development dur-
 ing the period covered. While the first contains the basic information, the
 others will reset this so that conclusions of historical import may be drawn.
