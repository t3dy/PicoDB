AL-GHAZALI AND GIOVANNI PICO DELLA MIRANDOLA
ON THE QUESTION OF HUMAN FREEDOM AND THE
CHAIN OF BEING



Craig Truglia
Teachers College, Columbia University



Introduction


The person most often credited as the first to free humanity from its bonds in the
chain of being was the Renaissance humanist Giovanni Pico della Mirandola. His
Oration on the Dignity of Man (1486), often referred to as the “Manifesto of the Re-
naissance,” is widely regarded as a beautiful declaration of the greatness of human
freedom and ability.1 He is the first to elucidate this idea by invoking and altering
the metaphysical chain of being. However, it will be argued here that Pico was not
the first philosopher to break the metaphorical chain in order to laud the transcen-
dent abilities of all human beings, exercised through their free will. In fact, around
1106—nearly four hundred years before Pico—the Muslim mystic and philosopher
al-Ghazali denounced the idea that humans have a fixed position in the chain of be-
ing. Yet al-Ghazali’s role in the history of the chain-of-being doctrine has not been
noted by scholars. As this article will show, not only was al-Ghazali the first to claim
that humans use their free will in order literally to ascend to and descend from their
place in the chain of being; he might have been Pico’s inspiration.
   The thesis of this article will be substantiated in the following order. First, Pico’s
chain-of-being doctrine is analyzed in order to contextualize the invocations of the
doctrine by other writers. Thus, after analyzing the Oration, the Picoesque elements
of medieval European works that scholars believe are Pico’s possible inspirations
will be scrutinized and the probability assessed that Pico invoked these works in
the Oration. Afterward, al-Ghazali’s approach to the chain-of-being doctrine will be
scrutinized. Finally, we will consider the possibility that al-Ghazali’s writing influ-
enced Pico.


Giovanni Pico della Mirandola and the Chain of Being

The chain of being, also known by other names such as the hierarchy of being, per-
vaded philosophy from the days of Ancient Greece until adherence to the doctrine
gradually dissipated during the seventeenth century. Its popularity among Neopla-
tonists made it “a fixed element in medieval thought.”2 This meant that the world-
view of those educated in philosophy in those times was radically different from
that of the present day. It was believed that everything existed on a certain step in
a metaphysical ladder, with minerals, plants, and then animals on the bottom and
angels/intellects and God on top, with humanity in the middle.3 Humanity’s place

       as-microcosm idea. Though Pico adds shortly afterward that humans have “no fixed

        the chain of being to the afterlife.7 In Pico’s dialogue between God and Adam, God

            Constrained by no limits, you may determine it [Adam’s place in the chain of being] for
              yourself, according to your own free will. . . . It will be in your power to degenerate into
             the lower forms of life, which are brutish; you shall have the power, according to your
              soul’s judgement, to be reborn into the higher orders, which are divine.8

       Though humans can be brutes in their present life, they can only “be reborn to the
        higher orders.”
          However, Pico’s later comments in the Oration do not place such eschatological
         limitations on humans, revealing that Pico believed in a sort of dual ascent up the
       chain of being (once in life and once again in the afterlife). He declares the following
       about man:

                     If he [Man] cultivates his vegetable seeds, he will become a plant. If he cultivates his
              sensitive seeds, he will become brutish. If he cultivates his rational seeds, he will become
            a heavenly animal. If he cultivates his intellectual seeds, he will be an angel and a son
              of God. And if he is not contented with the fate of any creature, he will gather himself
              into the center of his own unity and, [having] become one spirit with God, will join the
               solitary darkness of the Father, who is above all things, and will stand ahead of all things.
        Who will not wonder at this chameleon?9

Pico, instead, is focused on extolling the transcendent possibilities of the Chameleon-like

What truly sets Pico apart from his European predecessors is that he “positively em-
phasizes the changing nature of man,” instead of merely making an allegorical and
theological point concerning eschatology or the brutishness of humanity.12 Such an
assertion appears to be substantiated by Pico himself, as he extols human beings as
worthy of worship, based on their divine qualities in the present life:

       If you see a philosopher discerning things with right reason, worship him; he is a heavenly
    not an earthly animal. If you see a pure contemplator, oblivious to his body, absorbed in
    the recesses of the mind, this is neither an earthly nor a heavenly animal: this is a superior
      spirit, clothed with human flesh.13

Despite the preceding sentiment, some scholars do not accept that Pico was literally
arguing against humanity’s fixed position in the chain of being. First, they point out
that the true title of the Oration is unknown.14 The reason this is significant is because
if Pico was not talking about “the dignity of man,” it makes it less clear what he was
actually arguing. Second, these scholars assert that Pico was not arguing that all hu-
mans were born free. Presently, “freedom” implicitly has political undertones. Unlike
Erasamus, Pico was not concerned with the sociological implications of the chain
of being. Instead, he was concerned with the metaphysical side to human freedom,
and the freedom Pico spoke of belonged only to philosophers—an intellectual elite
akin to the Guardians of Plato’s Republic. Thus, the “freedom” Pico spoke of is not
the same as what is presently understood in the modern era, because freedom to the
philosopher was merely the ability to acquire knowledge.15
    Taking the preceding into consideration, this dissenting scholarship asserts that
the Oration was prepared by Pico merely as a defense of philosophy to be used for
his upcoming trial in a Papal Court and so it cannot be interpreted as a humanistic
philosophical work.16 Though Pico never had to attend that trial, these scholars assert
that one must take the Oration’s intended audience and other necessary contextual
factors into account when interpreting the work. If one cannot interpret Pico’s mag-
nificent words about human freedom to mean that everyone has the capability to
choose freely their station in life through their actions, the “Manifesto of the Renais-
sance” was hardly humanistic at all. William Craven writes that “Pico’s fable” was
not “a serious philosophical statement about man” because it did not “liberate man
from a constricting cosmos or from creaturely dependence.”17 Pico was merely mak-
ing a “traditional metaphor” that was “neither new nor daring.”18
   Even lending credence to the points of the opposition scholarship, Pico’s Ora-
tion is still a significant philosophical work. First, even if the Oration was prepared
merely as a defensive speech for Pico’s trial, it is still quoted at length in several of
his subsequent works. This demonstrates that the Oration contains ideas instrumental
to Pico’s philosophy.19 Second, Craven notes that most scholars believe that “even
if” his stance on human freedom was “not modern, Pico’s doctrine of man was sig-
nificant as the systematic and speculative development of a vague idea which had
dominated humanist thought for generations.”20 Thus, Pico’s doctrine of man can be
considered a significant advance in humanist philosophy even if his sentiment was

       The Chain of Being in Early European Works


        Taking the preceding into account, one may question whether authors prior to Pico
         either presented a metaphor or literally declared the belief that humans in this life
       can choose their station in the chain of being. This can be determined by evaluating
        the works identified by scholars to be Pico’s possible influences. Kristeller suggests
         that Marsilio Ficino’s Platonic Theology (1474) might have inspired Pico, though
       he asserts that their doctrine of man is different.21 Craven cites Nicholas of Cusa as
        Pico’s inspiration.22 Riva observes that spiritual writers such as Allan of Lille and John
        Scotus Eriugena might have been Pico’s inspiration, though like Kristeller he is more
        apt to believe that Pico was original in his own right.23 Thus, the works of Ficino,
       Cusanus, Eriugena, and Lille are analyzed in the following so as to make clear the
         similarities and differences between Pico’s view of human freedom and that of his
       European predecessors.
             Ficino’s Platonic Theology is the first work in this analysis for several reasons.
          First, Platonic Theology contained much of Ficino’s humanist ideas. Second, Ficino
      was Pico’s mentor and later his colleague. Third, Pico owned a copy of Ficino’s Pla-
        tonic Theology.24 Thus, it would not be surprising if it was Ficino who inspired Pico’s
        sentiment in the Oration. This may at first glance appear to be the case when one
         realizes that the title to book 3, chapter 2 of Platonic Theology states the following:
       “The Soul is the middle level of being. It links and unites all the levels above it and
       below it when it ascends to the higher and descends to the lower levels.”25 Through-
        out the book Ficino expounds the notion of humankind’s universality because of its
        privileged place in the center of the chain of being, in order to substantiate his views
      on cosmology and the immortality of souls.
          However, there are several reasons why Ficino is not stating that humans literally
      move up and down the chain of being in accordance with the faculties they nurture,
        as Pico did. First, the soul’s acensus-decensus is never the result of specific actions
      on the part of an individual, as Pico asserts. Such mobility up and down belongs to
       everyone as a “natural instinct” and thus is inherent in the soul’s makeup, regardless
        of free will.26 Second, it is true that Ficino asserts that the human soul is “all things
         [in the chain of being] together,” reflecting aspects of all the beings in the chain,
        thus not to be grouped together with “the remaining things below God [which] are
       each individually something singular in themselves.”27 However, we cannot confuse
         this reading as indicating he believed that the soul has literal metaphysical powers
        of ascent or descent up or down the chain. Ficino later makes clear that humankind

has a fixed place in the chain of being. He compares humanity’s ability to ascend or
descend and its role as an “intermediary” to that of the fixed elements (air and fire).28
Furthermore, Ficino invokes an astronomical metaphor where the heavenly bodies
are in fixed orbits with humans constituting the “lowest star” (the moon) below the
“highest stars” (angels), which both “reflect” the Sun’s (God’s) divine light.29 This is
because, according to Ficino, “[T]he lower parts of each higher order must always be
linked . . . to the highest part of the lower order,”30 making humans a “certain lower
angel,”31 which can reflect divinity through contemplation, but no more.
    Third, any true ascent up the chain of being is delayed to the eschatological
future, as Ficino makes explicit.32 Lastly, the “ascent” toward God as described by
Ficino needs to be understood in Neoplatonic terms and not as a radical break with
its doctrine. Michael J. B. Allen’s The Absent Angel in Ficino’s Philosophy argues
that in Platonic Theology humankind essentially supersedes the angel because hu-
mans can reach divine union with God without any intermediaries.33 Nonetheless,
this does not mean that humankind’s divine union necessitates a literal rise above
the angels. If anything, Ficino is very careful not to allow this to happen, because it
would alter his carefully constructed cosmology.34 Divine union, to Ficino, is merely
when one “grasps the infinite God,” because the mind’s supposed infinite capacity
is, by Ficino’s admission, qualified as not moving beyond one’s human contempla-
tive faculty.35
    This is hardly an innovation in Neoplatonism. Mystical union with / contempla-
tion of the One, bordering on a mystical religious experience, was common. For
example, Porphyry reported that he had one such experience and Plotinus had four.
Such an experience did not constitute a literal ascent from one’s place in the chain of
being, nor, for that matter, did Plotinus or Ficino make such an assertion, with Ficino
describing their “divine” rebirth as “assiduous contemplation.”36 Ficino clearly as-
serts that the mind/soul through contemplation does not leave the body and thereby
does not literally descend or ascend. When rhetorically asked, “In performing these
miracles [reflecting the divine], does this soul withdraw from the body. . . ?” he re-
sponds, “The soul is certainly in the body through[out] its idolum [the fantastic divine
experience].”37 Ficino’s metaphysics accorded humans great dignity, but not literal
transcendent ability, thereby putting him in line with standard Neoplatonist doctrine.
    Ficino never challenged the standard chain-of-being doctrine, and, as Kristeller
points out, this is “a fact perfectly compatible with his historical position [the early
Renaissance].”38 Kristeller further notes that Ficino’s Platonic Theology discussed
“man’s central position in the hierarchy of being, and the universality of his thoughts
and aspirations,” but

    Pico clearly took over the notion of man’s universality, where he replaced the idea of
    man’s central position with that of man’s unbound freedom, taking man entirely outside
    the universal hierarchy of being. . . . Thus Pico gave an independent development even
     to those ideas which he clearly borrowed from Ficino and from the Platonic tradition.39

Therefore, Ficino’s view of the Soul was greatly revised, if not qualitatively changed,
in Pico’s Oration.

A more interesting comparison to Pico’s chain-of-being doctrine can be found in
       a writing of the mid-fifteenth-century theologian Nicholas of Cusa. Craven identifies
        the following passage from Cusanus “as a particularly close parallel” to the descrip-
        tion of the chain of being in the Oration.40 In his Metaphysical Speculations (1440)
       Cusanus writes:

             Since [man] surmises that all things are attained by the senses or by reason or by intellect,
           and since he sees that these powers of his are enfolded within his oneness, he supposes
              that he can proceed unto all things in a human way. For man is god, but not unqualifiedly,
             since he is man; therefore, he is a human god. Man is also world but is not contractedly
                 all things, since he is man; therefore, man is a microcosm, or a human world. Therefore,
             the region of humanity encompasses, by means of its human power, God and the entire
             world. Therefore, man can be a human god; and just as he can be a god humanly, so he
           can be a human angel, a human beast, a human lion, or a human bear, or any other such
              thing. For within the power of humanity all things exist in their own way. Therefore, just
             as in the universe all things are unfolded after the fashion of the universe, so in humanity
                 all things are unfolded in a human way, since [man] is a human world.41

        This statement, even though it shares with Pico the idea that humans can be any be-
        ing within the cosmological hierarchy, is clearly a different argument. Cusa is simply
         stating that humans are a microcosm of existence as a whole. “Powers” are auto-
        matically “enfolded” in humans; that is, a human can experience being an animal
        or an angel. One experiences this as a human, bonded in the center of the chain of
        being, instead of literally transcending (or descending from) the human level, as Pico
         asserts. This is not a ringing endorsement of the ability of humans through reason to
       ascend the chain of being and approach God. Humans, in Cusanus’ metaphysics,
        are clearly fixed in the middle of the chain of being. He is simply repeating the old
       human-as-microcosm doctrine.
           Cusanus’ belief that humans have a fixed position in the chain of being is made
        clearer in his other works. During the same year that Metaphysical Speculations was
         written, Cusanus in his On Learned Ignorance (1440) specifically elaborated his idea
       concerning the different natures enfolded in humanity:

           [H]uman nature is that [nature] which, though created a little lower than the angels, is
             elevated above all the [other] works of God; it enfolds intellectual and sensible nature and
             encloses all things within itself, so that the ancients were right in calling it a microcosm,
             or a small world. Hence, human nature is that [nature] which, if it were elevated unto
            a union with Maximality, would be the fullness of all the perfections of each and every
              thing, so that in humanity all things would attain the supreme gradation. Now, humanity
                  is present only contractedly in this or that. Therefore, it would not be possible that more
            than one true human being ( Jesus) [homo] could ascend to union with Maximality.42

       As this passage makes clear, humans would only have the ability to be all things
             if they were in a divine union with God. However, Maximality is inaccessible to
       humankind—Jesus alone was the only human to attain this. In Cusanus’ view,
      humans are nothing more than an inferior microcosm, while Jesus was a perfect
       microcosm. All other humans are relegated to a station below the angels, capable

of experiencing what is above and below them on the chain of being only in the
“human mode” per se. Cusanus’ thought concerning this matter never changed.
Twenty years later, in the Scrutiny of the Koran (1460), Cusanus states that “the
world’s fecundity, its offspring, and its love are participated in by angels angelically,
by men humanly, by non-rational animals non-rationally, by plants vegetatively, by

goodness to the extent of their capacity to do so. Humans cannot exceed the limita-

    After exhaustively searching through twenty-five different works by Nicholas
of Cusa and finding no additional references to the chain of being then the ones
just mentioned, the author concludes that Cusanus never explicated ideas akin to
Pico’s.44 Furthermore, there is evidence that suggests Cusanus’ work did not even
serve as inspiration for Pico’s Oration. First, Pico did not own any of Cusanus’ works
in his library.45 Second, Pico did not visit Cusanus’ library until 1488, two years after
the Oration was written.46 Lastly, as Kristeller notes, most scholars believe that “the
influence of Cusanus” is not “very tangible in the writings of Pico.”47 When the pre-
ceding is taken into account, one has little reason to agree with Craven that Cusanus
was Pico’s inspiration.
    Another possible influence on Pico is John Scotus Eriugena. He was a ninth-
century theologian who, despite his Neoplatonic leanings, never once read a book by
a Neoplatonist philosopher.48 However, by reading the works of the Church Fathers
(which were saturated with Neoplatonism) he acquired the idea of the chain of being
and incorporated it into his metaphysics. In his most popular work, Periphyseon on
the Division of Nature (865), there are allusions to a Picoesque chain of being.
   Human beings hold a privileged position in Eriugena’s metaphysics, as revealed
in his invocation of the chain-of-being doctrine. First, Eriugena subscribed to the
microcosm idea. He writes, “Wise men agree that in man universal creation is con-
tained.”49 Second, Eriugena is unlike Cusanus in his claim that humans are not an
inferior microcosm—in fact, Godlike “attributes are the same in man as in celestial
essences.”50 This is a bolder claim than Pico’s or Cusanus’ in regard to the sort of
microcosm that humans are. Nevertheless, the question is whether he goes as far
as Pico in asserting the duality of humanity to be in some ways a microcosm of the
chain of being while also holding a special independence from it. On this matter
Eriugena writes the following:

    [M]an as whole can become both like an animal and spiritual. He can become like an
    animal merely by free choice; but he can become spiritual by free choice along with
    grace, without which the natural power of the will by no means suffices to move man to
      spirit. Man is therefore made and said to be like an animal when he abandons the motions
    which, according to reason and intellect, focus on the knowledge of Creator and creation,
    and by spontaneous appetite falls into the irrational motions by which brutish animals
    are aroused by the desires of the body. In this latter way man absorbs himself completely
     in the death-bearing delights of temporal or destructible things which tend towards non-
    being. The spiritual man, however, changing his whole way of life for the better, is wholly
    transformed into the likeness of celestial essences.51

This chain-of-being doctrine resembles that in the Oration. Of particular interest is
        the clear emphasis on the freedom of choice, allowing one to use personal discre-
        tion in order to determine where on the chain of being (s)he will be stationed. As
       Donald Duclow notes, “[H]umanity, not the celestial hierarchy, plays the pivotal role in
        Eriugena’s system.”52 Consequently, humans are afforded great dignity.53
          However, there are subtle differences between the philosophies of Eriugena and
        Pico. First, Eriugena is clearly invoking similes and metaphors as opposed to making
       a philosophical statement regarding humankind.54 Remember, one is “said to be like
       an animal” when her or his focus is not God. Thus, Eriugena’s comments on the abil-
          ity of humans to climb up the chain of being must be interpreted metaphorically. This
           is confirmed by the fact Eriugena declares earlier in the Periphyseon: “Surely you do
        not believe that the plan of Divine Wisdom can become manifest to intellects, either
        of men or angels. . . . [T]he capacity of their nature is not equal to beholding such
         things.”55 This illustrates that Eriugena did not believe humans can truly grasp the
        divine. Second, while Pico assumes that philosophers can approach God by nurtur-
        ing their higher faculties, Eriugena believes that “free will along with grace” is neces-
         sary.56 Lastly, and most importantly, Eriugena is merely presenting an eschatological
         allegory. As he makes clear, not focusing on God and partaking in “the desires of the
       body” are “death-bearing delights.” Instead of achieving eternal life as elucidated in
        the New Testament, one earns death/damnation by not nurturing the appropriate fac-
          ulties. As Willemien Otten notes, “[M]an’s paradisiacal state is in effect still possible,
       though he defers it to the eschatological future.”57 When the preceding argument is
       considered alongside the fact that Pico only owned one work of Eriugena’s (which
      was not the Periphyseon), one may conclude that the evidence does not suggest that
        Eriugena served as Pico’s inspiration.58
            Another work that might have inspired Pico  is Allan of  Lille’s The Plaint
        of Nature (1176?). Lille writes the following in book 6, prose 3: “[T]he one [i.e.,
         sensuality], corrupting man, changes him into a beast, the other [reason] has the
      power to transform man into a god.”59 Like Pico, he attributes humanity’s reason in
       making humans like God: “The image of this perfectly organized state shines forth
        in man. . . . For native ability, power of reasoning, as well as the faculty of recalling
        the past dwell in the various compartments of the [God]head and obey Wisdom
       with enthusiasm.”60 Lille’s sentiment appears to align closely with Pico’s, because it
       seems that he believes that humans in this life shift throughout the chain of being in
       accordance with their actions.
              Yet, Lille does not go far enough to endorse such a claim. He is arguing, like Eri-
       ugena, that because humans are made in God’s image, they reflect certain positive
         qualities of God (specifically, reason). However, it is unlikely that Lille believed that
       reason allowed humans to ascend the chain of being. While he literally asserts that
        sensuality “changes” a human “into a beast,” he does not use a present-tense verb
        to describe how the faculty of reason changes humans. It appears that the faculty of
       reason having “the power to transform . . . into a god ” is merely a potentiality that
       can only be fulfilled after the End Times. Lille, as Riva asserts, merely was making an

eschatological allegory.61 Humans literally change into beasts by living up to their

    Overall, the evidence suggests that Lille’s comments did not serve as Pico’s in-

The Plaint of Nature, Lille opines that almost all humans are brutish slaves to sin—

contemplate humanity’s greatness. Second, Pico not own any of Lille’s works; his
library contained only a few pieces of twelfth-century European literature. This has
led Pearl Kibre, a scholar who has studied Pico’s library, to suspect that he was not
interested in works from that period.62 This decreases the probability that Pico ever
read anything by Lille. Lille did not elucidate an idea concerning humanity’s freedom
in regard to the chain of being; and he probably did not inspire Pico.
    Pico’s place in history, as the first to situate humanity outside the constraints of
the chain of being, appears safe if one were to look only at the earlier European pre-
cedents. It seems that Pico was the first European to make the sort of statements
found in the Oration. However, as the rest of this article argues, al-Ghazali preceded
Pico in asserting that humans have the freedom to ascend or descend the chain of
being in accordance with the faculties they nurture.


Ghazali’s Notion of the Ability of Humans to Ascend Up the Chain of Being

This article argues that Ghazali’s The Alchemy of Happiness (1096–1106), The Niche
of Lights (1096–1106), and The Just Balance (1096–1106)63 are the earliest works
both to invoke the chain-of-being doctrine and to assert that humans, using their
free will, have the capacity to transcend their station in the chain during this life.
Scholarly research in this area has been scanty. European Medievalists alongside
Islamic Medievalists make little mention of Ghazali’s chain-of-being doctrine. One
exception, Marion Holmes Katz, asserts that Ghazali believed there was “a hierar-
chy in which it [a being] shares some powers with the beings below it and enjoys
other, additional powers which link it with those above” where “[h]uman beings can
achieve perfection . . . by fully realizing those potentialities which they share with
the angels, rather than those which they share with animals and plants.”64 Such an
observation, in principle, certainly agrees with Pico’s. Yet, implicit in Katz’ claim is
that Ghazali only believed that humans were microcosms of the universe. However,
it will be shown that Ghazali argued more than this. He not only believed, like
Pico, that humans were microcosms, he asserted that they were free to ascend and
descend the chain of being in their lifetime as well. This makes Ghazali’s doctrine of
man revolutionary for his time.
   The most important work of Ghazali’s that dealt with this subject was The
Alchemy of Happiness (1106?). This is because it most clearly states his position con-
cerning humanity’s place in the chain of being. Ghazali begins his work, similar to
Pico’s Oration, by praising humanity’s greatness:

[Man] was not created in jest or at random. . . . [H]is spirit is lofty and divine. When in

              that state, he finds his heaven in the contemplation of Eternal Beauty. . . . The spiritual

        Like Pico, he does not abandon the idea of the human as microcosm: “Some of thy
         attributes are those of animals, some of devils, and some of angels.”66 Ghazali, just
        as in Pico’s Oration, then separates the chain of being by the faculties found on its
         different levels:

           The occupation of animals is eating, sleeping, and fighting; therefore, if thou art an ani-
            mal, busy thyself in these things. Devils are busy stirring up mischief, and in guile and de-

             thine origin, that thou mayest know and contemplate the Most High, and be delivered
            from the thralldom of lust and anger.67

         Clearly, Ghazali asserts that one must decide which faculty to nurture, hopefully not
        allowing the “lower faculties to dominate the higher.”68
                   It initially appears that Ghazali believes the ascent up the chain of being to
       be something that is achieved only after death, which would put Ghazali more in
         line with the Medieval European writers discussed previously. For example, Ghazali
        declares, “[T]he highest faculty in him is reasons [sic], which fits him for the con-
        templation of God. If this predominates in him, when he dies, he leaves behind him
          all tendencies to passion and resentment, and becomes capable of association with
        angels.”69 Nonetheless, showing a progression in thought similar to the Oration, the
         rest of The Alchemy of Happiness situates humanity’s ascent up the chain of being as
       something not only possible in the next life, but also in this one.
         What may explain Ghazali’s divergence with the standard monotheistic thought
        of the period? Ghazali was in many ways a defender of the orthodox Sunni Muslim
        against what he viewed as corruptive influences, such as Talimism (an Ismaili
         Shiite sect) and Hellenistic philosophy. Nonetheless, he was specifically a Sufi (a
       Muslim mystic). Sufis, like other Muslims, believe that people will be closer to
      God in the afterlife. However, they also believe that humans can attain fana’ or “self-
        annihilation,” which is the state of consciousness when all one contemplates and lit-
         erally experiences is God itself, in this life.70 Therefore, Ghazali would have assumed
         that God was approachable to the point of literally “tasting” the divine (dhawq, a Sufi
        term) in the present life. This state is attained through prayer and contemplation, but,
         interestingly enough, not through human reason.
           Nonetheless, one may conclude that Ghazali’s chain-of-being doctrine contains
       a Picoesque duality because he notes that an ascent up the chain of being is pos-
         sible not only in the afterlife but also in this one—achieving the status of God in this
          life.71 Not long after declaring that one ascends the chain of being only after death,
       Ghazali declares, “The more a man purifies himself from fleshly lusts and concen-
         trates his mind on God” the more able he is to have “prophetic inspiration”72—a

in his Deliverance from Error.73 Later in this work, he applies the preceding claim to
the chain-of-being doctrine, describing “the journey of man through the world” as
something that can “be divided into four stages,” with “a fifth [stage], known to the
prophets and saints, whose [ascent up the chain of being] may be compared to flying
through the air.”74 This flying up the chain of being, attained in this life, is achieved
by spiritually gifted people—a sort of elite (the saints and prophets).75 However, even
those with the capacity to soar may fail to do so if they do not nurture their divine
qualities. Ghazali then specifically invokes the chain of being to make his point:

    [M]an is capable of existing on several different planes, from animal to the angelic, and
     precisely in this lies his danger, i.e., of falling to the very lowest. In the Koran it is written,
   “We proposed the burden (i.e., responsibility or free-will ) to the heavens and the earth
    and the mountains, and they refused to undertake it. But man took it upon himself: Verily
    he is ignorant.” Neither animals nor angels can change their appointed rank and place.
    But man may sink to the animal or soar to the angel, and this is the meaning of his un-

     pilgrims [the spiritual elite], whom they far out-number.76

The preceding clearly elucidates that certain people (the spiritually gifted in Ghazali’s
mind, philosophers in Pico’s) can literally soar up the chain of being. On the other
hand, anyone can descend down it. It is specifically “free-will” in this passage that
decides in what direction one will travel. Therefore, as the book progresses, Ghazali
reveals that he believes that humans are not constrained by the chain of being. Fur-
thermore, Ghazali reconciles the seemingly opposing claims that the ascent up the
chain of being happens in both this life and the afterlife:

   God is the highest possible object of knowledge, the knowledge of Him must afford more
     delight than any other. He who knows God, even in this world, dwells, as it were, in a
     paradise. . . . [T]he knowledge of God acquire[d] on Earth will in the next world change
     into the Vision of God.77

When Ghazali’s preceding claims are taken into account, one can more fully under-
stand his initial synopsis of humanity’s greatness:

    [W]e have attempted, in some degree, to expound the greatness of man’s soul. He who
    neglects it and suffers its capacities to rust or to degenerate must necessarily be the loser
     in this world and the next. The true greatness of man lies in his capacity for eternal prog-
     ress, otherwise in this temporal sphere he is the weakest of all things, being subject to
    hunger, thirst, heat, cold, and sorrow. . . . In truth, man in this world is extremely weak
    and contemptible; it is only in the next [life] that he will be of value, if by the means of the
    “alchemy of happiness” he rises from the rank of beasts to that of angels.78

Thus, Ghazali, as a devout Muslim, emphasizes that nothing in this life will approach
the afterlife in greatness. Nonetheless, he maintains at the same time that humans
can ascend the chain of being toward God. Therefore, Ghazali’s conception of the
chain of being in The Alchemy of Happiness mirrors that in Pico’s Oration, as both

           Ghazali puts forth the same idea in another work of his, The Niche of Lights. In

       Ghazali believed that humans can literally supersede the angels, who are in a fixed
        position gazing downwards. In addition, he believed that a select few can enter the
       “World of the Realm Celestial” and be in direct contact with God. Thus, Ghazali
       accorded humans great dignity. As Pico said, humans are “the most fortunate of be-
        ings and consequently worthy of all admiration . . . , a condition to be envied not
       only by brutes but even by the stars and by the intelligences dwelling beyond this
        world.”81 Even if Pico meant the preceding statement in a fashion that only applied to
         intellectually gifted philosophers, this certainly did not contradict Ghazali’s thought.
       Ghazali believed that the ascent is “too difficult for most minds” and suitable only
         for “the Few of the Few” belonging to the spiritual elite.82
             In what sense was Ghazali’s doctrine of man ahead of its time? It was not because
        other Muslim and Jewish thinkers adopted the same line of thinking—philosophers
       from the tenth to thirteenth centuries such as al-Farabi,83 the Brotherhood of Purity,84
         al-Biruni,85 Ibn Sina,86 Ibn Bajjah,87 Ibn Tufayl,88 Ibn Rushd,89 al-Suhrawardi,90 Mai-
       monides,91 and Ibn Arabi.92 They were all in line with standard Neoplatonic thought.
        Neither was Ghazali reminiscent of either a humanist or an adamant Neoplatonic
        philosopher.93 In fact, his most famous work, The Incoherence of Philosophy, is a
       polemic that attempts to disprove Neoplatonism’s premises. Furthermore, Ghazali
         directly denounced emanationism in The Incoherence of the Philosophers, while
       Pico adhered to this idea.94 While emanationism encouraged Pico to believe there
      was a chain of being, perhaps it was Ghazali’s abhorrence of emanationism, and his
         Sufi insistence that supra-rational knowledge was available to one during a mystical
        experience, that led him to believe that humans are not constrained by the chain-

   However, this may be far too simplistic a view. In other, perhaps earlier, works
of Ghazali’s, such as The Infamies (1096–1106) and The Ninety-Nine Names for
God (1096–1106), he makes more limited claims concerning the place of humans
in the chain of being. In some works he implies that a human is slightly more than a
microcosm.95 In The Mysteries of the Human Soul Ghazali even directly denounces
the idea that humans can change their place in the chain of being.96 According to
Craven, Pico sometimes adhered to the chain-of-being doctrine, and thus he did not
always have “a consistent doctrine of man.”97 This applies to Ghazali as well, as he
appears similar in this respect. It is easy to oversimplify the opinions of people in
the past, not taking into account that people’s beliefs generally change in time and
that they often mean something different from what their words suggest to modern
eyes and ears. However, whether Ghazali and Pico were inconsistent is not particu-
larly important. Both writers wrote about an idea that no one besides them ever elu-
cidated up to that point in time. Because Ghazali wrote about it hundreds of years
before Pico, one must consider the possibility that Pico was inspired by Ghazali.


Considering the Possibility that Ghazali Inspired Pico


If one is to consider the possibility that Ghazali inspired Pico, one must first specu-
late why Pico might have been motivated to borrow an idea from Ghazali. The an-
swer may lie in his library. Pico, who was interested in Jewish mysticism, might have
found Muslim mysticism appealing as well. However, as implied above, his library
contained neither Ghazali’s The Alchemy of Happiness nor The Niche of Lights.
However, Pico’s library does contain two copies of Ibn Rushd’s The Incoherence
of the Incoherence (a polemic against Ghazali) and two works of Ghazali’s. While
this is not a great number of books when one considers the fact that the library had
over a thousand of them, it is worth pointing out that Pico owned four books about
or written by Ghazali, compared to only one work from Cusa, Eriugena, and Lille
combined. Therefore, one can conclude that Pico had at least a cursory knowledge
of Ghazali. The first book by Ghazali in the inventory is the Philosophia cum com-
mento, or “Philosophy with comments.”98 None of Ghazali’s work had such a title,
but it is likely that the work referred to in the inventory was his widely popular The
Aims of the Philosophers (a work that merely summarized the philosophies of Plato,
Aristotle, and the Neoplatonists). The other work is curiously titled Statera specu-
lationis et alia, or “The Value of Contemplation and Other Things.”99 Ghazali does
not have a single work remotely close to that title. When one takes into account the
incorrect titles, it is obvious that they were mistranslated into Hebrew and/or Latin.100
Thus, it is lost to history precisely what works these were.
   However, there are grounds for reasonable suspicion that Statera speculationis et
alia might be Ghazali’s The Standard Measure of Knowledge, The Balance of Action,
or The Just Balance. The preceding are all considered philosophical, as opposed to

        “scale”/”balance” in the title available in English in the United States is The Just Bal-

        discussed previously, the library records would reveal a greater possibility that Pico
      was inspired by Ghazali’s thought. For the purpose of this article, the probability that
         specifically the The Just Balance could have inspired Pico to revise his chain-of-being
        doctrine will be analyzed.
            Several of the beginning chapters in The Just Balance include observations con-
        cerning the properties of humanity as it pertains to the chain of being. For example,
       Ghazali observes that “man is not stone [a mineral], nor stone man” and that humans
        are an “admixture [that] contains both animal and vegetable” providing room for
       “growth and development.”102 However, Ghazali’s most cryptic discussion of hu-
        manity’s place in the chain of being is as follows:


           [W]e ascend from the ordering of the human domain to the description of its maker,
           which is knowledge; then again we ascend from knowledge to life, and from thence to
             the essence. This is the spiritual ascension, and these rules are steps by which to ascend to
             the skies, and these principles are the stages of the ascent. As for bodily ascent, no power
                  is sufficient for this, save that which is reserved for the Prophet.103

        Thus, as one’s knowledge of God grows, the closer one is to God spiritually, to the
        point of rising “to the essence [of God].” Though Ghazali reserves the miracle of
        physical ascension to heaven to Muhammad alone, the claim that humans can spiri-
         tually ascend the chain of being is still revolutionary.104 Thus, if Pico had The Just
        Balance, he had the resource necessary to expand upon the traditional portrayal of
        the chain of being. Therefore, taking the relevant passage above into account, it is
        possible that Pico’s Oration was specifically inspired by this Ghazali work.
              Yet, one must also consider the possibility that Pico came across manuscripts to

        discussions of the chain of being. Granted, there is no direct evidence that Pico had
        ever read these works, and there are no known Latin translations from the Middle

        of the Philosophers.105 However, this does not mean that Pico did not come across

This has led scholars to suspect that Ghazali’s writings influenced many European
thinkers. Some have attempted to prove that parallels between Descartes’ Discourse
on Method and Ghazali’s Deliverance from Error reveal that Descartes was inspired
by Ghazali.107 Scholars have also made similar studies concerning Ghazali’s effect
on other philosophers, including Pascal and Kant.108 This article is like previous
studies in the way that it draws attention to the similarities between Ghazali and a
European philosopher. This has been done in order to shed light on the question of
whether Pico was dependent on Ghazali. The similarities between the Oration and
The Niche of Lights, and especially The Alchemy of Happiness, offer suggestive evi-
dence that Ghazali inspired Pico.
    Furthermore, there is circumstantial evidence that Ghazali influenced Pico. In
1485, Pico first began learning Hebrew and Arabic, and in the process started ac-
quiring many Eastern works.109 It might not be all that coincidental that in 1486 Pico
developed his most famous works, the 900 Theses and the Oration, and that these
works incorporated elements of Eastern philosophy. If there was any time in Pico’s
life best suited for incorporating Ghazali’s philosophy, it was during 1486, when he
wrote the Oration. When the preceding analyses are taken into account, one must
consider the possibility that Pico had drawn inspiration from The Just Balance, The
Alchemy of Happiness, and/or The Niche of Lights.
    Nevertheless, there is no sure way to discern what sources Pico was working
with. One does not know what works Pico ran across that were in the Vatican Library
(where records are scanty) or in his travels. There is no specific written evidence that
he had access to the previously discussed Ghazali works or that he did not. Fur-
thermore, even Pico’s library cannot reveal to historians what works inspired Pico.
The library records are a result of an inventory made by Antonio Pizzamano four
years following Pico’s death in 1498, when Cardinal Domenico Grimani bought
the books.110 One cannot know whether Pico had his hands on a certain work, like
Ghazali’s elusive “The Value of Contemplation and Other Things,” before or after
1486. As Kristeller states, “The inventories of Pico’s library, and the records of the
books borrowed or owned by him, are often unclear or incomplete, and hence make
it difficult to draw specific conclusions from them.”111 Even though Pico often states
his sources, he does not always do this, because he often “absorbed, digested and
transformed” what he learned “in the course of his intellectual development.”112
Therefore, one can only conjecture concerning what his inspirations were, since
many were not specifically cited.


Conclusion


When one takes the preceding arguments into account, it is possible to make the
following conclusions: there is no evidence that Pico was inspired by the Christian
mystics or philosophers. He owned none of their theological works, and his opinions

and ideas clearly differed from theirs, as the analysis shows. Even though Pico was
        inspired by Ficino, Ficino never developed a theory on human freedom that paral-
        leled that of the Oration. However, the following is known. Pico likely had more
         interest in Ghazali than the other writers (aside from Ficino) discussed here, as evi-
       denced by the fact that he owned more of Ghazali’s books. It is possible that Pico
      owned The Just Balance, because Ghazali wrote only two other books with the word
       “balance” in the title, and the inventory of Pico’s library attributes a book to Ghazali
       with the word “balance” in it. Pico plausibly had access to Ghazali’s works, because
        they were available in Europe along with the works of the medieval European writers.
         Lastly, Ghazali’s opinions on humanity’s ability to go outside the chain of being mir-
         ror Pico’s. At the very least, one must put Ghazali in the same sentence with Cusa,
          Lille, and Eriugena. At most, one must conclude that Ghazali was Pico’s inspiration
       and that Ghazali’s ideas, though in line with medieval orthodox Muslim thought,
       were what made the “Manifesto of the Renaissance” so revolutionary in Europe. Even
             if Pico was not inspired by Ghazali, one cannot doubt that Ghazali’s chain-of-being
        doctrine preceded Pico’s and is worthy of recognition for being the first in the history
        of the doctrine to denounce the chain’s bonds in favor of a more expanded role for
      human freedom. It is hoped that further research will be conducted in the area of
        the manuscript availability of Muslim works as well as an investigation into whether
        Ghazali’s The Standard Measure of Knowledge and The Balance of Action contained
       a Picoesque doctrine of man, so that the full extent of the effects of Muslim thought
        in Europe can be appreciated.
