  Given the stunning scope and depth of The
Limits of Inﬂuence, there are impressively few
ﬂaws. In Chapter 1, Vanden Broecke presents
a series of preliminary remarks on early mod-
ern astrology  that evince a superior way of
understanding the multifaceted nature of the
subject, though he does not make clear how his
observations improve upon the impressions of
others. In Chapter 8, Hemminga’s Astrologiae
refutatae liber (1583) is regarded as a revolu-
tionary break, by which ‘direct epistemologi-
cal relevance’ was referred to particular (rather
than common) experience (p. 256), but the pre-
cise relation of Hemminga’s work to the wider
historical narrative is uncertain. If the conclud-
ing example of Tycho Brahe conﬁrms Vanden
Broecke’s claim that Danish court practition-
ers,  ‘like  their Louvain colleagues’,  resorted
to social secrecy when ‘the public prediction
of  political  particulars’  became  impossible
(p. 264), additional evidence is necessary. The
occasional typographical error hinders neither
the pace nor the power of Vanden Broecke’s
argument, which  represents an extraordinary
contribution  to  the  relevant  historiography.
Although Vanden Broecke is reluctant to per-
mit comparison with other contexts in the period,
The Limits of Inﬂuence should allow historians of
other times and places to establish connections
in a similar manner that Louvain mediated the
reform and relegation of astrology.
