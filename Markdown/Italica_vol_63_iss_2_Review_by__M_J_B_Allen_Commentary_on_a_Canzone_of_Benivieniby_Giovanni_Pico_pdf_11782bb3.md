Giovanni Pico della Mirandola: Commentary on a Canzone of Benivieni,
   trans. Sears Jayne. American University Studies, Series II, Romance
  Languages and Literature, Volume 19. New York-Berne-Frankfurtam
  Main, Peter Lang,1984. Pp. 289.
   Pico's Commento is an interesting collection of notes in Italian that he
anticipated working up into either a commentary on Plato's Symposium, or a
treatise on love, or a full-blown poetic theology. It was probablybegun in
1486, the year of his celebrated proposal to defend 900 theses in Rome and
before the papal commission's  condemnation  of 13 of these  theses.
Purportingto be a commentary on the love poem "Amor dalle cui" by his
gifted friend, Girolamo Benivieni, it was first published indeed, not among
Pico's own works, but in the 1519 Giunti edition of Benivieni's poems (andin
subsequent  editions  of these poems in 1522, 1524, and 1731). It is
remarkable, however, as an exposition in three books (plus a section of
detailed glosses) of some of the leading ideas and motifs of Florentine
Neoplatonism and Neoplatonic mythology, and as tantalizing evidence of
major disagreements with  Pico's distinguished mentor and "brother
Platonist," Marsilio Ficino, the author of the age's most influential love
treatise and its most subtle poetic theologian. Pico attacks Marsilio by name;
he faults his logic and acuity; he complains of his ignorance of particular
authorities; and he takes issue with his views on the nature of true
friendship, on the metaphysical status of beauty and love, on the meaning of
Plato's two Venuses, on the correct interpretation of the Symposium's
presentation at 179D of the Orpheus and Eurydicestory, and so on. Whether
Ficino saw these contentious notes is unclear. We certainly have nothing
like the rebuke he administered in the forty-ninth chapterof his Parmenides
Commentary where he chastises Pico's decision in the De Ente et Uno of
1491 to adopt the Aristotelian errorof predicatingbeing of the One.
   Pico himself was clearly not satisfied with the first draft(s) of the
Commento, for he continued to fiddle with it until his death in 1494. Our
evidence here is later manuscripts where the material has been rather
strikingly redistributed and reordered.More striking still is that, soon after
his death, his friends worked over the Commento and excised the offensive
references to Ficino and some of the more combative and heretical remarks.
It was this revised and expurgated version that appearedin the Benivieni
editions and under Pico's own name eventually in the Basel 1557, 1572, and
1601 editions  of his Opera Omnia. In addition, the Commento was
cannibalized by Pico for his Heptaplus, by Benivieni for a series of marginal
glosses to the 1500 edition of his own poems (an edition, ironically, that did
not include the "Amor dalle cui"), and by Benivieni's grand-nephew when
he was drafting a proposal for yet another edition of Girolamo's collected
poems. The whole intriguing story has been unravelled this century by
Eugenio Garin and Roberto Ridolfi. Despite this unpropitious and quixotic
fortuna, the Commento did make an impact on English Renaissance poets,
notably Spenser, Chapman, and Jonson via the partial Frenchtranslation by
Chappuys;and some of its allegorizations have been mined by art historians
working on Botticelli and Michelangelo.

   This is the second complete English translation (the first was by StephenJ.
Salchenbergerfor a JohnsHopkins dissertation in 1967).It is based on Garin's
authoritative 1942 critical edition, but takes into account two MSS brought
to light since, both of them representingthe expurgatedstage (one has only a
handful of interesting variants; the other is of even less use in that it gives us
Benivieni's personal adaptation,not transcription,of the work). Sears Jayne
has decided to follow the order of the material in the unexpurgatedstage,
while Garin adoptedthe orderin the expurgatedMSS and the 1519 and later
editions. This is intellectually defensible, but it does not make for ease of
reference to the Garin text. The translation is fluent and accurate. It is
prefacedby a useful introduction and a controversialstemma reconstruction
that elevates the status of Benivieni's adaptationand also postulates four lost
drafts for the unexpurgated stage and a lost draft for the expurgated.Even
more useful are the end notes which suggest a wide range of references and
point to possible  solutions  of a number of the many interpretative
 difficulties. The bibliography, however, has incorrect entries, proofing
errors,and a swarm of missing French accents (forexample, sub Dagens, Di
Napoli, Dorez, Festugiere,Lebegue,Lefranc,Mathieu, M6nch, Nardi, Robin,
Schmidt, Schmitt, Simonin, Sozzi, Weber, Wind, and Zanier). There is no
index.

   In this book, W. S. Di Piero presents a selection of the poetry of the
Lucanianwriter, LeonardoSinisgalli (1908-1981), in facing-pagetranslation.
Though hailed at the beginning of his careerin Italy as a majorpoetic talent,
Sinisgalli has remained relatively unknown abroad.Today, a few years after
his death, his earlierwork is recognized as one of the leading examples of the
so-called second generation of Hermetic poetry, whereas his later work
reflects the struggle to create a different poetic expression in the altered
existential climate of the postwar period. Sinisgalli is also rememberedas a
personality of extraordinarilydiverse talents and interests. Throughoutmost
of his careerhe earnedhis living as a technical and commercial designer,but
it is for his work as a painter, filmmaker (of skillfully crafteddocumentaries)
and writer that he earned a prominent place in Italian culture of the time. In
an age of excessive specialization he pursued the ideal of a diversified
humanist and artist.
