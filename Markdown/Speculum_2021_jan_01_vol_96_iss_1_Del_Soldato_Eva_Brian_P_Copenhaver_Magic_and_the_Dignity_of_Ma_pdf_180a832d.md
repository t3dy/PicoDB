Giovanni Pico della Mirandola’s Oration (1486) has for decades been considered a sort of
manifesto of ﬁfteenth-century philosophy of man, one that celebrates human freedom and dig-
nity. After all, the text was entitled Oration on the Dignity of Man, was it not? The answer is
actually no. For starters, though it was associated with the text very early, this was not the title
Pico gave to his work. And the meaning of Pico’s Oration was more complex and, in many
ways, less appealing to modern readers. In Magic and the Dignity of Man, Brian Copenhaver
painstakingly reconstructs the story, or rather stories, of how Pico and his Oration were read
and misread over the centuries. This is very much a project of love for Copenhaver, who has
already published extensively on Pico’s Oration, and the book’s imposing size reﬂects his long
engagement with the text.
  The ﬁrst chapter deals with the word dignity, a term which, according to Copenhaver,
deceived post-Kantian readers. As said, the title under which the Oration circulated for cen-
turies was not of Pico’s creation, and the early modern meaning of “dignity” was different
from the one of “autonomy,” typically associated with Kant. Copenhaver claims that the
interpretation of the Oration as a celebration of human freedom was also supported by the
fact that few actually read the text in its entirety and investigated its content, purpose, and
sources. As Copenhaver has argued in previous articles, Pico’s Oration would be not, in
fact, a celebration of human excellence, but a mystical text that outlines a path to becoming
godly, according to a precise philosophical curriculum, in which kabbalistic knowledge plays
a major part. The Oration served as an introduction to the 900 Conclusions Pico wanted to
defend before the Roman Curia in 1487, and would mirror the kabbalistic organization of the
theses themselves. Copenhaver acknowledges and makes an excellent use of the contributions of
Chaim Wirszubski, who offered a full investigation of the presence of kabbalah in Pico’s works.
Wirszubski’s studies did not have widespread or immediate impact on scholarship, because
of their extreme technicality. The ﬁnal section of Copenhaver’s book (chapters 11–13), which
is a commentary on the Oration read in parallel with the 900 Conclusions (and the textual
appendixes A–C are a thoughtful addition), especially allows one to appreciate the inﬂuence
of the kabbalah on Pico.
  The emphasis on kabbalah is particularly important, since much of the previous scholar-
ship, discussed by Copenhaver in chapters 2–10, either minimized or dismissed its impor-
tance for Pico. Indeed, as a result of historical and local circumstances, Pico himself was subject
to all kinds of treatments. He was at times celebrated as a wunderkind, attacked for his empty
scholarship, sanctiﬁed for his Christian conversion under the guidance of Savonarola, and

reviled for his fanaticism. Copenhaver’s widespread survey of Pico’s fortunes and misfortunes
includes all major and minor scholarly contributions on Pico, occasionally noting absences,
and entertainingly recovering unexpected voices (including tourist guides!). Italian scholar-
ship on Pico has the lion’s share, and especially Eugenio Garin, one of the most important Pico
scholars of the last century. Garin’s attitude toward Pico was rather complex, to the extent that
he refused to have his pioneering 1937 monograph on Pico reprinted. Copenhaver focuses
on Garin’s publications on Pico, but it would have been interesting to highlight the fact that,
toward the end of his career, Garin intensely studied Alberti and Pomponazzi, that is, authors
that worked as explicit and implicit opposites to “his” Pico (on this see Michele Ciliberto,
Eugenio Garin: Un intellettuale del Novecento [2011]).
  Copenhaver suggests that discussing the scholarly reception of Pico and his Oration is
tantamount, in many ways, to discussing the meaning of Renaissance philosophy, and also
to rethinking the convenience of the term “humanism.” At the end of the chapter that deals
with existentialistic readings of Pico (158), he advocates for the replacement of the ambig-
uous, and potentially deceptive, term humanism with a more neutral classicism.
  The fourteen chapters in the book are accompanied by four appendices, including the
abovementioned English translations of the Oration, a selection of the 900 Conclusions
and a summary of their contents. There is also a glossary that makes the book more approach-
able to non-specialist readers.
  Authors and texts are frequently reappropriated, misunderstood, and exploited, and the
story of Pico and his Oration is no different from many others. Yet the relevance this text
has acquired in the last two centuries, for deﬁning the Renaissance, fully justiﬁes the scope
of Copenhaver’s investigations. Whether one agrees or disagrees with all of his conclusions,
we can only be grateful to Copenhaver for emphasizing in such a stimulating way the com-
plexity of the Oration, a text which remains a manifesto of the religious inquietude and intel-
lectual curiosity of the late ﬁfteenth century.

                Eva Del Soldato, University of Pennsylvania

An ugly Breton boy who came to be constable of France; a poor knight, for whom the king
paid thousands of ﬂorins in ransom; a witty commander who was followed by dukes and
brigands alike. Such is the legendary reputation of Bertrand du Guesclin (c. 1323–80),
one of the most notable personalities of the ﬁrst half of the Hundred Years’ War. His life
story has come down to us as a mix of fact and ﬁction, in the form of an epic tale of
25,000 verses written by his ﬁrst biographer, Cuvelier. This skillful propagandist wrote
the Chanson de Bertrand du Guesclin right after the death of the knight that he portrays,
between 1380 and 1385. He does so in a vivid tale that conjures up lively images and that
captivates its audience with exciting renderings of battles as well as funny anecdotes. This
text is now available in an English translation by Nigel Bryant, which holds up brilliantly to
this standard: in modern English, it is still a sparkling tale for the modern reader to enjoy,
and it is certainly worthy of the wide readership at which it is aimed.
  Indeed, this publication can be seen as a revaluation of a frequently underestimated source,
since it has made the text accessible to a general, non-French-speaking audience. As a schol-
arly edition, on the other hand, this translation has its shortcomings, since the introduction
to the text and the justiﬁcation of the working method lack precision and contain some out-
dated information.
