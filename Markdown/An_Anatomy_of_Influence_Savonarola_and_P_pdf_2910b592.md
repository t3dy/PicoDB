                An Anatomy of Influence: Savonarola and Pico’s Hidden Affinities1



       At first glance, Girolamo Savonarola and Giovanni Pico della Mirandola scarcely appear

cognate. Indeed, the men barely seem to belong to the same historical milieu. Historians have cast

Savonarola as a medieval book-burning preacher and Pico as the neo-Platonic prodigy who wrote

the banner of the secular Renaissance, the Oration on the Dignity of Man. But both men inhabited

the same historical milieu and shared a long and profound friendship.2 While friendship does not

require shared belief, the question arises: what was the source of their affinity? Were their views

compatible, and if so, how? Why was Pico drawn into Savonarola’s orbit and what was the nature

of their relationship? 3 While I cannot offer a complete response to all of these questions today, I

will here offer an account of some of the affinities between Pico’s and Savonarola’s thought and

point out that both their views have suffered from a certain degree of interpretive distortion,

though for differing historiographical reasons. On the one hand, Pico’s Oration has been

interpreted in a wholly secular fashion and his remaining works have been largely ignored.4 On the

other hand, Savonarola’s political engagement and extremism have eclipsed much of the

theological work which was the foundation of his popularity.5 Here I focus on Pico’s Oration but

       Pico (1463-94) and Savonarola’s (1452-98) relationship probably began in the late 1470s

when they were both studying at Bologna, then continued a few years later while both were

studying at Ferrara. A definite encounter can be placed at around1480, when Pico, who would

have been 17, was invited to the Dominican chapter at Reggio Emilia for a disputation given by

Savonarola, who would have been 28 at the time.6 In 1486, Pico wrote his Oration. The following

year, after his proposal to debate his 900 Theses was rejected, Pico fled to France, only to be

imprisoned there as a heretic by papal servants. Lorenzo de Medici offered Pico safe harbor in

Florence, and it was at this moment that Pico seems to have converted to religious orthodoxy. As a

consequence of his revived religious convictions, he persuaded Lorenzo to request Savonarola’s

presence at Florence. In 1490, Pico began to attend Savonarola’s sermons and the two remained

       Again, on the surface, the incongruities between Pico and Savonarola would seem to

outweigh the correspondences. To wit: Savonarola was outspoken against Medician tyranny; Pico

supported the Medici, at least for a time. Pico was a lover of women, Savonarola, deeply

suspicious of them.17 Savonarola adamantly disavowed philosophy while Pico enshrined it as the

road to divine wisdom.18 And the differences between Pico’s and Savonarola’s programs are easily

identifiable. Savonarola’s scheme called for personal conversion and purgation and saw its fullest

expression in millennialist expectation and public spectacle. Pico’s 900 Theses were a call of a

very different sort: an invitation to learned men to engage in public, intellectual debate. Pico

seemed to exult in forensic aesthetics, at least until his conversion; Savonarola frowned on such

frivolities, at least in principle. But if one looks more carefully at their writings, Pico and Savonarola

shared many common assumptions, and today I will touch on those of a theological-mystical and

rhetorical nature.

concerned to impart a sense of structure to past and present history via theological analysis, and

they both therefore advanced ambitious and comprehensive programs to this end.22.

      Both Pico and Savonarola reckoned with a diversity of bodies of thought. Pico’s synthesis

of Christian, Hebrew, Greek and other traditions was openly inclusive. Savonarola, despite

declarations to the contrary, drew upon Platonic, Aristotelian, and other pagan philosophies in

both his cosmological and political designs. And both advanced relatively conservative theologies

based largely upon Dionysus the Areopagite’s Neoplatonism. Most importantly, both struggled to

articulate man’s relationship with the divine; in particular, the extent and manner in which man

might connect with God—responding, perhaps, to the chasm which Scotism had opened up in this

regard. While their methods were very different, at their theological core, their programs were

consonant.

      While Savonarola and Pico differed in their views toward natural knowledge,23 they agreed

that above all terrestrial knowledge stood a mysterious and unknowable God. For Savonarola, no

name could compass god’s “indescribable majesty.” God could neither be “seen by bodily eyes nor

grasped by created intellect, nor explained by the tongues of men or of angels.” Pico, too,

considered God far beyond human comprehension. “God is neither an intellectual nor intelligent

nature, but unspeakably exalted above all intellect and knowledge.”24 In Of Being and Unity, Pico

wrote,


       Of Him there is no definition, neither is there a name nor a science of Him. He is neither darkness
       nor light, neither error nor truth, in short, every affirmation and every negation in regard to Him is
        equally impossible.25



For both, God was incomprehensible and unnameable; beyond language and even human

thought.26

ascending through stages of contemplation and illumination. Pico wrote how through

contemplation, one might move “rapt to the heights of love” and characterized the end of spiritual

      Thus far, the theological views of Pico and Savonarola are relatively unproblematic. But

both of them encountered the same problems. Did anything else besides likeness fuel man’s

spiritual ascent to God? Was there anything else man might do to obtain or maintain divine union?

This last was the perennial Christian dilemma of grace versus free will. Might man actively ascend

the ladder toward God? Savonarola’s view was more consistently and explicitly orthodox, for he

constantly spoke about grace as informing all human activity. Man might desire salvation, but

grace fueled even this. One could not consider any aspect of the process of spiritual ascent to

depend upon one's own will, for to imply volition was to slip into Pelagianism. Man could not even

claim authorship of his own thoughts: We “are not able to think something on our own as if it

came from us.”48 Instead, one had to see all human effort in terms of merely cooperating with God

and to consider one’s ascent from the sensual world as aided by infused grace. Savonarola

      Both Pico and Savonarola avoided heresy by maintaining an orthodox separation between

the human and the divine. God and man inhabited—had to inhabit—separate spheres.66 But this

inevitably led to a problem: if God and man were so distinct, how did God communicate with man

at times other than those of rapt, ecstatic union?67 Both Pico and Savonarola seemed preoccupied

with identifying a means of direct correspondence with or from God that avoided long chains of

angelic mediation. Both resolved this problem through the notion of revelation.

        Pico, like Savonarola, inclined to metaphors of intellectual and illuminatory revelation: “a

greater, even divine, intellect illuminates the intellect in us…”. He also placed great emphasis on

God’s revealed word, and considered pagan and Jewish philosophies to have been mere

signposts along the way toward ultimate truth as embodied in scripture.68 He considered the word

of the apostles and prophets inerrant, and seems to have been moving toward the position of sola

scriptura.69 Savonarola too employed illuminative metaphors for how God disclosed himself to

man: “All God’s saints and elect receive from God a special light that inclines them to believe firmly

those things which God does or shows in His Church…” This illumination bestows infallibility: such

people “do not err.”70 In regards to his own person, illumination bestowed prophecy. He himself,

had not earned or merited this gift of supernatural light, but having received it, he knew for certain

that his revelations were true. But for Savonarola scripture and doctors were merely ‘exterior ways’

      Both Pico and Savonarola were interested in providing a role for man that might offer him

freedom of thought, movement, and interpretation, but one which would also avoid overstating

human efficacy. Both advocated purificatory rites in preparation toward mystical ascent. God’s

decision to communicate to man in mysteries led both to privilege a kind of freeform exegesis,

which each used to justify his life’s work. And while both were wedded to a form of inward

contemplation, each sought public—if not spectacular—rhetorical engagement, while in theory

remaining opposed to rhetorical exercise for the sake of mere eloquence.

        In the end, Pico’s and Savonarola’s respective projects were far from identical and there

remain important divergences in attitude, temperament, and methods between the two. But their

views were far from incommensurable and they seem to have found a great deal upon which to

agree. And since we are able to find so many points of agreement and since we can locate

examples of Savonarola employing Neoplatonic concepts and metaphors some years after Pico

first employed the same, perhaps its is most appropriate to frame the connection between these

two figures not as one of distant admiration on the part of Pico for the elder Savonarola, but rather

as one at the very least, of reciprocal, mutual exchange.
