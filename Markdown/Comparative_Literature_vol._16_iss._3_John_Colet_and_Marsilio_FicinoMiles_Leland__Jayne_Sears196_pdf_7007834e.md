   In John Colet and Marsilio Ficino, Professor Jayne now provides the Latin
text and a careful English translation of this important new source material. The
three flyleaf letters (two from Ficino, one from Colet) suggest that the younger
Colet's enthusiasm for Ficino's writings must have been considerable since, after
returning (in 1496) to Enlgland, Colet obviously wrote to the Florentine in ex-
tremely flattering terms. Ficino's answer (Letter B) refers to Colet's "devoted
and eloquent" letter, and indicates that Colet had affirmed himself an "admirer"
and "lover" who idolized Ficino as a "sun." Colet replied in Letter C: "While I am
reading your books, I seem to live, just as I should be more alive if I could see
you in person, Marsilio" (Jayne, p. 82).
    It would be difficult to overestimate the value of these letters, especially Letter
C. The tradition that Colet visited Florence, and sat at the feet of Ficino, has
been wistfully repeated by Colet biographers all the way up through Marriott
(1933) and IHunt (1956). Actually, there have been gentle warnings for almost
half a century that Colet probably studied at Rome rather than Florence. Pietro
Egidi in 1914 (Fonti per 1a storia d'Italia, XLV), Wallace Ferguson in 1934
(AHR, XXXIX), and especially V. J. Flynn in 1938 ("Englishmen in Rome dur-
ing the Renaissance," MP, XXXVI, 121-138), all supplied tantalizing evidence
that Colet's Italian activities centered around Rome. Now the Colet-Ficino cor-
respondence published by Professor Jayne should permanently dispose of what
was an appealing but erroneous tradition. The All Souls volume seems to constitute
conclusive evidence that Colet (1) never visited Florence; (2) never met Ficino;
 (3) was well read in some of Ficino's books and. at least at the time of the cor-
respondence (1498-99?), admired them.
   In his imaginative and commendably provocative Introduction, Professor Jayne
plunges boldly into disputatious waters by seeking to construct some meaningful
order of composition for Colet's works. Necessarily relying chiefly on internal
evidence, he argues that Colet must have read Ficino's Theologia platonica
 (1482) and Pico della Mirandola's Heptaplus (1489) around April 1498, as
preparation for his own Genesis commentary, the Letters to Radulphus. Such
reading, Jayne believes, explains the contrast between the pre-Radulphus Exposi-
tion of Romans I-V, which shows no interest in Greek thought, and the post-
Radulphus Lectures on Romans VI-XI, which cites Pico and Ficino as authorities
while simultaneously explaining Paul's meaning in terms of analogous doctrines
from the Platonic tradition. According to this scheme, Colet again dropped his
Romans project to study Dionysius (whence Colet's Dionysian abstracts) and
the Epistolae. He then added a supplement (Chapter XVI) to his Celestial Hier-
archy abstract before moving on to the Corinthians commentary, the De sacra-
nmentis, and the final phase of the Romans project, Lectures on Romans XII-XVI
 (Jayne, pp. 25-37, especially charts pp. 33, 37).
   Generally speaking, this suggested order of composition is compellingly logical
and based on sound textual evidence. For example, Jayne's contention that Colet
read Pico's Heptaplus as preparation for his own Mosaic :Account is quite reason-
able in light of Colet's paraphrase from Pico's commentary in Letters to Radulphus
 (p. 10). Despite this paraphrase, however, it is debatable whether Colet's com-
mentary "slavishly follow:s" Mirandola. Actually, there are five hierarchial

the elements; (3) propagating power; (4) eternity-perpetuity-time; (5) the
divine, angelic, heavenly, and sublunar worlds. Only the last of these shows much
evidence of having been inspired by Pico. A more likely chief model for the
Radulphian letters is Augustine, whose Christianized adaptation of Plotinian
creation Colet appears to imitate in almost every detail (see the documentation in
the reviewer's John Colet and the Platonic Tradition, pp. 31-65).
  A more crucial problem is that Jayne's order of composition would confine
the bulk of Colet's writing to the period October 1497-October 1499 (see Jayne's
chart, p. 37). Yet Erasmus says in his letters (ed. Allen, IV, 515) that Colet lec-
tured on all the Pauline Epistles. If Jayne is correct this would mean, by my
calculation, that in twenty-four months Colet produced a minimum of twenty
works, including the De comrposiion s tion corporis Christi mystico. Such an
output seems startling even if we concede that some of the items are short, and
even if we assume that Colet had no other major demands on his time as he
moved toward the B.D. (1500). Some scholars might want to argue for lengthen-
ing Colet's early writing period so as to embrace the years 1496-1500.
  Throughout his resourceful Introduction, Professor Jayne reiterates the posi-
tion that Colet's youthful enthusiasm for Platonism "cooled" during the last
twenty years of his life (pp. 39, 54, 70, 77, 78). If most of Colet's writing must be
assigned to his youthfiul Oxford period, one might question whether there is
sufficient evidence on which to argue this point. In any event, if we examine the
few works extant from Colet's later career as pastor and educator-practical
works in which we have little reason to expect philosophical speculation-we find
ideas which are surely indebted to various segments of the Platonic tradition. At
the outset of the Right Fruitful Monition pamphlet (1505-10?), for example,
Colet would appear to be consciously citing the Socratic doctrine of self-knowl-
edge (Lupton's Life, Appendix D, pp. 305-306). Further, the Convocation Sermon
 (1512) combines an unmistakable allusion to the Republic with an ingenious ap-
plication of Plato's doctrine that the harmonized soul governs the body (compare
Lupton's Appendix C, p. 302 with Republic, III, 403, IV, 443-4, and with Phaedo,
80, 94). Even Colet's last major address, the sermon at Wolsey's installation
 (1515), is clearly built around the Dionysian angelic orders and the Areopagite's
notion that earthly things symbolize the things of heaven (see Lupton, Life, p.
195). The preserved extracts of this sermon would not seem to confirm the impli-
cation (Jayne, p. 29) that Colet turned against Dionysius after Grocyn's 1501
lecture at St. Paul's. After all, the Grocyn expose merely demoted Dionysius to
the status of Plato and Plotinus. For these pagan thinkers Colet still had a
qualified regard.
   Corollary to Jayne's thesis of Colet's "cooling" Platonism is the thesis (p. 54)
that Colet approached Ficino's Epistolae with enthusiasm but left it with "an
attitude of antagonism." In arguing this proposition, Professor Jayne skillfully
documents the text-marginalia cleavage between Colet and Ficino on the nature
of man and the value of academic learning (pp. 56 ff., 70 ff.). An even more
serious breach between the two men occurs where Ficino observes that the soul
alone is immortal. At Marginalia 36 Colet repudiates the whole passage with two
terse words, "the resurrection," and in the subsequent Corinthians commentary
 (p. 63) again insists on the Christian doctrine of "complete resurrection."
   Yet the paradox of Colet's intellectual character is such that he seems to have

Jayne's chronology, we find ideas in Colet's post-Epistolae writings which, even
if not derived from that Ficinian work, are nonetheless parallel to it. Such evi-
dence hardly suggests that Colet carried away a hostility for such ideas as he
found them in the Epistolae. For example, the theme of Ficino's essay, "That a
Friend Is in His Friend," annotated by Colet at Marginalia 23, is later paralleled
in the Right Fruitful Monition, p. 309: "As a man loveth, so he is, for the lover
is in the thing loved more properly than in himself." Similarly, Colet's view of a
priesthood of all sanctified men (De sacramentis, Rowe translation, pp. 30, 34)
is quite similar to the Epistolae position, paraphrased by Colet at Marginalia 50,
that "the perfect priesthood... consists... in the desire for wisdom, in prayer
hope, and love."
  The possibility that such parallels are coincidental is considerably lessened by
the fact that in every instance the given idea, as found in the Epistolae, has been
annotated by Colet for later use. This is not to suggest that the bulk of Colet's
ideas derive from Ficino. But, again assuming the general accuracy of Jayne's
chronology, the evidence is overwhelming that in many instances the Epistolae
reinforced notions with which Colet was already cautiously tinkering. The result
of this reinforcement, for Colet, was obviously to ignite an uncertain Platonic
spark into a Platonic bonfire.
  For example, in the Epistolae tract "De raptu Pauli" Ficino associated the
Pauline virtues with the three Dionysian angelic groups and their respective
functions. Colet had actually toyed with such an intermeshing earlier (Eccles.
Hier., p. 115). Now in his copy of the Epistolae we find him-with apparent
excitement-elaborately reproducing the correspondences in tabular form at Mar-
ginalia 41. These same correspondences then reverberate like a symphony in
Colet's later supplement to the Celestial Hierarchy abstract, pp. 36-43. Similarly,
the concept of God as a Sun, which Colet no doubt derived initially from Plotinus
(Enleads, IV, iv, 12, V, i, 6) and Augustine (De civitate dei, VIII, 9), seldom
appears in his earlier works. But the idea is extensively annotated by Colet in
Epistolae Marginalia 7, 37, 40, 41, 44 (twice). Then suddenly, in the subsequent
Lectures on Corinthians, the God-Sun analogy bursts forth in at least seven differ-
ent passages (pp. 4, 58-59, 125, 126, 127-129, 132, 152). Such evidence suggests
that the influence of Ficino on Colet was greater than Professor Jayne seems
willing to concede. If Colet "depaganized" Ficino and rejected the Florentine's
heretical notions, he also clearly accepted and used those of Ficino's views which
were compatible with orthodox Christianity.
   In addition to a richly documented commentary and raw material which opens
up new avenues of research, Jayne supplies what is probably the most compre-
hensive Colet bibliography ever assembled. It includes data both on manuscript
sources and on readily available printed sources in both Latin and English for
many primary works, plus an invaluable index to Colet-Erasmus correspondence
found in Allen's Latin edition (1906-58) and Nichol's English translation (1901).
Together with the Colet bibliography assembled by Carl Meyer of Concordia
Seminary (available in mimeographed form), we now have an exhaustive index
to works by and about Colet as of 1963. Thus Professor Jayne's book takes its
place as a pivotal work in the modern revival of interest in Dean Colet.
