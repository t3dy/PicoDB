                         INTRODUCTION

Pico’s Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem has been referred to as
‘the most extensive and incisive attack on astrology that the world had
yet seen’.1 The text was never completed due to Giovanni Pico’s sudden
death in 1494 and was published posthumously by his nephew Gianfran-
cesco Pico della Mirandola in 1496.2 Though  this large  treatise, which
consists of twelve books, is stylistically less than perfect, Pico is consistent
in pursuing his argument through the whole work, in an attempt to dis-
credit astrology as a dangerous superstition.3 One of the main polemical
strategies against astrology used by Pico was to ﬁnd contradictions within
astrological writings, analysing different sources in detail. This formed the
basis  for  Giovanni  Pico’s  attack:  his  analysis  of  astrological  practices
came  ex  principio, that  is, from studying Claudius Ptolemy’s writings on

from what he considered a group of less gifted astrologers. That is why in the
Disputationes he put Ptolemy at the beginning of the long astrological tradition.

George of Trebizond completed his own translation of the Almagest and
‘accompanied his version of Ptolemy’s Almagest with a commentary of enor-
mous length’.9 He intented to dedicate it to Nicholas V but had to abandon
this idea.10 In his work, George sought to purify Ptolemy’s original ideas from
medieval, both Arabic and Latin, intepretations, as well as to oppose Bessarion,
who had advised him to use Theon of Alexandria’s commentary as an example.
The attempt to prove that Theon of Alexandria’s inﬂuence on medieval
thinkers was negative, incited a response from one of Bessarion’s most famous
pupils, Regiomontanus, who wrote a special treatise against George known as
the Defensio Theonis contra Trapezuntium.11 Other ﬁgures, such as Georg von
Peuerbach and Niccolo Perotti, were also involved in the debates, which along
with the famous Plato – Aristotle controversy formed the essence of the Bessa-
rion–George conﬂict.12 Later in the sixteenth century Ptolemy was in the
centre of Girolamo Cardano’s ‘restored’ Greek astrology.13 Thus, the idea of
reconsidering the position of Ptolemy within the astrological tradition seems to
be typical for Renaissance scholars. Pico’s Disputationes was an important land-
mark in that astrological debate.
  Despite the apparent existence of such an astrological tradition, Pico comes
to the conclusion that Ptolemy and his followers contradicted each other in key
theoretical positions and in their astrological practice. In this approach,
Giovanni Pico differentiates between Ptolemy, an astrologer of a higher level,
and his followers, who distorted his legacy. In order to conﬁrm his idea of the
fundamental dichotomy within the astrological tradition Pico used two modes
of argumentation. On the one hand, he shows that Abu Ma‘shar and others
often made mistakes in interpreting Ptolemaic astrological terms and as a result
used them in a completely different sense. On the other hand, commenting on
the Centiloquium, Giovanni Pico constantly disputes the points that contradict
Ptolemy’s other texts, but at the same time agree with the writings of more

      THE USE OF ASTROLOGICAL TECHNIQUES AND ITS CONTROVERSIES

Three examples illustrate the ﬁrst case of errors in the usage of astrological
terms. The ﬁrst one deals with the solar eclipse for which Giovanni Pico turned
to the passage in the Tetrabiblos that discusses this phenomenon. According to
Ptolemy, someone watching the solar eclipse should note the place where it
occurs and calculate the angle from this place:17 if the eclipse occurred in the
ninth region of sky, then the angle of the tenth is calculated and so on. How-
ever, Pico claims that Ptolemy’s followers incorrectly translated the term soῦ
ἑpolemot jemsqot as anguli praecedentis, i.e. the angle of the previous area, which
completely changed the very principle of this calculation.18 In Pico’s opinion,
this error occurs in some authors and it was caused by the incorrect translation
of the concept.19 Continuing the examination of the misinterpretation of this
passage, Giovanni Pico gives another example. Speaking about calculating the
eclipse angle, Ptolemy wrote that the angle might refer to both the eastern and
the middle parts of the skyline. Pico adds that ‘the Greek commentator’, the

over sixty editions being made. It was also translated into English by the famous
English humanist Thomas Linacre. The popularity of the Sphaera by ‘Proclus’

seems to be a rather traditional text in terms of astrological/astronomical calcu-

and humanists about its attribution to Proclus. Giovanni Pico most probably
shared that doubt and hence preferred to call the author of the Sphaera ‘the
Greek commentator’, without mentioning the name of the prominent Neopla-
tonic philosopher.

He admits that ancient astrologers believed in the existence of eight celestial
spheres – an idea later supported by many famous philosophers, including
Plato and Aristotle.30 The concept of the existence of only eight spheres had
dominated for a long time and Ptolemy came under its inﬂuence. Giovanni
Pico claims that many followers wrongly attributed to Ptolemy the idea of a
ninth sphere, but in fact, he had never written about that: Pico tried to prove
this by quoting the Almagest. According to this text, Ptolemy afﬁrmed that
mobile stars belonged to the eighth sphere, while immovable planets belonged
to the remaining seven. Ptolemy’s medieval commentators assumed that apart
from the moving stars of the eighth sphere the ‘teacher’ had deﬁned different
heavenly bodies as parts of the ninth sphere, which received impulses from the
Primum Mobile. According to Pico, however, Ptolemy limited his celestial uni-
verse to the eighth sphere and said nothing about the existence of a ninth
one.31 The idea of a ninth sphere was ﬁrst introduced by King Alfonso of Castile
and shared by two authors, who in Pico’s treatise are referred to as ‘ancient’
and ‘excellent mathematicians’: Proclus and Leo the Jew (the latter, appa-
rently,  is Levi ben Gershon, better known in the West as Gersonides).32
Giovanni Pico explains why medieval astronomers and astrologers needed to
introduce a ninth sphere. According to them, non-wandering stars move from
West to East, while the celestial sphere with other stars move in the opposite
direction. The ninth sphere, directing heavenly bodies from East to West, was
introduced in order to explain this mysterious phenomenon.33 Some philoso-
phers offered their own solutions, for instance, by endowing the sky with Soul
or attributing to the highest sphere the main function of the moving impulse.34
Pico admits that some, like Campanus of Novara, Messahala, Leopold of Austria
and Alfonso of Castile, had even tried to solve the problem by introducing a
tenth sphere. To give their notion some credibility they had attributed a similar
idea to Ptolemy and as a consequence many medieval philosophers, such as
Albert the Great, mistakenly followed this ‘false idea’, as Giovanni Pico had
called it.35 But besides all these contrivances, Pico believed astrologers were in a
difﬁcult and uncertain position, full of contradictions and dangerous to the
integrity of their theory. He further supported his opinion by citing the incerti-
tude of the basic planet order relative to the Sun.36

                          CONCLUSION

To summarize, in the Disputationes adversus astrologiam divinatricem, dealing with
the history of astrology, Giovanni Pico della Mirandola focused on three trea-
tises of Claudius Ptolemy, subjecting them to scrutiny and criticism. In this dis-
cussion, Ptolemy appears as a major astrological authority who laid the
foundations of astrological theories and practices, largely distorted in the subse-
quent tradition. In his intention to reconsider Ptolemy’s legacy, Pico followed
his medieval predecessors including Nicolas Oresme94 and the Greek emigres,
though he presented the controversial character of the astrological tradition in
a more systematic way. However, Pico’s attack did not lead him to advocate a
Ptolemaic astrology; for Pico, astrology is a dangerous superstition. Pico’s attack
on Ptolemy and the astrological tradition was subject to numerous debates after
the publication of the Disputationes. While Kepler was inﬂuenced by but
remained critical of Pico’s technical achievements,95 Pico’s nephew, Gianfran-
cesco Pico della Mirandola thought that with removing errors from ancient
texts including the Centiloquium and thus doing an important philological work
Giovanni Pico undermined astrology as a discipline.96
  In Books II and III of the Disputationes Pico aimed at revealing its incompati-
bility with philosophy and religion. At the same time, unlike most of his prede-
cessors, he did not limit himself to these polemical issues. With two thirds of
the Disputationes dedicated to astrological texts, practices and techniques, Pico’s
treatise combined theological or philosophical arguments against astrology
with systematic criticism of its practical side. The ways in which Pico interpreted
his sources, resorting to some manipulations with texts and terms, shed light
on his polemical strategies. Over his itineraire philosophique, Pico did not refrain
from interpreting philosophical arguments in ways that were contrary to their
original versions. Thus, the ambivalent use of sources in the Disputationes is of a
great interest, speciﬁcally in the context of Pico’s intellectual laboratory. Pico’s
reading of astrological sources leads him to three main arguments against
astrology. First, judicial astrology, which deals with predictions, is a false form of
knowledge. To  it, Pico opposes ‘mathematical astrology’, which observes
