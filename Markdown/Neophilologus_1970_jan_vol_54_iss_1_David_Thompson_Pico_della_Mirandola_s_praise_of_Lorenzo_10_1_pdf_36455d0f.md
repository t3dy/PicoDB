    Even  if we should grant that Petrarch's writings are more elegant,
we must  still consider the difference between his subject and Lorenzo's:
"Reading Petrarch we find him delicate, polished, pleasing; but if he had
had to handle your thoughts we should find him crabbed, rude and un-
pleasing, for you can see that whenever he approaches any such thing he
is shrill, entangled or knotty; his style falls as the thought rises." Probe
the ornamented surface "and you would sometimes see there the void of
Epicurus:  either no meaning underneath,  or a feeble and  trivial one."
So  if Lorenzo cannot be said to excel Petrarch as a stylist, neither is he
Petrarch's inferior.
   Pico's comparison of Lorenzo with Dante opens on a very Horatian
note: "Many perhaps would quarrel about him, for there are lots of peo-
ple who in comparing writers do not so much reckon merits as count years:
they bid others read the old writers with reverence while they themselves
cannot read contemporary writers without  ill-will." 8 Lorenzo's style is
far superior to Dante's, for even Dante's supporters admit that in this
respect he leaves much  to be desired; and  if they assert that Dante  is
nevertheless loftier and more sublime in his thoughts, Pico replies:
Pray tell me, what is so wonderful about his philosophizing on a philosophical subject,
which by its very nature forced him to do so and of itself supplied his thoughts? If he
deals with God, the soul and the blessed, he repeats what Thomas and Augustine wrote
on these subjects, which he pondered and meditated upon constantly and assiduously.
Then again, in recounting great matters public and private, it was not so splendid of
Dante to have done this as it would have been shameful not to.

  So much for that prodigious learning which had impressed humanists
as diverse (and learned) as Boccaccio, Salutati and Filelfo.
   If Dante's subject almost guaranteed him a sublime poem, Lorenzo's
militates against it; therefore Lorenzo deserves the greater credit: "What
you offer was undoubtedly a work of the highest genius, to make ama-
tory themes philosophical and, by covering them with charm, make lovely
things that are somewhat harsh by their own severity."

topic in an extraordinary fashion. Landino had observed that Lorenzo's

tempo hanno consumato nelrozio literario ene gli studii," but that it was
a greater marvel for some one engaged in ,,tanto e si varie e  si grandi sue
occupazioni nel publico governo." As examples of those who have spent

  To Bruni and Manetti, Dante and Petrarch had served as contrasting
examples, Dante of the active, civic life, Petrarch of the comtemplative,
literary career. But even in Manetti's Vitae the contrast had been blurred
by a suggestion that Dante could have accomplished much more had he
not been burdened by political duties (or a wife); and Landino's Platonic
Dante owes more to Manetti than to Bruni. 9 Dante's civic stature has
become attenuated indeed if Pico can class him with Petrarch; from this
description one would never surmise that Dante had been a Prior of the
Republic of Florence.
  Although Pico's letter may strike us initially as outrageous flattery, it
is well to keep in mind that the Count of Mirandola was no mere kept
intellectual; however extravagant his estimate of Lorenzo, Pico's enco-
mium represents an important landmark in Italian literary history.
  An encomiastic letter of a century earlier may help us to grasp its
significance. Soon after hearing of Petrarch's death, Coluceio Salutati
has written to Roberto Guidi, Count of Battifolle, about his grief "at
seeing extinguished not just the shining splendor of Florence, but the
light of all Italy, the light of our age." 10 Praising Petrarch's literary
works, Salutati uses an argument similar to that which Pico was to em-
ploy in praise of Lorenzo. He observes that "Cicero himself, the fount
of eloquence, was an admirable prose writer but a failure in verse,"
whereas Virgil, the greatest ancient poet, "was as inferior in prose as he
was superior in poetry." This being the case, "we may proudly and not
unjustly prefer our Francesco to each of them, since he was so gloriously
successful in both prose and verse." 11
   Salutati's standards of measurement are the masters of Latin literature;
and although Landino's Petrarch is the author of sonnets and canzoni,
not a Latin epic (or Latin prose treatises), he too still assesses Petrarch
via-A-vis the Greek and Latin classics. But Pico compares Lorenzo with
vernacular poets. His de-mystifying comments on the founders of Italian
poetry might seem at first sight to lessen their stature; but if Dante is no
longer a fount of wisdom, or Petrarch the saint of humanism, they are
both acknowledged Florentine  classics,  veteres  seriptores.  Moreover,
they are literary figures whose works must bear judgment in the light
of subsequent poetry; Pico would not have sympathized with Bembo's
static vernacular classicism. His view of literary history might rather be
compared with that of the Latin poets of the Silver Age. Horace and
