In September 1486, Giovanni Pico wrote to Marsilio Ficino that some Chaldean books – if
they were books, and not treasures – had recently come into his hands, “possibly neither
by chance nor fortuitously, but by the decision of God and of the divine will propitiating
my studies”. The treasures he had laid his hand on were: (1) “the oracles of the magi Ezra,
Zoroaster and Melchiar, among which the oracles that, among the Greeks, circulate faulty
and incomplete [mendosa et mutila] can be read unaltered and complete [integra et abso-
luta]”; (2) “a brief and rugged interpretation (but full of mysteries) of the Chaldean
wisemen”; and (3) “a booklet on the dogmas of the Chaldean theology with a divine
and plentiful elucidation thereof by the Persians, the Greeks and the Chaldeans”.2 In
the following, I refer to this set of texts as M. On 15 October 1486, Pico wrote from

  The Chaldean Oracles of which Pico claimed to have found the original are a set of
hexametric texts in Greek. According to Hans Lewy, their author was Julianus the
Theurgist (second century), who transposed into hexameters the divine revelations
received by his father Julianus the Chaldean.4 Édouard des Places and Pierre Hadot,
however, warn that the sources do not allow for the drawing of a clear-cut conclusion
concerning the authorship of these texts.5 The Chaldean Oracles present, on the one
hand, information concerning the divine, demonic and material entities emanating
from the Father and, on the other, the instructions that the theurgist must follow in
order to let his soul separate from the body, ascend to the superlunary region and
“[shut] the god within her, completely inebriated” (dP97/Ps17/G11).6 The greatest part
of the Chaldean Oracles has been preserved in works by Proclus, Damascius and
Michael Psellus.7 Iamblichus wrote a Chaldean Theology, a small section of which survives
in Damascius’s De principiis (I.86)8; in Iamblichus’s extant works, the doctrines of the
Chaldeans are often referred to but no Chaldean oracle is quoted. Marinus informs us
that Proclus wrote an extensive commentary on the Chaldean Oracles9; the work is not
extant, but some passages  thereof have been handed down by  Psellus.10 In  late
ﬁfteenth-century Italy, the Chaldean Oracles could be read not only scattered throughout
Proclus’s works but also gathered together in the collections prepared by the Byzantine
historian and philosopher Michael Psellus (1018–1078) and by the Byzantine philosopher
Georgios Gemistos Plethon (c. 1355–1452). Psellus was the author of a commented collec-
tion of forty-one oracles, entitled “Ἐξήγησις τῶν χαλδαικῶν ῥητῶν”.11 Plethon derived
from  it a collection of thirty-four oracles (the “Μαγικὰλόγια τῶν ἀπὸΖωροάστρου
μάγων”, which, as the title makes clear, he attributed to the depositories of Zoroaster’s
doctrines – the Magi) and wrote a commentary thereon.12 One of the principal aims of
Psellus’s commentary was to point out that the Chaldean Oracles did not contradict
Christian doctrines and were, on the contrary, compatible with them. Ms. Vat. lat. 3122
contains a Latin translation of Psellus’s Ἐξήγησις; Enrico V. Maltese has suggested that
the author of this translation may be Giovanni Tortelli (c. 1400–1466) and that the
scribe of the manuscript may be identiﬁed as Demetrio Guazzelli (c. 1450–1511), the
ﬁrst oﬃcial custos of the Vatican Library.13
   In the inventories of Pico’s library, neither Psellus’s Ἐξήγησις nor Plethon’s collection
and commentary are recorded.14 Still, in Pico’s Commento sopra una canzona de amore
composta da Girolamo Benivieni, the translations of two oracles attested only in Psellus’s
Ἐξήγησις and Plethon’s Μαγικὰλόγια are found (dP165/Ps20/G13 and dP112/Ps18/
G21).15 Pico’s translation of the two oracles into Italian adheres to the Greek text; it
follows that Pico was familiar not only with the oracles quoted in Neoplatonic works
and in M, but also with either or both of Psellus’s and Plethon’s collections.
   Pico drew from M in his Oratio and, more extensively, in his Conclusiones. The only
direct quotation of a whole sentence from M is the “famous saying of the Chaldeans
[illud Chaldeorum]” reproduced in the so-called ﬁrst version of the Oratio (§ 44). In
the Oratio, Pico also paraphrases an extensive passage of the interpretes Chaldei (§§
130–34); in the ﬁrst version of Pico’s Oratio, this passage contains four words extrapolated
from M.16 Chaim Wirszubsky and Hans Jakob Polotsky have demonstrated that the

  A section of the Conclusiones bears the title Conclusiones numero XV secundum pro-
priam opinionem de intelligentia dictorum Zoroastris et expositorum eius Chaldeorum
(Fifteen Conclusions according to the Author’s Opinion as to How the Sayings of Zoroaster

clusions).18 In conclusions 8 > 5, 8 > 11 and 8 > 14, the Latin translations of three
oracles of Zoroaster’s from M are found. Another reference to M is found in conclusion
24.31: “Secundum precedentem conclusionem ex Procli mente potest exponi unum ex
dictis Zoroastris secundum quod apud Graecos legitur, quanquam apud Chaldeos aliter
et legitur et exponitur”.19 In other words, according to Pico, in their original version
the Chaldean oracles not only read diﬀerently but also evinced a diﬀerent meaning,
which the Greek version had obscured.
   In a recent article I have argued:

1. that the Latin translation of the pseudo-original text of dP162 that one reads in con-
   clusion 8 > 5 presents a lexical peculiarity, indicating that for this oracle the author
   of M based himself on Plethon’s collection;
2. that the discrepancies between the “famous saying of the Chaldeans” quoted by Pico in
   the ﬁrst version of his Oratio and its ostensible “cue” (dP157/Ps21/G3) indicate that the
   author of M was familiar with the speculations concerning man’s nature that Pico was
   to develop in his Oratio;
3. that God’s discourse to Adam in Oratio §§ 18–23 is an elaboration of the anagogical
   interpretation of Genesis 1:26 and 5:2 presented by the thirteenth-century Italian kabb-
    alist Menachem Recanati in his Commentary on the Torah. According to Recanati’s
   kabbalistic interpretation, while the souls of  all other created beings each derive
   from one single seﬁrah, man’s soul and body originate from, replicate and are tightly
   related to the seﬁrotic realm as a whole. In Pico’s view, kabbalistic lore thus revealed
   that Plato’s conception of man’s soul as the epitome of all created beings and the Aris-
   totelian description of the human soul as being made up of a vegetative soul (shared
   with plants), an appetitive soul (shared with animals) and a rational soul should be
   viewed as coherent with the hidden meaning of the biblical texts describing man’s cre-
   ation and his nature;
4. that the familiarity shown by the author of M with Hebrew, Aramaic, and Ethiopic
   characters, Recanati’s Commentary and Pico’s speculations points to the conclusion
   that  he  was none  other  than  Guglielmo Raimondo Moncada,  alias  Flavius
   Mithridates.20

  A converted Jew from Sicily, Moncada studied in Naples. In 1477, he went to Rome as a
protégé of Giovanni Battista Cybo (bishop of Molfetta and future Innocent VIII) and later
entered Sixtus IV’s court; it was in this period that he arguably learnt the Ethiopic alpha-
bet.21 In 1483, he traveled beyond the Alps, returning to Italy in 1485 where Pico hired
him as his teacher of Hebrew and Arabic and a translator of philosophical and kabbalistic
texts. Among the texts translated by Moncada are Recanati’s Commentary on the Torah
and Commentary on the Daily Prayers.22

   Pico’s belief in the authenticity of M must be considered within the context of the
humanistic attitude toward books and the knowledge and of its development. Petrarch
and the early humanists overturned the medieval conﬁdence that Western Christianity
had safeguarded all the theological, philosophical and scientiﬁc books that were worthy
of being preserved. The notion developed that a treasure of rhetorical, ethical and scientiﬁc
wisdom had been overlooked and neglected for centuries, and that all eﬀorts should be
made to search for and retrieve its witnesses and restore their original texts. The libraries
of monasteries were examined in search of Latin books; by the beginning of the ﬁfteenth
century, Greek books were sought after and sent for, ﬁrst in the south of Italy and then in
the Byzantine empire:

    It doesn’t matter that this ancient wisdom [included] forgeries and mystiﬁcations, and that
   the new “library” was full of apocrypha, and that this supposed antiquity contained much of
   the Middle Ages – and that many myths were constantly mingled with history. In the great
  myth of the “rebirth”, the apocrypha acquired an authenticity of their own, the myths became
   history and moved history.23

Thus, to give but one example, both Ficino and Pico accepted the attribution of the De
caelesti hierarchia to Dionysius the Aeropagite, which was yet questioned by Lorenzo
Valla.24 By identifying the ultimate source of Greek wisdom in the Egyptian Hermes Tris-
megistos and then, under Plethon’s inﬂuence, with Zoroaster, Ficino pushed the search for
wisdom further back into history. But if he was correct in assuming that the author of the
Chaldean Oracles was Zoroaster, the Greek Chaldean Oracles must be a translation – thus,
where should one look for the original Chaldean Oracles, and what was the language in
which they had originally been written?
    If physical books had been neglected and entire traditions lost sight of, the texts of the
books that had been preserved had not enjoyed a better fortune. Leonardo Bruni’s Dialogi
ad Petrum Histrum, written between 1401 and late 1407, has been described by Paolo Viti
as a “‘manifesto’ of the new humanistic culture”.25 Here Bruni recorded a conversation
that had taken place at Coluccio Salutati’s house, between Salutati and his younger
friends Niccolò Niccoli, Bruni and Roberto Rossi. Responding to Salutati, who had
reproached his friends for neglecting the art of disputing (“disputandi usum exercitatio-
nemque”), Niccoli had replied with a vehement discourse, culminating with the words:
“But as the better part of these books is lost, while those that remain are so faulty
[mendosi] that they are but  little removed from death, how do you think we are to
learn philosophy?”26 With these problems in mind, in his De fato et fortuna II.6 (1396–
1399), Coluccio Salutati expressed the wish that “teams of librarians should collate the
ancient manuscripts systematically in order to reach a sound and correct reading of the
texts”.27 Furthermore, due to their ignorance of Greek and classical Latin, medieval trans-
lators had produced versions that did not suit the standard set by the humanists. In the
foreword to his Latin translation of Aristotle’s Nicomachean Ethics (1416–1417), Bruni
harshly  criticized the version produced in 1243 by Robert Grosseteste, bishop of
Lincoln.28 Shortly afterwards, in his De recta interpretatione (1420), Bruni admitted to
having been “a little too vehement in [his] reproaches”,29 but claimed that

   this happened out of indignation, because, when I saw those books that in Greek are so full of
   elegance, so full of delectability, so full of an inestimable beauty, I grieved within myself and I
   was pained at seeing those very books polluted and disﬁgured by such a bad translation.30

   Finally, the humanists did not research in ancient texts only a rhetorical model but also
a positive treasure of knowledge. Concerning the humanistic attitude toward the scientiﬁc
texts of Greek Antiquity, George Sarton once wrote (and Eugenio Garin subscribed to his
words):

  The discovery of a new text was hailed with admiration and with joy, not as we hail today the
   resurrection of an Egyptian or Babylonian treatise, but rather as if that text contained new
   knowledge of immediate value. […] The discovery of a new text was not simply of archae-
   ological importance; it was (or might be, at least) a positive addition to the workable knowl-
   edge of contemporary men of science or physicians.31

How did Pico ﬁt within this context? In an essay entitled “Pico ﬁlologo”, published in
1994, Sebastiano Gentile remarked that in the ﬁrst version of the Oratio (§§ 110–17)
“we ﬁnd a methodological statement on Pico’s side, which, at least in the spirit and the
good intentions, brings him near to the soundest humanistic tradition”.32 The passage
reads as follows:

   These are, most reverend fathers, the things that did not prompt – but compelled – me to the
   study of philosophy. In order to fully attain what I so ardently aspired to, two are the things
   that I’ve always deemed of primary beneﬁt. The ﬁrst one was not to swear upon anybody’s
   word, to attend all philosophy teachers, to examine every single leaﬂet, to be acquainted with
    all the diﬀerent schools. I saw that to perform this work it was necessary to know not only
   Greek and Latin, but also Hebrew and Chaldean and – what I have for the ﬁrst time
   begun to sweat upon under [the guidance of] Mithridates Gulielmus [i.e. Moncada], consum-
   mate translator from those languages – Arabic. Indeed, almost every wisdom [sapientia]
   gushed out [manavit] from the Barbarians to the Greeks and from the Greeks to us. Thus,
   as concerns philosophy [in philosophandi ratione], our fellow countrymen have always
   relied on alien discoveries and deemed  it enough for them to adorn those alien things;
   they deemed it necessary to look for the most holy writings and the most secret mysteries,
   ﬁrst [primum] among the Jews and the Chaldeans, and secondarily [tum] among the
   Greeks. The Arabs partake in the other arts and all aspects of philosophy with the Greeks.
    If one did not consult them, how could one make any progress in those studies when very
  many [permulti] and the most precious of those books of theirs have not reached unto us,
   not even in a single translation, while as for those that have reached unto us [in translation],
  on the one hand their translators have more often perverted them rather than translated
  them and, on the other, they have veiled them with all kinds of obscurities, up to the
   point that what among them is easy, clear and plain, among us is rendered so pitiful,
   obscure and intricate that they thwart and frustrate the attempts of many scholars?33

The reference to the “fellow countrymen” who had deemed it necessary to look for the
most holy writings and the most secret mysteries among the Jews and the Chaldeans
was possibly an allusion to Ficino, who in his Theologia Platonica had assigned to Zoroas-
ter the ﬁrst position in the line of the prisci theologi. The “barbarians” from which omnis
sapientia had emanated to the Greeks and from the latter to the Latins were arguably the
Chaldeans, who are referred to as “Barbarians” in Proclus’s Platonic Theology.34 Pushing
even further back into history, taking up the archeological approach of his humanistic pre-
decessors and widening the geographical boundaries so as to include the Arabs, Pico had
engaged in studying the languages that would give him access to the original sources. In
putting this program into practice, he had encountered the same disappointments experi-
enced by Niccoli and Bruni; the very words used by Pico to characterize the original text of
the oracles and their Greek translation (“integra”, “mendosa”, “mutila”) were part of the

ries, engaging in the study of the languages in which they were written, comparing the
(Greek) translation of the Chaldean Oracles with the oracles found in M, revealing that
the translation known in the West was faulty and misleading and ﬁnally pointing out
the contrasting interpretations of the Chaldeans and the Greeks, Pico was walking in
the footsteps of the humanists who had preceded him.
   Gentile nonetheless formulated a sharply negative judgment of Pico’s “philological”
activities. Until 1488, Gentile writes, Pico displayed very few signs of practicing

   the same kind of activity at which his dearest fellow Angelo Poliziano excelled, namely phi-
   lology […] only at the end of his life did [Pico] approach the texts in a more rigorous manner
  […] The fruits of this activity [i.e. Pico’s search for books and his study of Greek, Hebrew and
   Chaldean] were the Conclusiones, which in their ﬁnal version certainly do not reﬂect a scru-
   tiny of the texts as systematic and faithful as the one a “philologist” would perform, but rather
   the research – admirable and disorderly at the same time – of an extraordinarily sharp mind,
   which however summarizes and distorts his sources in an all but “philological” way and
   makes it extremely hard for the modern reader to identify the exact origin of the texts he
   intended to submit to the assembly of the wise men that, as we know, never took place.36

And as a perfect illustration of Pico’s inadequacy in the ﬁeld of philology, Gentile made
reference to M and Pico’s reliance on it.37
   Gentile’s judgment is based on a comparison between Pico and Poliziano, where Poli-
ziano is taken as a standard, and not on an analysis of the Conclusiones themselves. As the
passage quoted above makes clear, Pico’s perception of his own work was radically
diﬀerent. Since the Conclusiones were statements that Pico planned to illustrate or demon-
strate during a debate that never took place, one cannot determine exactly what doctrines
Pico meant to sustain.38 Still, as the eﬀorts of various scholars have shown, one can often
determine with a fair degree of certainty the source or sources upon which Pico planned to
base his individual statements. In his Oratio, Pico described the scientia cabale as “secre-
tiorem quoque et veram legis enarrationem” (§ 235) and as “ineﬀabilem de supersubstan-
tiali deitate theologiam, sapientiae fontem, idest de intelligibilibus angelicisque formis
exactam metaphysicam, idest de rebus naturalibus ﬁrmissimam philosophiam” (§ 250).
The primary rank that Pico assigned to Kabbalah is made evident by the very structure
of his Conclusiones, which are divided into two parts, both culminating in a section
devoted to kabbalistic doctrines. Did Pico agree with his “fellow countrymen’s” assump-
tion, according to which the Chaldeans’ books were as holy and full of secret mysteries as
those of the Jews? What kind of relationship did he perceive between kabbalistic and Zor-
oastrian texts? And if there was any, how did he mean to account for it?
  The ﬁrst section of this article provides an initial assessment of the role of Kabbalah in
Pico’s Zoroastrian Conclusions. Then, after presenting a sketch of the major themes
touched upon by Pico in this set of conclusions and an overview of the tenets of Recanati’s
Zohar-based Kabbalah, I oﬀer an analysis of the sources of conclusions 8 > 6 and 8 > 7
which shows that, according to Pico, a comparison of Zoroastrian magic and Recanati’s
Commentary on the Torah demonstrated not only their being based on the same theurgical
principles but also their common condemnation of witchcraft (which in Recanati’s Com-
mentary on the Torah is equated with idolatry, whose archetypal form is identiﬁed with the
original sin). In the second section I oﬀer an analysis of conclusion 8 > 11, which points
out that: (1) the oracle quoted in this conclusion is demonstrably a back translation of

concrete demonstration of the mutual dependence – and, in the last analysis, the corre-

1. Pico’s Zoroastrian Conclusions and Recanati’s Commentary on the Torah:
some preliminary remarks

The Zoroastrian Conclusions represents the last but three in the series of Pico’s Conclu-
siones, being followed only by the twenty-six magical conclusions, the  thirty-one
Orphic conclusions and the kabbalistic conclusions according to Pico’s opinion. In his
Zoroastrian Conclusions, Pico draws and comments on the various texts described in
his letter to Ficino. Depending on the section of M on which they are based, we can classify
them as follows:

1. conclusions in which Zoroaster’s oracles are fully quoted in Latin translation (8 > 5, 8 >
   11, 8 > 14);
2. conclusions in which reference is made either to oracles by Zoroaster or to terms used
   therein (8 > 1, 8 > 3, 8 > 5, 8 > 10, 8 > 11, 8 > 12, 8 > 14);
3. conclusions in which amphorismi (i.e. oracles) are referred to but no clue as to their
   speciﬁc author is given (8 > 6, 8 > 7, 8 > 8);
4. conclusions where the interpretation of the Chaldean interpreters is referred to (8 > 1,
   8 > 5, 8 > 6, 8 > 7);
5. conclusions where remarks of unspeciﬁed interpretes are referred to (8 > 2, 8 > 3, 8 >
   13);
6. conclusions where the interpretation of the Magi is referred to (8 > 8);
7. conclusions where the interpretation of the expositores is referred to (8 > 10).

   Pico’s Zoroastrian Conclusions can be thematically divided as follows: (1) the structure
of the emanation (8 > 1); (2) the possibility for the magician to operate with animated
beings (8 > 2); (3) the animated nature of the creation (8 > 3–8 > 4); (4) man’s soul, uniﬁ-
cation with the Godhead and idolatrous practices (8 > 5–8 > 13); (5) the end times (8 > 14);
and (6) again the animated structure of the creation, namely good and evil angelic entities
(8 > 15). In seven conclusions, more or less explicit parallels with kabbalistic notions
appear (8 > 5, 8 > 6, 8 > 7, 8 > 9, 8 > 11, 8 > 14, 8 > 15). The kabbalistic notions hinted at
by Pico in ﬁve of these conclusions can be traced back to Recanati’s Commentary on

  According to Recanati’s Zohar-based Kabbalah, prayer is a process through which man
puriﬁes his mind and body and brings about the uniﬁcation of the ten seﬁrot (the ema-
nated entities through which God has created the world). Each part of man’s body corre-
sponds with a seﬁrah, and bodily puriﬁcation makes it possible to establish a theurgical
connection between man and the seﬁrotic realm. Each ritual act has the purpose of pur-
ifying a speciﬁc part of the body, whereas, on the other hand, each part of the liturgy – that
is, the single prayers, the single sentences and the single words of each prayer – aims at
unifying one seﬁrah with the other. The Šema‘  Yiśra’el, the culmination of Jewish
prayer, is not simply the proclamation of God’s oneness – it also brings about the theur-
gical uniﬁcation of all the seﬁrot and the union of the worshipper with the tenth seﬁrah,
which represents a state of perfect communion with God. By contrast, all impure thoughts
and all violations of any of God’s commandments are caused by (and cause) bodily and
spiritual impurity; any failure to achieve bodily puriﬁcation will be bound to impair the
seﬁrotic realm and strengthen the disruptive power of the ﬁfth seﬁrah. The Šechinah,
i.e. the tenth and lowest seﬁrah, represents the aim of the worshipper wishing to
commune with God and the passageway of all divine power from the seﬁrotic realm to
the celestial and lower world. Its action depends on the power it receives from above;
when the worshipper acts in compliance with God’s commandments, the tenth seﬁrah
is well connected to the upper seﬁrot and thus receives the inﬂuence of the fourth
seﬁrah, i.e. piety – and when this is the case, the tenth seﬁrah pours benign eﬀects onto
the world and elevates the worshipper. By contrast, when the worshipper is not properly
puriﬁed, the tenth seﬁrah remains separate from the rest of the seﬁrotic realm, and the
maleﬁcent inﬂuence of the ﬁfth seﬁrah is received instead; it is then called “Middat ha-
Din [the property of Judgment]”, which inﬂicts calamity upon the world and nourishes
the demons that have the power to prevent the worshipper from ascending and bringing
about the theurgical aim of prayer.

the prohibition is meant to foil a certain bodily contamination of the soul, whereas in
Pico’s Latin translation the prohibition is connected to the passage of an elusive entity
called Lictor. In an attempt to identify this entity, let us now turn to Pico’s Hebrew sources:

What did Pico mean when he stated that the Chaldean oracle could be perfectly explained
when compared with God’s injunction to the Israelites not to go out of their houses on the
night of Passover? Judging from these passages, one can safely argue that Pico identiﬁed
the Lictor with Middat ha-Din (all the more if one considers the last biblical quotation
found in the passage translated above, where the “passing” of Kavod,  i.e. the tenth
seﬁrah, is interpreted by Recanati as a threat from which Israel is protected). In order
to test this hypothesis, however, one must explain on what grounds Pico might have ident-
iﬁed the Lictor with Middat ha-Din.
  The term translated with “lictor” must be a Hebrew (or Aramaic) word. In the passage
quoted above the term ispeqlatorin appears. Marcus Jastrow translates the Aramaic term
ispeqlator as “guardsman”, “arms-bearer”.74 This may seem a suitable correspondent for
the Latin “lictor”. Indeed, in ancient Rome lictors were bodyguards charged with the pro-
tection of the king, and, later, of Roman magistrates; they were armed with bundles of rods
in order to smite anyone who might dare to approach. The oracle, however, speaks of one
single lictor, and the ispeqlatorin mentioned in the passage above may be connected to
Middat ha-Din only in a tortuous way. One may therefore try another path and search
for the term “lictor” in the Latin translations of kabbalistic texts made by Moncada for
Pico. In the translations hitherto published in the series The Kabbalistic Library of Pico
della Mirandola, the term “lictor” does not occur. Still, one occurrence of the term can
be found in the Latin version of Sefer ha-tzeruf, a work generally attributed to the circle
of Avraham Abulaﬁa. The translation bears the title Liber combinationum and appears
at the beginning of Ms. Vat. ebr. 190, which contains a series of translations made by
Moncada only after he turned into Latin Recanati’s Commentary on the Torah.75 In this
text one reads the following passage:

   Dies autem ly septima est Ginath bitham, paradisus domus, cuius secretum est melech
   amaxiah, id est christus rex, cuius secretum est hezrath, id est salvator, et ipse est qui
   accepit ab eo sex dies Geneseos ut essent sub potestate eius et regere eas, et hec est diﬀerencia
   inter portam que dicitur traha et salvatorem qui dicitur hezrath, quia addidit illi et recepit ab
   eo. Et secretum hoc est quod indicat sex litteras que sunt in nomine Mattatron, quia nomen
   unius est ut nomen alterius, et sic vocatur omnis custos qui dicitur lictor. Inde barbari seu
   greci vocant lictorem monterium, quod non derivatur a suo ydiomate sed ab hoc. Et hic
    est de quo scribitur Ecce ego micto angelum ante te ut et cetera [Exodus 23:20].76

niqre kol šomer [and thus is called every guardian]”. The words “qui dicitur lictor” do not
ﬁnd a counterpart in the Hebrew text. They can be interpreted as a gloss aimed at convey-
ing to the reader (i.e. Pico) the notion that the custos spoken of in the Hebrew text was not
a generic “guardian”, but namely the one that Pico knew as lictor. This gloss may therefore

The root š.m.r. is associated with the tenth seﬁrah and Šomer Yiśra’el is an epithet of the
tenth seﬁrah. As we have seen above, when the tenth seﬁrah operates under the inﬂuence
of an upper seﬁrah, it can happen to be called after the name of that very seﬁrah. Accord-
ingly, here the tenth seﬁrah is called Middat ha-Din – which, properly speaking, would be
an epithet of the ﬁfth seﬁrah.78 By hinting at the equivalence of mišmeret and matrat,
Recanati means to point out the equivalence of Šomer Yiśra’el and Metatron. In the
Oratio (§ 35), as Pico enumerates the texts alluding to the transformation of single men
into entities of a different nature, he writes: “Nam et Hebreorum theologia secretior
nunc Enoch sanctum in angelum divinitatis, quem vocant מטטרון [i.e. Metatron], nunc
in alia alios numina reformant”.79 With all of this considered, one can conclude that in
M8 > 11 the term translated by Pico with lictor was šomer.
   This assumption must yet stand a test. The comparison between Ps3/G16 and P8 > 11
has allowed us to reconstruct with a fair degree of certainty part of how M8 > 11 must read
and to explain the discrepancies between Ps3/G16 and P8 > 11 as diﬀerent interpretations
of M8 > 11. In order to prove that indeed Lictor construed šomer, suspension of disbelief is
required once more and the following question must be asked: how did it happen that the
word šomer gave origin to the (faulty) translations “τι ἔχουσα ἐξίῃ” (Ps3) and “ἐξιοῦσα ἔχῃ
τι” (G16)? A comparison between G16 and M8 > 11 does not seem to oﬀer any suitable
explanation. By contrast, faulty as it might be, the rendering of šomer with “τι ἔχουσα”
can be rationally accounted for. The stem pa‘al of the root š.m.r. has various meanings:
“to observe” (e. g. the Shabbat or a commandment), “to keep” (e.g. a covenant), “to
protect”. The Greek verb “ἔχω” is no less polysemous. Its primary meaning is “to have”,
but it also means, among other things, “to carry”, “to keep” and, with reference to divi-
nities, “to  protect”. Hence, Hebrew “šamar” and Greek “ἔχω” share  at  least one
meaning: “to protect”. In short, Moncada arguably meant to suggest that, as the shift
from  the masculine  to  the feminine and  the superﬂuous  interpolation  of  “ἐξίῃ”
conﬁrmed, due to his lack of familiarity with the kabbalistic lexicon, the Greek translator
had failed to understand the true meaning of “šomer” and misinterpreted it as referring to
the soul. One can therefore conclude that for M8 > 11 Moncada based himself on Ps3.

  Why did Moncada decide to render šomer with “lictor”? This choice may have been dic-
tated by Moncada’s desire to convey that, like the Roman lictor, given its association with
Middat ha-Din, the šomer was not simply a guardian: he was a protector, but also a chas-
tiser.80 Be that as it may, if the line of reasoning that I have developed is correct, by con-
clusion 8 > 11 Pico meant to demonstrate Zoroaster’s familiarity with the kabbalistic
notion according to which what determines the disposition of the tenth seﬁrah, which
can alternately favor or impede man’s endeavors at communing with the divine, is his
degree of bodily and spiritual purity, that  is, his conduct – his freedom to choose
whether to be plant, animal, man, angel or God, as Pico put it in his Oratio. Bodily or spiri-
tual impurity is bound to turn the tenth seﬁrah – which, if man complies with the divine
commandments, represents the divine entity with which he can totally unify – into Middat
ha-Din, which will in turn bring punishment to the inadequate worshipper – i.e. to all
those who ignore the secrets of the godhead and the creation.


3. Conclusion

Sometime during the ﬁrst half of 1486, after having translated Recanati’s Commentary on
the Torah for Giovanni Pico, Guglielmo Raimondo Moncada produced a pseudo-original
of the Chaldean Oracles, implemented with commentaries and additional material.
Moncada embedded in M such terms and notions as might suggest the existence of doc-
trinal aﬃnities between Zoroastrian magic and Kabbalah. His purpose was arguably to
provide Pico with the missing link in the chain connecting Kabbalah, whose mythical
origins lay on the Sinai, to Plato and Greece at large. The pseudo-original oracles con-
tained in M were based partially on Psellus’s and partially on Plethon’s collection, depend-
ing on the reading that best suited Moncada’s aims. M8 > 11 was based on Ps3 (whose
reading diﬀers from G16), which in turn was an interpretative elaboration of the initial
words of Plotinus’s Henneads I.9, where Psellus saw an embedded Chaldean oracle. In
his back translation, Moncada took the very textual element that represented the core
of Psellus’s concern and turned it into a seﬁrotic name. This was hardly a coincidence;
Moncada arguably meant to suggest that due to his ignorance of the kabbalistic lexicon
and doctrines, the Greek translator had misinterpreted M8 > 11, thus misleading later
interpreters.
  According to Pico, Kabbalah was a divine interpretation of the Torah explaining how
the latter could be read as an anagogical depiction of the structure of the Godhead, the
celestial and lower worlds and man’s body and soul, as well as an explanation of the
fact that divine commandments can serve as a means of attaining perfect uniﬁcation
with the Godhead, whereas their infringement leads to bodily and spiritual impurity
and thus to illicit and idolatrous communion with demons. Modeled on Pico’s recent dis-
coveries, expectations and desires, M easily won his reliance. Through the analysis of
M8 > 11 one could see that Zoroaster’s oracles and Kabbalah even presented lexical par-
allels that the Greek translator had failed to render. As his praise of Plotinus in Oratio
§ 226 shows, however, Pico does not seem to have attributed an excessive importance
to such linguistic discrepancies. Indeed, in the last version of the Oratio, Pico removed
the distinction in rank between the Jews and the Chaldeans, on the one side, and the
Greeks, on the other. Certainly, its divine origin set Kabbalah at a higher level of precision
and reliability; still, what probably retained the greatest importance for Pico was that the

## Page 21

92        G. CORAZZOL


discovery of the doctrinal and lexical concord between Kabbalah and the oracles of
Zoroaster provided the hinge on which the concord between Greek thought and Kabbalah
could turn. After all, faulty as his interpretation of the words “ἔχουσά τι” might be, Plo-
tinus’s words and his interpretation of Phaedo 62a–c were perfectly in line with kabbalistic
doctrines and, anagogically intended, the injunction not to commit suicide under any cir-
cumstances could be interpreted as an injunction for the soul not to leave the body unless
in a state of perfect purity.
  The method of analysis of the holy texts used by Pico to formulate his Zoroastrian con-
clusions was based on the research of books, the study of languages, the comparison of
original texts with their translations and the textual comparison of books written by
diﬀerent authors – i.e. the very activities in which Pico’s fellow humanists engaged. The
peculiarity of Pico’s enterprise was that he applied these methods to texts with which
no other humanist was familiar and which he came to identify as the most complete
and trustworthy treasuries of divine doctrines.
  As Pico wrote to Ficino, he was seriously disposed to thinking that M had come into his
hands by God’s decision. Since Petrarch, humanists had strived to smooth the contrasts
that may have been perceived between studia humanitatis and studia divinitatis, conceiv-
ing of studia humanitatis as a means of moral, religious and political renovation. In a letter
to Giovanni Dominici, shortly before his death in 1406, Coluccio Salutati argued that
without a perfect knowledge of languages and their grammar one could comprehend
neither the Scriptures nor the commentaries of the fathers of the Church, concluding:
“Humanistic studies and divinities are so closely bound together that a true and complete
knowledge of either of them cannot be attained without the other [Connexa sunt huma-
nitatis studia; connexa sunt et studia divinitatis, ut unius rei sine alia vera completaque
Scientia non possit haberi]”.81 By his Conclusiones Pico arguably meant to prove that
the bond between studia humanitatis and studia divinitatis was even tighter; the former
was not merely a tool for the study of the latter, but rather – even more signiﬁcantly –
the historical movement that had ignited the process leading to the rediscovery of
Latin, Greek, Hermetic, Zoroastrian and kabbalistic books. In Pico’s Conclusiones the
boundary between studia humanitatis and studia divinitatis was ﬁnally superseded. In
this sense, one can reasonably argue that Pico viewed his Oratio and Conclusiones as
the virtual accomplishment of studia humanitatis. The Sinaitic origin of Kabbalah was
a myth and M was a forgery, but the process and method through which Pico had
created and pinpointed that myth were fully in the spirit of studia humanitatis: the
feeling that something vital had been overlooked and lost – and the conﬁdence that
through eﬀort and study this loss might be overcome.

Abbreviations

chretism in the West; as concerns the Chaldean oracles quoted in the article, “dP” refers to the oracle
in Des Places’s edition, “Ps” to the oracles in Psellus’s Ἐξήγησις τῶν χαλδαικῶν ῥητῶν and “G” to
the oracles in Georgios Gemistos Plethon’s collection; the number following these abbreviations
refers whether to the number attributed to the oracle by the editors (dP and G) or to the relative
position of the single oracles within the collection (Ps). In references to Talmudic tractates, “b”
refers to the Babylonian Talmud. Manuscripts from the Hebrew collection of the Biblioteca Apos-
tolica Vaticana are referred to as “Vat. ebr.” Save for some modiﬁcations, the translations of biblical
quotations are taken from the King James Bible.
