   According to Pico, at issue in the Neoplatonic elevation of
unity over being are the following two claims: (1) unity  is
“simpler” (simplicius) than being, and (2) unity is “more com-
mon” (communius)  or  universal  than  being.11 While  Pico
himself offers  little explanation about what is entailed in his
first  claim—unity’s  greater  simplicity—his  meaning  is  not
difficult  to  discern  given  the  commonplace  Neoplatonic
understanding of being as involving some sort of duality or
multiplicity. As  is well known, for Plotinus being, which he
identifies  with  noûs,  involves  the  duality  of knower and
known.12  Prior  to  Plotinus,  Plato  himself  located  plurality
within being through  his introduction of the Form of the
Other13 or, as Giovanni Reale puts  it, the “Infinite Dyad.”14
Inasmuch as unity transcends being, then,  it transcends  dif-
ference,  plurality, and multiplicity, and retains a primordial
simplicity.
  With respect to the second claim, namely, that unity is “more
common” than being, Pico understands the matter in a fashion
similar to that of Thomas. In a passage from De Malo (q. 1, a.
2), Thomas considers the relationship among good, evil, and
being, and in so doing notes clearly the Platonic elevation of the
good over being. On Thomas’s reading, the Platonic theory
boils down to the claim that the good has the greatest extension

   In the end, Pico’s efforts to reconcile a Platonic henology
with an Aristotelian ontology involve compromises for both the
Aristotelian and the Platonist; each camp has its bitter pills to
swallow. Pico’s project, as found in De ente et uno and, one
may speculate, envisioned more generally in the Concord of
Plato and Aristotle, may not be quite the synthesis he promises,
but it may be more accurately characterized as an overcoming—
in the Heidegerrian sense of Verwindung117—of the two in a
direction that favors the metaphysical vision of Thomas Aqui-
nas. Of course, given Pico’s own eclectic proclivities, one  is
hardly surprised  if, in the end, even his use of Thomas  is
attenuated, subjected to the synthesizing tendencies of the same
author who gave us the 900 theses, a cobbling of irreducibly
distinct positions that he somehow made work and was willing
