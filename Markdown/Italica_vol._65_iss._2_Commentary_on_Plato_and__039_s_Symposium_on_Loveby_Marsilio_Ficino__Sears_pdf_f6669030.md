Marsilio Ficino: Commentary on Plato's Symposium on Love. Translated,
   with an Introductionand Notes by SearsJayne.Dallas, SpringPublications,
   1985. Pp. 213.
  When Marsilio Ficino translated into Latin the entirety of Plato's works
he gave the greatest possible impetus to Francesco Petrarca's dream, a
century earlier, of reviving Platonism. Thanks to the researchof Paul Oskar
Kristeller and many other scholars, the unfolding of that revival and its
penetration of all the arts as well as of Renaissance philosophy is now a
well-known  story. Though today we read Plato's acknowledged literary
masterpiece with eyes quite differentfrom those of Ficino and his followers,
the Symposium has never lost its magic. The picture of Athenian society, the
gradatedspeeches in praise of love culminating in Diotima's ladderonly to be
upstaged by the drunken Alcibiades' encomium of Socrates-what reader
can forget them? Little wonder that the Italian Renaissance, whose favorite
themes were those of the Symposium itself, should have embracedFicino's
Commentary to the point of reiterating it in a new literary '.enre, the
Platonic trattato d'amore.
   Sears Jayne,well known to students of Ficino both for his 1944 edition of
the Commentary and for his article, "Ficinoand the Platonism of the English
Renaissance" (ComparativeLiterature4, 1952, 214-38), has made admirable
use of the forty intervening years of scholarshipsince his earlieredition. The
present translation is based on Raymond Marcel's 1956 edition of Ficino's
autographof the De amore with infrequentdeparturescarefullynoted.
   In his Introduction to this carefully edited and attractively printed
volume Jayne places strong emphasis on both the non-rational and the
esoteric aspects of Ficino's treatise. "What Ficino is trying to do in the De
amore is to defend the proprietyof personallove by showing that it is merely
a natural part of a perfectly respectable cosmic process...  ." (12-13). Its
subsequent history throughout Western Europe, he declares, shows the
strong appeal of its idealization of love to an aristocratic elite. "What he
intended for his friends to see in it was its esoteric meaning, the reasoned
defense of 'Socratic'love" (18).
   Jayne points out that Ficino's actual commentary is limited to only six
specific passages of Plato's dialogue; its main thrust consists rather in the
exposition of Ficino's ideas about love. Ficino's awareness of the dual nature
of his treatise is reflected in a disparity of titles: he called his 1474 Tuscan
version of the work Sopralo amore and often called his original Latin treatise
simply De amore with no referenceto "Commentary."
   Ficino, following in part the emanative system of Plotinus, traces the
human soul's descent from, and return to, the Deity, as "partof a natural
cosmic process" (Jayne).His edition of Plato's Opera,as is well known, was
the basic edition for all Europefor some three centuries. That his readingof
Plato is not without relevance even for today's readermay be illustrated by
his intuition (155) that Plato's description of Love is also, in fact primarily,a
description of Socrates-a  situation that would have proven awkwardhad
Plato not attributed the entire portrayalto Diotima ratherthan to Socrates.

Country,  painful  settling   in, and hardwon  assimilation   into  the
mainstream-has   by now been satisfactorily  detailed by' historians,

conceived it, he tells us in the Preface,as a "personalmemoir" for his many
grandchildren,"a little segment of urbanmiddle class America" (13).
