  While the surgery comprised only about twenty per cent of the al-Tasrif, very
little else was translated. The twenty-eighth treatise, part of the pharmaceutical
section, was translated by Simon of Genoa and Abraham Judaeus of Tortosa at

widely read, and was printed at Venice as early as 1471. Two other sections, the
first and second treatises, were also translated into Latin, but the rest of the al-
Tasrif remained untranslated and almost unknown, although such authors as
Nicholas of Salerno incorporated much of the al-Tasrif into their own works, in
this case the Antidotarium, without giving credit to its author. Surprisingly al-
Zahrawl was generally ignored in the Islamic world, and as a result considerable
misinformation has accumulated about him and his writings. Even though Lucien
Leclerc, George Sarton, and others, had established the basic facts about him,
some other recent writers ignored their findings.
  The book under review adds considerably more detail to the biographical infor-
mation, brings some sort of classification scheme to the al-Tasrif, and most im-
portantly, since the authors are pharmacists as well as historians, translates and
comments on exerpts of the twenty-fifth treatise on adhdn. Al-adhdn can be freely
translated as the fatness or oily essence which can be extracted from various
substances by pharmaceutical processes. In effect it is a sort of technical guide for
would-be pharmacists, and it is one of the theses of this book that the extensive
instruction and information on pharmaceutical processes given by al-Zahrawi led
to the development of professional pharmacy in Spain. While this seems too
strong a statement for the available evidence, the thesis is at least worth consider-
ing. The processes given by al-Zahrawi for extracting various oils are of interest
not only to pharmacists, but to all those interested in the history of chemistry and
of technology. Obviously such devices as the present day separatory funnel were
much more widely used than has often been realized.
  The book is well footnoted, adequately indexed, and includes an impressive
bibliography. It adds a great deal to the meager amount of information previously
available on al-Zahrawi, throws some light on the sources of mediaeval medicine,
and is a pioneer study in the development of pharmacological techniques. At the
same time it tends to make al-Zahrawi a much more important figure in the
development of pharmacy than has generally been recognized.
                                        VERN L. BULLOUGH

SEABB JAYNE, John Colet and Marsilio  Ficino. London and New York: Oxford University Press,
   1963. Pp. 172; 6 plates. $7.20.
THE subject of this book is the information provided by the marginal annotations
in John Colet's copy of Marsilio Ficino's Epistolae (Venice, 1495). Colet's own
copy of the Epistolae, while known shortly after the turn of this century, was
recently rediscovered in the library of All Souls College, Oxford, by Mr Neil Ker,
to whom the present study is dedicated, and referred by him to Professor Sears
Jayne.

  The author edits and translates all the manuscript materials in Colet's hand;
these consist of copies of two letters from Ficino, a draft of Colet's reply, and some
five thousand words of marginalia. The marginal notes range from one-word sub-
ject headings to paraphrases of Memo's text, and lengthy comments of Colet's
own concerning the topic under discussion by Ficino. The edition of the mar-
ginalia is designed for use in conjunction with the 1495 edition of the Epistolae. It
is arranged on the page in three parallel columns with, from left to right, the loca-
tion of the gloss by folio and line in the printed text of the Epistolae, the Latin text
of Colet's annotation, and its translation, the whole being supplied with extensive
footnotes and cross-references. In addition the author lists the seventeen tracts
from the table of contents of the Epistolae which Colet marked for reading, and
prints those passages from Ficino's text which Colet underlined.
  The marginal comments add considerably to the limited amount of source
material available for the study of Colet's life and thought. Since John Colet "is a
man of whom we write much more than we know," the new information necessi-
tated a re-examination of both the details of Colet's biography and the relation-
ship between Colet and Ficino. It is to this re-examination that the first half of
the book is devoted. The author is able to fill out somewhat the meager account
of Colet's twenty-one years at Oxford, and to suggest a new chronology for Colet's
major writings during those years.
  The revision of the standard account of Colet's relationship to Ficino is a sub-
stantial one. While most of Colet's biographers have suggested that he visited
Ficino in Florence, Colet's letter to Ficino makes it plain that they had not yet
met; and this letter was almost certainly written after 1496, following which time
neither of the two men left his own country. With regard to Colet's intellectual
relationship to Ficino's Neo-Platonism, the new evidence in the All Souls volume
helps to resolve the two conflicting views propounded by Colet's biographers —
on the one hand, that Colet was committed to classical philosophy, on the other,
that he deplored it. Professor Jayne considers both views to contain an element of
truth, but that essentially "one belongs after the other." Colet turned to Neo-
Platonism for help in his studies of the writings of St Paul, which were considered
to be deeply Hellenized; but his own insistence upon a fundamentally moral ap-
proach to salvation conflicted directly with the excessively intellectual emphasis
of the Ficinians. Eventually, therefore, he rejected the classics and enjoined his
students against reading philosophers.
  The brief volume concludes with a discussion of the problem of identifying
Colet's hand, accompanied by five representative plates, and an excellent bibliog-
raphy. In spite of the problems involved in producing such a text, the Oxford
University Press has produced a fine book, both technically and esthetically. The
following typographical errors were, however, noted in passing: p. 3, 1. 2, for
"?1467" read ?U66; p. 18, n. 1, for "ta" read vita; p. 36, n. 1, for "B.D. neces-
sary regency" read M.A. necessary regency; p. 63,1. 15, for "xii" read xiii; p. 89,
1. 35, for "desier" read desire; p. 127,11. 4,6, for "daemon" read demon; p. 142, n.
3, for "no. 9" read n. 9.

  The book ably demonstrates what can be achieved through the careful explora-
tion of marginal glosses. It is difficult to interpret and reconstruct a meaningful
picture of a man's thought from fragmentary comments; there is a temptation
to read into them meanings never intended by their author. It is also important
to realize that marginalia  "will not when reassembled constitute a coherent
treatise." Cognizant of these pitfalls, Professor Jayne has used this source in an
accurate and realistic fashion to enlarge our knowledge of Colet's life and to dis-
cern his place in Renaissance thought.

  St Nicholas is often said to be the most popular saint of the Middle Ages, and
his popularity is reflected in the fact that the first mediaeval secular dramas had
St Nicholas as their subject. Professor Jones suggests for this popularity a most
simple explanation: the legends of St Nicholas were crassly sensational, his
powers were of advantage to mariners and merchants, and the rise of his cult was
coincident with the rise of trade and the bourgeoisie — in short, "the most popular
saint in Christendom may prove to be the least essentially religious saint of all"
(p. 4).
  The purpose of this monograph is to "define the effect of a popular image by
tracing the spread of the Christian worship that incorporated it" (p. 4). Its con-
tent is highly technical, and its style is if anything too spare. Chapter II attempts
to establish that the British Museum manuscript of the Nicholas liturgy, Cotton
Nero E. 1, was written down under the direction of Wulfstan (d. 1092), and that
Lorraine, not Normandy, was the source of Wulfstan's Nicholas cult. Chapter III
is a text of this manuscript; the notes, which compare it to other manuscripts, use
a truncated system of abbreviations that will mean little except to liturgical
scholars. Chapter IV lists eleven legends of St Nicholas, reporting about each of
them five kinds of bibliographical information labeled a, b, c, etc., so that the
reader must keep referring back to the key. Chapter V discusses two legends about
the St Nicholas liturgy — one concerning its introduction into the Cluniac office,
the other concerning its composition in Bari. The second of these, about a youth
in love with a widow, shows unmistakable influence from courtly love, and de-
serves attention as an example of the way mediaeval clerks could "develop their
courtly idiom while still limited to pious subjects" (p. 63). Chapter VI argues that
the liturgy was written by Reginold of Eichstatt (d. 991) before he became bishop
in 966, and suggests that he may have used some lost Byzantine compositions.
